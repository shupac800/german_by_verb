# TRAUEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Trauen** means to trust, to dare, to marry. These three senses, seemingly unrelated, share a common root in Old High German *truen* — to have confidence, to believe in. To trust is to have confidence in another. To dare is to have confidence in yourself. To marry is to entrust yourself to another before witnesses. Hold this thread of confidence and faith in mind: every prefix below modifies that core act of trusting.

---

## The Prefixes

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive, directed at an object)

**Betrauen:** to entrust with. The prefix *be-* takes the act of trusting and stamps it onto a specific person and task. You *betraust* someone with a responsibility — you entrust them, you place your confidence in them to carry out a charge. This is formal and official: a minister is *betraut* with a portfolio, an employee is *betraut* with a project.

Mnemonic: "be-" = "apply trust onto." You are not just trusting in general; you are trusting *this person* with *this task*.

### GE- (intensifier / archaic completive)

**Getrauen:** to dare, to venture. The prefix *ge-* here is an old intensifier, reinforcing the "dare" sense of *trauen*. *Sich getrauen* means to summon up enough confidence to attempt something — to dare. This form is literary and somewhat archaic; modern German more commonly uses *sich trauen* or *wagen*. But the meaning is transparent: mustering trust in yourself sufficiently to act.

Mnemonic: think of gathering (*ge-*) your courage. You collect enough confidence to dare.

### HER- ("hither, toward the speaker")

**Hertrauen:** to dare to come here. The prefix *her-* directs the daring toward the speaker's location. *Sich hertrauen* means to venture near, to dare to approach. The child doesn't *traut sich her* — doesn't dare come over. It is a small, specific compound: self-confidence directed spatially toward a point.

Literal mnemonic: "dare hither." Do you dare come here?

### MISS- ("wrong, ill, against")

**Misstrauen:** to trust wrongly, to distrust. The prefix *miss-* negates or inverts. Where *trauen* is to have confidence, *misstrauen* is to withhold it — to suspect, to doubt, to be suspicious of. German *miss-* works exactly like English "mis-" or "dis-" here: trust becomes mistrust, confidence becomes suspicion.

Literal mnemonic: "mistrust." The English and German words are cognates and mean exactly the same thing.

### VER- (intensification, deepening, commitment)

**Vertrauen:** to trust deeply, to confide in, to have faith in. The prefix *ver-* intensifies and commits. Where *trauen* is to trust, *vertrauen* is to trust fully — to place your confidence irrevocably, to confide. You *vertraust* a friend with your secrets. You *vertraust* in God. The noun *Vertrauen* means trust, confidence, faith — the state of having given your trust completely. *Ver-* here deepens the basic act into something abiding and consequential.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "thoroughly, completely." *Vertrauen* is trust carried through to full commitment. English "confide" (from Latin *con-* + *fidere* = trust together fully) captures the same intensification.

### ZU- ("to, toward, credited to")

**Zutrauen:** to trust to, to believe capable of, to credit with. The prefix *zu-* directs the trust toward someone as an attribution. You *traust* someone something *zu* — you believe they are capable of it, you credit them with the ability. *Ich traue ihm das zu* = I believe he can do it. The trust is directed at a person in relation to a specific capacity.

Literal mnemonic: "trust (it) to (them)." You trust the ability *to* the person — you credit them with it.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem encodes confidence, not just trust.** The three base meanings of *trauen* — trust, dare, marry — all radiate from the same center: having enough confidence to act. This unifying concept makes the prefix family coherent. Every compound verb is a variation on "where does the confidence go?"

**The prefix family is small but semantically tight.** With only six prefixed forms, *trauen* is one of the more compact stem families. Each prefix hits a distinct facet of trust: *be-* entrusts officially, *ver-* trusts deeply, *zu-* credits with ability, *miss-* withdraws trust, *ge-* and *her-* modify the "dare" sense.

**English cognates are unusually close.** *Trauen* = trust. *Misstrauen* = mistrust. *Vertrauen* = confide/have faith. The Germanic roots are shared, and the logic maps almost perfectly. Use these cognate bridges — they are among the most reliable in the German prefix system.

**Inseparable prefixes dominate.** *Be-, ge-, miss-, ver-* are all inseparable, reflecting the fact that trust is an internal, psychological state rather than a spatial action. Only *her-* (spatial: "dare to come here") and *zu-* (directed: "credit to someone") are separable. The pattern holds: inner states get inseparable prefixes; outward, spatial actions get separable ones.
