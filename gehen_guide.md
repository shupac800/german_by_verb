# GEHEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Gehen** means to go, to walk. It is cognate with English "go" — both descend from the same Proto-Germanic root. Where *legen* is about placing things deliberately, *gehen* is about motion itself: moving from one point to another, proceeding, departing. Hold this image in mind: a person walking forward. Nearly every prefix below redirects that forward motion — turning it, halting it, reversing it, or projecting it into metaphor. Because going is so fundamental, *gehen* has one of the largest prefix families in German: thirty-three forms, touching movement, perception, decay, deception, and time.

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abgehen:** to go off, to come off. The prefix pulls the motion away from a surface or a place. A button *geht ab* — it comes off, detaches. Colloquially, something *geht ab* when things are happening, when energy is flying off in all directions. To depart is to go off from where you stood.

Literal mnemonic: "go off." English uses this naturally — "the button went off," "the paint is going off the wall," "things really went off last night."

### AN- ("on, onto, up against")

**Angehen:** to go at, to go on. The prefix drives the motion toward contact or engagement. You *gehen* a problem *an* — you go at it, tackle it. Something *geht dich an* — it goes onto you, it concerns you, it is your business. A light *geht an* — it goes on, it begins. In every case the motion arrives and makes contact.

Literal mnemonic: "go at / go on." "That doesn't go on you" = that's none of your business. "Go at the problem" = tackle it.

### AUF- ("up, upon, open")

**Aufgehen:** to go up, to go open. This is one of the richest clusters in the family. A door *geht auf* — it goes open. Dough *geht auf* — it rises, goes up. The sun *geht auf* — it comes up over the horizon. An idea *geht jemandem auf* — it dawns on someone, rising into clarity. A math problem *geht auf* — it comes out even, opens up cleanly with no remainder. The connecting logic is emergence: something that was closed, flat, or hidden goes upward or outward into visibility. Opening, rising, and dawning are all versions of the same motion — what was below or within comes up into the light.

Literal mnemonic: "go up / go open." English "it opened up to me" and "it dawned on me" capture the same upward-into-awareness gesture.

### AUS- ("out, outward")

**Ausgehen:** to go out. The prefix sends the motion outward, beyond an enclosure. You *gehen aus* — you go out for the evening. A fire *geht aus* — it goes out, dies down. Supplies *gehen aus* — they run out, they go out to nothing. You *gehen von etwas aus* — you go out from an assumption, you proceed from a starting point. The direction is always outward, whether physically leaving a room or abstractly departing from a premise.

Literal mnemonic: "go out." English maps almost perfectly: "go out socially," "the fire went out," "we ran out," "going out from the assumption that..."

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Begehen:** to go over thoroughly. *Be-* transforms intransitive motion into transitive action stamped onto an object. You *begehen* a crime — you carry it out, you go through with it. You *begehen* an anniversary — you observe it, you walk through the ceremony. The prefix forces the going to cover something completely: a transgression is gone through to completion, a celebration is walked through from start to finish.

Mnemonic: think of "be-" as "go all over." To *begehen* a crime is to go all the way through it. To *begehen* a holiday is to walk through its observance.

### DARAN- ("at it, to it")

**Darangehen:** to go at it, to set about it. The prefix points the motion toward a task waiting to be started. You *gehen daran* — you go to the thing, you get down to it. Straightforward and concrete: the motion meets the work.

Literal mnemonic: "go at it." "Let's go at it" = let's set about the task.

### DURCH- ("through")

**Durchgehen:** to go through. The prefix drives the motion through an obstacle or a body of material. You *gehen* a text *durch* — you go through it, review it, examine it line by line. A horse *geht durch* — it goes through, bolts, breaks through all restraint. Passengers *gehen durch* — they pass through, transit. The motion is always penetrative: entering on one side, exiting the other.

Literal mnemonic: "go through." English works identically: "go through the document," "the horse went through the fence," "go through customs."

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Eingehen:** to go in, to go into. This prefix turns the motion inward — and the results are remarkably diverse. You *gehen auf etwas ein* — you go into a topic, you agree to terms, you enter into a contract. Fabric *geht ein* — it goes in, it shrinks (the fibers draw inward). A plant *geht ein* — it goes in, it dies, it withdraws from life. The range — enter, agree, shrink, die — looks chaotic, but the underlying gesture is consistent: motion directed inward, whether into a commitment, into a smaller size, or into nonexistence. Dying is the ultimate going-in: withdrawing from the world entirely.

Literal mnemonic: "go in / go into." "Go into a deal" = agree. "It went in on itself" = it shrank. The dying sense is harder — think of "going into the ground."

### ENTGEGEN- ("toward, to meet")

**Entgegengehen:** to go toward, to go to meet. The prefix sends you forward to encounter something approaching from the opposite direction. You *gehen* someone *entgegen* — you walk out to meet them. You *gehen* a challenge *entgegen* — you face it, you advance toward it head-on.

Literal mnemonic: "go to meet." English "go out to meet" captures the mutual approach.

### ENT- ("away from, removed from")

**Entgehen:** to go away from, to escape. *Ent-* strips the motion away from danger or notice. Something *entgeht* you — it goes away from your perception, you miss it. A prisoner *entgeht* capture — the motion carries them clear.

Literal mnemonic: "get away." The logic is transparent: to go away from what would catch you.

### ENTLANG- ("along")

**Entlanggehen:** to go along. The prefix aligns the motion parallel to a path, a river, a street. You *gehen die Straße entlang* — you walk along the street. Purely spatial and concrete.

Literal mnemonic: "go along." "Go along the river" = *den Fluss entlanggehen.*

### ER- (achievement, completion, sometimes emergence)

**Ergehen:** to go forth, to be issued. *Er-* pushes the motion to its completed form. A decree *ergeht* — it goes forth, it is issued, it reaches its audience. Reflexively, *sich ergehen* means to take a stroll (to go forth at leisure) or to hold forth on a topic (to let one's words go forth at length). The prefix converts raw motion into something that has reached its destination.

Mnemonic: "go forth to completion." A decree has gone forth. A stroller has gone forth at ease.

### FEHL- ("wrong, mis-")

**Fehlgehen:** to go wrong, to go astray. The prefix deflects the motion off course. You *gehen fehl* when you miss the mark, take the wrong path, arrive at the wrong conclusion. The image is of a walker who veers off the trail.

Literal mnemonic: "go wrong." English "go astray" or "misfire" captures the same deflection.

### FORT- ("away, onward")

**Fortgehen:** to go away, to go on. The prefix carries the motion forward and out of sight. You *gehen fort* — you leave, you depart. Life *geht fort* — it goes on, it continues. The prefix adds departure or continuation to the basic act of walking.

Literal mnemonic: "go forth." "He went forth and did not return" captures the departure. "Life goes forth" captures the continuation.

### HERAUF- ("up from below, up to here")

**Heraufgehen:** to go up, to ascend. The prefix lifts the motion upward toward the speaker's position. You *gehen herauf* — you come up the stairs, you walk up the hill to where the speaker stands.

Literal mnemonic: "go on up (to here)." The motion is upward and toward.

### HERAUS- ("out from within, out to here")

**Herausgehen:** to go out, to exit, to come out. The prefix sends the motion outward from an interior toward the speaker. You *gehen heraus* — you come out of a building, you step outside.

Literal mnemonic: "come on out." The motion exits an enclosure and arrives where the speaker is.

### HER- ("hither, toward the speaker")

**Hergehen:** to come here, to walk along. The prefix draws the motion toward the speaker. Impersonally, *es geht heiß her* — things are going on hotly, there is heated activity happening (here, around us).

Literal mnemonic: "go here / come along." The impersonal sense is like English "things are going on around here."

### HIN- ("thither, toward a point away from the speaker")

**Hingehen:** to go there, to go over to a place. The prefix aims the motion away from the speaker toward a destination. You *gehen hin* — you go there, you go to the place in question. Simple directional motion, pointed away.

Literal mnemonic: "go (over) there." "I went there" = *Ich bin hingegangen.*

### HINTER- ("behind")

**Hintergehen:** to go behind — to deceive, to betray, to double-cross. This is one of the most vivid metaphors in the family. The prefix takes "go" and routes it behind someone's back. To *hintergehen* is literally to go behind a person — to act where they cannot see, to move in the space of secrecy. English preserves the identical image: "to go behind someone's back." German simply fused it into a single inseparable verb.

Literal mnemonic: "go behind." "She went behind his back" = *Sie hat ihn hintergangen.* The metaphor is perfectly shared between the two languages.

### HINWEG- ("away, over and past")

**Hinweggehen:** to go past, to pass over. The prefix carries the motion up and over something, leaving it behind. You *gehen über etwas hinweg* — you pass over a mistake, you overlook it, you disregard it. The image is of stepping over an obstacle without stopping.

Literal mnemonic: "go over and away." English "pass over" and "overlook" use the same vertical metaphor.

### LOS- ("loose, free, released")

**Losgehen:** to go loose, to let go. The prefix releases the motion from restraint. Things *gehen los* — they get going, they kick off. A gun *geht los* — it goes off, it fires. The image is of something held back that suddenly breaks free into motion.

Literal mnemonic: "go off / get going." "The alarm went off" and "let's get going" both capture the release.

### MIT- ("with, along with")

**Mitgehen:** to go with, to go along. The prefix adds companionship to the motion. You *gehen mit* — you come along, you accompany. The simplest social extension of walking: doing it together.

Literal mnemonic: "go with." "Come go with me" = *Geh mit mir.*

### NACH- ("after, behind, following")

**Nachgehen:** to go after. The prefix trails the motion behind something else. You *gehen* a lead *nach* — you follow it, you investigate, you go after the clue. A clock *geht nach* — it goes behind, it runs slow, it loses time. The spatial logic is consistent: moving in the wake of something else, whether pursuing a suspect or lagging behind the correct hour.

Literal mnemonic: "go after." "Go after that lead" = investigate it. A slow clock "goes behind" the real time.

### UM- ("around")

**Umgehen:** this verb splits in two depending on stress, and the two meanings pull in opposite directions.

*Separable (UMgehen):* to go around. A ghost *geht um* — it goes around, it haunts, it circulates through the house. A rumor *geht um* — it goes around, it spreads. The motion is circular, looping, returning.

*Inseparable (umGEHEN):* to go around in the sense of avoidance — to bypass, to circumvent, to evade. A regulation is *umgangen* — gone around, skirted. But also: to handle, to deal with — *mit etwas umgehen* means to go around with something, to manage it through familiarity and practice.

The opposition is striking. The separable form means the motion itself is circular (haunting, circulating). The inseparable form means the motion avoids something by routing around it — or masters something by working around and through it repeatedly.

Literal mnemonic: separable = "go around" (a ghost goes around the house). Inseparable = "get around" (get around a rule) or "go around with" (handle through practice).

### UNTER- ("under, beneath")

**Untergehen:** to go under. The prefix drops the motion beneath a surface, and the mapping to English is nearly perfect. A ship *geht unter* — it goes under, it sinks. A civilization *geht unter* — it goes under, it perishes, it falls. The sun *geht unter* — it goes under the horizon, it sets. English "go under" works in every case: a business "goes under," a swimmer "goes under," the sun "goes under." This is one of those rare cases where the German metaphor and the English metaphor are identical across all senses.

Literal mnemonic: "go under." No translation needed — English says the same thing.

### VER- (transformation, displacement, fading)

**Vergehen:** to go away through transformation. *Ver-* takes the motion and pushes it past a threshold into change or disappearance. Time *vergeht* — it goes past, it elapses, it passes. Color *vergeht* — it fades, it goes away. Appetite *vergeht* — it vanishes. Reflexively, *sich vergehen* means to transgress, to offend — to go beyond the boundary of what is permitted. The prefix consistently marks a going that crosses into loss, change, or violation.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "go away into change." Time passes (*vergeht*). Beauty fades (*vergeht*). A criminal goes too far (*vergeht sich*).

### VOR- ("before, forward, in front of")

**Vorgehen:** to go forward, to go before. The prefix pushes the motion ahead of the rest. You *gehen vor* — you proceed, you advance, you act. Something *geht vor* — it goes on, it happens, it occurs in front of witnesses. A clock *geht vor* — it goes before, it runs fast. The motion is always ahead of the expected position: advancing, taking the lead, or being too far forward.

Literal mnemonic: "go before / go forward." "What's going on?" = *Was geht vor?* A fast clock "goes before" the correct time.

### WEG- ("away, gone")

**Weggehen:** to go away. The simplest departure. You *gehen weg* — you leave, you walk off, you are gone. No metaphor, no abstraction — just motion away from where you were.

Literal mnemonic: "go away." "Go away" = *Geh weg.*

### ZER- ("apart, to pieces")

**Zergehen:** to go to pieces, to dissolve. The prefix scatters the motion into disintegration. Butter *zergeht* — it melts, it goes apart into liquid. Chocolate *zergeht auf der Zunge* — it melts on the tongue, it dissolves into nothing. The motion is centrifugal: the thing goes outward in all directions until it ceases to hold together.

Literal mnemonic: "go to pieces." "It melted away to nothing" captures the dissolving motion.

### ZU- ("to, toward, closed")

**Zugehen:** to go toward, to go shut. The prefix drives the motion toward a target or toward closure. You *gehen auf jemanden zu* — you walk toward someone, you approach. A door *geht zu* — it goes to, it closes, it shuts. Impersonally, *es geht lustig zu* — things go on merrily, there is merry activity going toward you. The double sense — approach and closure — reflects *zu*'s dual nature: motion toward a point can mean arriving *at* something or closing *up* something.

Literal mnemonic: "go to / go shut." "Go to the door" = approach. "The door went to" = it closed (archaic English preserves this: "the door went to with a bang").

### ZURÜCK- ("back, backward")

**Zurückgehen:** to go back. The prefix reverses the motion. You *gehen zurück* — you return, you retreat, you walk back. Numbers *gehen zurück* — they decline, they recede, they go back down. Something *geht auf etwas zurück* — it goes back to an origin, it dates back to a source. The motion is always rearward: returning to a prior position, shrinking toward a prior level, or tracing a line back to a starting point.

Literal mnemonic: "go back." "Sales went back" = declined. "This goes back to the 18th century" = dates back.

### ZUSAMMEN- ("together")

**Zusammengehen:** to go together. Separate things move until they converge. Colors *gehen zusammen* — they go together, they harmonize, they match. Fabric *geht zusammen* — it goes together, it shrinks (its edges draw toward each other). People *gehen zusammen* — they walk together, they act in unison.

Literal mnemonic: "go together." "Those colors go together" = *Die Farben gehen zusammen.*

### ÜBER- ("over, across, beyond")

**Übergehen:** to go over. The prefix carries the motion above and across. You *übergehen* a detail — you go over it, you pass over it, you skip it, you overlook it. Something *geht über* into a new state — it transitions, it changes over, it passes into something else. The motion clears a boundary: either stepping over something (and therefore ignoring it) or crossing over into new territory (and therefore transforming).

Literal mnemonic: "go over." "I went over that detail" = I skipped it. "It went over into chaos" = it transitioned.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**English "go" and German *gehen* share an extraordinary number of metaphors.** "Go under" = *untergehen*. "Go out" = *ausgehen*. "Go through" = *durchgehen*. "Go back" = *zurückgehen*. "Go off" = *losgehen*. Where *legen* diverges from English "lay" in many of its prefix forms, *gehen* and "go" often land on exactly the same image. Use this as a massive advantage — if you can say it with "go" in English, there is a good chance the German prefix form works the same way.

**Separable forms preserve physical motion; inseparable forms drift into abstraction.** The separable verbs — *ausgehen, durchgehen, zurückgehen* — tend to keep one foot in literal walking. You can picture someone going out, going through, going back. The inseparable verbs — *begehen, vergehen, hintergehen, entgehen* — have left the sidewalk entirely. You do not picture walking when you hear *begehen* (commit) or *vergehen* (elapse). The motion has become purely structural: a direction without a body.

**UMGEHEN is the family's most instructive split.** Separable *umgehen* (a ghost goes around) and inseparable *umgehen* (to bypass, to handle) share a prefix and a stem yet point in opposite directions. The separable form is circular and restless — the motion keeps going around. The inseparable form is purposeful — the motion routes around an obstacle or works around a problem. This pair illustrates why stress matters in German: the same letters can encode opposite relationships to the world.

**EINGEHEN shows how one spatial metaphor can fracture.** Enter, agree, shrink, die — four meanings from "go in." But each makes sense if you hold the inward motion steady: going into a building (enter), going into an agreement (accept), going into a smaller size (shrink), going into the earth (die). The metaphor does not break; it refracts, bending the same light through different contexts.

**AUFGEHEN shows how one spatial metaphor can expand.** Open, rise, dawn, divide evenly — four meanings from "go up." A door goes up (opens). Dough goes up (rises). An idea goes up into consciousness (dawns). A number goes up cleanly with no remainder (comes out even). Where *eingehen* fractures inward, *aufgehen* radiates outward — everything that goes up becomes available, visible, resolved.

**Time, light, and death are all forms of going.** Time *vergeht* (goes away). The sun *aufgeht* and *untergeht* (goes up and goes under). Plants *eingehen* (go in = die). Civilizations *untergehen* (go under = perish). Butter *zergeht* (goes apart = melts). German treats all change as movement — and *gehen* is the verb that carries it.
