# RUFEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Rufen** means to call, to shout, to cry out, to summon. It implies projecting the voice outward — directing sound toward someone or something with purpose. Hold this image in mind: a person opening their mouth and sending a call across distance. Nearly every prefix below redirects that call — aiming it, channeling it, amplifying it, or abstracting it into institutional and legal acts far removed from any literal shout.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abrufen:** to call off, to call away. The prefix pulls the call toward retrieval — you call something away from where it is stored and bring it to hand. Data is *abgerufen* — called up from a database, fetched from storage. Money is *abgerufen* — drawn down from an account. A performance is *abgerufen* — summoned on demand. The gesture is always the same: you call and something detaches from its resting place and comes to you.

Literal mnemonic: "call off (the shelf)." Think of calling a file out of the archive — you reach in with your voice and pull it free.

### AN- ("on, onto, up against")

**Anrufen:** to call on, to call at. The prefix directs the call toward a target. In its most common modern sense, you *rufst jemanden an* — you call someone on the telephone, directing your voice at them through the line. But the older, more formal sense survives in law and religion: you *rufen ein Gericht an* — you appeal to a court, you call upon its authority. You *rufen Gott an* — you invoke, you call on a higher power. In every case the call is aimed at someone and asks for a response.

Literal mnemonic: "call on." English preserves both senses — "call on the phone" and "call on someone for help."

### AUF- ("up, upon, open")

**Aufrufen:** to call up, to call upon. The prefix lifts the call into public space. A teacher *ruft einen Schüler auf* — calls upon a student, summons them upward from the group. A government *ruft zum Streik auf* — calls people up to strike, issues a public summons. In computing, a function is *aufgerufen* — called up, invoked. The call is always elevating: it raises someone or something out of the background and into active duty.

Literal mnemonic: "call up." English works identically — "call up a witness," "call upon the crowd."

### AUS- ("out, outward, spread")

**Ausrufen:** to call out. The prefix pushes the call outward so everyone hears. You *rufen* a warning *aus* — you cry it out into the open. A republic is *ausgerufen* — proclaimed, called out into existence before the world. A market vendor *ruft seine Waren aus* — cries out the goods for sale. The gesture is broadcast: the call leaves the speaker and spreads in all directions.

Literal mnemonic: "call out" or "cry out." "They called out the results" = *Sie haben die Ergebnisse ausgerufen.*

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Berufen:** to call upon thoroughly — to appoint. *Be-* takes the act of calling and stamps it onto a person with institutional force. To *berufen* someone is to call them into a role: to appoint, to nominate, to designate. A professor is *auf einen Lehrstuhl berufen* — called to a chair, summoned into a position by formal authority. A committee is *berufen* — convened, called into being. The prefix transforms a shout into an act of official selection. The person so called does not merely hear — they are claimed.

Mnemonic: think of "be-" as "all over." To *berufen* is to call upon someone so completely that they are bound to the role. English "vocation" comes from Latin *vocare* (to call) — *Beruf* (profession, calling) is its Germanic twin.

### HERAUS- ("out from within")

**Herausrufen:** to call out from inside. You stand outside and direct your call inward, summoning someone or something out. You *rufen jemanden heraus* — you call them out of a building, out of a crowd, out of hiding. The prefix specifies the trajectory: the call enters an enclosed space and draws its target outward.

Literal mnemonic: "call out (from in there)." "She called him out of the house" = *Sie hat ihn herausgerufen.*

### HER- ("hither, toward the speaker")

**Herrufen:** to call here, to call over. The prefix pulls the call's target toward the speaker. You *rufst jemanden her* — you call them over to where you stand. Simple and spatial: the voice goes out, the person comes back.

Literal mnemonic: "call here." "Call the children over" = *Ruf die Kinder her.*

### VER- (transformation, displacement — here fossilized)

**Verrufen** (adj.): called into disrepute. *Ver-* often signals that an action has gone wrong or reached a negative extreme. Something *verrufen* has been called about so much — denounced, cursed, shouted against — that the calling itself has become its permanent condition. The verb is no longer active; only the adjective survives, describing a person or place whose reputation has been destroyed by what people say. The calling has fossilized into a stain.

Literal mnemonic: "mis-called" or "called to ruin." A *verrufene Gegend* is a neighborhood that has been talked down, called out so often that the bad name sticks.

### WIDER- ("against, counter")

**Widerrufen:** to call against, to call counter to. You issue a call that opposes and cancels a previous one. To *widerrufen* is to revoke, to retract, to recant — to speak against what you once spoke for. A confession is *widerrufen* — recanted. A permit is *widerrufen* — rescinded, its authority called back. The image is adversarial: your new call stands against your old one and negates it.

Literal mnemonic: "counter-call." Think of it as "calling it off" with the emphasis on contradiction — not just stopping, but formally opposing what was said before. Martin Luther was asked to *widerrufen* at the Diet of Worms — to call back his writings, to unsay them.

### WIEDER- ("again, once more")

**Wiederrufen:** to call again. Straightforward repetition — the prefix simply adds "once more" to the calling. You called and no one answered, so you call again. Not to be confused with *widerrufen* (revoke), despite the similar sound; *wieder* means "again," *wider* means "against."

Literal mnemonic: "call again." The spelling difference matters: *wieder* = again, *wider* = against.

### ZU- ("to, toward")

**Zurufen:** to call to, to shout to. The prefix directs the call toward a specific person. You *rufst jemandem etwas zu* — you shout something to someone, you call across to them. The emphasis is on directing the message at a recipient: not a broadcast (that would be *aus-*), not a summons (that would be *auf-*), but a targeted shout.

Literal mnemonic: "call to." "He called out to her" = *Er hat ihr zugerufen.*

### ZURÜCK- ("back, backward, in return")

**Zurückrufen:** to call back. Two parallel senses: (1) you return someone's phone call — you call back. (2) you recall something — you call it back from where it has gone. A defective product is *zurückgerufen* — recalled, called back from the market. An ambassador is *zurückgerufen* — recalled from their post. The prefix reverses the direction: what went out is called to return.

Literal mnemonic: "call back." English uses the same construction in both senses — "I'll call you back" and "the manufacturer recalled the product."

### ZUSAMMEN- ("together")

**Zusammenrufen:** to call together. You issue a call and people converge. A meeting is *zusammengerufen* — convened, its members called from their separate places into one room. Parliament is *zusammengerufen* — assembled by summons. The prefix gathers: the call goes out in many directions, and everyone arrives at the same point.

Literal mnemonic: "call together." "The king called his council together" = *Der König hat seinen Rat zusammengerufen.*

### ÜBER- ("over, above, beyond")

**Überrufen:** to call over the top — to overbid. The prefix pushes the call beyond a limit. In an auction or card game, *überrufen* means to bid more than another, to call a higher number. The sense is narrow and specialized: your call goes over theirs.

Literal mnemonic: "over-call." You call a price that goes over the previous one.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays vocal; the prefixes go institutional.** *Rufen* never stops meaning "to call." But the prefixes push that call from the personal to the official. *Ausrufen* (call out) becomes "to proclaim a republic." *Berufen* (call upon) becomes "to appoint to a professorship." *Widerrufen* (call against) becomes "to revoke a legal decision." The voice's physical gesture — projecting sound across distance — is projected onto acts of authority, law, and governance.

**Separable prefixes tend to be spatial and concrete.** *Ab, an, auf, aus, her, hin, zu, zurück, zusammen* — these preserve a clear directionality. You can picture where the call goes: outward (*aus-*), toward someone (*zu-*), back (*zurück-*). Even in figurative use, the spatial image remains legible.

**Inseparable prefixes tend to be transformative and opaque.** *Be-, ver-, wider-, über-* fuse with the verb and shift its meaning further from any literal shout. *Berufen* does not sound like calling; it sounds like appointing. *Widerrufen* does not sound like shouting against; it sounds like revoking a legal order. These compounds must often be learned as whole units.

**The calling/vocation parallel runs deep.** English "vocation" and "calling" are synonyms because Latin and Germanic both used "to call" to describe being summoned to a role. German makes this visible in the word family: *rufen* (to call), *Beruf* (profession), *Berufung* (appointment; calling; appeal). When you learn *berufen*, you are learning a metaphor that English shares but has split across Latin and Germanic roots.

**Watch the** ***wider-*** **/** ***wieder-*** **distinction.** *Widerrufen* (inseparable, stress on *-ru-*) means to revoke — to call against. *Wiederrufen* (separable, stress on *wie-*) means to call again. They sound nearly identical in speech but mean opposite things: one cancels a call, the other repeats it. Context and stress pattern are your only guides.
