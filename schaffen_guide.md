# SCHAFFEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Schaffen** means to create, to manage, to work. This is a verb with a split personality rooted in its own history: the strong verb *schaffen* (*schuf, geschaffen*) means to create, to bring something into existence — God *schuf* the world. The weak verb *schaffen* (*schaffte, geschafft*) means to manage, to accomplish, to work hard — you *schaffst* the exam, you *schaffst* it by effort. Hold both images: the hand of creation shaping something from nothing, and the laboring body pushing through a task. The prefixes pull on one side or the other — or occasionally both.

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abschaffen:** to create off, to work away from. The prefix strips something out of existence. To *abschaffen* is to abolish — to take something that was established and work it away until it is gone. Laws are *abgeschafft*, taxes are *abgeschafft*, institutions are *abgeschafft*. The image is the reverse of creation: what was once made is un-made, swept off the table.

Literal mnemonic: "do away with." English uses this exact phrase — "do away with the old rules." German builds it from "create-off."

### AN- ("on, onto, up against")

**Anschaffen:** to create onto, to acquire. The prefix pushes the action toward the subject: you bring something onto yourself, into your possession. To *anschaffen* a car is to buy it, to add it to what you own. The colloquial meaning — to prostitute oneself — follows a grimmer logic: to *schaffen an*, to work on the street, to sell oneself. In both cases, the prefix implies contact and acquisition: something is being obtained or earned through effort.

Literal mnemonic: "get on" or "take on." You take on a new purchase; in the darker sense, you take on work of a particular kind.

### AUS- ("out, outward, through to completion")

**Ausschaffen:** to work out, to complete. The prefix drives the action outward to its end point. Something is *ausgeschafft* when it has been worked through to completion — accomplished, finished, brought all the way out. A straightforward and uncommon compound: the task is worked out.

Literal mnemonic: "work out" in the sense of "work something out to completion."

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Beschaffen:** to work something thoroughly into your hands. *Be-* stamps the action fully onto an object — you do not merely create or work, you direct your effort onto a specific target and acquire it. To *beschaffen* materials is to obtain them, to procure them, to get hold of them through deliberate effort. The word implies that getting the thing requires work: sourcing, tracking down, arranging. A *Beschaffung* is a procurement — the organized effort of laying hands on what is needed.

Mnemonic: think of "be-" as "upon" — to work upon the problem of getting something until you have it. English "procure" carries the same sense of effortful obtaining.

### ER- (achievement, completion, bringing into being)

**Erschaffen:** to create into existence. *Er-* signals that the action reaches its full realization. Where plain *schaffen* can mean simply to make or to work, *erschaffen* means to create in the grandest sense — to call into being from nothing. God *erschuf* the heavens and the earth. A poet *erschafft* a world in verse. The prefix elevates creation from the workaday to the monumental: this is not making a sandwich, this is making a universe.

Mnemonic: "create forth" or "create into being." The *er-* marks the moment when what did not exist crosses into existence. Compare *erfinden* (to invent), *erbauen* (to build up) — the prefix always signals that something new has been brought conclusively into the world.

### FORT- ("away, onward, gone")

**Fortschaffen:** to work away, to remove. You direct your effort at getting something gone. To *fortschaffen* rubble is to cart it off, to haul it away. The prefix gives direction: away from here. The thing is not destroyed (that would be *abschaffen* in the abstract sense) but physically moved elsewhere through effort.

Literal mnemonic: "carry forth" or "get away." The emphasis is on the labor of removal — this is not a polite disappearance but a hauling-off.

### HERAUS- ("out from within")

**Herausschaffen:** to work out from inside. You reach into a space and remove what is within through effort. Furniture is *herausgeschafft* from a building. The prefix is spatial and specific: the motion is outward, toward the speaker. Simple, literal, and uncommon.

Literal mnemonic: "get out (from in here)."

### HER- ("hither, toward the speaker")

**Herschaffen:** to get here, to bring here. You work to move something toward your location. The prefix gives direction — hither, to where you stand. "Can you *herschaffen* the equipment?" means: can you get it here, bring it to this place?

Literal mnemonic: "get here." The effort implied by *schaffen* means this is not a casual fetching — it is managing to get something to your location.

### HIN- ("thither, toward a point away from the speaker")

**Hinschaffen:** to get there, to transport there. The mirror image of *herschaffen*: the prefix directs the effort away from the speaker, toward a destination. You work to move something to that place over there.

Literal mnemonic: "get there." If *herschaffen* is "get it here," *hinschaffen* is "get it there."

### VER- (transformation, change, intensification)

**Verschaffen:** to work something into a changed state — specifically, into someone's possession. *Ver-* transforms the general labor of *schaffen* into the specific act of obtaining or securing. To *verschaffen* someone access is to get it for them, to procure it, to make it happen. Reflexively, *sich verschaffen* means to obtain for yourself — to secure an advantage, to gain entry, to get hold of something through one's own effort or connections.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "into a new state." What was unavailable is now available; what was out of reach is now in hand. Compare *beschaffen*, which emphasizes the effort of procurement, while *verschaffen* emphasizes the result — the thing has been transferred into someone's possession. "She *verschaffte* him a job" = she got him a job, she made it happen.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem's two identities sort the prefixes.** The creative *schaffen* (*schuf, geschaffen*) surfaces clearly in *erschaffen* — grand creation, bringing into being. The industrious *schaffen* (*schaffte, geschafft*) dominates in most other compounds: *abschaffen* (work something out of existence), *anschaffen* (work to acquire), *fortschaffen* (work to remove). Knowing which *schaffen* a prefix leans on tells you whether the verb is about making or about effort.

**Beschaffen and verschaffen are near-synonyms with different angles.** Both mean to obtain or procure. *Beschaffen* foregrounds the process — the work of sourcing, the logistics of getting hold of something. *Verschaffen* foregrounds the result — the thing has been secured, the access has been opened. In practice they overlap heavily, but *beschaffen* tends toward official or material procurement (supplies, documents), while *verschaffen* tends toward abstract or personal advantage (access, respect, a position).

**Abschaffen is creation in reverse.** Where *erschaffen* brings something into being from nothing, *abschaffen* takes something that exists and removes it from being. The two verbs are conceptual mirror images: *er-* builds up, *ab-* tears down. This symmetry is a reliable pattern across German verb stems.

**Separable prefixes stay physical.** *Fort-, heraus-, her-, hin-* — these compounds are about moving things through space by effort. The labor of *schaffen* is directed somewhere specific: away, out, here, there. You can picture someone hauling, carrying, transporting.

**Inseparable prefixes go abstract.** *Be-, er-, ver-* — these fuse with the stem and shift into less visible territory. *Erschaffen* is metaphysical creation. *Beschaffen* and *verschaffen* are about the invisible machinery of procurement. The physical labor of *schaffen* has been transmuted into organizational, social, or even cosmic work.
