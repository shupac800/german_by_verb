# REINIGEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Reinigen** means to clean, to cleanse, to purify, to refine. At its core is the adjective *rein* (clean, pure), and *reinigen* is the act of making something *rein* — removing dirt, impurity, or contamination so that what remains is clean. Hold this image in mind: wiping a surface until it is spotless, or filtering a substance until it is pure. The two prefixed forms below each reshape this cleaning action in a different direction.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abreinigen:** to clean off, to wipe clean. The prefix pulls the action outward and away — you are not just cleaning, you are cleaning *off*, removing the dirt from a surface. The emphasis shifts from the result (purity) to the process of removal. Grime, residue, stains — these are wiped away, scraped off, stripped from the thing being cleaned. Where *reinigen* focuses on making something pure, *abreinigen* focuses on getting the impurity off.

Literal mnemonic: "clean off." English uses the same construction — "clean off the counter," "wipe the mud off." The *ab-* makes the removal explicit.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Bereinigen:** to clean up thoroughly — but in the abstract sense. *Be-* is not spatial; it is a grammatical transformer that stamps the action fully onto an object. Here, what gets "cleaned" is not a physical surface but a situation: a dispute is *bereinigt* — cleared up, resolved. An account is *bereinigt* — rectified, set straight. A misunderstanding is *bereinigt* — settled and put in order. The prefix lifts *reinigen* from the physical world into the domain of problems, errors, and complications that need to be "cleaned out" of a situation.

Mnemonic: think of "be-" as "all over, thoroughly." To *bereinigen* a problem is to clean it up completely — to purify the situation of everything that was wrong. English "clear up" captures the same metaphor: a mess (physical or social) is made clean.

---

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays physical; the prefixes go in opposite directions.** *Reinigen* never stops meaning "to clean." But *ab-* keeps the cleaning concrete and physical (wiping dirt off a surface), while *be-* takes it entirely abstract (clearing up a dispute). This tiny family illustrates in miniature the same pattern visible in large verb families like *legen*: separable prefixes stay close to the body, inseparable prefixes reach for metaphor.

**Separable ab- is spatial and concrete.** *Abreinigen* is something you do with your hands — you can picture the wiping motion, the dirt coming *off*. The prefix adds direction to the cleaning without changing what cleaning means.

**Inseparable be- is transformative and opaque.** *Bereinigen* cannot be done with a sponge. The prefix shifts the entire action into a new domain. You "clean" a situation the way you might clean a wound — by removing what does not belong — but the physical gesture is gone. Only the logic of purification remains.

**English preserves the same metaphors.** "Clean off" = *abreinigen*. "Clear up" or "clean up" (a situation) = *bereinigen*. In both languages, the spatial "off" stays physical while the intensifying prefix goes abstract. Where English switches verbs (from "clean" to "clear" or "settle"), German keeps the same stem and lets the prefix do the work.
