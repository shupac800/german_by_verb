# SCHLAGEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Schlagen** means to hit, to strike, to beat. It implies forceful contact — a fist, a hammer, a blow landing on a surface. Hold this image in mind: an arm swinging and connecting. Nearly every prefix below redirects that blow — aiming it at a target, amplifying it, misdirecting it, or abstracting it into metaphor. Where *legen* is about deliberate placement, *schlagen* is about decisive impact.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abschlagen:** to strike off. The blow severs — it knocks a piece away from the whole. A branch is *abgeschlagen* — chopped off. A head was *abgeschlagen* — struck off. From this violent separation, the word extends to refusal: to *abschlagen* a request is to knock it away, to cut it off before it lands. The gesture is the same whether you are chopping wood or declining an invitation — a sharp, clean rejection.

Literal mnemonic: "strike off." English "knock off" works the same way — both for removing a piece and for dismissing something.

### AN- ("on, onto, up against")

**Anschlagen:** to strike on, to strike against. The blow makes contact with a surface. A notice is *angeschlagen* — struck onto a board, posted. An elbow is *angeschlagen* — bumped against something. Medicine *schlägt an* — it strikes home, takes effect, lands where it is supposed to. In every case the prefix directs the impact toward a target: something hits, sticks, or registers.

Literal mnemonic: "strike on." Think of "striking a note" — the key hits the string and the sound takes effect.

### AUF- ("up, upon, open")

**Aufschlagen:** to strike open, to strike upon. The blow lands downward onto something and opens it. An egg is *aufgeschlagen* — cracked open by striking. A book is *aufgeschlagen* — opened (struck open at a page). A tent is *aufgeschlagen* — pitched, set up by driving stakes. A price is *aufgeschlagen* — marked up, struck higher. In tennis, you *schlägst auf* — you serve, striking the ball up and into play. The prefix lifts the impact upward or breaks something open from above.

Literal mnemonic: "strike open" or "strike up." An egg is "struck open." A tent is "struck up." A tennis serve is the ball "struck up."

### AUS- ("out, outward")

**Ausschlagen:** to strike out. The blow pushes outward — a horse *schlägt aus*, kicking out with its hooves. From this outward rejection comes the figurative meaning: to turn something down, to refuse, to strike an offer away from yourself. Plants *schlagen aus* in spring — buds strike outward, sprouting. The pendulum *schlägt aus* — it deflects, swinging out to the side. Every sense involves energy directed outward.

Literal mnemonic: "strike out." English "strike out" at baseball, or "lash out," captures the outward motion. A bud "strikes out" from the branch.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Beschlagen:** to strike upon thoroughly. *Be-* stamps the action fully onto the object. To *beschlagen* a horse is to shoe it — to strike horseshoes onto its hooves with a hammer, covering each hoof completely. Windows become *beschlagen* — moisture strikes onto the glass and covers it, fogging it up. The adjective *beschlagen* splits into two seemingly unrelated senses that share a root logic: (1) fogged up, steamed over — covered, struck upon by condensation; (2) knowledgeable, well-versed — thoroughly struck, well-hammered, tempered by experience. A *beschlagener* expert is someone who has been "hammered into shape" by their field.

Mnemonic: think of "be-" as "all over." A fogged window is struck all over with moisture. A knowledgeable person has been struck all over by experience — well-forged.

### DURCH- ("through")

**Durchschlagen:** to strike through. As a separable verb: the blow penetrates — a bullet *schlägt durch*, it comes through, breaks through. As an inseparable reflexive (*sich durchschlagen*): you strike your way through — you struggle through obstacles, fight your way through difficulty. The image is of battering through a barrier, whether the projectile does it in one clean blow or you do it through sustained effort.

Literal mnemonic: "strike through." A nail "strikes through" the board. You "fight through" hardship.

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Einschlagen:** to strike in. The blow drives something inward. A nail is *eingeschlagen* — hammered in. A window is *eingeschlagen* — smashed in. A path is *eingeschlagen* — taken, struck into, as though you drive yourself into a particular route. Lightning *schlägt ein* — it strikes in, hitting a target. The prefix channels all the violence inward, into a surface or a direction.

Literal mnemonic: "strike in." English "drive in a nail," "smash in a window," "strike into a path" — all follow the same logic.

### ER- (achievement, completion, finality)

**Erschlagen:** to strike dead. *Er-* pushes the blow to its terminal point — the hitting does not merely wound but finishes its object. To *erschlagen* is to slay, to beat to death, to kill with a blow. This is one of the most ancient and visceral *er-* verbs: the prefix converts impact into fatality. Every *er-* verb carries this sense of reaching the end — *erlegen* (lay down for good), *ersticken* (suffocate to completion), *erschlagen* (strike to the end).

Mnemonic: "strike to death." The blow lands and the thing does not get up.

### FEHL- ("wrong, amiss, mis-")

**Fehlschlagen:** to strike amiss. The blow misses its target — the plan that was supposed to hit home goes wide. To *fehlschlagen* is to fail, to backfire, to miscarry. The image is precise: you swing and miss. An investment *schlägt fehl* — it strikes wrongly, fails to land. A military operation *schlägt fehl* — the blow goes astray.

Literal mnemonic: "mis-strike." English "miss the mark" or "backfire" captures the same picture of a blow gone wrong.

### HERAUS- ("out from within")

**Herausschlagen:** to strike out from inside. You reach into something and knock a gain loose. To *herausschlagen* a profit is to extract it — to beat it out of the deal. To knock something out of a container is literal *herausschlagen*. The prefix specifies direction: the blow goes outward from an interior.

Literal mnemonic: "knock out (from in there)." Think of "knocking a concession out of a negotiation."

### LOS- ("loose, free, released")

**Losschlagen:** to strike loose, to let strike. The restraint is released and the blows begin. To *losschlagen* is to attack, to lash out, to launch into a fight. Like *loslegen* (lay loose = get started), the prefix unleashes the action — but where *loslegen* bursts into activity, *losschlagen* bursts into violence.

Literal mnemonic: "let strike" or "cut loose striking." The fists are unleashed.

### NACH- ("after, following, imitating")

**Nachschlagen:** to strike after. Picture a scholar who encounters an unknown word: the text has moved on, but the reader strikes back after it — turns to a reference and looks it up. To *nachschlagen* is to consult a dictionary, to look something up, to search after a fact that has already passed by. The prefix captures the gesture of going back after something you missed and striking upon it.

Literal mnemonic: "strike after." You "strike after" the meaning of a word — you go back and hunt it down. English "look up" is pedestrian by comparison; German's version has the urgency of pursuit.

### UM- ("around, over, down")

**Umschlagen:** to strike over, to strike around. As a separable verb: something tips over, capsizes — a boat *schlägt um*, struck over by a wave. Weather *schlägt um* — it turns over, changes suddenly, as though a blow flipped it. As an inseparable verb: to wrap, to fold over — a collar is *umgeschlagen*, turned over. The prefix always implies a reversal of orientation: what was one way is struck into the opposite.

Literal mnemonic: "strike over." Weather "overturns." A boat is "struck over." A collar is "folded over."

### UNTER- ("under, beneath, suppressed")

**Unterschlagen:** to strike under, to hide beneath. The blow goes downward, out of sight. To *unterschlagen* is to embezzle — to strike money under the table, to conceal it, to divert it where no one can see. To withhold evidence is also to *unterschlagen* — to suppress it, to keep it struck beneath. The inseparable prefix fuses tightly with the verb, making the concealment total. There is no honest *unterschlagen* — the prefix "under" here always means "hidden, illicit, kept from view."

Literal mnemonic: "strike under." Think of slipping a card under the table — the English phrase "under the table" for illicit payments maps perfectly onto the German image.

### VER- (transformation, displacement, error)

**Verschlagen:** the prefix displaces the blow, sending it somewhere unintended. As a verb, *verschlagen* means to drive off course — wind and waves *verschlagen* a ship to a foreign shore. The blow of fate carries you somewhere you did not intend to go. The adjective *verschlagen* means cunning, sly, crafty — a person who has been "displaced" from straightforwardness, knocked sideways into deviousness. Like a ship blown off course, the *verschlagene* person does not travel in a straight line; they approach obliquely, by misdirection.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "mis-" or "away into change." A ship is *verschlagen* to a distant shore — struck off course. A cunning person is *verschlagen* — struck sideways, never approaching directly. The same displacement that moves a ship moves a character.

### VOR- ("before, forward, in front of")

**Vorschlagen:** to strike before. You bring your idea forward — you place a blow before the group, putting it on the table for consideration. To *vorschlagen* is to suggest, to propose, to recommend. The image is of the blacksmith who strikes the metal first to show the shape he intends — or the leader who strikes forward, showing the path. The suggestion is a blow delivered in advance: "here is what I think we should aim for."

Literal mnemonic: "strike forth" or "strike before." English "put forward a proposal" uses the same spatial logic, but German makes it more muscular — you do not merely *place* the idea, you *strike* it forward.

### ZER- ("apart, to pieces, asunder")

**Zerschlagen:** to strike apart. The blow shatters — what was whole becomes fragments. A vase is *zerschlagen* — smashed to pieces. An organization is *zerschlagen* — dismantled, broken up by force. The adjective *zerschlagen* means exhausted, shattered — the body feels as though it has been beaten to pieces. The prefix does not redirect the blow but multiplies its consequences: one strike, many fragments.

Literal mnemonic: "strike asunder." English "shatter" and "smash to pieces" match the German, but *zerschlagen* also carries the bodily sense — you feel "beaten apart" after a hard day.

### ZURÜCK- ("back, backward, in return")

**Zurückschlagen:** to strike back. The blow returns to its sender. To *zurückschlagen* is to retaliate, to fight back, to repulse an attack. The prefix reverses the direction of impact: the one who was struck now strikes in return. An army *schlägt zurück* — it counterattacks. A boxer *schlägt zurück* — he hits back.

Literal mnemonic: "strike back." English uses exactly the same construction.

### ZUSAMMEN- ("together")

**Zusammenschlagen:** to strike together. Multiple blows converge on one target, or multiple things are smashed into one heap. To *zusammenschlagen* someone is to beat them up — to rain blows down from every side. To *zusammenschlagen* furniture is to wreck it — to smash it together into rubble. The prefix gathers the violence, concentrating it.

Literal mnemonic: "strike together." Think of "smash together" — fists come together on a target, or things are smashed into a pile.

### ZU- ("to, toward, shut")

**Zuschlagen:** to strike to, to strike shut. The blow lands hard — you *schlägst zu*, you hit hard, you deliver the decisive strike. A door *schlägt zu* — it slams shut, struck closed. A surcharge is *zugeschlagen* — added on, struck onto the bill. The prefix drives the impact toward closure: a deal, a door, a blow — all brought to a sharp conclusion.

Literal mnemonic: "strike shut" or "strike home." A door is "struck shut." A fighter "strikes home." A tax is "struck on."

### ÜBER- ("over, above, beyond")

**Überschlagen:** to strike over in the mind. The inseparable *über-* lifts the blow above the details. To *überschlagen* costs is to estimate roughly — to pass your mental eye over the numbers without striking each one precisely. Reflexively, *sich überschlagen* means to roll over, to somersault — the body strikes over itself. To skip a page is to *überschlagen* it — to strike past it, leaping over. The prefix always implies going over the top: above the details, above the surface, above the normal orientation.

Literal mnemonic: "strike over." You "run over" the numbers quickly. A car "rolls over." You "skip over" a chapter.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays physical; the prefixes go abstract.** *Schlagen* never stops meaning "to hit." But the prefixes redirect that blow into astonishing metaphors. *Vorschlagen* (strike before) becomes "to suggest." *Nachschlagen* (strike after) becomes "to look up." *Unterschlagen* (strike under) becomes "to embezzle." The arm-swinging gesture of hitting is projected onto intellectual and social actions. Once you see this, the abstractions become memorable: a suggestion is a blow struck forward; a reference lookup is a blow struck after the fact; embezzlement is a blow struck under the table.

**Separable prefixes tend to be spatial and violent.** *Ab, an, auf, ein, los, zurück, zusammen* — these preserve the physical force of *schlagen* even in figurative use. You can picture the direction of the blow. *Einschlagen* (strike in) means to hammer in a nail or smash in a window — the violence is literal. Even *losschlagen* (strike loose) maintains the image of fists unleashed.

**Inseparable prefixes tend to be transformative and opaque.** *Be-, er-, ver-, unter-, über-, zer-* — these fuse with the verb and often carry it far from literal hitting. *Beschlagen* (thoroughly struck) gives us both "fogged up" and "knowledgeable." *Verschlagen* (displaced by a blow) gives us both "driven off course" and "cunning." These must often be learned as units, though the underlying image — once uncovered — can serve as a powerful mnemonic.

**The body remembers what the mind forgets.** More than most verb families, *schlagen* compounds retain a physical energy that helps with memorization. *Erschlagen* (slay) has the finality of a killing blow. *Zerschlagen* (shatter) has the splintering of impact. *Fehlschlagen* (fail) has the frustration of a swing that misses. Even *nachschlagen* (look up) has the urgency of chasing after something. Let your body feel the blow's direction, and the meaning follows.

**English sometimes preserves the same logic.** "Strike back" = *zurückschlagen*. "Strike dead" = *erschlagen*. "Strike off" = *abschlagen*. "Knock out" = *herausschlagen*. Where English and German share the metaphor, use it as an anchor. Where they diverge — and they diverge dramatically with *vorschlagen*, *nachschlagen*, *unterschlagen*, and *beschlagen* — notice that German simply extends the striking metaphor further than English dares. German sees a blow where English sees only an abstraction.
