# LECKEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Lecken** carries two unrelated meanings. The first and older sense is *to lick* — the tongue's motion across a surface. The second is *to leak* — liquid escaping through a crack or seam. Both have been part of the verb since Middle High German, though they descend from different roots that converged in form. For the learner, the key fact is this: all four prefixed forms below belong to the "lick" sense. The "leak" meaning stands alone, unprefixed, and context always makes clear which is meant.

Hold the image of a tongue meeting a surface. Each prefix below redirects that gesture — removing what the tongue finds, making initial contact, reaching inside, or covering thoroughly.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Ablecken:** to lick off, to lick clean. The prefix pulls the action away from the surface. A child *leckt* ice cream from a spoon *ab* — the tongue removes it. A cat *leckt* its paw *ab* — it licks until nothing remains. The emphasis is on removal: the tongue works across a surface and takes something away.

Literal mnemonic: "lick off." English uses the identical construction — "the dog licked the peanut butter off the spoon."

### AN- ("on, onto, up against")

**Anlecken:** to lick, to give a lick. The prefix pushes the action toward first contact — the tongue touches the surface briefly, tentatively. You *lecken* a stamp *an* — one quick lick to moisten the adhesive. You *lecken* an envelope *an* — tongue meets glue. This is a light, preliminary lick rather than a thorough one. Where *ablecken* finishes the job, *anlecken* merely starts it.

Literal mnemonic: "lick at" or "give a lick to." Think of touching the tongue to something just enough to test it.

### AUS- ("out, outward")

**Auslecken:** to lick out, to lick clean. The prefix directs the tongue inward into a container and brings what is inside out. A child *leckt* a bowl *aus* — the tongue reaches in and cleans out the last traces. A dog *leckt* a can *aus* — it works the inside surfaces until nothing is left. The emphasis is on emptying: the tongue goes in and brings everything out.

Literal mnemonic: "lick out." "She licked out the yogurt cup" — the tongue enters the vessel and extracts what remains.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Belecken:** to lick, to lick all over. As with other *be-* verbs, the prefix stamps the action thoroughly onto an object. Where plain *lecken* describes the tongue's motion, *belecken* focuses on the surface being licked — covering it completely. A cow *beleckt* her calf — she licks the calf all over. The inseparable prefix transforms the gesture from a simple action into a comprehensive treatment of the object.

Mnemonic: think of *be-* as "all over." To *belecken* something is to give it a complete licking, the way "bespatter" means to spatter all over.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The "leak" sense takes no prefixes.** German handles the "leak" meaning with *lecken* alone or turns to other verbs entirely (*auslaufen*, *durchsickern*). The four prefixed forms all picture a tongue at work. This makes the family easier to learn: if you see a prefix, it means licking.

**The prefixes divide the licking action neatly.** *An-* is the tentative first contact. *Ab-* removes something from a surface. *Aus-* empties a container. *Be-* covers an object thoroughly. Together they sketch four distinct scenes from a single gesture, and none overlaps with another.

**Separable vs. inseparable follows the usual rule.** *Ab-, an-, aus-* are spatial adverbs that can stand alone — they separate. *Be-* is a grammatical transformer that fuses with the verb and never separates. The separable forms stay close to physical direction; *be-* shifts the focus from the action to the object receiving it.

**English parallels are unusually close here.** "Lick off," "lick at," "lick out" — English uses the same spatial particles for the same meanings. Only *belecken* lacks a direct English equivalent, because English has no productive "be-" prefix. But "lick all over" captures it precisely.
