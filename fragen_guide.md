# FRAGEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Fragen** means to ask, to inquire. It is the most basic German verb for posing a question, directly related to the noun *die Frage* (the question). Hold this image in mind: one person turning to another and putting a question forward. Nearly every prefix below reshapes that basic act — redirecting it, intensifying it, narrowing its purpose, or pushing it into specialized social territory.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abfragen:** to ask off, to quiz. The prefix pulls the asking into a checking-off motion — you work through a list, testing knowledge point by point. A teacher *fragt Vokabeln ab* — she asks vocabulary off the list, quizzing the student until each item has been tested and dismissed. A computer *fragt Daten ab* — it queries a database, polling entries one after another. The image is systematic extraction: asking in order to pull answers off a surface and verify them.

Literal mnemonic: "ask off." Think of checking items off a list — each question removes one more unknown.

### AN- ("on, at, toward")

**Anfragen:** to ask at, to inquire toward. The prefix directs the asking toward an institution, a person, an authority — you approach someone with a request. You *fragen bei einer Firma an* — you inquire at a company. An *Anfrage* is a formal inquiry, a request directed at someone who holds what you need. The tone is polite and procedural: you are not demanding, you are approaching and asking.

Literal mnemonic: "ask at." English "inquire at the desk" captures the same spatial gesture — directing your question toward a point of contact.

### AUS- ("out, outward, exhaustively")

**Ausfragen:** to ask out, to question thoroughly. The prefix pushes the asking outward and onward until everything has been extracted. To *ausfragen* someone is to grill them, to pump them for information, to keep asking until the well is dry. The tone is persistent and often uncomfortable — this is interrogation at the personal level, the friend who will not stop asking about your date, the detective who presses until every detail has been drawn out.

Literal mnemonic: "ask out" in the sense of "squeeze out." You ask and ask until the information has been fully extracted.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Befragen:** to question someone (directly, formally). *Be-* stamps the asking fully onto a person — the grammar shifts so that the person questioned becomes the direct object. You *befragen einen Zeugen* — you question a witness. You *befragen die Bevölkerung* — you survey the population. Where *fragen* simply asks, *befragen* applies the asking to someone in a structured, official way. Interviews, surveys, legal depositions — these are *Befragungen*.

Mnemonic: think of "be-" as "all over, upon." To *befragen* is to direct your questions upon someone comprehensively. Compare *ausfragen*: both intensify the asking, but *befragen* is formal and methodical, while *ausfragen* is relentless and personal. A journalist *befragt* a politician; your nosy aunt *fragt dich aus*.

### DURCH- ("through")

**Durchfragen:** to ask one's way through. The prefix threads the asking through a space — you navigate by asking. *Sich durchfragen* means to get somewhere by asking directions at each turn: you ask at one corner, walk, ask at the next, walk again. The image is a chain of small questions strung through a route, each one carrying you a step further.

Literal mnemonic: "ask through." You ask your way through an unfamiliar city, question by question, until you arrive.

### ER- (achievement, attainment through effort)

**Erfragen:** to find out by asking, to ascertain through inquiry. *Er-* signals that the asking reaches its goal — you ask and you obtain the answer. Where *fragen* simply poses the question, *erfragen* implies that the effort succeeds. Information is *erfragt* — it has been acquired, won through the act of asking. The prefix adds completion: the question was not just asked but answered.

Mnemonic: "ask to a result." *Er-* marks the endpoint. You asked, and you got what you were after.

### HINTER- ("behind")

**Hinterfragen:** to ask behind, to question critically. This is one of the most culturally resonant verbs in modern German. The prefix sends the asking behind the surface — behind appearances, behind claims, behind assumptions. To *hinterfragen* is to probe, to scrutinize, to refuse the first answer and dig for what lies beneath. It is the verb of critical thinking: not accepting something at face value but asking what is hidden behind it.

Literal mnemonic: "ask behind." When you *hinterfragen* a policy, you are not asking about it — you are asking behind it, probing the motives, logic, and assumptions that hold it up. English has no single verb for this; the closest approximations are "to question critically," "to challenge," "to scrutinize." German collapses all of this into one word: ask behind.

### NACH- ("after, following, further")

**Nachfragen:** to ask after, to follow up. The prefix adds a second pass — you asked once, and now you ask again, pursuing the matter further. To *nachfragen* is to follow up, to check back, to press for more information after the initial exchange. In economics, *die Nachfrage* is demand — the market asking after goods, the continued call for something.

Literal mnemonic: "ask after." English "inquire further" or "follow up" captures the sense. The question comes after the first one, chasing the answer forward.

### UM- ("around")

**Umfragen:** to ask around. The prefix sends the asking in a circle — you survey, you canvass, you poll by asking many people in turn. *Eine Umfrage* is a survey or opinion poll: questions sent around a group to gather a collective picture. The image is rotational: the asking sweeps around a population.

Literal mnemonic: "ask around." English uses the same construction — "I asked around and found out" means you surveyed the people near you.

### ÜBER- ("over, beyond")

**Überfragen:** to ask beyond, to stump. This is an unusual verb — the prefix pushes the asking past someone's capacity. To say *da bin ich überfragt* is to admit that the question has gone beyond your knowledge: you have been asked over your head. The verb is almost always used in this passive, confessional sense. It is not an aggressive act but a quiet acknowledgment: the question exceeds what I can answer.

Literal mnemonic: "ask over" or "ask beyond." The question sails over the person's head — they cannot reach it. English "that's beyond me" captures the same spatial logic, though German builds it directly into the verb.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays social; the prefix adjusts the power dynamic.** *Fragen* never stops meaning "to ask." But the prefixes recalibrate who holds the power, how persistent the asking is, and what it aims to achieve. *Befragen* is institutional and controlled. *Ausfragen* is relentless and personal. *Hinterfragen* is intellectual and probing. *Überfragen* flips the dynamic entirely — the asker has the power, and the one asked is overwhelmed. The same basic act of posing a question becomes interrogation, inquiry, scrutiny, or defeat, depending on the prefix.

**Ausfragen vs. Befragen: intensity and register.** Both mean "to question someone thoroughly," but they occupy different social spaces. *Befragen* belongs to courtrooms, surveys, and journalism — it is formal, structured, and implies a protocol. *Ausfragen* belongs to kitchens, bars, and interrogation rooms — it is informal, relentless, and implies that the questioner will not stop until they have everything. A researcher *befragt* subjects; a suspicious parent *fragt* a teenager *aus*.

**Hinterfragen has no English equivalent.** English can say "question critically" or "challenge assumptions," but these are phrases, not a single verb. *Hinterfragen* packages the entire act of looking behind claims into one word, which reflects how deeply the concept is embedded in German intellectual culture. Where English speakers must explain ("I want to examine the assumptions underlying this"), German speakers simply *hinterfragen*.

**Separable prefixes remain concrete and directional.** *Ab-, an-, aus-, durch-, nach-, um-* — these preserve a physical sense of motion even when applied to abstract asking. You quiz *off* a list, inquire *at* an office, question *out* of someone, ask *through* a city, follow up *after*, poll *around*. The spatial image survives intact.

**Inseparable prefixes transform the act itself.** *Be-, er-, hinter-, über-* — these fuse with the verb and reshape its fundamental character. *Befragen* changes the grammar. *Erfragen* adds success. *Hinterfragen* adds critical depth. *Überfragen* adds defeat. These cannot be pictured as simple directional movements; they must be understood as transformations of what "asking" means.
