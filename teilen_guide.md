# TEILEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Teilen** means to divide, to split, to share. The core image is of something whole being separated into parts. A cake is *geteilt*, an opinion is *geteilt*, costs are *geteilt*. Hold this picture in mind: a hand drawing a line through a whole, creating portions. Every prefix below redirects, specifies, or transforms that basic act of division.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abteilen:** to part off, to section off. The prefix *ab-* cuts a portion away from the whole. You draw a boundary and separate one section from the rest — screening off a room with a partition, dividing a warehouse into departments. The emphasis is on creating discrete compartments by cutting off.

Literal mnemonic: "divide off." Think of "partitioning off a corner of the room."

### AUF- ("up, open, into pieces")

**Aufteilen:** to divide up. The prefix *auf-* opens the whole and spreads it out into portions. Where *teilen* simply divides, *aufteilen* distributes the result — the pieces go somewhere. An inheritance is *aufgeteilt* among heirs. Territory is *aufgeteilt* between nations. The prefix adds the sense of allocating the divided pieces outward.

Literal mnemonic: "divide up." English uses exactly the same construction — "divide up the spoils."

### AUS- ("out, outward")

**Austeilen:** to deal out, to hand out. The prefix *aus-* sends the divided portions outward, away from the divider. Cards are *ausgeteilt* — dealt out. Flyers are *ausgeteilt* — distributed. Punishments are *ausgeteilt* — administered, dealt out. The image is centrifugal: portions fly outward from a central hand.

Literal mnemonic: "deal out." English "mete out" and "hand out" carry the same outward distribution.

### EIN- ("in, into, within")

**Einteilen:** to divide into, to organize within. The prefix *ein-* directs the dividing action inward — into categories, into degrees, into a structure. You *teilst* time *ein* — you budget it, organize it into slots. You *teilst* species *ein* — you classify them, sort them into groups. Where *aufteilen* scatters portions outward, *einteilen* arranges them inward into an orderly system.

Literal mnemonic: "divide in(to)." Think of "sorting into categories" — the pieces go into boxes.

### ER- (achievement, bestowal)

**Erteilen:** to grant, to bestow, to issue. The connection to *teilen* has become opaque, but the etymology is clear: to deal out something of value to someone — a permission, a lesson, a reprimand. *Erteilen* is formal: you *erteilen* approval (*Genehmigung*), instruction (*Unterricht*), or a rebuke (*Verweis*). The prefix *er-* signals that the act reaches its recipient and takes effect.

Mnemonic: think of "deliver a share of authority." A teacher *erteilt Unterricht* — deals out instruction. An official *erteilt Genehmigung* — deals out permission.

### MIT- ("with, along, together")

**Mitteilen:** to share with, to communicate. The prefix *mit-* means "with" — you share your portion of knowledge with someone. To *mitteilen* is to give someone a share of what you know: to inform, to tell, to communicate. The image is of dividing your knowledge and passing a part to another person.

Literal mnemonic: "share with." English "impart" captures the same idea — giving someone a part of what you have.

### UNTER- ("under, sub-, below")

**Unterteilen:** to divide under, to subdivide. The prefix *unter-* pushes the division to a lower, finer level. What has already been divided is divided again — categories are broken into subcategories, sections into subsections. The direction is downward into finer granularity.

Literal mnemonic: "subdivide." English "subdivide" works exactly the same way — dividing under the first level of division.

### VER- (distribution, scattering, transformation)

**Verteilen:** to distribute, to spread out, to disperse. The prefix *ver-* scatters the divided portions widely. Where *austeilen* hands things out from a fixed point, *verteilen* spreads them across an area. Troops are *verteilt* across a region — deployed, dispersed. Weight is *verteilt* evenly — spread out. Cancer cells *verteilen sich* — they metastasize, scattering through the body. The prefix adds breadth and thoroughness to the distribution.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "all over." The portions are not just dealt out; they are scattered far and wide.

### ZER- ("apart, to pieces, asunder")

**Zerteilen:** to divide asunder, to cut to pieces. The prefix *zer-* is destructive — it tears apart. Where *teilen* divides neatly, *zerteilen* rips, cleaves, sunders. Waves are *zerteilt* by a ship's bow. Clouds are *zerteilt* by wind. The whole is not just divided but broken apart, often violently or irreversibly.

Literal mnemonic: "tear apart." Think of Moses parting the Red Sea — the water is *zerteilt*, cleaved open.

### ZU- ("to, toward, assigned to")

**Zuteilen:** to divide toward, to assign, to allocate. The prefix *zu-* directs a portion toward a specific recipient. Rations are *zugeteilt* — allocated, portioned out to individuals. Tasks are *zugeteilt* — assigned to people. The emphasis is on the destination: each portion goes to someone specific.

Literal mnemonic: "allot to." English "assign to" captures the directed allocation.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays concrete; the prefixes steer the distribution.** *Teilen* always means to divide. But the prefixes specify *how* and *where* the portions go. *Auf-* opens and distributes. *Aus-* deals outward. *Ein-* organizes inward. *Ver-* scatters broadly. *Zu-* assigns to recipients. Once you see that each prefix answers "where do the pieces end up?", the family becomes navigable.

**Separable prefixes are spatial and transparent.** *Ab, auf, aus, ein, mit, zer, zu* — these are concrete directional words, and their compounds with *teilen* are largely predictable from the parts. You can usually picture the physical gesture.

**Inseparable prefixes shift meaning further.** *Er-* (grant), *ver-* (distribute widely), and *unter-* (subdivide) fuse more tightly with the stem. *Erteilen* has drifted the furthest — "to grant" is hard to derive from "to divide" without knowing the history. *Verteilen* and *unterteilen* remain more transparent.

**English parallels exist but fragment.** German keeps "divide" as the root throughout, while English jumps between "divide," "distribute," "share," "communicate," "grant," "allocate," and "subdivide." Where English uses Latin-derived vocabulary (distribute, communicate, allocate), German stays with its Germanic stem and lets the prefix do the work. Recognizing this pattern reduces the apparent vocabulary burden — ten German verbs, one root.
