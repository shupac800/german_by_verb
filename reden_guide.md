# REDEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Reden** means to talk, to speak, to converse. It implies the act of producing speech — sustained, purposeful talking rather than a single utterance (that would be *sagen*). Hold this image in mind: a person speaking at length, directing words at someone or into a situation. Nearly every prefix below redirects that stream of speech — aiming it at a target, channeling it toward a purpose, or distorting it through excess.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abreden:** to talk off, to talk away from. The prefix pulls the speech away from a position — you direct your words at someone's intention until they step back from it. To dissuade is to talk someone *off* their plan. The image is speech used as a lever, prying someone loose from something they were holding onto.

Literal mnemonic: "talk off." English "talk someone off the ledge" captures the same gesture — words aimed at pulling someone away from a decision.

### AN- ("on, onto, at")

**Anreden:** to talk at, to talk onto. The prefix pushes the speech toward a person, making contact. To *anreden* someone is to address them — to direct your words onto them, to open a channel. It carries a touch of formality: this is the moment you turn your speech toward a specific person and begin.

Literal mnemonic: "talk at" or "speak to." Think of "addressing" someone — you put the address on your words and send them to a recipient.

### AUS- ("out, outward, to completion")

**Ausreden:** to talk out. Two distinct senses sharing one spatial logic. (1) *Dissuade:* you talk someone *out of* an idea — your words push the intention outward until it is expelled. (2) *Finish speaking:* you talk *out* to the end — you let the speech run its full course outward. *Lass mich ausreden* means "let me talk out" — let me finish, let the speech come all the way out.

Literal mnemonic: "talk out." English uses both: "talk someone out of it" and "hear me out."

Note the mirror with **einreden** below: *ein-* talks ideas *into* someone's head; *aus-* talks ideas *out* of it.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Bereden:** to talk over thoroughly. *Be-* stamps the action fully onto its object — you talk *about* something until it has been covered from all sides. To discuss, to debate, to talk over. Where *reden* is just speaking, *bereden* is speaking *about something specific* with the intent to examine it. The prefix converts aimless talking into purposeful discussion.

Mnemonic: think of "be-" as "all over." To *bereden* a matter is to talk it over completely, to cover it with words.

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Einreden:** to talk into. You direct a stream of speech *into* someone's head until an idea takes root there. To persuade, to talk into, to put something into someone's head. The image is insistent: you keep talking *into* someone until your words penetrate. *Jemandem etwas einreden* — to talk something into somebody — suggests persistence, even manipulation.

Literal mnemonic: "talk in." English "talk someone into it" is an exact match. The words go in and the decision follows.

Note the mirror with **ausreden** above: *ein-* pushes ideas inward; *aus-* pulls them out.

### HERAUS- ("out from within")

**Herausreden** (reflexive: *sich herausreden*): to talk one's way out from inside. You are trapped — in a situation, an accusation, a corner — and you use speech to extract yourself. The prefix is directional: out from within. You talk yourself out, producing excuses, deflections, explanations until you have escaped.

Literal mnemonic: "talk out of (from inside)." English "talk your way out of it" is the same metaphor. The trouble is the container; the speech is the escape route.

### HER- ("hither, toward the speaker, vaguely")

**Herreden:** to talk hither, to talk along vaguely. The prefix *her-* often implies directionless motion toward the speaker — and applied to speech, it produces aimless, empty talking. To ramble, to talk nonsense. The words come forth (*her*) but go nowhere meaningful. *Was redet er da her?* — What is he going on about?

Literal mnemonic: "talk along" without destination. The speech drifts toward you but carries nothing.

### MIT- ("with, along with")

**Mitreden:** to talk with, to talk along. The prefix adds you to an existing conversation — you join in, you have a say, you participate. *Da kann ich mitreden* means "I can talk along on that" — I have knowledge or standing to contribute. The image is walking alongside others in conversation, matching your speech to theirs.

Literal mnemonic: "talk with" or "talk along." To have a say is to be allowed to talk *with* the others, not just listen.

### NACH- ("after, behind, in imitation of")

**Nachreden:** to talk after. Two senses from two readings of "after." (1) *Repeat:* to say after someone — to echo their words, to parrot. (2) *Gossip:* to talk *behind* someone — to say things after they have left the room. *Üble Nachrede* is slander — bad talk that follows someone around. Both senses share the same image: speech that comes *after*, whether in echo or in their absence.

Literal mnemonic: "talk after." Repeating is talking after someone's words. Gossiping is talking after someone's departure.

### ZER- ("apart, to pieces, asunder")

**Zerreden:** to talk apart, to talk to pieces. The prefix is destructive — it shreds. You take a subject and talk it into fragments. To overanalyze through discussion, to talk something to death. A good idea, *zerredet*, loses its shape and energy — the excessive speech dismantles it the way *zerlegen* disassembles a machine. Where *bereden* covers a topic productively, *zerreden* covers it until nothing useful remains.

Literal mnemonic: "talk to pieces." English "talk it to death" captures the same exhaustion. The speech doesn't clarify; it destroys.

### ZU- ("to, toward, at someone")

**Zureden:** to talk toward, to talk at someone encouragingly. The prefix directs speech *toward* a person with intent to move them. To encourage, to urge, to coax. Unlike *einreden* (which pushes ideas *into* someone's head, with overtones of manipulation), *zureden* aims words *at* someone to give them strength or resolve. *Gut zureden* — to talk soothingly toward someone — is the classic usage.

Literal mnemonic: "talk to" in the nurturing sense. "She talked to him encouragingly" — the words flow toward, supporting rather than penetrating.

### ÜBER- ("over, across" — inseparable)

**Überreden:** to talk over. The prefix passes *over* the listener's resistance — your speech sweeps across their objections until they yield. To persuade, to convince, to win over. Because *über-* is inseparable, the verb fuses into a single concept: this is not spatial talking-over but a complete act of persuasion. Compare *einreden* (talk into, insistent, possibly unwelcome) with *überreden* (talk over, successful, result-oriented). *Einreden* emphasizes the pressure; *überreden* emphasizes the outcome.

Literal mnemonic: "talk over" — not in the sense of interrupting, but of passing over resistance. "She talked him over to her side" captures it.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays interpersonal; the prefixes steer the purpose.** *Reden* never stops meaning "to talk." But the prefixes reshape what the talking accomplishes. *Einreden* (talk into) plants ideas. *Ausreden* (talk out) removes them. *Zerreden* (talk apart) destroys them. *Überreden* (talk over) overcomes resistance. The act of speaking remains constant; the prefix determines what the speech *does*.

**EINREDEN and AUSREDEN are a mirror pair.** This is one of the cleanest prefix oppositions in German. *Ein-* directs speech inward — into someone's head — to implant an idea. *Aus-* directs speech outward — out of someone's head — to extract one. "Talk into" and "talk out of" work identically in English, making this pair easy to internalize. Where *ein-* fills, *aus-* empties.

**Persuasion has three textures.** German offers three ways to talk someone into something, each with a different flavor. *Einreden* (sep.) is insistent, repetitive, possibly manipulative — you hammer the idea in. *Zureden* (sep.) is warm, encouraging, supportive — you coax gently. *Überreden* (insep.) is effective, result-focused — you get it done. Choosing among them tells the listener not just *what* happened but *how* the persuasion felt.

**Separable prefixes preserve the direction of speech.** *Ab, an, aus, ein, her, heraus, mit, nach, zu* — these are spatial words that tell you where the talking is aimed. You can picture the trajectory: inward, outward, toward, away, alongside. This makes most separable *reden* compounds transparent once you know the prefix.

**The inseparable prefixes fuse with the stem.** *Be-* and *über-* merge with *reden* into unified concepts — *bereden* (discuss) and *überreden* (persuade). They no longer feel like "talk + direction" but like single verbs with their own weight. This is typical: inseparable prefixes transform the stem rather than merely redirecting it.

**English parallels are unusually strong here.** "Talk into" = *einreden*. "Talk out of" = *ausreden*. "Talk over" = *bereden*. "Talk to pieces" = *zerreden*. "Talk your way out" = *sich herausreden*. Because English phrasal verbs with "talk" follow the same spatial logic as German prefix verbs with *reden*, this stem is one of the most accessible for English speakers. Use these parallels as anchors.
