# ZIEHEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Ziehen** means to pull, to draw, to drag. It is one of the most physical and versatile stems in German — a hand gripping something and moving it through space. But *ziehen* also means to move, to migrate, to relocate (people *ziehen* from one place to another), and to steep or brew (tea *zieht*). Hold this image in mind: a sustained force applied along a line — pulling a rope, drawing a bow, dragging a suitcase. Nearly every prefix below redirects that pulling force.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abziehen:** to pull off, to draw away. The prefix peels the pulling gesture away from a surface or total. You *ziehen* a skin *ab* — you strip it off. You *ziehen* a number *ab* — you subtract it, pulling it away from the sum. Troops are *abgezogen* — withdrawn, pulled away from their position. The image is always removal: something attached is pulled free.

Literal mnemonic: "pull off" or "draw off." English "deduct" literally means "lead away" — *abziehen* is "pull away."

### AN- ("on, onto, toward")

**Anziehen:** to pull on, to draw toward. The prefix directs the pull onto the body or toward the self. Clothing is *angezogen* — pulled on, put on. A magnet *zieht an* — it draws objects toward itself, it attracts. The two senses share one gesture: bringing something close, pulling it on or toward you.

Literal mnemonic: "pull on" or "draw toward." "Pull on your coat" and "the magnet draws things on" — same prefix, same direction.

### AUF- ("up, open")

**Aufziehen:** to pull up, to pull open. The prefix lifts or opens. You *ziehen* a clock *auf* — you wind it up, pulling the spring tight. You *ziehen* a child *auf* — you raise it, pull it up into adulthood. You *ziehen* a zipper *auf* — you pull it open. And colloquially, you *ziehen jemanden auf* — you tease them, "pull their leg." The common thread is upward or outward motion: winding up, raising up, pulling open.

Literal mnemonic: "pull up" or "wind up." "Wind up the clock," "bring up a child," "pull someone's leg."

### AUS- ("out, outward, off")

**Ausziehen:** to pull out, to take off. The prefix sends the pull outward. Clothing is *ausgezogen* — pulled off, removed. A tenant *zieht aus* — they move out, pulling themselves and their belongings from the dwelling. A table is *ausgezogen* — extended, its leaves pulled out. Removal and departure in every sense.

Literal mnemonic: "pull out" or "pull off." "Take off your shoes" = pull them off. "Move out" = pull yourself out.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Beziehen:** to pull upon, to cover, to relate to. *Be-* stamps the pulling action onto an object. A bed is *bezogen* — its sheets pulled over it, it is covered. A salary is *bezogen* — drawn, received. An opinion is *auf etwas bezogen* — related to something, drawn into connection with it. The prefix makes the pulling comprehensive: you pull something fully over or fully into relation.

Mnemonic: "be-" = "all over." To *beziehen* a bed is to pull covers all over it. To *beziehen* a statement on something is to draw it fully into connection.

### DURCH- ("through")

**Durchziehen:** to pull through. The prefix sends the pulling gesture through a space from one side to the other. A thread is *durchgezogen* — drawn through a needle. A plan is *durchgezogen* — carried through to completion. The image is persistence: pulling something all the way through, not stopping halfway.

Literal mnemonic: "pull through." "Pull the thread through" and "see it through" — same spatial logic.

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Einziehen:** to pull in. The prefix directs the pull inward. A tenant *zieht ein* — moves in, pulls themselves and their goods into a new dwelling. Claws are *eingezogen* — retracted, pulled in. Taxes are *eingezogen* — collected, pulled in by the authorities. Soldiers are *eingezogen* — drafted, pulled into service. The gesture is always inward: gathering, retracting, incorporating.

Literal mnemonic: "pull in" or "draw in." "Move in," "draw in the claws," "pull in taxes" — everything moves inward.

### ENTLANG- ("along, alongside")

**Entlangziehen:** to pull along, to move along. The prefix traces a line — you move along a boundary, a river, a road. The pulling is horizontal, following a course. Simple and spatial.

Literal mnemonic: "move along." "The parade moved along the street."

### ENT- ("away from, removal, de-")

**Entziehen:** to pull away from, to withdraw, to deprive. *Ent-* reverses possession — something is pulled out of someone's grasp. Rights are *entzogen* — withdrawn. A license is *entzogen* — revoked, pulled away. *Sich entziehen* means to evade, to pull yourself away from an obligation. In the medical sense, to detoxify — to pull the substance away from the body.

Literal mnemonic: "de-pull" or "draw away." Think of "deprive" — pulling the thing away from the person.

### ER- (achievement, result, bringing into being)

**Erziehen:** to pull into being, to raise, to educate. *Er-* brings the pulling to fruition — you pull a child upward into a formed human being. *Erziehen* means to educate, to bring up, to raise. The image is of long, sustained pulling that shapes someone into maturity. A parent *erzieht* — they draw the child through years of formation.

Mnemonic: "raise up to completion." The pulling is developmental — not physical but formative. "Bring up a child" = pull them up into adulthood.

### FORT- ("away, onward")

**Fortziehen:** to pull away, to move away. The prefix sends the departure onward — you move away from where you were, migrating, emigrating, gone.

Literal mnemonic: "move forth." "They moved forth from the city" = they departed.

### GLEICH- ("equal, even, level")

**Gleichziehen:** to pull even, to draw level. The prefix makes the pull match something else — you catch up, you reach the same level. In a race, you *ziehen gleich* — you draw level with the leader.

Literal mnemonic: "draw level." "Draw even with the competition."

### HERAUF- ("up from below toward the speaker")

**Heraufziehen:** to pull up. The prefix adds specificity — the pulling is upward and toward the speaker. A bucket is *heraufgezogen* from a well. Clouds *ziehen herauf* — they come up over the horizon, drawing near.

Literal mnemonic: "pull up (toward here)."

### HERAUS- ("out from within")

**Herausziehen:** to pull out from inside. The prefix specifies the direction: out from within an enclosure. A splinter is *herausgezogen* — pulled out from the skin. A book is *herausgezogen* from a shelf. The extraction is from inside to outside.

Literal mnemonic: "pull out (from in there)."

### HER- ("toward the speaker, hither")

**Herziehen:** to pull toward here, to draw near. Physically, to move closer. Colloquially, *über jemanden herziehen* means to speak ill of someone — to "pull them over" in gossip, dragging their reputation toward you to tear it apart.

Literal mnemonic: "pull hither." The colloquial sense: "drag someone's name through the mud."

### HINTER- ("behind, covertly")

**Hinterziehen:** to pull behind, to evade, to embezzle. *Hinter-* sends the action behind a screen — out of sight, hidden. Taxes are *hinterzogen* — evaded, pulled behind a concealment. Money is *hinterzogen* — embezzled, pulled behind the scenes. The prefix implies deception: the pulling happens where no one can see.

Literal mnemonic: "pull behind" or "pull covertly." Tax evasion is pulling money behind a curtain.

### HIN- ("thither, toward a point, drawing out")

**Hinziehen:** to draw out, to protract. The prefix stretches the pulling toward a distant point — things *ziehen sich hin* when they drag on, extending further than expected. A negotiation *zieht sich hin* — it pulls itself toward an ever-receding endpoint.

Literal mnemonic: "pull out (toward there)." "The meeting dragged on" = it pulled itself further and further out.

### HINZU- ("in addition, toward")

**Hinzuziehen:** to pull in, to consult. The prefix adds someone to the group — you pull in an expert, you call in an advisor. The gesture is inclusive: drawing another person toward the situation to contribute.

Literal mnemonic: "pull in (additionally)." "Call in a specialist" = pull them toward the problem.

### LOS- ("loose, free, off")

**Losziehen:** to pull loose, to set out. The prefix releases — you break free and begin moving. *Losziehen* means to set off, to depart, to start a journey. The image is of someone released from a starting line.

Literal mnemonic: "pull free" or "set off." "They set off on their journey" = they pulled loose and went.

### NACH- ("after, following")

**Nachziehen:** to pull after, to follow suit. The prefix trails behind — you pull something along after you, or you follow the example set by another. In cards, you *ziehen nach* — you follow suit. A lagging group *zieht nach* — they catch up, pulling themselves after the leaders.

Literal mnemonic: "pull after" or "follow suit." "Drag it after you" and "follow the leader's example."

### UM- ("around, re-, change")

**Umziehen:** to pull around, to change. Two senses from the same prefix. You *ziehen um* your residence — you move, you relocate, pulling your life around to a new place. You *ziehen sich um* — you change clothes, pulling new garments around yourself. Both involve transformation through a change of wrapping.

Literal mnemonic: "change around." "Move house" and "change clothes" — both are re-arrangements.

### UNTER- ("under, beneath, through")

**Unterziehen:** to pull under, to undergo. As an inseparable verb: *sich einer Prüfung unterziehen* — to undergo an examination, to pull yourself under its demands, to submit to it. As a separable verb: to put underneath — *ein Kleidungsstück unterziehen* — to pull a garment on underneath.

Literal mnemonic: "pull under." To undergo is to go under — to submit yourself to the process.

### VER- (transformation, displacement, going wrong)

**Verziehen:** to pull awry, to distort. *Ver-* twists the pulling in multiple directions. The face is *verzogen* — contorted, pulled into a grimace. A child is *verzogen* — spoiled, pulled out of proper shape by excessive indulgence. Clouds *verziehen sich* — they disperse, pulling apart and disappearing. A family *verzieht* — they move away, pulled to a new location. The common thread: the pulling changes something from its proper form or place.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "awry, out of shape." A spoiled child has been pulled out of true. A grimace is a face pulled wrong. Clouds pull apart. A family pulls away.

### VOLL- ("full, complete")

**Vollziehen:** to pull to fullness, to execute, to carry out. *Voll-* drives the action to completion — you pull it through to its full conclusion. A sentence is *vollzogen* — executed. A marriage is *vollzogen* — consummated. A transformation *vollzieht sich* — it takes place, it completes itself. The prefix demands totality: the pulling reaches its end.

Literal mnemonic: "fulfill by pulling through." To execute is to carry something to its full completion.

### VOR- ("before, forward, in preference")

**Vorziehen:** to pull forward, to prefer. The prefix moves something to the front of the line. You *ziehen etwas vor* — you pull it forward, you prefer it, you give it priority. A curtain is *vorgezogen* — pulled forward, drawn across. An appointment is *vorgezogen* — brought forward, moved to an earlier time. Preference is spatial: what you favor is pulled to the front.

Literal mnemonic: "pull forward." "I prefer tea" = "I pull tea forward." "Draw the curtain" = pull it before the window.

### WEG- ("away, gone")

**Wegziehen:** to pull away, to move away. The simplest directional prefix — the pull goes away, and the thing (or person) is gone. You *ziehen weg* — you move away, you depart. A cloth is *weggezogen* — pulled away, snatched off.

Literal mnemonic: "pull away." "Pull it away" and "move away" — both mean gone.

### ZURÜCK- ("back, backward")

**Zurückziehen:** to pull back, to withdraw. The prefix reverses the pull — what went forward now comes back. Troops are *zurückgezogen* — withdrawn. An offer is *zurückgezogen* — retracted. *Sich zurückziehen* — to retreat, to retire, to pull yourself back from engagement.

Literal mnemonic: "pull back." "Withdraw" literally means "draw back" — the English and German are perfectly parallel.

### ZUSAMMEN- ("together")

**Zusammenziehen:** to pull together, to contract. The prefix gathers — things are pulled inward toward each other. Muscles *ziehen sich zusammen* — they contract. Troops are *zusammengezogen* — assembled, massed. A couple *zieht zusammen* — they move in together, pulling their separate lives into one place.

Literal mnemonic: "pull together." "Contract" literally means "draw together" (Latin *con-* + *trahere*) — same logic.

### ZU- ("to, toward, closed, additional")

**Zuziehen:** to pull to, to draw shut. The prefix closes or adds. A curtain is *zugezogen* — drawn shut, pulled to the closed position. A knot is *zugezogen* — pulled tight. An expert is *zugezogen* — called in, pulled toward the situation. An injury is *sich zugezogen* — incurred, pulled toward oneself. And a newcomer *zieht zu* — moves to a place, pulls toward it.

Literal mnemonic: "pull to." "Draw the curtain to" (shut). "Pull the knot to" (tight). "Called to" (consulted).

### ÜBER- ("over, beyond, excess")

**Überziehen:** to pull over, to coat, to exceed. *Über-* sends the pull beyond a surface or a limit. A cake is *überzogen* — coated, pulled over with frosting. An account is *überzogen* — overdrawn, pulled beyond its balance. A speech is *überzogen* — run over time, pulled past its limit. A coat is *übergezogen* — pulled on over everything else. The prefix always implies coverage or excess: over the surface, over the limit.

Literal mnemonic: "pull over." "Overdraw" = pull over the limit. "Coat" = pull a layer over. "Overrun" = pull past the boundary.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem is pure force along a line.** *Ziehen* is one of the most physical verbs in German — it is always about exerting directional force. The prefixes do not change this; they only redirect it. *Ein-* pulls in. *Aus-* pulls out. *Zurück-* pulls back. *Zusammen-* pulls together. Once you feel the tug in every compound, the meanings become spatial intuitions rather than vocabulary items.

**This is one of the largest verb families.** With over 25 prefixed forms, *ziehen* rivals *legen*, *stehen*, and *gehen* in productivity. The sheer number reflects how fundamental pulling is as a metaphor: movement, attraction, extraction, migration, education, execution — German maps them all onto one physical gesture.

**Separable prefixes tend to be spatial and concrete.** *Ab, an, auf, aus, ein, fort, gleich, herauf, heraus, her, hin, hinzu, los, nach, um, vor, weg, zurück, zusammen, zu* — these are directional words that preserve the physical pull. You can always picture the motion.

**Inseparable prefixes transform the pull into abstraction.** *Be-* (relate, cover), *ent-* (deprive), *er-* (educate), *hinter-* (evade), *voll-* (execute), *über-* (coat, exceed), *ver-* (distort, spoil) — these fuse with the stem and produce meanings that require knowing the compound as a unit. The pulling force is still there conceptually, but it has been sublimated into institutional, psychological, or social domains.

**English "draw" and "pull" parallel *ziehen* remarkably well.** "Draw back" = *zurückziehen*. "Draw in" = *einziehen*. "Draw out" = *herausziehen*. "Pull through" = *durchziehen*. "Pull off" = *abziehen*. "Withdraw" = *entziehen*. "Overdraw" = *überziehen*. Where the languages share the metaphor, use it. Where they diverge — *erziehen* (educate) has no English "pull" equivalent — notice the gap and learn the German logic on its own terms.
