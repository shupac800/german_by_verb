# KONTROLLIEREN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Kontrollieren** means to check, to inspect, to control, to monitor. It entered German from French *contrôler* (itself from *contre-rôle*, "against the roll" — checking entries against a master list). Hold this image in mind: someone running a careful eye over something to verify it is correct. The verb covers the full spectrum from casual checking (glancing at a ticket) to systematic oversight (monitoring a process, supervising a workforce).

---

## The Prefixes

### NACH- ("after, again, following up")

**Nachkontrollieren:** to check after, to check again. The prefix adds a second pass — the initial check has already happened, and now you go back and do it once more. A mechanic *kontrolliert nach* — she re-inspects the repair to make sure nothing was missed. A teacher *kontrolliert die Hausaufgaben nach* — she double-checks the homework a second time. The prefix is temporal: it places the action after an earlier instance of the same action.

Literal mnemonic: "check after" or "after-check." English uses "re-check" and "double-check" for the same idea — going back over what was already inspected to catch what the first pass might have missed.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**A borrowed stem limits the prefix family.** Native Germanic stems like *legen* or *stehen* attract dozens of prefixes because they are old, monosyllabic, and deeply embedded in the language's spatial logic. Latinate borrowings like *kontrollieren* resist this. German speakers rarely feel the urge to coin *auskontrollieren* or *einkontrollieren* — the stem is already semantically specific, leaving less room for prefixes to reshape it. The result is a tiny family: just the stem and one prefixed form.

**NACH- does what it always does.** Across many verb families, *nach-* adds the meaning "again after the fact" — *nachprüfen* (re-examine), *nachmessen* (re-measure), *nachzählen* (re-count). With *kontrollieren*, the pattern is identical. If you know what *nach-* does to one checking verb, you know what it does to all of them.

**Where German prefixes and English "re-" overlap.** English handles the "do it again" idea with the prefix "re-" (recheck, reinspect, reverify). German uses *nach-* for the same purpose but adds a nuance English lacks: *nach-* implies the second check follows the first in time, not merely that the action is repeated. To *nachkontrollieren* is specifically to follow up — to check *after* someone else has already checked, or after a process has run its course. The temporal sequence is built into the prefix.
