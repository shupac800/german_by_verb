# STELLEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Stellen** means to put, to place, to set upright. Where *legen* implies laying something flat, *stellen* implies standing something up — placing it so it is upright, positioned, or set in its proper orientation. Hold this image in mind: a hand taking an object and standing it in place. *Stellen* is the causative partner of *stehen* (to stand): *stehen* is the state of standing; *stellen* is the act of making something stand, of putting it where it should be. Nearly every prefix below redirects, intensifies, or abstracts that basic gesture of deliberate positioning.

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abstellen:** to put off, to put away. The prefix pulls the action away from activity. A suitcase is *abgestellt* — set down, put aside. An engine is *abgestellt* — switched off, its operation put to an end. A bad habit is *abgestellt* — stopped, put away for good. The thread connecting all these senses is cessation through removal: you take something out of its active position.

Literal mnemonic: "set off." You set the engine off, set the bag off to the side, set the problem off — put an end to it.

### AN- ("on, onto, up against")

**Anstellen:** to set on, to put onto. The prefix pushes something into engagement. An employee is *angestellt* — set on, put into position, hired. A machine is *angestellt* — turned on, set into operation. Reflexively, *sich anstellen* means to set yourself onto a queue — to line up. Colloquially, *was stellst du an?* means "what are you getting up to?" — what are you setting in motion? Every sense involves putting something into active contact with a role, a process, or a line.

Literal mnemonic: "set on." English preserves this in "to take someone on" (hire) and "to turn something on."

### AUF- ("up, upon, open")

**Aufstellen:** to set up, to stand up. The prefix lifts the placement upward and outward. A tent is *aufgestellt* — set up, erected. Soldiers are *aufgestellt* — lined up, deployed. A candidate is *aufgestellt* — put up, nominated. A rule is *aufgestellt* — set up, established. The gesture is always one of raising something into visible, functional position — making it stand where others can see it and engage with it.

Literal mnemonic: "set up." English works identically — you set up a tent, set up a team, set up rules.

### AUS- ("out, outward")

**Ausstellen:** to set out, to put out for viewing. Two clean branches grow from one image. Objects are *ausgestellt* — put out on display, exhibited. Documents are *ausgestellt* — issued, put out into the world with official standing. In both cases, something is taken from a private or internal state and set out where it becomes public and visible.

Literal mnemonic: "set out" or "put out." A museum puts paintings out; an office puts a certificate out.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Bestellen:** to place fully upon, to put in order. *Be-* stamps the action completely onto its object. To *bestellen* goods is to place an order — to set your claim firmly upon what you want. To *bestellen* a field is to till it — to work the ground thoroughly, putting it in cultivated order. To *bestellen* someone is to summon them — to place a demand upon them to appear. The underlying logic is always one of commanding arrangement: you impose order on something by placing your will upon it.

Mnemonic: "be-" as "all over, thoroughly." You place your order upon the goods, your plow upon the field, your summons upon the person.

### DURCH- ("through")

**Durchstellen:** to put through. A telephone call is *durchgestellt* — connected, put through from one end to the other. The prefix sends the placement straight through a barrier or intermediary. Simple, spatial, and transparent.

Literal mnemonic: "put through." English uses the same phrase — "I'll put you through to the manager."

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Einstellen:** to set in, to put into. This is one of the richest verbs in the family, because "setting something inward" takes so many forms. A dial is *eingestellt* — set to the right position, adjusted, tuned inward to the correct mark. A worker is *eingestellt* — set into a position, hired, put into the organization. Production is *eingestellt* — set in, i.e. brought to rest, ceased, discontinued (the machinery is set inward to a stop). Reflexively, *sich einstellen auf* means to set yourself into readiness — to prepare for, to adapt to. The common thread: something is placed into a defined position, whether that position is a dial setting, a job role, a state of cessation, or a mental posture.

Literal mnemonic: "set in." The dial is set in place. The worker is set in a role. Operations are set in — suspended, settled into stillness. You set yourself in for what is coming.

### ENTGEGEN- ("against, toward, in opposition")

**Entgegenstellen:** to set against. You place something in the path of an opposing force. *Sich entgegenstellen* means to set yourself against something — to oppose, to resist, to counter. The prefix is both spatial (facing toward) and adversarial (standing in the way).

Literal mnemonic: "set against." You set your argument against theirs; you set yourself against the tide.

### ENT- ("away from, removed from" — here transformative)

**Entstellen:** to set away from its proper form. *Ent-* strips away the original character. A face is *entstellt* — disfigured, its features displaced from their natural arrangement. A report is *entstellt* — distorted, its meaning pulled away from the truth. The prefix removes the thing from how it should be set, leaving it deformed, misrepresented, unrecognizable.

Literal mnemonic: "un-set" or "dis-place from form." To disfigure is literally to remove (*ent-*) from its proper setting (*stellen*).

### ER- (achievement, completion, bringing into existence)

**Erstellen:** to set into existence. *Er-* here signals the completion of a creative act. To *erstellen* a report is to produce it — to bring it into being by setting it up from scratch. Documents, databases, plans — all are *erstellt*, created and delivered as finished products. The prefix converts the neutral act of placing into one of origination.

Mnemonic: "set forth into being." What is *erstellt* did not exist before; now it stands.

### FEST- ("firm, fixed, tight")

**Feststellen:** to set firm. Three senses branch from one root image. To *feststellen* a fact is to determine it — to set it firmly, to establish it beyond doubt. To *feststellen* something unexpected is to notice it — to fix your attention on it, to register it as firm reality. To *feststellen* a position publicly is to state it — to declare it, to set it firmly on the record. In every case, something uncertain or fluid becomes fixed: locked down, pinned in place, set firm.

Literal mnemonic: "set fast." A fact is set fast when it is determined. An observation is set fast when it is noticed. A declaration is set fast when it is stated.

### GEGENÜBER- ("across from, face to face")

**Gegenüberstellen:** to set face to face. You place two things across from each other for comparison. Suspects are *gegenübergestellt* — placed in a lineup, confronted. Ideas are *gegenübergestellt* — juxtaposed, set side by side for contrast. The prefix enforces a spatial symmetry: two things standing face to face.

Literal mnemonic: "set opposite." A lineup sets the suspect opposite the witness; an essay sets two theories opposite each other.

### GLEICH- ("equal, same, even")

**Gleichstellen:** to set equal. You place two things at the same level. Rights are *gleichgestellt* — equated, put on equal footing. The prefix flattens any hierarchy: what was above or below is now set at the same height.

Literal mnemonic: "set level." To equalize is to set everything at the same position.

### HERAUS- ("out from within")

**Herausstellen:** to set out from inside. You take something embedded or hidden and place it where it can be seen. To *herausstellen* a point is to emphasize it — to pull it out and set it in front. Reflexively, *es stellt sich heraus* means "it turns out" — something sets itself out from concealment into plain view, proving to be something unexpected.

Literal mnemonic: "set out (from within)." A key point is set out for emphasis; the truth sets itself out — it emerges.

### HER- ("hither, toward the speaker, connoting production")

**Herstellen:** to set forth, to produce. The prefix *her-* pulls the action toward the speaker, toward existence, toward completion. To *herstellen* something is to manufacture it — to set it into being, to bring it here into the world. A connection is *hergestellt* — established, brought into existence. The sense is always productive: raw materials are set into the form of a finished product; an absent link is set into reality.

Literal mnemonic: "set here" or "set forth." The factory sets products forth; the diplomat sets a connection into place.

### HIN- ("thither, down, toward a point away from the speaker")

**Hinstellen:** to set down, to put in place. The prefix gives direction: away and down. You *stellen* something *hin* — you set it down in a spot. Reflexively, *sich hinstellen* means to go stand somewhere — to set yourself in position. The most basic, physical sense of placing an upright object.

Literal mnemonic: "set down (over there)." "I set the vase down on the table" = *Ich habe die Vase hingestellt.*

### NACH- ("after, behind, re-")

**Nachstellen:** to set after. Two senses follow from the idea of pursuing or repeating. A clock is *nachgestellt* — set back, readjusted (set after the correct time to catch up). A person is *nachgestellt* — stalked, pursued, set after relentlessly. The prefix adds the dimension of following: either you re-set something to match a standard, or you set yourself after someone.

Literal mnemonic: "set after." You set the clock after the correct time; the stalker sets himself after his target.

### UM- ("around, over, about" — separable vs. inseparable)

**Umstellen** has two distinct lives depending on stress and separability:

*Separable (um-):* to set around differently — to rearrange, to reorganize, to switch over. Furniture is *umgestellt* — rearranged, placed in new positions. A diet is *umgestellt* — switched over, adjusted to a new pattern. The prefix means "around" in the sense of reconfiguration: everything gets set in a new arrangement.

*Inseparable (um-):* to set around — to surround, to encircle. Police *umstellen* a building — they place themselves all around it, closing it off. The prefix means "around" in the sense of enclosure: the placement forms a ring.

Literal mnemonic: separable = "re-set" (set things about in a new way); inseparable = "set around" (form a perimeter). The separable verb changes the arrangement; the inseparable verb creates an encirclement.

### UNTER- ("under, beneath" — separable vs. inseparable)

**Unterstellen** splits into three senses across the separable/inseparable divide:

*Separable (unter-):* to set under shelter — to store, to put something under cover. A bicycle is *untergestellt* — placed under a roof, put away out of the rain. The prefix is purely spatial: under a protective surface.

*Inseparable (unter-, sense 1):* to set under authority — to subordinate. A department is *unterstellt* to a director — placed under their command. The prefix establishes hierarchy: what is *unterstellt* stands beneath.

*Inseparable (unter-, sense 2):* to set underneath someone's words — to insinuate, to impute, to attribute falsely. *Das unterstelle ich dir* means "I'm imputing that to you" — placing a motive or meaning beneath what someone said. The prefix slides something covertly underneath.

Literal mnemonic: separable = "set under cover"; inseparable = "set under authority" or "set beneath (someone's intentions)." The shift from shelter to subordination to insinuation traces a path from concrete space to social hierarchy to rhetorical underhanding.

### VER- (transformation, displacement, error, intensification)

**Verstellen:** *ver-* pushes the setting into alteration and concealment. Three senses:
- *Adjust/alter:* to set differently — to shift, to change the position of something. A seat is *verstellt* — adjusted, moved to a new setting.
- *Block:* to set in the way — to obstruct. Furniture *verstellt* the path — it is placed so as to block passage.
- *Disguise:* to set falsely — to feign, to put on. A voice is *verstellt* — disguised, set to sound like something it is not. Reflexively, *sich verstellen* means to dissemble — to set yourself in a false position.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "mis-set" or "set away from the true." The seat is set to a different position. The path is set wrong with obstacles. The voice is set falsely. In each case, the original setting is displaced.

### VOLL- ("full")

**Vollstellen:** to set full. You place so many things that the space fills up. A room is *vollgestellt* — cluttered, crowded with objects standing everywhere. The prefix adds saturation to the placement.

Literal mnemonic: "set full." The garage is set full of boxes — there is no room left.

### VOR- ("before, forward, in front of")

**Vorstellen:** to set before. One of the most important verbs in the family, with three major branches:

- *Introduce:* to set someone before others — to present them. *Darf ich Ihnen Herrn Schmidt vorstellen?* — "May I set Mr. Schmidt before you?" — "May I introduce him?"
- *Imagine (reflexive):* *sich vorstellen* — to set something before your mind's eye. To imagine is to place an image in front of yourself internally. *Stell dir vor!* — "Set it before yourself!" — "Just imagine!"
- *Move forward:* to set something forward in time or space — to advance. A clock is *vorgestellt* — set forward.

Literal mnemonic: "set before." You set a person before company (introduce). You set an image before your mind (imagine). You set the clock before the current time (advance). The prefix is always "in front of" — physically, socially, or mentally.

### ZURÜCK- ("back, backward, in return")

**Zurückstellen:** to set back. You place something back where it was, or back in time. A product is *zurückgestellt* — put back on the shelf, returned. A decision is *zurückgestellt* — deferred, set back to a later date. Personal wishes are *zurückgestellt* — put aside, set back behind more pressing concerns. The prefix reverses the placement or postpones it.

Literal mnemonic: "set back." You set the bottle back. You set the decision back to next month. You set your own needs back behind others'.

### ZUSAMMEN- ("together")

**Zusammenstellen:** to set together. You take separate things and arrange them into a unified collection. A team is *zusammengestellt* — assembled, its members set together. A report is *zusammengestellt* — compiled, its pieces set together into a whole. A menu is *zusammengestellt* — composed, its dishes set together into a selection.

Literal mnemonic: "set together." "Let's put together a team" = *Stellen wir ein Team zusammen.*

### ZU- ("to, toward, closed")

**Zustellen:** to set to, to deliver or to block. Two senses follow from "toward" and "closed." A package is *zugestellt* — delivered, set to its destination. A door is *zugestellt* — blocked, something is set against it so it cannot open. In delivery, the prefix pushes something toward the recipient. In blocking, the prefix pushes something to close off passage.

Literal mnemonic: "set to." The mail is set to your door (delivered). The hallway is set to (blocked off).

### ÜBER- ("over, across")

**Überstellen:** to set over, to transfer. You move someone or something across from one jurisdiction, location, or authority to another. A prisoner is *überstellt* — transferred, handed over. The prefix carries the placement across a boundary.

Literal mnemonic: "set over (to the other side)." The detainee is set over from one facility to another.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**Stellen is the causative of stehen, and this matters.** *Stehen* means "to stand" (a state); *stellen* means "to make stand, to put in position" (an action). This causative relationship — shared by *liegen/legen*, *sitzen/setzen*, *hängen/hängen* — is one of German's deep structural patterns. When you *stellen* something, you are causing it to *stehen*. Every prefixed form inherits this: *aufstellen* is to cause something to stand up; *feststellen* is to cause something to stand firm.

**Where legen is horizontal, stellen is vertical.** *Legen* lays things flat; *stellen* stands things upright. This distinction pervades daily speech: books are *gestellt* (stood on a shelf), plates are *gestellt* (set upright in a rack), but a blanket is *gelegt* (laid flat). The prefix families parallel each other — *auflegen/aufstellen*, *einlegen/einstellen*, *vorlegen/vorstellen* — but the base orientation differs, and this shapes the metaphors. *Vorlegen* (lay before) tends toward presenting documents; *vorstellen* (set before) tends toward presenting people and ideas.

**The biggest verbs carry the most senses.** *Einstellen*, *vorstellen*, *feststellen*, *umstellen*, and *unterstellen* each carry three or four seemingly unrelated meanings. But each meaning is a different metaphorical extension of the same spatial gesture. *Einstellen* — setting inward — becomes adjusting (setting a dial inward to its mark), hiring (setting a person inward into an organization), ceasing (setting operations inward to rest), and preparing (setting yourself inward for what comes). The spatial core holds them together.

**Separable prefixes are spatial and concrete.** *Ab, an, auf, aus, ein, hin, vor, zurück, zusammen* — these are standalone adverbs and prepositions. They preserve a physical directionality. You can picture the motion: *aufstellen* raises, *abstellen* removes, *zusammenstellen* gathers.

**Inseparable prefixes are transformative and opaque.** *Be-, ent-, er-, ver-, über-* fuse with the verb and shift meaning further from the literal. *Entstellen* (de-set, un-place) becomes "to disfigure." *Erstellen* (set into existence) becomes "to create." *Verstellen* (mis-set) becomes "to disguise." These must often be learned as complete units, though the prefix logic usually rewards reflection.

**The sep./insep. splits are systematically meaningful.** Both *umstellen* and *unterstellen* split into separable and inseparable forms with different meanings. The pattern is consistent: the separable form is spatial and concrete (rearrange, store under shelter), while the inseparable form is abstract and figurative (surround, subordinate, insinuate). This is not accidental — it reflects a general principle in German: when a prefix fuses inseparably with a verb, the meaning moves further from physical space into social, institutional, or psychological territory.

**English sometimes preserves the same logic.** "Set up" = *aufstellen*. "Set aside" = *zurückstellen*. "Put through" = *durchstellen*. "Put together" = *zusammenstellen*. Where English and German share the metaphor, lean on it. Where they diverge, notice the pattern: German extends the "setting/placing" metaphor into domains where English switches to entirely different verbs — "to manufacture" for *herstellen*, "to imagine" for *sich vorstellen*, "to determine" for *feststellen*. The German speaker still sees someone setting something in place; the English speaker has long since abandoned the spatial image.
