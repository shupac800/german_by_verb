# DRÜCKEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Drücken** means to press, to push, to squeeze. It is a highly physical verb of applied force — the image is of a hand bearing down on something, compressing it, forcing it into or through a surface. A button is *gedrückt* — pressed. A hand is *gedrückt* — squeezed in greeting. A weight *drückt* on the shoulders — it presses down, burdens. The verb can also shade into avoidance: *sich drücken* means to press yourself away from a duty, to shirk. But the core image remains: sustained, deliberate pressure applied by one thing onto another. Nearly every prefix below redirects that force — aiming it, intensifying it, reversing it, or abstracting it into metaphor.

A note on a close relative: **drucken** (no Umlaut) means to print. Printing is pressing — a press pushes ink onto paper. The two verbs share a root, and the connection is worth holding in mind: wherever *drücken* appears, the ancient logic of pressing is at work.

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abdrücken:** to press off. The prefix pulls the pressing action away from a resting state. With a firearm, you press the trigger off its seat — you fire, you discharge. With a mold, you press an object off its form — you take an impression, capturing the shape by pressing into soft material and then lifting away. In both cases, the pressure is applied and then released, producing an effect at the moment of separation.

Literal mnemonic: "press off." A trigger is pressed off; an impression is pressed off a surface.

### AN- ("on, onto, up against")

**Andrücken:** to press on, to press against. The prefix directs the force toward contact. You press something onto a surface so it sticks, adheres, or holds. A bandage is *angedrückt* — pressed on. A sheet of wallpaper is *angedrückt* — pushed firmly against the wall. The gesture is always the same: applying pressure so that one thing meets another and stays.

Literal mnemonic: "press on." Think of pressing a stamp onto an envelope — forcing it into firm contact.

### AUF- ("up, upon, open")

**Aufdrücken:** to press open. The prefix lifts or opens. You apply force and something gives way upward or outward — a door is *aufgedrückt*, pressed open. A tube is *aufgedrückt*, squeezed so its contents come out. The image is of pressure overcoming a closure: you push and the thing opens, yields, releases.

Literal mnemonic: "press open" or "press up." Where *zudrücken* presses shut, *aufdrücken* presses open — the two are natural opposites.

### AUS- ("out, outward")

**Ausdrücken:** to press out, to squeeze out. Physically, you apply pressure and something emerges — juice is *ausgedrückt* from a lemon, water is wrung out of a cloth, a cigarette is *ausgedrückt* (stubbed out, its fire pressed out of existence). But the most important leap is figurative: to *express* is to press outward. When you *drücken* a thought *aus*, you squeeze meaning out of yourself and into words. English "express" follows exactly the same logic — from Latin *exprimere*, "to press out." The metaphor is identical in both languages: what is inside (a feeling, an idea) is pressed outward into language so others can receive it. *Sich ausdrücken* means to express oneself — to press one's inner meaning out into the open.

Literal mnemonic: "press out." English "express" is Latin for "press out" — the metaphor is the same.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Bedrücken:** to press upon. *Be-* takes the pressing force and stamps it fully onto a person. The result is emotional: to be *bedrückt* is to feel pressed upon, weighed down, burdened. This is oppression felt inwardly — not political suppression but the heavy, personal sensation of something sitting on your spirit. A *bedrückendes Gefühl* is a feeling that presses down on you. The physical force has become psychological: the hand pressing on a surface is now sadness or worry pressing on a soul.

Mnemonic: think of "be-" as "upon, all over." To *bedrücken* someone is to lay pressure all over them — they feel pressed, troubled, weighed down from every side.

### DURCH- ("through")

**Durchdrücken:** to press through. The prefix drives the force forward until it penetrates or prevails. Physically, you press something through a sieve or opening. Figuratively, you force a proposal through opposition — you push it through resistance until it passes. The image is of pressure that does not stop at the surface but continues until it breaks through to the other side.

Literal mnemonic: "press through." English "push through" captures the same idea — forcing something past every obstacle.

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Eindrücken:** to press in. The prefix directs the force inward. You press and the surface gives way — collapses, dents, caves in. A box is *eingedrückt* — crushed inward. A skull can be *eingedrückt* — pressed in. The gesture is always inward deformation: pressure that does not bounce off but sinks into the object, leaving a mark or a cavity.

Literal mnemonic: "press in." Think of pressing your thumb into soft clay — the surface yields inward.

### ER- (completion, finality, sometimes destruction)

**Erdrücken:** to press to completion — to crush. *Er-* signals that the pressing reaches its terminal point: the object is not merely compressed but destroyed. To *erdrücken* is to crush fatally, to smother under weight, to overwhelm so completely that nothing remains. A person is *erdrückt* by a collapsing building. Figuratively, someone is *erdrückt* by debt, by responsibilities — the pressure is so total that it annihilates. This belongs to the family of *er-* verbs where the prefix means "to do something to the point of finality" — *erschlagen* (strike dead), *ersticken* (suffocate), *ertrinken* (drown).

Mnemonic: "press to death." The force is applied until the thing under it is finished.

### LOS- ("loose, free, released")

**Losdrücken:** to press loose, to let off. The prefix releases. With a firearm, you press and the shot is released — you fire, you pull the trigger and let the bullet loose. Like *abdrücken*, this is the moment of discharge, but *los-* emphasizes the release rather than the separation: something held back is suddenly set free by the press of a finger.

Literal mnemonic: "press loose" or "let press." The trigger is pressed and the shot goes free.

### UNTER- ("under, beneath")

**Unterdrücken:** to press under. The prefix drives the force downward, beneath the surface. To *unterdrücken* is to suppress, to oppress, to hold down. A people is *unterdrückt* — pressed under the weight of authority. A feeling is *unterdrückt* — pushed beneath consciousness, repressed. A rebellion is *unterdrückt* — forced down, quashed. The physical image is vivid: something tries to rise and a hand presses it back under. Where *bedrücken* is the emotional sensation of being weighed upon, *unterdrücken* is the active, deliberate act of keeping something down — political, social, psychological. The oppressor presses; the oppressed are held beneath.

Literal mnemonic: "press under." English "suppress" is Latin for "press under" (*sub-* + *premere*) — the metaphor is structurally identical.

### VER- (transformation, displacement, intensification)

**Verdrücken:** to press away, to press into a different state. *Ver-* transforms the pressing in several directions. With food, *verdrücken* means to polish off, to put away — you press the meal into yourself until it vanishes (colloquial, humorous). With fabric, it means to crush or crease — pressure applied wrongly, leaving wrinkles. Reflexively, *sich verdrücken* means to slip away, to press yourself out of a situation quietly, to slink off. In each case *ver-* twists the straightforward pressing into something displaced or transformed: the food is pressed into disappearance, the cloth is pressed into the wrong shape, the person presses themselves away from where they were.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "away into change." *Verdrücken* a meal = press it away (devour it). *Sich verdrücken* = press yourself away (sneak off).

### WEG- ("away, gone")

**Wegdrücken:** to press away. The simplest "away" prefix — you apply force and push something aside, thrust it away from you. No metaphor, no abstraction. The thing is pressed elsewhere and dismissed.

Literal mnemonic: "press away." You push it away from you and it is gone.

### ZER- ("apart, to pieces, asunder")

**Zerdrücken:** to press apart, to press to pieces. The prefix is destructive — it scatters. You apply pressure until the object loses its form. A berry is *zerdrückt* — squashed, mashed. A can is *zerdrückt* — crushed flat. Potatoes are *zerdrückt* — mashed, their structure pressed into pieces. Unlike *erdrücken*, which emphasizes fatal finality, *zerdrücken* emphasizes fragmentation: the thing is not merely overwhelmed but broken apart by force.

Literal mnemonic: "press asunder." Think of crushing a grape between your fingers — the skin splits, the contents scatter.

### ZU- ("to, toward, closed")

**Zudrücken:** to press shut. The prefix closes. You apply pressure and something that was open is now sealed — an eye is *zugedrückt*, pressed closed. A door is *zugedrückt*, pushed shut. The idiom *ein Auge zudrücken* (to press one eye shut) means to turn a blind eye, to let something slide. The gesture is always toward closure: pressure that seals rather than opens.

Literal mnemonic: "press shut." The opposite of *aufdrücken* — where that presses open, this presses closed.

### ZUSAMMEN- ("together")

**Zusammendrücken:** to press together. You take something and compress it — squeeze its parts toward each other. A sponge is *zusammengedrückt* — squeezed together, its volume reduced. The prefix gathers the force inward from all sides, compacting whatever is caught between. This is pure compression: the space between things is pressed out until they meet.

Literal mnemonic: "press together." Think of squeezing a ball of clay in both hands — pressing all sides together.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays physical; the prefix goes abstract.** *Drücken* never stops meaning "to press." But the prefixes push that pressure into metaphor. *Ausdrücken* (press out) becomes "to express." *Bedrücken* (press upon) becomes "to distress." *Unterdrücken* (press under) becomes "to suppress." The body's gesture of bearing down on something is projected onto emotional, social, and intellectual life. Once you see the pressing hand behind every prefix, the abstractions become less arbitrary.

**Physical force maps onto social and psychological force.** *Bedrücken*, *unterdrücken*, and *erdrücken* form a triad: pressed upon (emotional burden), pressed under (suppression and oppression), and pressed to destruction (crushing, overwhelming). These three prefixes take the same physical force and aim it at different depths — *be-* sits on the surface, *unter-* pushes beneath it, *er-* drives through to annihilation.

**Latin and German share the same metaphors.** "Express" = *ex-* (out) + *premere* (press) = *ausdrücken*. "Suppress" = *sub-* (under) + *premere* = *unterdrücken*. "Depress" = *de-* (down) + *premere* = *bedrücken*. "Compress" = *com-* (together) + *premere* = *zusammendrücken*. English inherited these Latin compounds, and German built the same meanings from its own roots. Where the parallels exist, use them — they are exact structural mirrors.

**Drücken and drucken: pressing and printing.** The verb *drucken* (to print) is *drücken* without the Umlaut — a historical sibling. A printing press is a pressing press: it pushes inked type against paper. *Der Druck* means both pressure and print. *Der Ausdruck* means both expression (pressing out meaning) and printout (pressing out onto paper). Keeping this family connection in view helps anchor both verbs in the same physical image: ink, meaning, and force all pressed outward.

**Separable prefixes stay concrete; inseparable prefixes go deeper.** *Ab, an, auf, aus, ein, los, weg, zu, zusammen* — these preserve a visible directionality. You can picture where the force goes. *Be-, er-, unter-, ver-* — these fuse with the verb and transform its meaning further from the literal. *Bedrücken* does not mean "to press a specific direction" but "to press upon someone's spirit." *Erdrücken* does not redirect the force but intensifies it to its fatal conclusion. As with other German verb families, the inseparable prefixes demand more interpretation and less spatial imagination.
