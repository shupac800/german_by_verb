# SPRECHEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Sprechen** means to speak, to talk, to say. It implies the act of producing language — directing words at someone or into the world. Hold this image in mind: a mouth opening, voice meeting air, meaning reaching a listener. Nearly every prefix below reshapes that basic utterance — redirecting it, intensifying it, completing it, or warping it into metaphor.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Absprechen:** to speak off, to speak away. Two distinct threads run from this prefix. In one direction, *ab-* means "off to the side, between parties" — you speak with someone off to the side and reach an agreement. To *absprechen* a time is to arrange it, to agree on it by talking it out. In the other direction, *ab-* means "away from, stripped of" — you speak something away from a person, denying them a right or quality. To *jemandem etwas absprechen* is to dispute their claim to it, to strip them of it by declaration.

Literal mnemonic: "speak off." You speak off to the side to arrange something, or you speak a quality off someone — deny it, strip it away.

### AN- ("on, onto, up against")

**Ansprechen:** to speak onto, to speak at. The prefix pushes the voice toward a target. You *sprechen* someone *an* — you direct speech at them, you address them, approach them with words. From this contact comes the sense of appeal: something that *spricht dich an* speaks to you, touches you, makes an impression. And when you *sprechen* a topic *an*, you touch on it, bring it up — your words land on the subject for the first time.

Literal mnemonic: "speak to / speak at." English "that speaks to me" captures the appeal sense. "Address" (literally "direct toward") mirrors the approaching sense.

### AUS- ("out, outward, to completion")

**Aussprechen:** to speak out. The prefix pushes the act of speaking outward, to its full extent. To *aussprechen* a word is to pronounce it — to push it fully out of the mouth with all its sounds intact. To *aussprechen* a thought is to express it, to voice it openly, to declare it. Reflexively, *sich aussprechen* means to speak oneself out — to finish speaking, to say everything one has to say until nothing remains inside.

Literal mnemonic: "speak out." English "speak out" means the same — to voice something fully and openly. "Pronounce" (from Latin "put forth") captures the articulation sense.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Besprechen:** to speak over thoroughly. *Be-* stamps the action fully onto an object. You do not merely speak — you speak over something, covering it with discussion. To *besprechen* a plan is to discuss it, to talk it over, to examine it from all sides through speech. A book that is *besprochen* has been reviewed — spoken about thoroughly and critically.

Mnemonic: think of *be-* as "all over." To *besprechen* is to cover a subject with speech the way *belegen* covers something with toppings. English "talk over" is the closest parallel.

### DURCH- ("through")

**Durchsprechen:** to speak through. The prefix directs the conversation through a topic from beginning to end. You *sprechen* a problem *durch* — you talk through it, working your way from one end to the other, addressing each point in sequence until you emerge on the other side. Nothing is skipped.

Literal mnemonic: "talk through." English uses the identical construction — "let's talk this through."

### ENT- ("away from, out of, in response to")

**Entsprechen:** to speak in response to, to answer to. *Ent-* here carries the sense of emerging in reply — speech that comes forth to meet something. To *entsprechen* is to correspond to, to match, to meet a standard. The logic: what *entspricht* a requirement has answered it, spoken back to it in kind, matched its call with an equivalent response. The verb has fully left the world of literal speech — it lives entirely in the metaphor of alignment and adequacy.

Literal mnemonic: "speak to meet." Think of "corresponding to" — a letter that *corresponds* answers the one that came before it. *Entsprechen* is the German version of that answering relationship.

### LOS- ("loose, free, released")

**Lossprechen:** to speak loose, to speak free. The prefix releases, and when speech releases, it liberates. To *lossprechen* is to absolve, to acquit, to exonerate — to pronounce someone free from guilt or obligation. The judge speaks, and the accused is loosed. The priest speaks, and the sinner is freed. The image is of words that unbind: a verbal unlocking.

Literal mnemonic: "speak loose." English "absolve" comes from Latin *absolvere* (to loosen from). *Lossprechen* is the Germanic equivalent — to speak someone loose from their bonds.

### NACH- ("after, following")

**Nachsprechen:** to speak after. The simplest directional prefix — you speak after someone else, repeating their words. A teacher says a phrase; the student *spricht nach*. The prefix places your utterance behind theirs in time, an echo following the original.

Literal mnemonic: "speak after." English "repeat after me" captures exactly the same structure.

### VER- (transformation, displacement, error)

**Versprechen:** one of the most revealing *ver-* verbs, because the prefix pulls in two opposite directions at once.

As a straightforward verb, *versprechen* means to promise — to speak forth with binding force. The *ver-* here is transformative: ordinary speech is converted into a commitment, a pledge. Words spoken under *ver-* become something more than words; they become obligations. A *Versprechen* (the noun) is a promise — speech that has been transformed into a bond.

Reflexively, *sich versprechen* means to misspeak — to make a slip of the tongue. Here *ver-* is the error prefix, the same force that turns *legen* (lay) into *verlegen* (mislay). You meant to say one thing but *ver-* twisted the action astray, and the wrong word came out. A Freudian slip is, linguistically, a *Versprecher* — a speaking-gone-wrong.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "into transformation" or "into error." *Versprechen* as promise = speech transformed into commitment. *Sich versprechen* as misspeak = speech gone wrong. The same prefix, two opposite outcomes — binding truth and accidental falsehood.

### VOR- ("before, forward, in front of")

**Vorsprechen:** to speak before, to speak forth. The prefix places speech in front of an audience. To *vorsprechen* is to recite — to speak words aloud before listeners, to pronounce something for someone so they can hear the correct form. From this comes the theatrical sense: to audition is to *vorsprechen*, to step forward and speak before the directors, demonstrating your voice and ability.

Literal mnemonic: "speak before." An audition is literally a "speaking-before" — you present yourself through speech in front of those who will judge.

### WIDER- ("against, counter")

**Widersprechen:** to speak against. The prefix is adversarial — it turns speech into opposition. To *widersprechen* is to contradict, to disagree, to object. Where someone has said one thing, you speak the opposite. The image is of two voices laid against each other, one negating the other. Unlike *besprechen* (discuss cooperatively) or *absprechen* (arrange together), *widersprechen* is combative: your words push back against what was said.

Literal mnemonic: "speak against." English "contradict" is from Latin *contra-dicere* — to say against. *Widersprechen* is its exact Germanic counterpart.

### ZU- ("to, toward, additional")

**Zusprechen:** to speak toward, to speak to someone's benefit. The prefix directs speech toward a recipient in a formal, consequential way. To *zusprechen* is to award, to grant, to adjudge — the court *spricht* custody *zu*, directing the right toward one party by spoken judgment. The act is performative: the speaking itself creates the allocation. Words are sent toward someone, and with them comes a right or a possession.

Literal mnemonic: "speak to / speak toward." Think of a judge "speaking a verdict toward" the plaintiff — the speech itself delivers the award.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays vocal; the prefixes go institutional.** *Sprechen* never stops meaning "to speak." But the prefixes push that speaking into law, religion, theater, and social protocol. *Lossprechen* (speak loose) becomes absolution. *Zusprechen* (speak toward) becomes a legal award. *Vorsprechen* (speak before) becomes an audition. German treats speech as an act with real-world force — words that bind, free, grant, and deny — and the prefixes specify what that force accomplishes.

**Separable prefixes tend to be spatial and concrete.** *Ab-, an-, aus-, durch-, los-, nach-, vor-, zu-* — these preserve a physical directionality. You can picture where the speech is going: outward (*aus-*), after someone (*nach-*), toward someone (*zu-*), before an audience (*vor-*). Even when the meaning is figurative, the spatial image remains accessible.

**Inseparable prefixes tend to be transformative and opaque.** *Be-, ent-, ver-, wider-* — these fuse with the stem and shift meaning further from literal speech. *Entsprechen* (correspond to) has almost nothing to do with speaking anymore. *Versprechen* (promise or misspeak) transforms speech into something qualitatively different. These compounds must often be learned as wholes, though knowing the prefix's force still provides a foothold.

**English often mirrors the same logic through Latin.** "Contradict" = *widersprechen* (speak against). "Pronounce" = *aussprechen* (speak out fully). "Correspond" = *entsprechen* (speak in answer to). "Absolve" = *lossprechen* (loosen by speaking). Where English reached into Latin and French for these concepts, German built them from its own native parts. Recognizing the parallel can make both languages more transparent.
