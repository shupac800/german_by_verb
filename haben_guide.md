# HABEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Haben** means to have, to possess, to own, to contain. It is cognate with English "have" and one of the most fundamental verbs in German — both as a main verb of possession and as an auxiliary verb forming the perfect tenses. Hold this image in mind: a hand holding something, keeping it within one's grasp. The prefixed forms below all work with *haben* as a main verb of possession or wearing. This is worth noting at the outset: although *haben* as an auxiliary pervades the entire language, it resists prefixing in that role. Every compound below is about having, holding, wearing, or keeping — never about forming a past tense.

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abhaben:** to have gotten off, to have received a share. The prefix pulls a portion away from a whole: you *have* a piece *off* of something. A child wants to *abhaben* — to get a piece of the cake, to have some taken off for them. The sense is always partitive: something has been separated from the larger quantity, and now you have it.

Literal mnemonic: "have off." Think of "having a bit off the top" — a portion removed and now in your possession.

### AN- ("on, onto, up against")

**Anhaben:** to have on, to be wearing. The prefix pushes possession into contact with the body. You do not merely *have* a coat — you *have it on*, it is against you, touching you. This is one of the most common colloquial compounds of *haben*. It can also mean to be able to harm someone: *jemandem etwas anhaben* — to "have something on" someone, to have leverage against them.

Literal mnemonic: "have on." English works identically — "what do you have on?" means "what are you wearing?"

### AUF- ("up, upon, open")

**Aufhaben:** to have upon, to have open. The prefix lifts in two directions. First, wearing: a hat is *aufgehabt* — you have it up on your head. Second, openness: a shop *hat auf* — it has its doors open, it is open for business. Third, obligation: homework is *aufgehabt* — it has been laid upon you, placed on your shoulders. Three senses, one spatial logic: something sits up on top of you or stands open.

Literal mnemonic: "have up" or "have open." "I have my hat up on my head." "The shop has its shutters up." "I have three pages of math up for tonight."

### DABEI- ("with, along, present")

**Dabeihaben:** to have with one, to have on hand, to have along. The prefix means "there-with" — something is present alongside you. You *haben* your passport *dabei* — you have it with you, it is there beside you. The sense is portable possession: not stored at home, but carried on your person or brought to the occasion.

Literal mnemonic: "have there-with." "Do you have your ID with you?" = *Hast du deinen Ausweis dabei?*

### DA- ("here, there, present")

**Dahaben:** to have here, to have available, to have in stock. The prefix anchors possession to a location. Something is *da* — it is here, it exists in this place — and you *have* it. A shopkeeper *hat* fresh bread *da* — it is on hand, available right now. The emphasis is on presence and availability rather than ownership.

Literal mnemonic: "have here." "We have it here" = it is in stock, it is available, it is present.

### ER- (achievement, completion, emergence)

**Erhaben** (adj.): sublime, lofty, exalted, majestic. This is one of the most fascinating fossilized forms in German. *Er-* signals a completed elevation — something raised up to its fullest extent. The literal image is "raised having," something that has been lifted above the ordinary and now possesses that height permanently. A cathedral is *erhaben*. A moral principle is *erhaben*. The word traveled from the physical — a surface that is raised, embossed, standing above the plane — to the spiritual: that which towers above the mundane and inspires awe. The journey from "raised up" to "sublime" mirrors the Latin *sublimis* (literally "up to the lintel, under the threshold of heaven"). Both languages reached for height when they needed a word for grandeur.

Mnemonic: "raised into having." What has been raised up completely now *has* elevation as a permanent quality. The embossed coin and the sublime symphony share the same root gesture: rising above the flat surface.

### HERAUS- ("out from within")

**Heraushaben:** to have figured out, to have gotten out. The prefix reaches inward and draws something forth. You *haben* the answer *heraus* — you have extracted it, pulled it out from where it was hidden. You *haben* a splinter *heraus* — you have gotten it out. The sense is always extractive: something was inside, concealed or stuck, and now you have it out.

Literal mnemonic: "have out (from in there)." "I've got it out" — the answer, the stain, the stuck bolt.

### HER- ("from, hither, from a source")

**Herhaben:** to have gotten from, to have obtained from a source. The prefix asks about origin: where does something *come from*? *Wo hast du das her?* — where do you have that from? The sense is not about current possession but provenance. The prefix traces the line back to the source.

Literal mnemonic: "have from (here/there)." "Where did you have that from?" — tracing an object back to its origin.

### UM- ("around, about")

**Umhaben:** to have around oneself, to be wearing something draped or wrapped. The prefix wraps the having gesture around the body. A scarf is *umgehabt* — you have it around your neck. A blanket is *umgehabt* — you have it wrapped around yourself. The sense is specifically circular or enveloping, distinct from *anhaben* (which is general wearing) and *aufhaben* (which sits on top).

Literal mnemonic: "have around." "She has a shawl around her shoulders" = *Sie hat einen Schal um.*

### UNTER- ("under, beneath, among")

**Unterhaben:** to have mixed in, to have blended. The prefix pushes something underneath and among. You *haben* eggs *unter* the batter — they have been folded in, incorporated beneath the surface. This is a domestic, culinary word: the action of blending one ingredient into another so that it disappears under the mass.

Literal mnemonic: "have under." The eggs are now under the surface of the dough, mixed in and invisible.

### VOR- ("before, in front of, ahead")

**Vorhaben:** to intend, to plan, to have in mind. This is the most important compound of *haben* and one of the most common German verbs. The prefix places possession in front of you — not behind, not beside, but ahead, in your line of sight. What you *have before* yourself is what you mean to do, your intention, your plan. The noun *das Vorhaben* (the project, the undertaking) makes the spatial metaphor concrete: your plan stands in front of you, waiting to be walked into.

Literal mnemonic: "have before (oneself)." English "I have something before me" suggests a task or plan ahead. *Was hast du heute vor?* — "What do you have before you today?" = "What are your plans?"

### ZU- ("to, toward, closed, shut")

**Zuhaben:** to be closed, to be shut. The prefix pushes toward closure: *zu* is "shut" in German (*die Tür ist zu* — the door is shut). A shop *hat zu* — it has its doors to, it is closed. Your eyes *haben zu* — they are shut. This is the opposite of *aufhaben* in its "open" sense. Where *auf* opens, *zu* closes.

Literal mnemonic: "have shut." "The store has shut" = *Der Laden hat zu.* The doors are to, pushed closed.

### ÜBER- ("over, above, beyond")

**Überhaben:** to have over, to have on over other clothing; colloquially, to be fed up with. The prefix lays something across the top. Literally, a jacket is *übergehabt* — you have it on over your sweater. Figuratively and colloquially, you *have something over* you — you have had it up over your head, you are sick of it, fed up. The physical image of something piled on top becomes the emotional image of excess and exhaustion.

Literal mnemonic: "have over." "I've had it over and over" — the thing piled on you one too many times. "I have a coat over my shirt."

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**Haben resists prefixing as an auxiliary.** This is the single most important structural point. German uses *haben* constantly to form perfect tenses (*ich habe gemacht*, *sie hat gesagt*), but not one of the compounds above serves that grammatical function. Every prefixed form works with *haben* as a main verb — a verb of possession, wearing, keeping, or intending. The auxiliary *haben* stands alone and unadorned.

**Most compounds are colloquial and separable.** Unlike *legen*, whose prefix forms range from everyday speech to high literary register, the *haben* compounds cluster heavily in informal, spoken German. *Anhaben*, *aufhaben*, *dabeihaben*, *zuhaben* — these are the stuff of daily conversation, not formal prose. You will hear them constantly and read them rarely.

**Wearing is the dominant metaphor.** Several prefixes turn *haben* into a verb of wearing: *anhaben* (to have on generally), *aufhaben* (to have on top, especially hats), *umhaben* (to have around, scarves and wraps), *überhaben* (to have over, outer layers). German parcels the body into zones — on, upon, around, over — and assigns a different prefix to each. English collapses all of these into "have on" or "wear."

**Erhaben stands apart.** Where every other compound remains chatty and domestic, *erhaben* ascended into the philosophical vocabulary — Kant used it, Schiller wrote about it, it became the German word for the aesthetic sublime. It is the only member of this family that fossilized into a pure adjective and the only one that belongs to elevated rather than everyday register. The word for sublimity grew from the most ordinary verb in the language.

**English often preserves the same logic.** "Have on" = *anhaben*. "Have out" = *heraushaben*. "Have with" = *dabeihaben*. The spatial metaphors map closely because both languages descend from the same Germanic root. Where they diverge, German tends to be more specific — distinguishing *anhaben* from *aufhaben* from *umhaben* where English uses "wearing" for all three.
