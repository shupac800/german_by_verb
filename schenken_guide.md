# SCHENKEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Schenken** means to give, to bestow, to donate, to present. It is not neutral handing-over (*geben*) but gift-giving — the transfer of something freely, without expectation of return. The word carries an older meaning that still shapes two of its compounds: in Middle High German, *schenken* also meant "to pour" (a drink poured for a guest was a gift of hospitality). The *Schenke* — a tavern, a pouring-house — preserves this older sense. Hold both images in mind: the open hand offering a gift, and the hand tipping a jug to fill a guest's cup. The prefixes below reshape one or both of these gestures.

---

## The Prefixes

### AUS- ("out, outward, forth")

**Ausschenken:** to pour out, to serve drinks. The prefix directs the pouring gesture outward — liquid flows out of a vessel and into glasses. This is the tavern word: a *Schankwirt* pours drinks, and to *ausschenken* is to serve them, dispensing beer or wine to customers. The older "pour" meaning of *schenken* is fully alive here. The "gift" sense has faded; what remains is the physical act of pouring out.

Literal mnemonic: "pour out." Think of the bartender pouring out rounds — the drink flows *aus* (out of) the tap or bottle and into the waiting glass.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive, directing the action onto the recipient)

**Beschenken:** to give presents to, to shower with gifts. Where plain *schenken* focuses on the gift (*Ich schenke dir ein Buch* — I give you a book), *be-* redirects the verb's grammatical energy onto the person receiving. You *beschenken* someone — you bestow gifts upon them, lavish them, load them up. The object is no longer the gift but the recipient. *Be-* intensifies the giving into an act directed thoroughly at a person: they are the one being "gifted upon."

Mnemonic: think of *be-* as "all over." To *beschenken* someone is to cover them in gifts, to give at them until they are thoroughly bestowed upon. English "to shower someone with gifts" captures the same thorough, recipient-directed image.

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Einschenken:** to pour in, to fill a glass. The prefix directs the liquid inward — into the cup, into the glass. Where *ausschenken* follows the drink out of the bottle, *einschenken* follows it into the vessel. This is the word you hear at the dinner table: *Darf ich dir einschenken?* — May I pour for you? The older "pour" meaning of *schenken* is just as alive here as in *ausschenken*, but the direction reverses: the motion is inward, into the container being filled.

Literal mnemonic: "pour in." The wine goes *ein* (into) the glass. English "pour in" works the same way — "pour me in another glass" would make sense if we still used it.

### VER- (away, dispersal, using up)

**Verschenken:** to give away, to make a gift of. The prefix carries its dispersal force — something is given away from you, released outward into the world without return. To *verschenken* is to give something away freely, often with the implication that you are parting with it permanently and perhaps too generously. You might *verschenken* old furniture, or an opportunity (figuratively: to throw it away by giving it up). The *ver-* adds a sense of finality and sometimes waste — the gift leaves you for good.

Literal mnemonic: "give away." English "give away" is an exact match — "she gave away all her books" = *Sie hat alle Bücher verschenkt.* The *ver-* prefix, as in so many verbs, pushes the action past the point of return.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem splits between two historical meanings.** *Schenken* means "to give (as a gift)" in modern German, but its older meaning — "to pour (a drink)" — survives intact in *ausschenken* and *einschenken*. The link between the two is hospitality: pouring a drink for a guest was the original act of giving. Once you know this, the compound verbs sort cleanly into two families: the "gift" verbs (*beschenken*, *verschenken*) and the "pour" verbs (*ausschenken*, *einschenken*).

**Separable prefixes preserve the physical gesture.** *Aus-* and *ein-* are spatial: liquid flows out or in. These are the pouring verbs, concrete and picturable. You can see the motion.

**Inseparable prefixes transform the social act.** *Be-* and *ver-* do not describe a physical direction. Instead, they reshape who is affected and how thoroughly. *Be-* turns the recipient into the grammatical object; *ver-* pushes the gift away permanently. These must be learned as social gestures rather than decoded as physical ones.

**AUS- and EIN- are complementary, not redundant.** Both mean "to pour," but they track different halves of the same motion. *Einschenken* follows the liquid into the glass (filling); *ausschenken* follows it out of the bottle or tap (serving). German often splits what English treats as one action into two directional perspectives — this pair is a clean example.

**English has no verb family built on "give" the way German builds on *schenken*.** We say "give away," "give presents to," and "pour" — three unrelated constructions. German uses one stem and lets the prefixes do the differentiating. The economy is striking once you see it: four prefixes, one root, and the entire domain of giving and pouring is covered.
