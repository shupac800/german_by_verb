# LAUTEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Lauten** means to read, to be worded, to state. It describes how something sounds when spoken or written — the way a text, rule, or answer is phrased. The verb does not refer to loudness (that would be the unrelated adjective *laut*). Hold this image in mind: a sentence sitting on a page, and someone asking "how does it go?" The answer is how it *lautet*. The small family of prefixed verbs below stays remarkably close to the idea of sound — three of them are technical linguistics terms, and the fourth shifts into the domain of public disclosure.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Ablauten:** to undergo ablaut, to change vowel by ablaut. This is a linguistics term. *Ablaut* — literally "off-sound" — is the systematic vowel alternation found in strong verbs: *singen, sang, gesungen*; English *sing, sang, sung*. The prefix *ab-* suggests departure from the base sound: the vowel moves "off" from its original position. To *ablauten* is for a word to exhibit this shift.

Literal mnemonic: "sound off" — the vowel sounds off from its starting point, changing as it goes.

### AN- ("on, at, at the beginning")

**Anlauten:** to begin with a sound, to have as an initial sound. Another linguistics term. The prefix *an-* here means "at the start, at the onset." To *anlauten* is to have a particular sound at the front of a word. A word that *lautet mit einem Vokal an* begins with a vowel. The *Anlaut* is the initial sound of a word or syllable.

Literal mnemonic: "sound on" or "sound at the start." The sound that is "on" at the opening of the word.

### AUS- ("out, outward, at the end")

**Auslauten:** to end in a sound, to have as a final sound. The complement of *anlauten*. The prefix *aus-* here carries its sense of "out, at the exit." To *auslauten* is to have a particular sound at the tail of a word. A word that *lautet auf -en aus* ends in the sound *-en*. The *Auslaut* is the final sound of a word or syllable.

Literal mnemonic: "sound out" — the sound that plays "out" at the end of the word.

### VER- (transformation, diffusion, becoming public)

**Verlauten:** to be reported, to become known, to be disclosed. Here *ver-* carries its transformative force: the sound is not merely made but sent forth and changed in status — it passes from private to public. Something *verlautet* when it leaks out, is let be known, or is officially disclosed. The phrase *verlauten lassen* — "to let be sounded forth" — means to make a public statement or let information out.

Literal mnemonic: "sound forth" or "sound out into the world." What was silent becomes public knowledge — the sound has been *ver-*transformed into something heard by all.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**Three of four prefixed forms are linguistic terminology.** *Ablauten*, *anlauten*, and *auslauten* belong to the vocabulary of phonology and morphology. They are systematic and symmetrical: *an-* marks the beginning of a word, *aus-* marks the end, and *ab-* marks a vowel's departure from its base form. If you study German linguistics, you will encounter these terms constantly; in everyday speech, they are rare.

**The stem stays close to "how something sounds"; the prefixes specify where or how.** *Lauten* asks "how does it go?" The prefixes answer: at the beginning (*an-*), at the end (*aus-*), away from the original (*ab-*), or out into the public (*ver-*). The family is unusually coherent — there is no wild metaphorical drift here, unlike the sprawling families of *legen* or *stehen*.

**Separable prefixes remain concrete and positional.** *Ab-*, *an-*, and *aus-* all point to a specific location in a word's sound structure — the vowel shift, the initial sound, the final sound. They are separable and transparent. You can picture where in the word the sound sits.

**The inseparable prefix goes abstract.** *Ver-* is the only inseparable prefix in this family, and it is the only one that leaves linguistics behind. *Verlauten* does not describe a position within a word but a transformation in status: from unknown to known, from private to public. This follows the broader pattern: inseparable prefixes tend to transform the action rather than spatially redirect it.

**English borrowed the key term directly.** The word "ablaut" is used in English linguistics without translation — it was taken straight from German. This makes *ablauten* the easiest entry point into the family: if you know what ablaut is, you already understand the verb.
