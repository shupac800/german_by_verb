# WISSEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Wissen** means to know — specifically, to know a fact, to have knowledge of something, to be aware. Unlike *kennen* (to be familiar with, to know a person), *wissen* is about propositional knowledge: knowing that something is the case. Hold this image: a mind that possesses a piece of information. The English cognate is "wit" (as in "to wit," "unwitting," "witness") — all from the same Germanic root meaning to see, to perceive, to know.

---

## The Prefixes

### MIT- ("with, together, jointly")

**Mitwissen:** to know with, to share knowledge of. The prefix adds companionship to the knowing — you don't know alone; you know *with* someone. To *mitwissen* is to be privy to something, to share in the knowledge, especially knowledge of something secret or illicit. The noun *Mitwisser* (an accomplice in knowledge, someone who is "in on it") makes the image vivid: two or more people holding the same dangerous piece of information together.

Literal mnemonic: "know with." English "be privy to" captures the same idea — private knowledge shared among a small group. A *Mitwisser* is a "co-knower."

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**A small family with a powerful stem.** *Wissen* has very few prefixed forms because it is not an action verb — it describes a state of mind rather than a physical gesture. Prefixes that imply spatial direction (auf-, ab-, ein-, aus-) have nothing to grab onto. Only *mit-*, which adds a social dimension rather than a spatial one, combines naturally.

**Wissen vs. kennen.** English collapses both into "know," but German keeps them apart. *Wissen* takes facts and clauses (*Ich weiß, dass...*). *Kennen* takes objects and people (*Ich kenne ihn*). The prefix *mit-* attaches to *wissen* because shared guilty knowledge is about facts (knowing *that* something happened), not about familiarity.

**The noun family is richer than the verb family.** While *wissen* has few prefix compounds, its noun form *Wissen* (knowledge) and the related *Wissenschaft* (science, scholarship — literally "knowledge-craft") are central to German intellectual vocabulary. The verb may be small, but its conceptual footprint is enormous.
