# KOCHEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Kochen** means to cook, to boil, to prepare food. It covers both the culinary act of cooking a meal and the physical process of liquid reaching its boiling point. Hold this image in mind: a pot on the stove, water rolling and bubbling. The prefixes below redirect that boiling and cooking — limiting it, initiating it, intensifying it, preserving its result, or letting it go wrong.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abkochen:** to boil off, to boil away from contamination. The prefix pulls something away through the act of boiling. Water is *abgekocht* — boiled to drive off impurities, sterilized. Instruments are *abgekocht* — cleaned by boiling, the bacteria driven away by heat. The "off" signals removal: what was dangerous or unwanted has been boiled off, leaving something safe.

Literal mnemonic: "boil off." English uses "boil off" for evaporating impurities, and that captures *abkochen* precisely.

### AN- ("on, onto, up against")

**Ankochen:** to boil onto the beginning, to bring to a boil. The prefix marks the onset of contact with heat. You *kochst* something *an* — you start it cooking, you bring it to the initial boil without carrying it through to completion. This is parboiling: the food touches the boiling point and is pulled back. The prefix signals the beginning of the process, not its end.

Literal mnemonic: "cook on" in the sense of getting started. Think of "setting the cooking on" — initiating the process.

### AUF- ("up, upon, open")

**Aufkochen:** to boil up. The prefix lifts the action upward — the liquid rises, surges, breaks the surface. You bring something *auf* to a full, rolling boil. The image is vertical: calm water suddenly climbs and erupts. In a kitchen, *aufkochen lassen* means to let something come to a boil — to let the heat push the liquid up to its peak.

Literal mnemonic: "boil up." English says exactly the same thing — "bring it up to a boil."

### AUS- ("out, outward, spread")

**Auskochen:** to boil out. The prefix draws something outward through sustained boiling. You *kochst* flavors *aus* — you extract them, pulling essence out of bones or herbs into a broth. You *kochst* instruments *aus* — you sterilize them, boiling contaminants out. The action is extractive: heat opens the material and draws its contents outward into the surrounding liquid.

Literal mnemonic: "boil out." Think of "boiling the goodness out of the bones" or "boiling out the impurities."

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Einkochen:** to boil in, to boil down into. The prefix directs the action inward — toward concentration, containment, preservation. Liquid is *eingekocht* — reduced, boiled down until thickened. Fruit is *eingekocht* — preserved, cooked into jars, sealed inward for storage. The gesture is the opposite of *auskochen*: instead of drawing out, you are driving in — condensing, trapping, sealing.

Literal mnemonic: "cook in" or "boil in." English "boil down" captures the reduction, while "put up" captures the preserving. German unifies both under the single inward gesture of *ein-*.

### VER- (transformation, displacement, error, intensification)

**Verkochen:** to cook away into transformation, to boil to nothing. The prefix signals that the cooking has gone too far — past completion into ruin or disappearance. Liquid *verkocht* — it boils away entirely, transformed from substance into vapor. Food *verkocht* — it overcooks, its texture and structure destroyed by excess heat. *Ver-* here is the prefix of going-too-far, of crossing the threshold from done to overdone.

Literal mnemonic: "cook away" or "cook to pieces." Think of forgetting the pot on the stove — the water *verkocht*, boiled away to nothing.

### VOR- ("before, forward, in front of")

**Vorkochen:** to cook before, to cook in advance. The prefix places the cooking action ahead of the moment when it will be needed. You *kochst vor* on Sunday for the week ahead. Meals are *vorgekocht* — prepared beforehand, ready to reheat. The prefix is purely temporal: the cooking happens now, but the eating comes later.

Literal mnemonic: "precook." English uses the Latin prefix *pre-* where German uses the native *vor-*, but the logic is identical.

### ÜBER- ("over, above, beyond")

**Überkochen:** to cook over, to boil over. The prefix pushes the action beyond its boundary. Milk *kocht über* — it rises and spills over the rim of the pot. The boiling has exceeded the container's capacity. The image is of an upward motion that crosses a limit: the liquid goes over the edge. In figurative use, emotions can *überkochen* — boil over, spill past the point of control.

Literal mnemonic: "boil over." English matches perfectly — "the pot boiled over" is exactly *der Topf ist übergekocht*.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays physical; the prefixes shape the process.** *Kochen* never stops meaning "to cook" or "to boil." But each prefix isolates a different phase or direction of the cooking process. *An-* starts it. *Auf-* brings it to the peak. *Ein-* concentrates it. *Aus-* extracts from it. *Über-* lets it escape. *Ver-* lets it go too far. You are watching the life cycle of a pot on the stove, narrated by prefixes.

**Separable prefixes tend to be spatial and concrete.** *Ab, an, auf, aus, ein, vor* — these are adverbs that preserve a physical directionality. You can picture the motion in each: boiling off, boiling up, boiling out, boiling down into, cooking ahead. They describe what the heat is doing to the material in literal, visible terms.

**Inseparable prefixes tend to mark transformation or excess.** *Ver-* and *über-* fuse with the verb and signal that something has changed state irreversibly. *Verkochen* means the substance is gone — transformed beyond recovery. *Überkochen* means a boundary has been crossed. These are not directions you can point to; they are thresholds.

**English often preserves the same logic with phrasal verbs.** "Boil off" = *abkochen*. "Boil up" = *aufkochen*. "Boil out" = *auskochen*. "Boil down" = *einkochen*. "Boil over" = *überkochen*. The *kochen* family maps remarkably well onto English phrasal verbs — more so than most German verb stems. Use these parallels as anchors: the spatial logic is shared across both languages, and where the metaphors align, memorization becomes nearly effortless.
