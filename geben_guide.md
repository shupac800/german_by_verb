# GEBEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Geben** means to give — cognate with English "give" (both descend from Proto-Germanic *gebaną*). It implies transfer: something passes from one hand to another, from one possession to another. Hold this image in mind: an open hand extending something outward. Nearly every prefix below redirects that transfer — aiming it, reversing it, completing it, or abstracting it into metaphor.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abgeben:** to give off, to give away. The prefix pulls the giving away from the giver. You *geben* a package *ab* — you hand it over, deliver it, drop it off. A radiator *gibt Wärme ab* — it gives off heat. A vote is *abgegeben* — cast, given away from the voter into the record. In every case something leaves the giver and does not return: you emit it, submit it, surrender it.

Literal mnemonic: "give off." English uses this directly — "the engine gives off fumes," "give off a signal."

### AN- ("on, onto, up against")

**Angeben:** to give on. This prefix splits into two distinct paths, both traceable to the same gesture of putting something forward.

*Path one — boasting:* you give yourself on, you put yourself forward for display. To *angeben* is to brag, to show off, to swagger. The giver thrusts something — usually themselves — onto the audience's attention.

*Path two — specifying:* you give information on, you put data forward for the record. To *angeben* is to state, to indicate, to designate. A name is *angegeben* — given onto the form, placed on record.

The split is revealing: both senses involve pushing something forward for others to see. The boaster pushes himself forward; the declarer pushes facts forward. The difference is only in what is being thrust at the audience — ego or information.

Literal mnemonic: "give on" or "put on." English "putting on airs" captures the boasting sense; "put down your name" gets at the specifying sense.

### AUF- ("up, upon, open")

**Aufgeben:** to give up. This is a direct English parallel — the construction is identical. You *geben* something *auf* — you release it upward, you let it go. To *aufgeben* a plan is to abandon it. To *aufgeben* resistance is to surrender. The prefix lifts the act of giving into release: what goes up does not come back down.

The postal sense follows logically: to *aufgeben* a letter is to give it up to the system, to hand it over to the mail. You release it and it is no longer yours to control.

Literal mnemonic: "give up." One of those rare cases where English and German have built the exact same metaphor from the exact same parts.

### AUS- ("out, outward, spread")

**Ausgeben:** to give out. The prefix spreads the giving outward. Money is *ausgegeben* — spent, given out into the world. Cards are *ausgegeben* — dealt, distributed outward. To *sich ausgeben als* is to give yourself out as something — to pass yourself off, to present a false exterior to the world. The unifying image is dispersal: what was concentrated is given out in multiple directions.

Literal mnemonic: "give out." English uses this identically — "give out flyers," "the money gave out" (was spent). The imposture sense maps to "giving out" a false impression.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Begeben:** to give oneself (somewhere). *Be-* stamps the action fully onto its object, and here the object is the self. *Sich begeben* means to betake oneself, to proceed, to make one's way to a place. You give yourself over to the act of going — you commit your whole self to the movement. The rarer sense of *sich begeben* meaning "to occur" follows from the same logic: an event gives itself into being, it comes to pass.

Mnemonic: think of "betake" — an archaic English verb with the same structure. "He betook himself to the garden" = *Er begab sich in den Garten.*

### BEI- ("beside, alongside, with")

**Beigeben:** to give beside, to give along with. You yield, you add something alongside what is already there. To *klein beigeben* is to give in, to relent — to place your resistance alongside the other party's demand and let theirs win. The image is of someone laying their position down beside the opponent's and stepping back.

Literal mnemonic: "give by" or "give alongside." The yielding sense comes from offering your position up next to the stronger one — conceding the contest.

### DURCH- ("through")

**Durchgeben:** to give through. You pass information through a channel. A message is *durchgegeben* — transmitted, broadcast, passed along from sender through the medium to receiver. The prefix channels the giving through a conduit: radio, telephone, chain of command.

Literal mnemonic: "give through." Think of "pass it through" — the information is given through the system from one end to the other.

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Eingeben:** to give in, to give into. The prefix directs the giving inward, into a container or system. Data is *eingegeben* — entered, keyed into a computer. Medicine is *eingegeben* — administered, given into the body. An idea is *eingegeben* — inspired, given into the mind. In each case something is placed inside: a system receives input, a body receives treatment, a mind receives a thought.

Literal mnemonic: "give in(to)." English "input" is a direct parallel — something put in. The inspiration sense echoes the old idea of a muse breathing a thought *into* the poet.

### ER- (achievement, completion, result)

**Ergeben:** to give out as a result. This is one of the most complex entries in the family, because *er-* drives the giving toward several distinct endpoints.

*Yield / produce:* the calculation *ergibt* a sum — it gives forth a result, it yields an answer. The investigation *ergibt* evidence — it produces findings.

*Surrender:* *sich ergeben* means to give yourself over completely — to capitulate. The *er-* prefix pushes the giving to its terminal point: you give everything, holding nothing back.

*Arise / ensue:* *es ergibt sich* — it gives itself forth, it comes about. Opportunities *ergeben sich* — they emerge, they result from circumstances.

*Adjective — devoted:* *ergeben* as an adjective means devoted, loyal, submissive — the state of someone who has given themselves over. A faithful servant is *ergeben* — yielded to the master's will, fully given.

The thread connecting all four is completion: the giving reaches its end. The sum is fully yielded. The soldier gives everything. The consequence fully emerges. The loyal subject has given themselves entirely.

Mnemonic: *er-* = "give forth to completion." The result is yielded. The soldier yields himself. The situation yields its consequences. The devoted subject has yielded their independence.

### GEGEBEN (adj.)

**Gegeben:** given. The past participle fossilized as an adjective. Something *gegeben* is given, existing, established — a fact already placed on the table. *Unter den gegebenen Umständen* = under the given circumstances. The logic: what has been given is now simply there to be reckoned with.

Mnemonic: "given." English uses the identical metaphor — "given the circumstances," "a given fact."

### HERAUS- ("out from within")

**Herausgeben:** to give out from inside. Three related senses branch from this image. A publisher *gibt* a book *heraus* — gives it out from the interior of production into the world. A cashier *gibt* change *heraus* — gives back the excess from inside the register. An editor *gibt* a collected works *heraus* — draws the material out and presents it.

Literal mnemonic: "give out (from in here)." Publishing is giving a book out from behind the scenes. Making change is giving coins out from the till.

### HER- ("hither, toward the speaker")

**Hergeben:** to give hither, to give over. You release something from your possession toward the other party. *Das gebe ich nicht her* — I won't give that over, I won't part with it. The prefix emphasizes the direction of loss: the thing moves away from you, toward whoever is claiming it.

Literal mnemonic: "give here" or "hand over." The reluctance in *nicht hergeben* mirrors English "I won't give it up."

### HIN- ("thither, toward a point away from the speaker")

**Hingeben:** to give away toward something, to sacrifice. The prefix aims the giving away from the giver and toward a cause or recipient. You *geben* your time *hin* — you devote it, you sacrifice it. *Sich hingeben* means to surrender yourself to something — a passion, a task, a person. The giving is directional and total: outward, away from self-interest.

Literal mnemonic: "give forth." "She gave herself over to the work" captures the reflexive sense — full, directional devotion.

### HINZU- ("in addition to, toward")

**Hinzugeben:** to give in addition, to add. You give something toward an existing quantity — you supplement. Salt is *hinzugegeben* — added to the pot, given to what is already there. The prefix combines direction (*hin-*) and addition (*zu-*): something moves toward the whole and joins it.

Literal mnemonic: "give to (the mix)." English "add in" captures the same gesture.

### MIT- ("with, along with")

**Mitgeben:** to give along, to send with. You give something to someone to carry with them. A parent *gibt dem Kind ein Pausenbrot mit* — gives the child a sandwich to take along. Advice is *mitgegeben* — given to someone as they set off on their way. The prefix attaches the gift to the journey.

Literal mnemonic: "give with." You give something *with* the person — it accompanies them.

### NACH- ("after, following, yielding")

**Nachgeben:** to give after, to give way. The prefix implies following or yielding to pressure. A floor *gibt nach* — it gives way under weight. A negotiator *gibt nach* — gives in, relents, follows the other party's demand. The image is of something that bends under sustained force: it gives after resistance, it follows the pressure rather than holding firm.

Literal mnemonic: "give way." English "give way" and "give in" both capture this — the structure yields after being pressed.

### STATT- ("in place of")

**Stattgeben:** to give place to, to grant. A legal term: the court *gibt* a motion *statt* — it grants it, upholds it, gives it standing. The prefix means "in place" — you give something its place, you allow it to stand. What is *stattgegeben* has been recognized and permitted.

Literal mnemonic: "give standing." The court gives the request its place — it sustains it.

### UM- ("around")

**Umgeben:** to give around. This is one of the most vivid entries in the family. To *umgeben* is to surround — literally, to give around something on all sides. A wall *umgibt* a city — it wraps around it, gives itself around it. Trees *umgeben* a house — they encircle it. The inseparable prefix fuses "around" into the verb: the giving becomes enclosure, the transfer becomes envelopment.

Literal mnemonic: "give around." Picture hands passing something in a circle, closing it in on all sides. What is *umgeben* has been wrapped, enclosed, ringed.

### UNTER- ("under, beneath" — here as adjective only)

**Untergeben** (adj.): given under, subordinate. Someone *untergeben* has been placed beneath, given to the authority of another. The word survives only as an adjective — a *Untergebener* is a subordinate, someone positioned under a superior.

Literal mnemonic: "given under." A subordinate is one given under another's command.

### VER- (transformation, displacement, completion)

**Vergeben:** to give away completely. *Ver-* drives the giving to its transformative endpoint, producing two seemingly unrelated meanings that share a deep logic.

*Forgive:* to *vergeben* is to pardon, to absolve. You give away the debt, the grievance, the claim to justice — you release it entirely, transforming the relationship. What was owed is given away and ceases to exist as a burden.

*Allocate:* to *vergeben* is to assign, to award, to give away to a recipient. A contract is *vergeben* — awarded, given away to the winning bidder. A position is *vergeben* — filled, no longer available, given away.

The connection: in both senses, something is given away so completely that it is gone. The sin is forgiven — given away, no longer held. The position is filled — given away, no longer open. *Ver-* marks the point of no return: what is *vergeben* has left the giver permanently.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "give away for good." Forgiveness gives away the grudge. Allocation gives away the opportunity. In both cases, what was held is now released beyond retrieval.

### VOR- ("before, forward, in front of")

**Vorgeben:** to give before, to give forward. You put something forward as true — but it isn't. To *vorgeben* is to pretend, to feign, to claim falsely. The prefix places the false claim in front, as a screen. What is *vorgegeben* is given out in advance of reality — a facade placed before the truth.

Literal mnemonic: "put forward." English "put forward a false claim" mirrors the structure — the pretense is given first, placed in front, before anyone can see behind it.

### WEG- ("away, gone")

**Weggeben:** to give away. The simplest "away" prefix — you give something and it goes away. No metaphor, no abstraction. A book is *weggegeben* — given away, parted with. The thing leaves and is gone.

Literal mnemonic: "give away." Direct and literal in both languages.

### WIEDER- ("again, back")

**Wiedergeben:** to give back, to give again. Two branches from the same root. You *geben* something *wieder* — you return it, you give it back. Or you *geben* something *wieder* — you reproduce it, you render it, you play it back. A recording *gibt* sound *wieder* — it gives the sound again, it plays it back. A translation *gibt* meaning *wieder* — it gives the sense back in another language, it reproduces it.

Literal mnemonic: "give back / give again." Returning is giving back. Reproducing is giving again — the sound, the image, the meaning is given once more.

### ZU- ("to, toward, additional, closed")

**Zugeben:** to give to, to add to. The prefix pushes something toward an existing situation, producing two branches.

*Add:* you *geben* an ingredient *zu* — you add it to the mix, you give it to the pot. Something extra is thrown in.

*Admit:* you *geben zu* that something is true — you concede, you acknowledge, you add the truth to the record. What you were withholding is finally given to the listener. The admission is an addition: you add the fact that you were concealing.

Literal mnemonic: "give to" or "add to." Adding a spice to the soup and adding a confession to the conversation share the same structure — something held back is given to the situation.

### ZURÜCK- ("back, backward, in return")

**Zurückgeben:** to give back. You return what was given. A book is *zurückgegeben* — given back to the library. A compliment is *zurückgegeben* — returned. The prefix is straightforwardly directional: the giving reverses course, and the thing returns to its origin.

Literal mnemonic: "give back." Identical in both languages.

### ÜBER- ("over, above, across")

**Übergeben:** to give over. The prefix carries the giving across a boundary. You *übergeben* command to your successor — you hand over authority, transferring it across the line between your tenure and theirs. Documents are *übergeben* — delivered, surrendered, turned over to the other party. The reflexive *sich übergeben* means to vomit — to give over the contents of one's stomach, to expel them across the threshold of the body.

Literal mnemonic: "give over" or "hand over." English uses both — "hand over the documents," "turn over command." The vomiting sense follows the same spatial logic: the body gives its contents over and out.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**English "give" parallels run deep.** More than most German verb families, *geben* maps directly onto English constructions. "Give up" = *aufgeben*. "Give in" = *nachgeben*. "Give out" = *ausgeben*. "Give back" = *zurückgeben*. "Give away" = *weggeben*. These are not coincidences — they reflect a shared Germanic inheritance. Use them as anchors.

**Where the parallels break, the breaks are instructive.** *Angeben* (boast) has no English "give on" equivalent. *Umgeben* (surround) has no "give around." *Vorgeben* (pretend) has no "give before." These are the places where German extended the spatial metaphor of giving further than English did. Notice the logic: surrounding *is* giving-around; pretending *is* putting something forward before the truth. German simply kept building where English stopped.

**Ver- and er- carry the heaviest loads.** *Vergeben* (forgive + allocate) and *ergeben* (yield + surrender + result + devoted) are the most semantically dense members of the family. Both use inseparable prefixes that push "give" toward completion and transformation. *Ver-* gives away permanently; *er-* gives forth to a final result. Learning these two thoroughly is worth more than memorizing five simpler entries.

**Reflexive forms unlock new territory.** *Sich begeben* (proceed), *sich ergeben* (arise / surrender), *sich hingeben* (devote oneself), *sich übergeben* (vomit) — when the subject gives *itself*, the meanings shift dramatically. The self becomes both giver and gift. This reflexive pattern is one of German's most productive tools for generating new meaning from familiar stems.

**Separable vs. inseparable marks a real semantic boundary.** The separable compounds (*abgeben, aufgeben, ausgeben, nachgeben*) tend to preserve a concrete, directional sense of giving — you can picture the motion. The inseparable compounds (*begeben, ergeben, umgeben, vergeben, übergeben*) tend to be more abstract and further from the literal. *Umgeben* (surround) has left "giving" almost entirely behind; *ergeben* (result) requires effort to trace back to "give." The fused prefix signals a fused, less decomposable meaning.
