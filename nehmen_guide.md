# NEHMEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Nehmen** means to take, to get, to receive. It implies active acquisition — reaching out and grasping something for yourself. Hold this image in mind: a hand closing around an object and drawing it toward you. Nearly every prefix below redirects that basic grasp — specifying where you take something from, what you take it into, or how the act of taking is transformed into something more abstract.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from, down")

**Abnehmen:** to take off, to take down, to take away. The prefix pulls the taking action downward and away. A hat is *abgenommen* — taken off. A picture is taken down from the wall. From "taking away" come two figurative leaps: weight is *abgenommen* — taken off the body, lost; and a quantity *nimmt ab* — it takes itself away, it decreases, diminishes, wanes. When you *nehmen* something *ab* from someone, you accept it from them, relieve them of it.

Literal mnemonic: "take off." English uses the same construction — "take off your coat," "the fever took off," "take something off someone's hands."

### AN- ("on, onto, toward")

**Annehmen:** to take on, to take toward yourself. The prefix pushes the taking toward acceptance and adoption. You *nehmen* a gift *an* — you take it on, you accept it. You *nehmen* a habit *an* — you take it on, you adopt it. From here comes the intellectual leap: you *nehmen* something *an* as true — you take it on provisionally, you assume, suppose, presume. Both senses share the same gesture: drawing something toward yourself and letting it attach.

Literal mnemonic: "take on." English preserves both senses — "take on a challenge" (accept) and "take it as a given" (assume).

### AUF- ("up, upon, open")

**Aufnehmen:** to take up, to take in, to take upon. This is one of the broadest compounds in the family. A sponge *nimmt* water *auf* — takes it up, absorbs it. A host *nimmt* a guest *auf* — takes them in, admits them. A camera *nimmt* an image *auf* — takes it up onto film, records it. A craftsman *nimmt* work *auf* — takes it up, begins it. The prefix lifts the taking into an act of receiving and holding: whatever is *aufgenommen* is taken up into a new container — the sponge, the household, the tape, the schedule.

Literal mnemonic: "take up." English uses this widely — "take up a hobby," "take up residence," "the soil takes up moisture."

### AUS- ("out, outward")

**Ausnehmen:** to take out. The prefix pulls the taking outward from inside. Literally, to *ausnehmen* a fish is to take its innards out — to gut it, to eviscerate. Figuratively, to *ausnehmen* someone from a group is to take them out of the count — to exclude, exempt, except them. Both senses share the same physical gesture: reaching into something and drawing a part out.

Literal mnemonic: "take out." "Take out the guts" and "take someone out of the equation" both work.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive and reflexive)

**Benehmen:** to take oneself — that is, to conduct oneself, to behave. *Be-* transforms the raw act of taking into something thorough and self-directed. Almost always reflexive: *sich benehmen* means to manage yourself, to govern your own taking and acting in the social world. The image: you take yourself in hand, you contain your impulses, you hold yourself properly. A child that does not *sich benehmen* is one that has not taken hold of itself yet.

Mnemonic: "be-take yourself." The archaic English "betake" meant to take oneself somewhere or commit oneself. *Sich benehmen* is the social version: you take yourself into proper form.

### DURCH- ("through")

**Durchnehmen:** to take through. You take a topic and carry it all the way through — you go through it, cover it, deal with it from beginning to end. A teacher *nimmt* a chapter *durch* — takes the class through the material systematically. The prefix adds thoroughness: the taking passes through the whole thing, leaving nothing unexamined.

Literal mnemonic: "take through." "Let's take this through from the beginning" means to cover it completely.

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Einnehmen:** to take in. One of the richest compounds in the family, with five distinct sense domains — all rooted in the same inward gesture. Money is *eingenommen* — taken in, earned, collected. A fortress is *eingenommen* — taken into your possession, captured. Space is *eingenommen* — taken into use, occupied. Medicine is *eingenommen* — taken into the body, ingested. An audience is *eingenommen* — taken inward, captivated, charmed. In every case the prefix directs the taking inward: into the cashbox, into your control, into the seat, into the stomach, into the heart.

Literal mnemonic: "take in." English mirrors nearly all five senses — "take in money," "take the city," "take up space," "take your medicine," "she was completely taken in by his charm."

### ENTGEGEN- ("toward, against, in the face of")

**Entgegennehmen:** to take toward oneself from someone approaching. The prefix implies facing the source: something is coming your way and you reach out to receive it. A clerk *nimmt* a package *entgegen* — takes delivery of it. A dignitary *nimmt* congratulations *entgegen* — receives them from well-wishers. The gesture is formal and receptive: you stand facing the giver and accept what is offered.

Literal mnemonic: "take in receipt." Think of standing at the door and taking delivery.

### ENT- ("away from, out of" — inseparable)

**Entnehmen:** to take out from, to take away from. *Ent-* separates and extracts. You *entnehmen* a sample from a jar — you take it out. You *entnehmen* money from an account — you withdraw it. Figuratively, you *entnehmen* meaning from a text — you extract it, infer it, gather the sense from the words. The prefix always points away from a source: something is taken out of where it was contained.

Literal mnemonic: "take from out of." English "extract" captures the same Latin gesture (*ex-* = out, *trahere* = pull).

### FEST- ("firm, fixed, tight")

**Festnehmen:** to take firm, to take fast. You take someone and hold them fixed — you arrest them, detain them, apprehend them. The prefix locks the taking into a grip that does not release. A suspect is *festgenommen* — taken into custody, seized and held firm.

Literal mnemonic: "take fast" (in the old sense of "fast" = firmly). To be "held fast" is to be arrested, unable to move.

### FORT- ("away, onward, gone")

**Fortnehmen:** to take away, to take forth. The prefix sends the taking away from the current position. You take something and it is gone. Straightforward removal — the object is taken away and does not return.

Literal mnemonic: "take forth" in the archaic sense of taking something away from here.

### HERAUS- ("out from within, from in here outward")

**Herausnehmen:** to take out from inside. You reach into something and bring a piece out. A splinter is *herausgenommen* — taken out of the skin. A part is *herausgenommen* — removed from the whole. Reflexively, *sich Freiheiten herausnehmen* means to take liberties out of thin air — to presume, to help yourself to more than you were offered.

Literal mnemonic: "take out (from in here)." The *her-* marks direction toward the speaker: the item comes out toward you.

### HER- ("from there, toward the speaker")

**Hernehmen:** to take from somewhere, to get from. The prefix points back to a source: where did you *her*-take it from? The question *Wo soll ich das hernehmen?* means "Where am I supposed to get that from?" — the emphasis is on the origin, the source of the taking.

Literal mnemonic: "take from (over there)." The prefix asks: where does the taking come from?

### HIN- ("thither, toward a point away, down")

**Hinnehmen:** to take it, to take something lying down. The prefix directs the taking toward acceptance of something coming at you. You *nehmen* an insult *hin* — you take it, you put up with it, you tolerate it. The gesture is passive: something is thrust upon you and you absorb it without resistance. To *hinnehmen* is to endure, to swallow what is given.

Literal mnemonic: "take (it) down." Think of "taking it on the chin" — absorbing the blow without fighting back.

### MIT- ("with, along")

**Mitnehmen:** to take along, to take with. You take something or someone with you as you go. A bag is *mitgenommen* — taken along on the trip. A friend is *mitgenommen* — given a lift, taken along in the car. From this comes a figurative darkening: an experience *nimmt* you *mit* — it takes you along roughly, it takes a toll, wears you out, as if you were dragged along unwillingly.

Literal mnemonic: "take with." "Take it with you" is the core. "It really took it out of me" captures the exhaustion sense.

### NACH- ("after, additional, more")

**Nachnehmen:** to take after, to take more. The prefix signals repetition: you take again after the first helping. *Darf ich nachnehmen?* — May I take seconds, have another helping? The gesture is simple: the first taking is done, and *nach-* extends it further.

Literal mnemonic: "take more (after)." Think of going back to the buffet for a second round.

### UNTER- ("under, beneath" — inseparable)

**Unternehmen:** to undertake. *Unter-* here does not mean "beneath" in a spatial sense but rather "into" — you take something under your responsibility, you grasp it from below and carry it forward. To *unternehmen* is to undertake, to attempt, to set out to do something. An *Unternehmen* (noun) is an enterprise, an undertaking. The image: you reach under the task and lift it, shouldering the effort.

Literal mnemonic: "undertake." English preserves this exactly — "to undertake a project" is to take it under your charge.

### VER- (transformation, intensification, going wrong)

**Vernehmen:** to take completely in, to perceive. *Ver-* here intensifies the act of taking to the point of full comprehension. You *vernehmen* a sound — you take it in completely, you hear it, perceive it, learn of it. From this perceptual taking comes the legal sense: to *vernehmen* a witness is to take their testimony fully, to interrogate, question, examine them. The two senses share a root: the act of taking in everything someone says or signals, whether passively (hearing) or actively (cross-examination).

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "fully, thoroughly." To *vernehmen* is to take in completely — either by ear (perceive) or by authority (interrogate). A *Vernehmung* is a hearing in both the auditory and the legal sense.

### VOR- ("before, forward, in front of")

**Vornehmen:** to take before oneself, to place in front of yourself for attention. Almost always reflexive: *sich etwas vornehmen* means to take something up and set it before you — to intend, to plan, to resolve. The gesture is purposeful: you reach forward, take hold of a goal, and place it in front of you where you must face it. *Ich habe mir vorgenommen, mehr zu lesen* — I have taken it upon myself, I intend to read more.

Literal mnemonic: "take before (yourself)." Think of pulling a task in front of you and declaring: this is what I will do.

### WEG- ("away, gone")

**Wegnehmen:** to take away. The most direct removal prefix. You take something and it is gone — confiscated, removed, deprived. A toy is *weggenommen* from a child. Space is *weggenommen* — taken away, lost. The prefix is blunt: the thing was here, now it has been taken away.

Literal mnemonic: "take away." The simplest and most physical sense of removal.

### WIEDER- ("again, back")

**Wiedernehmen:** to take again, to take back. The prefix reverses or repeats the taking. What was given is *wiedergenommen* — retaken. Uncommon in everyday speech but transparent in meaning: the taking happens a second time.

Literal mnemonic: "retake." A city is retaken; an offer is taken back.

### ZU- ("to, toward, additional, closed")

**Zunehmen:** to take to, to take toward an increase. The prefix pushes the taking toward accumulation. The moon *nimmt zu* — it takes on more light, it waxes. A population *nimmt zu* — it grows, increases. A person *nimmt zu* — they put on weight, gain mass. In every case, something is being added: the taking goes toward more, not less. *Zunehmen* is the mirror of *abnehmen* — one adds, the other subtracts.

Literal mnemonic: "take on (more)." "The wind is picking up" captures the same idea of a quantity taking on more of itself.

### ZURÜCK- ("back, backward, in return")

**Zurücknehmen:** to take back. You reverse the original giving or stating. A gift is *zurückgenommen* — taken back. A statement is *zurückgenommen* — retracted, withdrawn. A law is *zurückgenommen* — revoked, rescinded. The prefix rewinds the action: what was put forward is now pulled back.

Literal mnemonic: "take back." English works identically — "I take it back" means to retract what was said or given.

### ZUSAMMEN- ("together")

**Zusammennehmen:** to take together, to gather up. Reflexively, *sich zusammennehmen* means to take yourself together — to pull yourself together, to compose yourself, to muster your self-control. Non-reflexively, it means to gather things up into one: *alles zusammennehmen* — to take everything together, to consider it as a whole. The prefix bundles the taking into a single, concentrated act.

Literal mnemonic: "take together." "Pull yourself together" is the perfect English parallel.

### ÜBER- ("over, above, beyond" — inseparable)

**Übernehmen:** to take over. The prefix lifts the taking above and across — you reach over and take control of what was someone else's. A company is *übernommen* — taken over. A responsibility is *übernommen* — assumed, shouldered. A method is *übernommen* — adopted, accepted from another. Reflexively, *sich übernehmen* turns the prefix into "beyond": you take on more than you can carry, you overdo it, you overtax yourself. The line between *über-* as "over" (taking control) and *über-* as "too much" (taking beyond capacity) is where ambition meets exhaustion.

Literal mnemonic: "take over" and "overtake yourself." "She took over the department" and "he overtook his own limits" capture both faces.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays physical; the prefix goes abstract.** *Nehmen* never stops meaning "to take." But the prefixes push that grasp into metaphor. *Festnehmen* (take firm) becomes "to arrest." *Vernehmen* (take in fully) becomes "to interrogate." *Einnehmen* (take in) leaps from cash registers to captivated hearts. The hand's gesture of closing around an object is projected onto legal, intellectual, and emotional actions. Once you see this, the abstractions dissolve into images.

**Nehmen and legen are mirrors.** Where *legen* (to lay, to place) is about putting something down, *nehmen* is about picking something up. Many prefixes produce opposite pairs: *annehmen* (take on) vs. *anlegen* (lay on); *abnehmen* (take off) vs. *ablegen* (lay off); *zunehmen* (increase by adding) vs. *zulegen* (add by placing). Studying the two families side by side reveals how German maps the full cycle of handling — placing and picking up, giving and taking — with the same set of spatial prefixes.

**Separable prefixes tend to be spatial and concrete.** *Ab, an, auf, aus, ein, fest, hin, mit, weg, zu, zusammen* — these can stand alone as adverbs or prepositions. They preserve a physical directionality even in figurative use. You can usually picture where the taking goes: off, on, up, out, in, firm, down, along, away, toward, together.

**Inseparable prefixes tend to be transformative and opaque.** *Be-, ent-, unter-, ver-, über-* — these fuse with the verb and often shift its meaning further from the literal. *Benehmen* (behave) is far from "take." *Vernehmen* (perceive, interrogate) takes two surprising turns. *Unternehmen* (undertake) repurposes "under" as "into one's charge." These must often be learned as whole units rather than decoded from parts — though the spatial logic, once explained, makes them stick.

**Abnehmen and zunehmen are the great pair.** Decrease vs. increase, lose weight vs. gain weight, wane vs. wax — they carve the world into "taking away" and "taking toward." Remembering one gives you the other for free.

**English sometimes preserves the same logic.** "Take on" = *annehmen*. "Take in" = *einnehmen*. "Take over" = *übernehmen*. "Undertake" = *unternehmen*. "Take back" = *zurücknehmen*. Where the metaphors align, use them as anchors. Where they diverge — *vernehmen* as "interrogate," *benehmen* as "behave," *hinnehmen* as "tolerate" — notice that German extends the taking metaphor further than English does, using "take" where English switches to an entirely different verb.
