# ARBEITEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Arbeiten** means to work — to apply effort toward a task. Unlike *legen* (which is a physical gesture), *arbeiten* is already somewhat abstract: it is sustained effort, labor, productive activity. The prefixes redirect, intensify, or constrain this effort in ways that often mirror English phrasal verbs built on "work."

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abarbeiten:** to work off. You have a debt, a backlog, a list — and you work through it until it is discharged. The prefix directs the effort away from you: what was owed or accumulated is worked off, reduced, removed by labor. English uses the identical construction: "work off a debt," "work off the calories."

Literal mnemonic: "work off."

### AUF- ("up, renewed, restored")

**Aufarbeiten:** to work up. The prefix suggests renewal — bringing something back up to standard. Old furniture is *aufgearbeitet*: restored, refurbished, worked over until it is good again. A backlog is *aufgearbeitet*: worked through and cleared. The past is *aufgearbeitet*: processed, worked through emotionally or intellectually. In every case, something neglected or degraded is worked back up.

Literal mnemonic: "work up" or "work over." Think of working something up into usable condition again.

### AUS- ("out, outward, fully")

**Ausarbeiten:** to work out. You take a rough idea and work it out into full, detailed form. A plan is *ausgearbeitet* — elaborated, hammered out, fleshed out. The prefix pushes the work outward from sketch to completion, from vague to precise. English works identically: "work out the details," "work out a solution."

Literal mnemonic: "work out."

### BE- (intensifier / makes transitive)

**Bearbeiten:** to work on something thoroughly. *Be-* takes the intransitive activity of working and stamps it onto a specific object. You *bearbeitest* wood (shape it), a case (handle it), a text (edit it), a person (badger them). The prefix says: this thing is being subjected to sustained, direct effort. Where *arbeiten* is general activity, *bearbeiten* is work applied forcefully to a target.

Mnemonic: think of *be-* as "upon" — to work upon something. "She's been working on that manuscript" = *Sie bearbeitet das Manuskript.*

### DURCH- ("through")

**Durcharbeiten:** to work through. You work through the night, through a stack of papers, through a problem. The prefix says the effort passes from one side to the other — no stopping, no gaps. It can mean working overtime (working through the boundary of the normal workday) or studying something thoroughly (working through every part of it).

Literal mnemonic: "work through." Identical in English: "I worked through the night," "work through the material."

### EIN- ("in, into")

**Einarbeiten:** to work in, to work into. You work someone into a new position — you train them, break them in. You work a new element into an existing structure — you incorporate it. The prefix directs the effort inward: the new person or thing is being fitted into something that already exists.

Literal mnemonic: "work in." "We need to work her into the team" = *Wir müssen sie einarbeiten.*

### EMPOR- ("upward, aloft")

**Emporarbeiten:** to work one's way up. *Empor* is a literary, slightly elevated synonym for "up." *Sich emporarbeiten* means to rise through effort — to climb the ranks, to pull yourself up by working. The image is vertical: you start below and labor upward.

Literal mnemonic: "work upward." A more dramatic, aspirational version of "work your way up."

### ENTGEGEN- ("against, counter to")

**Entgegenarbeiten:** to work against. You direct your effort in opposition to someone or something. To counteract a plan is to *entgegenarbeiten* — to work against its momentum. The prefix is transparently adversarial: *entgegen* means "toward [in opposition]," so the effort runs head-on into its target.

Literal mnemonic: "work against" or "work counter to."

### ER- (achievement through effort)

**Erarbeiten:** to work out, to earn through work. *Er-* signals that the effort reaches a result. You don't just work — you *er*work something, producing it through sustained labor. Knowledge is *erarbeitet* (acquired through study), a reputation is *erarbeitet* (built through deeds). The prefix says: this thing exists because someone worked to bring it into being.

Mnemonic: "work into existence." Where *ausarbeiten* means to elaborate a plan, *erarbeiten* means to produce something new through effort. The *er-* emphasizes the achievement.

### HERAUS- ("out from within")

**Herausarbeiten:** to work out from inside, to carve out. You extract something embedded — a shape from raw material, a key insight from dense data, a theme from a text. The effort goes inward to find, then outward to reveal. A sculptor *arbeitet* a figure *heraus* from stone. An analyst *arbeitet* the central problem *heraus* from a mass of information.

Literal mnemonic: "work out (from within)." Slightly different from *ausarbeiten*: here the emphasis is on extraction and revelation, not elaboration.

### HIN- ("toward a point, in that direction")

**Hinarbeiten:** to work toward. You direct your effort at a goal. *Auf etwas hinarbeiten* means to work toward something — a deadline, a qualification, a future state. The prefix gives the work a destination.

Literal mnemonic: "work toward." "We're working toward a solution" = *Wir arbeiten auf eine Lösung hin.*

### MIT- ("with, along with")

**Mitarbeiten:** to work with, to work along. You join your effort to others'. A *Mitarbeiter* is a co-worker — literally one who works with. The prefix adds companionship: the labor is shared, collaborative, joined.

Literal mnemonic: "work with." "She works with us on the project" = *Sie arbeitet am Projekt mit.*

### NACH- ("after, following, again")

**Nacharbeiten:** to work after, to work again. You go back over something and rework it — touching up, correcting, reviewing. It can also mean making up for missed work: working after the fact to catch up. The prefix says the first pass is done; now you follow after it.

Literal mnemonic: "rework" or "work over again." "I need to go back and rework that section."

### UM- ("around, anew, differently")

**Umarbeiten:** to work over into a new form. The prefix signals transformation. You take something finished and rework it — a dress is *umgearbeitet* (altered, remodeled), a manuscript is *umgearbeitet* (revised, reshaped). The effort doesn't start from scratch; it reshapes what already exists.

Literal mnemonic: "rework" or "work over." "The tailor reworked the suit" = *Der Schneider hat den Anzug umgearbeitet.*

### VER- (transformation, thorough processing)

**Verarbeiten:** to work through completely, to process. *Ver-* here means thorough transformation: raw material is *verarbeitet* into finished product, food is *verarbeitet* by digestion, a traumatic experience is *verarbeitet* by the mind. The input goes in and comes out changed. The prefix signals that the working is comprehensive — nothing remains in its original state.

Literal mnemonic: "process" (from Latin *processus* — "to move forward through"). What is *verarbeitet* has been worked all the way through to the other side.

### VOR- ("before, in advance")

**Vorarbeiten:** to work ahead, to work in advance. You get work done before it is strictly needed — preparing the ground, clearing the path for what comes next. Reflexively, *sich vorarbeiten* means to work one's way forward — to advance by effort, inch by inch.

Literal mnemonic: "work ahead" or "work forward."

### WEG- ("away, gone")

**Wegarbeiten:** to work away. You chip away at something through effort until it is gone. A stain, a backlog, an obstacle — it is gradually removed by working at it. The prefix says the target is being diminished and carried off by effort.

Literal mnemonic: "work away." "He worked away the rust."

### ZUSAMMEN- ("together")

**Zusammenarbeiten:** to work together. The most transparent compound: effort is combined, pooled, directed jointly. English "collaborate" (from Latin *co-* + *laborare*) is the exact structural equivalent — "together" + "work."

Literal mnemonic: "work together."

### ÜBER- ("over, excessively; across, upon")

**Überarbeiten:** carries two distinct meanings from two faces of *über-*:
- *Excess:* to overwork. *Sich überarbeiten* means to work too much, to overexert — the effort goes over the limit. The prefix says "past the boundary of what is sustainable."
- *Revision:* to work over, to revise. You go back over a text or product and rework it — editing, correcting, improving. The effort passes over the surface again.

Literal mnemonic: "overwork" for the first sense; "work over" for the second. "She overworked herself" vs. "She worked over the manuscript."

## Patterns Worth Noting

**Arbeiten parallels English "work" almost perfectly.** More than most German stems, *arbeiten* maps neatly onto English phrasal verbs: work off, work out, work through, work up, work in, work toward, work together, work over, work against. An English speaker who translates each prefix as a preposition or adverb attached to "work" will guess correctly more often than not.

**The dangerous exceptions are the inseparable prefixes.** *Bearbeiten*, *erarbeiten*, *verarbeiten*, and *überarbeiten* are where the one-to-one mapping breaks down. These prefixes don't correspond to simple English prepositions — they reshape the verb's meaning more fundamentally. *Verarbeiten* doesn't mean "work away" (that's *wegarbeiten*); it means "process thoroughly." These require learning as units.

**Intensity increases with abstraction.** The most physical compounds (*abarbeiten*, *durcharbeiten*, *wegarbeiten*) are the easiest to decode. The most abstract (*bearbeiten*, *verarbeiten*, *erarbeiten*) require the most context. This is a general principle across German prefix verbs, not unique to *arbeiten*.
