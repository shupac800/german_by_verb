# GELTEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Gelten** means to be valid, to count, to apply, to be in effect. It points to something that holds force — a law that *gilt* is one that stands, that carries weight. The word descends from a Germanic root meaning "to pay, to yield, to be worth," closely related to Old English *gieldan/geldan* (to pay, to yield), which gave modern English "yield." The same root produced German *Geld* (money) — what is yielded, what is paid, what counts. Hold this core image in mind: something that carries recognized value, something that stands as valid in the eyes of others. Every prefix below redirects that basic force toward acts of payment, compensation, and reciprocal exchange.

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abgelten** (sep.): to settle, to compensate, to pay off. The prefix pulls the action to completion and away — value is transferred and the obligation is discharged. To *abgelten* a claim is to settle it with payment, to square the account so the matter is laid to rest. Vacation days are *abgegolten* — compensated in lieu, their value paid out rather than taken. The image is of a debt that once hung in the air, now pulled down and away: settled, satisfied, done.

Literal mnemonic: "pay off" or "yield away." The obligation is yielded away from you through compensation.

### ENT- ("away from, removed from" — here: paying as a consequence)

**Entgelten** (insep.): to pay for, to compensate, to reimburse. *Ent-* here carries the sense of rendering what is due — paying for something received, bearing the cost of a consequence. Someone who must *entgelten* is paying the price, settling what is owed in return for what was given or done. The noun *Entgelt* (remuneration, compensation) is common in formal and legal contexts: payment rendered in exchange for service. The prefix suggests that value is drawn out from the person who owes it.

Mnemonic: think of *ent-* as "rendering out." To *entgelten* is to render payment, to yield up what is owed. English "pay for" captures it plainly — "you will pay for this" carries the same double edge of consequence and compensation.

### VER- (intensification, completion, reciprocity)

**Vergelten** (insep.): to repay, to requite, to retaliate, to avenge. This is the most important member of the family. *Ver-* here intensifies and completes — it takes the base idea of value-exchange and drives it to its full conclusion. To *vergelten* is to pay back in full measure, to return in kind what was received. Crucially, the return can be positive or negative. A kindness *vergolten* is a kindness repaid — reward, gratitude given back. A wrong *vergolten* is a wrong repaid — retaliation, vengeance, an eye for an eye. The verb does not choose sides; it simply insists that what was given will be returned.

The noun *Vergeltung* has settled firmly on the darker side: retaliation, retribution, punitive repayment. But the verb itself remains neutral — it is the context that determines whether the repayment is a blessing or a blow.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "fully back." To *vergelten* is to yield back completely, to close the circle of exchange. English "requite" works the same way — requite a love, requite an injury. "Retaliate" comes from Latin *re-* + *talis* (such, of that kind) — paying back in the same coin, which is exactly what *vergelten* means.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**This is a small, tightly unified family.** Unlike stems that scatter across dozens of prefixes with wildly different meanings, *gelten* produces only three prefixed verbs, and all three circle the same theme: reciprocal value exchange. Something is owed; something is paid. The differences lie in tone and direction — *abgelten* settles an account cleanly, *entgelten* renders payment as consequence, *vergelten* repays in full, for good or ill.

**The base verb drifted; the prefixed verbs held.** *Gelten* itself has moved from "to pay" to the more abstract "to be valid, to count." But all three prefixed forms preserve the older, concrete sense of paying — compensation, reimbursement, retaliation. The prefixes anchored the verb to its transactional root even as the simplex floated upward into abstraction.

**English "yield" traveled a parallel path.** Old English *gieldan* meant "to pay" (which gave us "guild" — a payment-association — and "gilt" — covered with gold). Modern "yield" has drifted toward "to produce, to give way," losing its payment sense. German *Geld* kept the money meaning; English kept the harvest meaning. The same root, two different metaphorical destinations.

**Vergelten is the verb to learn.** Of the three prefixed forms, *vergelten* is by far the most frequent and the most culturally loaded. It appears in proverbs, in scripture ("Mein ist die Rache, ich will vergelten"), and in political discourse (*Vergeltungswaffe*, *Vergeltungsschlag*). If you learn one word from this family beyond *gelten* itself, make it this one.
