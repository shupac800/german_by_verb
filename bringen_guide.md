# BRINGEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Bringen** means to bring, to carry, to convey. English "bring" is its direct cognate — same Germanic root, same core meaning. Hold this image in mind: someone carrying something from one place to another and delivering it. Nearly every prefix below redirects that carrying — changing its destination, its manner, its purpose, or its object. Because "bring" survives in both languages, an unusually large number of these compounds map directly onto English "bring + preposition" phrases. Use that overlap as your anchor.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abbringen:** to bring off, to bring away from. You bring someone away from their intention — you dissuade them, divert them, talk them off their course. The prefix pulls the action away from a fixed point: the person was heading somewhere mentally, and you bring them off that path.

Literal mnemonic: "bring off (course)." English "bring someone off an idea" or "talk someone off the ledge" captures the same gesture of redirecting someone away from where they were headed.

### AN- ("on, onto, up against")

**Anbringen:** to bring on, to bring onto. You bring something to a surface and fix it there. A shelf is *angebracht* — mounted, attached, brought onto the wall. A remark is *angebracht* — lodged, placed into the conversation. The prefix directs the carrying toward contact: the thing is brought up against something and stays.

Literal mnemonic: "bring on / bring to bear." Think of bringing a bracket onto the wall, or bringing a point to bear in argument.

### AUF- ("up, upon, open")

**Aufbringen:** to bring up. Two faces: (1) you bring resources up — you raise money, muster support, come up with what is needed. (2) you bring someone's temper up — you enrage them, rile them. In both cases, the prefix lifts something from below to the surface: funds are brought up from wherever they were hidden; anger is brought up from beneath composure.

Literal mnemonic: "bring up." English "bring up the money" and "that really brought up my blood" both apply.

### BEI- ("beside, alongside, with, to")

**Beibringen:** to bring to someone's side. Three related gestures: (1) you bring knowledge to someone — you teach them, impart it. (2) you bring bad news to someone gently — you break it to them, deliver it alongside comfort. (3) you bring harm to someone — you inflict a wound, deliver a blow. In every case, something is carried to a person and placed beside them, whether a lesson, a truth, or an injury.

Literal mnemonic: "bring to." "I'll bring it to him gently" captures the delicate delivery; "bring a wound to someone" captures the harsher sense.

### DURCH- ("through")

**Durchbringen:** to bring through. Three directions: (1) you bring a proposal through — you push it through opposition, you get it passed. (2) you bring a family through — you sustain them, you provide enough to carry them through life. (3) you bring money through your hands — you squander it, you push it all the way through and out the other side. The prefix always implies passage from one end to the other; what varies is whether the result is success, survival, or depletion.

Literal mnemonic: "bring through." "Bring the bill through committee" = get it passed. "Bring the family through hard times" = provide for them.

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Einbringen:** to bring in. You carry something inward — into the barn (harvest), into the treasury (earn, yield), into the discussion (introduce, contribute). The prefix directs the carrying gesture inward, across a threshold. Whatever is *eingebracht* has been brought inside from where it was before.

Literal mnemonic: "bring in." English works identically — "bring in the harvest," "bring in a motion," "bring in revenue."

### EMPOR- ("upward, aloft")

**Emporbringen:** to bring up, to raise, to elevate. A more literary, emphatic version of "bring up." The prefix stresses the upward direction — lifting something from a low position to a high one. A family is *emporgebracht* — raised up in status. Less common in everyday speech but transparent in meaning.

Literal mnemonic: "bring up high." Think of bringing someone up from poverty to prominence.

### ENTGEGEN- ("toward, in the direction of, to meet")

**Entgegenbringen:** to bring toward, to bring to meet someone. You carry something forward and extend it to another person. Trust is *entgegengebracht* — shown, displayed toward someone. Respect is *entgegengebracht* — extended, offered out to meet the other. The prefix emphasizes the outward, meeting gesture: you bring something forward as an offering.

Literal mnemonic: "bring forth to meet." Think of "bringing trust to meet someone halfway" — extending it toward them.

### ER- (achievement, completion, production)

**Erbringen:** to bring forth conclusively. *Er-* signals that the action reaches its result. To *erbringen* proof is to produce it — to bring the evidence into final existence. To *erbringen* a service is to furnish it, to deliver it as a completed product. The prefix transforms casual carrying into definitive production: what is *erbracht* has been brought to its finished state.

Mnemonic: "bring to completion." The proof is not merely carried — it is brought into being, produced as a final result.

### FORT- ("away, onward, gone")

**Fortbringen:** to bring away, to carry off. You bring something away from where it was — you remove it, take it off. Straightforward and concrete: the thing is carried forth and gone.

Literal mnemonic: "bring forth / bring away." "Bring it away from here" = carry it off.

### HERAUF- ("up from below, up toward the speaker")

**Heraufbringen:** to bring up (toward here). You carry something upward, from a lower place to where you stand. Luggage is *heraufgebracht* from the car to the apartment. The prefix specifies direction: upward and toward the speaker.

Literal mnemonic: "bring up (here)." "Bring it up here from downstairs."

### HERAUS- ("out from within")

**Herausbringen:** to bring out. You carry something from inside to outside — a product is *herausgebracht* (released, launched, published), a secret is *herausgebracht* (brought to light). The prefix directs the action outward from an enclosure: what was inside is now brought out into the open.

Literal mnemonic: "bring out." English works identically — "bring out a new book," "bring out the truth."

### HER- ("hither, toward the speaker")

**Herbringen:** to bring here. The simplest directional compound — you carry something toward the speaker. *Bring es her!* — bring it here, fetch it over. No metaphor, no abstraction.

Literal mnemonic: "bring here." "Bring it here" = *bring es her.*

### HIN- ("thither, toward a point away from the speaker")

**Hinbringen:** to bring there, to take there. The counterpart of *herbringen* — you carry something away from the speaker, toward a destination. *Ich bringe es hin* — I'll take it there, I'll bring it over.

Literal mnemonic: "bring there / take there." The parcel is brought to its destination.

### HINTER- ("behind")

**Hinterbringen:** to bring behind — to inform secretly, to tell tales, to report behind someone's back. This is one of the family's surprises. The prefix "behind" transforms bringing into covert delivery: information is carried behind the subject's back and deposited with someone else. A *Hinterbringer* is a tattle-tale, someone who brings word behind the scenes. The inseparable prefix fuses tightly with the verb, signaling that this is no ordinary delivery — it is clandestine, done out of sight. Where *überbringen* is an open, honorable conveyance, *hinterbringen* is its shadow: the same act of carrying a message, but routed behind the person it concerns.

Literal mnemonic: "bring behind (someone's back)." Think of "going behind someone's back" — the information is brought to a third party by a route the subject cannot see.

### MIT- ("with, along")

**Mitbringen:** to bring along, to bring with. You carry something along with you as you go. You *bringen* a gift *mit* to a party — you bring it along. You *bringen* experience *mit* to a job — you carry it with you. One of the most common and transparent compounds.

Literal mnemonic: "bring with / bring along." "Bring along a bottle" = *bring eine Flasche mit.*

### NACH- ("after, afterward, following")

**Nachbringen:** to bring afterward, to deliver later. Something was forgotten or left behind, and you bring it after the fact. The prefix adds the temporal dimension: the bringing happens after the main event.

Literal mnemonic: "bring after." "I'll bring it along afterward" = I'll follow up with it later.

### UM- ("around, over, down")

**Umbringen:** to kill — to "bring around" or "bring down." This is the family's most dramatic surprise. The prefix *um-* can mean "over" (as in tipping over, knocking down), and when you "bring someone over," you bring them from upright to fallen, from living to dead. The euphemistic logic is the same as English "do in" or "take out" — a neutral verb of motion repurposed for lethal finality. *Er hat ihn umgebracht* — he killed him, literally "brought him over/down." The separable prefix keeps the word colloquial and blunt: this is not the clinical language of the courtroom but the plain speech of everyday horror. Reflexively, *sich umbringen* means to kill oneself.

Literal mnemonic: "bring down." Think of "bringing someone down" carried to its absolute, permanent conclusion.

### UNTER- ("under, beneath")

**Unterbringen:** to bring under a roof — to accommodate, to house, to lodge, to find room for. You bring someone or something under shelter, under cover, into a place where they fit. Guests are *untergebracht* in a hotel — housed, placed, given quarters. Files are *untergebracht* in a folder — stored, placed where they belong. The inseparable prefix signals that the action is complete and contained: what is *untergebracht* has been tucked away, settled, placed under protection.

Literal mnemonic: "bring under (a roof / under cover)." "We brought them under our roof" = we housed them, we found room.

### VER- (transformation, displacement, passage)

**Verbringen:** to spend (time) — to "bring away" into passage. This is a notable semantic drift. *Ver-* often signals that something is transformed or consumed through the action, and here the thing consumed is time itself. You *verbringen* an evening, a holiday, your youth — you bring the time away, you carry it through until it is gone. The original sense of physical transport has been entirely abstracted: no object is moved, no destination is reached. Only time passes, brought through and spent.

Literal mnemonic: "bring through / bring away." Think of time being "brought along" until it is used up — the hours are carried forth and consumed. English "spend" (from Latin *expendere*, to weigh out) performs a similar abstraction: paying out time as if it were currency.

### VOLL- ("full, complete")

**Vollbringen:** to bring to fullness — to accomplish, to achieve, to carry out, to fulfill. The prefix signals completion: the task is not merely attempted but brought to its full end. A feat is *vollbracht* — accomplished, brought to completion. The word carries weight and dignity; it belongs to achievements, deeds, and accomplishments rather than errands.

Literal mnemonic: "bring to full completion." Think of "bringing a great work to fulfillment."

### VOR- ("before, forward, in front of")

**Vorbringen:** to bring forward, to put forward. You carry something to the front and present it for consideration. An argument is *vorgebracht* — advanced, stated, laid before the audience. A complaint is *vorgebracht* — raised, brought up, put forward. The prefix is spatial and rhetorical: what is *vorgebracht* is brought before others for judgment.

Literal mnemonic: "bring forward / bring before." "I'd like to bring forward a proposal" = *einen Vorschlag vorbringen.*

### WEG- ("away, gone")

**Wegbringen:** to bring away, to take away, to remove. You carry something away from where it is. Rubbish is *weggebracht* — taken away, carried off. The simplest "away" prefix: the thing is brought elsewhere and gone.

Literal mnemonic: "bring away." "Bring it away" = take it off, remove it.

### WIEDER- ("again, back")

**Wiederbringen:** to bring back, to return, to restore. You carry something back to where it came from — you bring it again to its origin. A borrowed book is *wiedergebracht* — returned. Lost trust is *wiedergebracht* — restored.

Literal mnemonic: "bring back / bring again." "Bring it back" = *bring es wieder.*

### ZU- ("to, toward, closed")

**Zubringen:** to bring to — to spend, to pass (time). Like *verbringen*, this verb has drifted from physical delivery into temporal passage. You *bringen* a night *zu* — you pass it, you spend it. The original gesture survives faintly: time is brought toward its conclusion, delivered to its end. Less common than *verbringen* but identical in meaning.

Literal mnemonic: "bring to (an end)." The hours are brought toward their close.

### ZURÜCK- ("back, backward, in return")

**Zurückbringen:** to bring back, to return, to take back. You carry something back to where it came from. A library book is *zurückgebracht* — returned. The most literal and common "bring back" compound, slightly more concrete and everyday than *wiederbringen*.

Literal mnemonic: "bring back." "Bring it back to the store" = *bring es zum Laden zurück.*

### ZUSAMMEN- ("together")

**Zusammenbringen:** to bring together, to gather, to assemble, to unite. You carry separate things toward each other until they meet. People are *zusammengebracht* — introduced, united, gathered. Money is *zusammengebracht* — collected, pooled. Evidence is *zusammengebracht* — assembled, compiled. The prefix is always convergent: scattered things are brought to one place.

Literal mnemonic: "bring together." "Bring everyone together" = *alle zusammenbringen.*

### ÜBER- ("over, across")

**Überbringen:** to bring over, to deliver, to convey. You carry something across a distance and hand it over — a message is *überbracht*, a greeting is *überbracht*. The inseparable prefix signals formal, complete conveyance: the thing has been brought across from sender to recipient. This is the dignified, ceremonial counterpart to casual delivery — ambassadors *überbringen* messages, bearers *überbringen* condolences.

Literal mnemonic: "bring over." Think of "bringing a message over" from one party to another, carrying it across the gap between them.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The cognate advantage is enormous.** Because English "bring" is a direct cognate of German *bringen*, an unusually large number of these compounds translate almost word-for-word. *Einbringen* = bring in. *Herausbringen* = bring out. *Mitbringen* = bring along. *Zurückbringen* = bring back. *Zusammenbringen* = bring together. *Vorbringen* = bring forward. *Wegbringen* = bring away. Where the match holds, trust it — the German is doing exactly what the English phrase does.

**The surprises deserve extra attention.** Three compounds break sharply from what the English cognate would predict. *Umbringen* (to kill) stretches "bring around/down" to its lethal extreme — English has "take out" and "do in" but would never use "bring" this way. *Hinterbringen* (to inform secretly) turns "bring behind" into covert reporting — the message is brought behind the subject's back. *Verbringen* (to spend time) abstracts physical carrying into temporal passage — no object moves, only hours are "brought through" and consumed. These three must be learned as units; the prefix logic is there, but it has traveled far from the obvious.

**Separable prefixes stay spatial and concrete.** *Ab, an, auf, ein, hin, her, mit, nach, vor, weg, zurück, zusammen* — these function as directional adverbs you can visualize. The bringing has a clear trajectory: in, out, up, down, along, back, together, away. You can picture the motion.

**Inseparable prefixes transform the action.** *Er-, ver-, voll-, über-, hinter-, unter-* — these fuse with the verb and push it further from literal carrying. *Erbringen* is not carrying but producing. *Vollbringen* is not delivering but accomplishing. *Hinterbringen* is not carrying behind but informing covertly. With inseparable prefixes, learn the compound as a whole word rather than trying to decode it on the fly.

**Time is something you "bring."** Both *verbringen* and *zubringen* use the metaphor of carrying time — bringing it through, bringing it to its end. English uses "spend" and "pass" for time, both originally physical metaphors (paying out, walking past). German chose "bring" — you carry the hours along with you until they are gone. Different image, same logic of treating time as something handled and consumed.
