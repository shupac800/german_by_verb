# HEISSEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Heissen** means to be called, to be named, to mean. It is related to the archaic English "hight" (as in "he was hight Sir Lancelot" — he was called Sir Lancelot). This is one of the most essential German verbs: *Ich heisse...* is how you say "My name is..." — literally, "I am called..." Hold this image in mind: a name being spoken over someone or something, declaring what it is. The verb sits at the intersection of identity and language — to name, to call, to signify.

## The Prefixes

### GE- (past participle prefix — here fossilized)

**Geheissen** (adj.): called, named, bidden. Originally the past participle of *heissen* — someone who has been called, who has been named. In older usage, also someone who has been bidden or commanded (reflecting *heissen*'s archaic sense of "to order"). The form survives as a fossilized adjective, much as *gelegen* survives from *legen*. The logic is straightforward: what has been called by a name is *geheissen*.

Mnemonic: "so called." A person *geheissen* Hans is a person "called Hans" — named, identified, spoken over.

### VER- (transformation, intensification, forward projection)

**Verheissen:** to promise, to augur, to portend, to foretell. The prefix *ver-* transforms the act of calling or naming into something prophetic. To *verheissen* is to name what is coming — to call out the future before it arrives. A *Verheissung* is a promise or prophecy: a solemn declaration of what will be. The transformation is elegant: naming what exists (*heissen*) becomes naming what does not yet exist (*verheissen*). To call out tomorrow's shape is to promise it.

Mnemonic: "call forth." Think of a prophet who does not merely name what is, but calls forth what will be. To name the future is to promise it. English "foretell" captures the same structure — telling (saying, naming) pushed forward in time.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**This is a very small family.** Unlike *legen*, which spawns dozens of prefix combinations, *heissen* resists prefixing almost entirely. Only two forms survive: a fossilized participle (*geheissen*) and a single inseparable verb (*verheissen*). The reason is intuitive — identity-naming verbs do not lend themselves to spatial manipulation. You can lay something *up, down, in, out, apart, together* — but you cannot call someone "out-named" or "apart-called." The act of naming is binary: something is called what it is called. There is no direction to redirect.

**The one productive prefix is the most abstract one.** It is telling that *ver-* is the only prefix that successfully attaches to *heissen*. The spatial prefixes (*ab-, an-, auf-, aus-, ein-*) have nothing to grip — naming has no direction. But *ver-*, which transforms rather than redirects, finds purchase: it shifts the naming from present reality into future prophecy. This is *ver-* at its most elegant, not displacing or distorting the action, but projecting it forward in time.

**The English cognate is dead; the German verb is alive.** English "hight" vanished from the living language centuries ago, replaced by the French-derived "called" and the Latin-derived "named." German *heissen* never lost its central position. It remains the first verb many learners encounter: *Wie heissen Sie?* Where English buried its Germanic word for naming, German kept it at the heart of daily speech.
