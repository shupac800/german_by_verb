# KOMMEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Kommen** means to come, to arrive. It is the fundamental verb of approach and arrival — movement toward a point, a state, or an event. Hold this image in mind: someone or something drawing nearer, reaching a destination. Nearly every prefix below redirects that motion — specifying where the coming leads, what it produces, or how it transforms the one who comes.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abkommen:** to come off, to come away from. Picture a path: you are walking along it, and then you come off it — you deviate, you stray. A bullet *kommt ab* from its intended trajectory. A conversation *kommt ab* from the topic. The prefix pulls the arrival away from the expected course. What was supposed to come straight now comes askew.

Literal mnemonic: "come off." English uses the same construction — "to come off course," "to get off track."

### AN- ("on, onto, at, toward arrival")

**Ankommen:** to come at, to come on arrival. The prefix marks the completion of approach — the moment of touching down, of reaching the destination. A train *kommt an*; a letter *kommt an*; a guest *kommt an*. Figuratively, *es kommt darauf an* — "it comes down to" — means it matters, it depends. The arrival is not spatial here but logical: everything converges on this one point.

Literal mnemonic: "come on" in the sense of "come on the scene," or "come at the goal." English "it comes down to" mirrors the figurative sense exactly.

### AUF- ("up, up onto, into being")

**Aufkommen:** to come up. Something rises from below into visibility. A wind *kommt auf* — it springs up. A trend *kommt auf* — it emerges, appears. A question *kommt auf* — it arises. The financial sense — to pay for, to bear the cost — is less transparent but follows: you come up to cover the expense, you step up to meet the bill.

Literal mnemonic: "come up." English works identically — "a storm came up," "a question came up," "who will come up with the money?"

### AUS- ("out, outward, to the end")

**Auskommen:** to come out with what you have. The prefix stretches the coming outward toward sufficiency — you come out all the way to the end with your resources. To *auskommen* with something is to manage, to get by, to make do. The image: you have only so much, and you must make it come out to cover your needs.

Literal mnemonic: "come out even." English "make out" (as in "we'll make out all right") carries a similar sense of managing to the end.

### BEI- ("beside, near, up to")

**Beikommen:** to come at, to get at. The prefix draws the motion near to, alongside, up against something. To *beikommen* a problem is to approach it close enough to tackle it, to get at it. The difficulty is not distant — it is right there — but you need to find the angle to come alongside it.

Literal mnemonic: "come by" in the sense of "get by" a difficulty, or "come at" a problem.

### BE- (intensifier / transitivizer)

**Bekommen:** to come by, to receive. This is one of German's most important false friends for English speakers. *Bekommen* does not mean "to become" — it means "to get, to receive, to obtain." The inseparable prefix *be-* stamps the coming fully onto an object: not "I come" but "something comes to me." A package is *bekommen*; a cold is *bekommen*; an impression is *bekommen*. The logic: *be-* turns the intransitive motion of coming into a transitive act of receiving — the arrival lands on you and delivers something.

Mnemonic: forget "become" entirely. Think of *be-* as "upon me." What *bekommt* you has come upon you. The waiter's *Haben Sie schon bekommen?* means "Have you received your order yet?" — not "Have you already become?"

### DARAN- ("at it, to it, onto it")

**Darankommen:** to come at it, to get to it. The prefix points at a specific thing: you come close enough to reach it, to touch it. *Ich komme nicht daran* — I can't get at it, it is beyond my reach. Also: to get one's turn — your moment to "come at it" arrives.

Literal mnemonic: "come at that." The shelf is too high; you can't come at the jar.

### DURCH- ("through")

**Durchkommen:** to come through. The prefix drives the motion straight through an obstacle or ordeal. A patient *kommt durch* — survives, pulls through the illness. A call *kommt durch* — gets through the connection. A traveler *kommt durch* a town — passes through. In every case, there is a barrier or medium, and the coming penetrates it.

Literal mnemonic: "come through." English is identical — "she came through the surgery," "the message came through."

### EMPOR- ("upward, aloft")

**Emporkommen:** to come up, to rise. The prefix is literary and elevating — it lifts the motion toward height and prominence. A person *kommt empor* — rises in society, advances from humble beginnings. More formal and dramatic than *aufkommen*, this verb carries the weight of ascent against gravity, of striving upward.

Literal mnemonic: "come up high." Think of "rise to the fore" — *emporkommen* is the ambitious, effortful version of coming up.

### ENTGEGEN- ("toward, against, to meet")

**Entgegenkommen:** to come toward, to come to meet. This is a beautifully dual verb. Physically, someone *kommt* you *entgegen* — they come toward you, they walk to meet you halfway. Figuratively, someone *kommt* you *entgegen* — they accommodate you, they oblige, they come to meet you in negotiation. The concrete and the abstract are the same motion: closing the distance between two parties, each moving toward the other.

Literal mnemonic: "come to meet." English "meet halfway" captures both the physical and figurative sense. German keeps them fused in a single verb.

### ENT- ("away from, out of")

**Entkommen:** to come away from, to come out of. The prefix pulls the motion out of a trap, a danger, a pursuer. To *entkommen* is to escape — to come away from what held you. The inseparable prefix gives it urgency and finality: this is not casual departure but flight, evasion, narrow extraction.

Literal mnemonic: "come away." Think of "come clear of" — the escapee comes clear of the prison, the quarry comes clear of the hunter.

### FORT- ("away, onward, gone")

**Fortkommen:** to come away, to get on. Two senses branch from one root. Getting away: you leave, you depart, you come forth from where you were. Getting on: you make progress, you advance — you come forward in life or work. The prefix pushes the coming outward and onward, away from stasis.

Literal mnemonic: "come forth." English "get on" captures the progress meaning; "get away" captures the departure.

### GLEICH- ("equal, same, even")

**Gleichkommen:** to come equal. The prefix levels the motion — you come to the same height, the same value, the same standard as something else. To *gleichkommen* a feat is to equal it, to match it, to rival it. The coming is not toward a place but toward parity.

Literal mnemonic: "come even with." English "come up to the same level" is the longer version.

### HERAUF- ("up from below toward the speaker")

**Heraufkommen:** to come up (toward here). The prefix specifies direction precisely: upward and toward the speaker. Someone *kommt herauf* from the cellar, from the garden, from the street below. The motion is vertical and oriented — it rises and arrives here.

Literal mnemonic: "come up here." The speaker is above; the comer ascends.

### HERAUS- ("out from within, toward the speaker")

**Herauskommen:** to come out. Something that was inside emerges. A book *kommt heraus* — it is published, released into the world. A secret *kommt heraus* — it comes out, becomes known. A result *kommt heraus* — it turns out, emerges from the calculation. The prefix pulls the interior outward and toward the observer.

Literal mnemonic: "come out (from in there)." English uses "come out" identically — a book comes out, the truth comes out, it all comes out in the end.

### HER- ("hither, from over there toward here")

**Herkommen:** to come here, to come from. Two related senses flow from one direction. Spatially: *komm her!* — come here, come toward me. By origin: *Wo kommst du her?* — where do you come from? The hither-ward motion is both literal (approach the speaker) and genealogical (trace your arrival back to its source).

Literal mnemonic: "come hither." English "where do you hail from?" asks the same question as *Wo kommst du her?*

### HIN- ("thither, toward a point away from the speaker")

**Hinkommen:** to get there, to get to. The prefix directs the coming away from the speaker, toward a destination elsewhere. *Wie komme ich hin?* — how do I get there? The motion is practical and spatial — the concern is reaching the target, arriving at the intended spot.

Literal mnemonic: "come thither." English lost "thither," so "get there" is the working equivalent.

### HINWEG- ("away over, past")

**Hinwegkommen:** to come past, to get over. The prefix sweeps the motion over and beyond an obstacle — particularly an emotional one. You *kommst* over grief *hinweg* — you get past it, you come beyond it. The image is of traveling over a ridge: the pain is the high point, and coming *hinweg* means descending the other side.

Literal mnemonic: "come over and away." English "get over it" is the exact equivalent.

### HINZU- ("in addition, joining")

**Hinzukommen:** to come in addition, to join. The prefix adds the comer to what already exists. A new factor *kommt hinzu* — it is added to the situation, it joins the existing elements. A bystander *kommt hinzu* — arrives on the scene and joins the group. The motion is accretive: the coming augments rather than replaces.

Literal mnemonic: "come added." English "on top of that" captures the additive sense — something else comes along to join what is already there.

### LOS- ("loose, free, off")

**Loskommen:** to come loose, to get free. The prefix releases the motion from constraint. You are stuck — physically, emotionally, practically — and to *loskommen* is to break free, to come loose from what holds you. The image is of a boat pulling free from a sandbar, a prisoner slipping a grip.

Literal mnemonic: "come loose." English "break free" or "get loose" is the equivalent.

### MIT- ("with, along")

**Mitkommen:** to come with, to come along. The simplest social prefix — you join someone else's motion. *Kommst du mit?* — are you coming along? Figuratively, to keep up — to come along at the same pace, to follow the argument or lesson. If you cannot *mitkommen*, you are falling behind.

Literal mnemonic: "come with." English is identical — "come with me," "I can't keep up" (= I can't come along at your speed).

### NACH- ("after, following, in compliance with")

**Nachkommen:** to come after. Two senses grow from sequence. Temporally: to come later, to follow — the rest of the group *kommt nach*. By obligation: to comply with, to fulfill, to meet a duty — you come after the demand, you follow through on what was asked. A request is *nachgekommen*: you came along after it, you met it.

Literal mnemonic: "come after." English "follow up" and "follow through" capture both senses — following in time and following in compliance.

### UM- ("around, over, down — transformation")

**Umkommen:** to come around to death, to perish. This is one of the most striking prefix transformations in the family. The prefix *um-* implies a turning over, a reversal — and when life "comes around," it comes to its end. To *umkommen* is to perish, to die, to be killed. The image is of a complete circuit: life has come all the way around and tipped over into death. There is no survival, no return. The coming reaches its terminal point.

Literal mnemonic: "come over" in the sense of being overturned. A ship comes over and sinks; a person comes around to death.

### UNTER- ("under, beneath, into shelter")

**Unterkommen:** to come under, to find shelter. The prefix draws the motion downward into protection. You *kommst unter* — you find a roof, a bed, a job. The coming arrives beneath something that covers you. To find accommodation is to come under a ceiling; to find work is to come under an employer's shelter.

Literal mnemonic: "come under cover." English "find shelter" replaces the spatial metaphor, but the German preserves it: you literally come under.

### VER- (transformation, deterioration, away into ruin)

**Verkommen:** to come to ruin. Of all its faces, *ver-* here wears its darkest: the prefix drives the coming toward decay, degeneration, waste. A building *verkommt* — it deteriorates, goes to ruin. A person *verkommt* — degenerates, goes to seed. The adjective *verkommen* (run-down, dilapidated, depraved) shows the end-state: what has fully "come away" into transformation is now ruined, changed beyond recovery.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "away into wrongness." What *verkommt* has come so far away from its original state that it is destroyed. English "gone to ruin" captures the trajectory — the going (coming) has led somewhere terrible.

### VOLL- ("full, complete" — here fossilized as adjective)

**Vollkommen** (adj.): fully come, complete, perfect. The verb has vanished; only the adjective survives. Something *vollkommen* has come to its full extent — it is complete, total, absolute, perfect. This is a fossilized compound: the prefix *voll-* sealed the coming into a state of completeness, and the word froze there. It no longer means "to come fully" — it means "having fully arrived at perfection."

Mnemonic: "fully come." What is *vollkommen* has come all the way, with nothing lacking. English "consummate" (from Latin *con-* + *summa* = brought to the sum) follows the same logic from a different root.

### VOR- ("before, forward, in front")

**Vorkommen:** to come before, to come forth. Three senses branch from one gesture. To happen: something *kommt vor* — it comes forward into occurrence, it takes place. To seem: something *kommt* you *vor* — it comes before your perception, it strikes you as something. To exist: a species *kommt vor* in a region — it is found there, it presents itself. In every case, the prefix pushes the coming forward into visibility or experience.

Literal mnemonic: "come forth." English "it comes to mind" and "it occurs" (from Latin "run toward") follow the same spatial logic.

### WEG- ("away, gone")

**Wegkommen:** to come away, to get away, to disappear. The prefix simply removes — the coming carries the subject or object away from where it was. Keys *kommen weg* — they go missing, they disappear. A person *kommt weg* — gets away, escapes. The motion is departure without destination: the point is leaving, not arriving.

Literal mnemonic: "come away." English "get away" and "go missing" cover the same ground.

### WIEDER- ("again, back")

**Wiederkommen:** to come again, to come back. The prefix resets the motion — you came before, and now you come once more. The simplest form of return: *komm wieder!* — come back. No metaphor, no abstraction. The coming is repeated.

Literal mnemonic: "come again." English is identical — "come again," "come back."

### ZU- ("to, toward, closed, additional")

**Zukommen:** to come toward, to be due to. The prefix directs the motion at a specific target. Something *kommt auf dich zu* — it comes toward you, it approaches. A right or privilege *kommt* someone *zu* — it is due to them, it befits them, it comes to them by entitlement. The coming has a claim: what approaches is owed.

Literal mnemonic: "come to." English "it's coming to you" works in both senses — something approaches, and something is owed.

### ZURÜCK- ("back, backward, in return")

**Zurückkommen:** to come back. The prefix reverses the motion — you came from here, you went away, and now you come back to the starting point. Also figuratively: to come back to a topic, to return to a point in discussion. The motion retraces itself.

Literal mnemonic: "come back." English is identical.

### ZUSAMMEN- ("together")

**Zusammenkommen:** to come together. Separate people or things converge into one group, one place, one occasion. A crowd *kommt zusammen* — it gathers. A committee *kommt zusammen* — it convenes. Money *kommt zusammen* — it accumulates, piece by piece. The prefix unifies the coming: many arrivals become one assembly.

Literal mnemonic: "come together." English works identically — "the group came together," "it all came together in the end."

### ÜBER- ("over, upon, across")

**Überkommen:** to come over, to overcome, to seize. The inseparable prefix drives the coming over and onto the subject — not you coming somewhere, but something coming over you. A feeling *überkommt* you — it seizes you, washes over you, overtakes you. Fear, exhaustion, nostalgia — these are forces that come from above and descend upon the person. The motion is invasive: you do not choose what *überkommt* you.

Literal mnemonic: "come over." English preserves this exactly — "a feeling came over me," "I was overcome with emotion."

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays physical; the prefixes go abstract.** *Kommen* never stops meaning "to come, to arrive." But the prefixes redirect that arrival into metaphor. *Aufkommen* (come up) becomes "to arise." *Gleichkommen* (come equal) becomes "to rival." *Nachkommen* (come after) becomes "to comply." The body's experience of movement toward a destination is projected onto events, obligations, and states of being. Once you see this, the figurative meanings become less arbitrary.

**The false friend.** *Bekommen* is the single most important word in this family for English speakers to learn correctly. It means "to receive," never "to become." German "to become" is *werden*. This confusion has launched a thousand restaurant misunderstandings — *Ich bekomme ein Bier* means "I'm getting a beer," not "I'm turning into one."

**Verb-to-adjective drift.** Two entries show how verbs fossilize into adjectives. *Verkommen* still functions as a verb (to deteriorate) but has also frozen into an adjective (run-down, depraved) — the end-state of the deterioration. *Vollkommen* has gone further: the verb is extinct, and only the adjective (perfect, complete) remains. These two words illustrate a spectrum of fossilization — one foot still in motion, the other fully solidified.

**Separable prefixes tend to be spatial and concrete.** *Ab, an, auf, aus, ein, hin, weg, zurück, zusammen* — these preserve a physical directionality even in figurative use. You can usually picture the motion: coming up, coming out, coming back, coming together.

**Inseparable prefixes tend to be transformative and opaque.** *Be-, ent-, ver-, über-* — these fuse with the verb and shift its meaning further from the literal. *Bekommen* (receive) has no trace of spatial "coming" left. *Entkommen* (escape) compresses flight into a single act. *Verkommen* (deteriorate) turns arrival into ruin. *Überkommen* (seize, overcome) reverses the agency — the subject becomes the object of the coming.

**Death and ruin hide in ordinary motion.** *Umkommen* (to perish) takes the blandest possible prefix — "around" — and produces the most final result. *Verkommen* (to deteriorate) takes the prefix of change and drives it to destruction. German verb families often harbor these dark corners where spatial metaphor meets existential finality, and the KOMMEN family is no exception.

**English often preserves the same logic.** "Come through" = *durchkommen*. "Come out" = *herauskommen*. "Come together" = *zusammenkommen*. "Come over me" = *überkommen*. Where the languages share the metaphor, use it as an anchor. Where they diverge — and *bekommen* vs. "become" is the sharpest divergence — mark the difference clearly and learn it as a unit.
