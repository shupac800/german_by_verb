# RECHNEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Rechnen** means to calculate, to compute, to reckon, to do arithmetic. It implies working with numbers — tallying, figuring, arriving at a result through count and measure. Hold this image in mind: a person running numbers, moving quantities around on a ledger. Nearly every prefix below redirects that mental arithmetic — pointing it at a target, extending its scope, reversing its direction, or pushing it into the territory of money and error.

English "reckon" is a direct cognate: both descend from the same Germanic root. Where modern English has let "reckon" drift toward opinion ("I reckon so"), German has kept *rechnen* firmly mathematical.

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from, settled")

**Abrechnen:** to reckon off, to settle up. The prefix pulls the calculation to a close — you finish the accounting and draw a line under it. A waiter *rechnet ab* — presents the bill and settles the tab. A business *rechnet ab* — closes its books, squares accounts. The deductive sense follows naturally: to settle up, you subtract what has been paid from what is owed.

Literal mnemonic: "reckon off." You reckon everything off the books until the balance is zero.

### AN- ("on, onto, toward")

**Anrechnen:** to reckon on, to credit toward. The prefix pushes a quantity onto a total — you count something toward an existing balance. A payment is *angerechnet* — credited, reckoned onto the account. Experience is *angerechnet* — counted toward your qualifications. The gesture is always additive and acknowledging: you take something and attach it to the ledger where it counts.

Literal mnemonic: "reckon on." English preserves a faint echo in "I'll count that in your favor" — *anrechnen* does the same with a bookkeeper's precision.

### AUF- ("up, upon, open")

**Aufrechnen:** to reckon up, to tally against. You gather scattered figures and pile them up into a total. But the distinctive sense is adversarial: to *aufrechnen* is to offset one claim against another — to set your debts against my debts and see who still owes. The prefix lifts both sides up onto the table for comparison.

Literal mnemonic: "reckon up." Think of "I'll tally that up against what you owe me."

### AUS- ("out, outward, through to the end")

**Ausrechnen:** to reckon out, to work out. You take a problem and calculate it through to a finished answer. The number is *ausgerechnet* — worked out, extracted from the raw data. Figuratively, you can *ausrechnen* your chances — figure out what the odds are, compute the result in your head. The prefix carries the action outward to completion: the answer emerges.

Literal mnemonic: "work out." English works identically — "I worked out the answer" = *Ich habe die Antwort ausgerechnet.*

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Berechnen:** to reckon upon thoroughly, to calculate and to charge. *Be-* stamps the action fully onto an object. Two distinct faces emerge from this. First, to *berechnen* something is to compute it with precision — to bring the full force of calculation to bear on a problem. Second, to *berechnen* someone is to charge them — to apply the calculation to their wallet. The bridge between the two is transparent: you calculate the cost, then you apply that cost to the customer.

Mnemonic: think of "be-" as "all over." To *berechnen* a bridge's load capacity is to calculate it thoroughly. To *berechnen* a customer twenty euros is to lay that calculated amount upon them. Both senses involve aiming arithmetic at a target and making it stick.

### DURCH- ("through, from start to finish")

**Durchrechnen:** to reckon through. You take a calculation from beginning to end, working through every step without skipping. A business plan is *durchgerechnet* — every number has been checked, every line computed from start to finish. The prefix insists on completeness: no shortcuts, no estimates, the full journey through the arithmetic.

Literal mnemonic: "reckon through." "Let me run through the numbers" captures the same motion.

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Einrechnen:** to reckon in, to factor in. You take a quantity that was sitting outside the calculation and bring it inside. Shipping costs are *eingerechnet* — factored into the price. Risk is *eingerechnet* — included in the assessment. The prefix directs the reckoning inward, into the total where the figure now lives.

Literal mnemonic: "reckon in." English uses "factor in" and "figure in" — both describe placing something inside the calculation.

### ER- (achievement, completion, arrival at a result)

**Errechnen:** to reckon forth, to arrive at by calculation. *Er-* signals that the arithmetic reaches its definitive result. You don't just calculate — you calculate and thereby obtain the answer. A scientist *errechnet* the distance to a star: the number is not guessed or estimated but earned through methodical computation. The prefix converts process into product.

Mnemonic: "reckon to a result." The *er-* marks the moment the answer crystallizes out of the work.

### HINZU- ("in addition, added to")

**Hinzurechnen:** to reckon in addition. You take something that was left out and add it to the count. Tax is *hinzugerechnet* — added onto the subtotal. The prefix is transparent and literal: *hinzu* means "to this, in addition," and the verb means exactly what its parts promise.

Literal mnemonic: "add on to the reckoning." Straightforward accumulation.

### MIT- ("with, along, together")

**Mitrechnen:** to reckon with, to count in. You include something in the running total — it comes along with the rest. Every expense must be *mitgerechnet* — counted in alongside the others. The prefix insists that nothing is left on the sideline: everything rides along in the calculation.

Literal mnemonic: "reckon along." "Don't forget to count that in too" = *Das muss man mitrechnen.*

### NACH- ("after, behind, re-checking")

**Nachrechnen:** to reckon after, to check the arithmetic. The prefix sends you back over the same ground a second time. Someone has already done the math; now you *rechnen nach* — you follow behind their work and verify it. A teacher *rechnet nach* — recalculates to see whether the student's answer holds up. The image is of a second pass, a verification run.

Literal mnemonic: "reckon after." Think of "going back over the numbers" — the "after" implies retracing steps.

### UM- ("around, over, into a different form")

**Umrechnen:** to reckon over into another form, to convert. The prefix signals transformation — the same quantity is re-expressed in a different unit. Dollars are *umgerechnet* into euros. Miles are *umgerechnet* into kilometers. The number itself does not change in value; it is simply reclothed, turned around into a new system of measurement.

Literal mnemonic: "reckon over." English "convert" hides the arithmetic; *umrechnen* keeps it visible — you are literally re-reckoning the number in new terms.

### VER- (transformation, displacement, error)

**Verrechnen:** one of the most revealing *ver-* verbs, because the prefix's two faces — completion and error — both appear side by side.

- *Settlement:* to reckon through to resolution — to settle, to offset, to charge against an account. Claims are *verrechnet* — cleared against each other until the balance is settled. The prefix drives the calculation to its transformative endpoint: what was owed is now resolved.
- *Miscalculation (reflexive):* *sich verrechnen* — to reckon wrongly, to make an error in calculation. Here *ver-* twists the action astray, just as *verlegen* means to mislay. You intended a correct result, but the prefix sent you off course.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "through to completion" or "off into error." *Verrechnen* a debt = reckon it away, settle it. *Sich verrechnen* = reckon wrongly, miscount. Context and reflexivity tell you which face is showing.

### ZURÜCK- ("back, backward")

**Zurückrechnen:** to reckon back. You reverse the direction of the arithmetic — calculating backward from a result to find an earlier state. A historian *rechnet zurück* — counts back through the calendar to date an event. The prefix simply reverses the arrow: instead of reckoning forward to a result, you reckon backward to a source.

Literal mnemonic: "reckon back." "Count back three days" = *Drei Tage zurückrechnen.*

### ZUSAMMEN- ("together")

**Zusammenrechnen:** to reckon together, to add up, to sum. You take scattered figures and gather them into a single total. Receipts are *zusammengerechnet* — added up, all laid together into one sum. The prefix is purely aggregative: separate numbers are unified by the act of totaling.

Literal mnemonic: "reckon together." "Add it all up" = *Alles zusammenrechnen.*

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays numerical; the prefixes supply the purpose.** *Rechnen* never stops meaning "to calculate." But the prefixes determine why you are calculating: to settle (*ab-*), to verify (*nach-*), to convert (*um-*), to charge (*be-*). The arithmetic is constant; the prefix tells you what you are doing with the result.

**Separable prefixes tend to be spatial and transparent.** *Ab, an, auf, aus, ein, durch, nach, um, zusammen* — these preserve a clear directional image even in figurative use. You can picture the numbers being moved: *in* (einrechnen), *out* (ausrechnen), *together* (zusammenrechnen), *back* (zurückrechnen). The spatial gesture maps reliably onto the arithmetic operation.

**Inseparable prefixes carry more weight.** *Be-* and *er-* intensify the calculation, making it thorough or conclusive. *Ver-* is the most complex: it can mean both "settle completely" and "miscalculate," depending on reflexivity. These must be learned as units rather than assembled from parts, but knowing the prefix's character still helps you remember.

**Berechnen's dual sense reveals how German thinks about charging.** To charge someone is to calculate upon them — the bill is not an arbitrary number but a computed result applied to a person. The two meanings (calculate and charge) are not a coincidence; they are the same action viewed from different angles. English once had this too: "to reckon up the bill" meant both to compute it and to present it.

**Verrechnen captures ver-'s split personality.** In its transitive form, *ver-* drives the reckoning to completion (settlement). In its reflexive form, *ver-* derails the reckoning into error (miscalculation). This dual nature — completion and corruption — runs through many *ver-* verbs and is one of the prefix's defining characteristics. Learning to feel comfortable with this ambiguity is a milestone in German prefix intuition.
