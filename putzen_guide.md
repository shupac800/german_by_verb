# PUTZEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Putzen** carries three distinct senses: to clean or polish (scrubbing a surface until it shines), to decorate or adorn (making something look fine), and to plaster or render (coating a wall with a smooth finish). The link between these meanings is the idea of working a surface — rubbing, buffing, or coating it until it looks right. Hold this triple image in mind: a hand scrubbing a shoe, a hand trimming a Christmas tree, a hand troweling plaster onto a wall. The prefixes below redirect and sharpen these gestures.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abputzen:** to wipe off, to clean off. The prefix pulls the action away from the surface — you are not cleaning something up but cleaning something *off*. Dirt, grime, a smear: you wipe it away, removing it from where it was. The focus is on what is being eliminated rather than on the surface being improved.

Literal mnemonic: "clean off." English works identically — "wipe it off," "clean that off."

### AUF- ("up, upon, open")

**Aufputzen:** to polish up, to spruce up; to decorate, to adorn. Here *auf-* does double duty, bridging the stem's two civilian senses. On the cleaning side, it intensifies: you do not merely clean, you polish *up*, you bring something to a higher state of finish. On the decorating side, it means to dress something up, to adorn it — to add ornament *upon* something plain. The boundary between "polished until it gleams" and "decorated until it dazzles" is exactly where *aufputzen* lives.

Literal mnemonic: "clean up / dress up." Think of sprucing up a room — half cleaning, half decorating.

### AUS- ("out, outward")

**Ausputzen:** to clean out; to prune, to trim. The prefix directs the cleaning gesture outward, extracting what does not belong. You clean *out* a gutter, removing debris. You prune *out* dead branches, trimming away excess growth. The action is subtractive — reaching in and pulling unwanted material out so that what remains is tidy.

Literal mnemonic: "clean out." English uses the same construction — "clean out the garage," "trim out the dead wood."

### HERAUS- ("out from within")

**Herausputzen:** to dress up, to spruce up, to deck out, to do up. This is the most vivid compound in the family. The prefix means "out from inside" — and the image is of bringing something (or someone) out into visibility by polishing and adorning them. You take a drab interior state and *clean it outward* until it shines for the world to see. A child is *herausgeputzt* for a wedding — scrubbed, combed, dressed in fine clothes, presented outward. A shopfront is *herausgeputzt* for the holidays — decorated so that its best face is turned out toward the street.

Literal mnemonic: "clean out into the open." What was hidden or plain is polished until it steps forward and commands attention.

### VER- (transformation, completion, intensification)

**Verputzen:** to plaster, to render; (colloquially) to polish off, to devour. Here *ver-* activates two entirely different faces of the stem. In its construction sense, *ver-* signals thorough completion: you plaster a wall completely, coating every inch, transforming raw masonry into a smooth finished surface. The job is done — the wall is *verputzt*. In its colloquial sense, the same logic of total completion applies to food: you *verputzen* a plate of Schnitzel the way you plaster a wall — you cover every bit of it, consuming it entirely, leaving nothing behind. To "polish off" a meal is the perfect English parallel: the surface is wiped clean, the job is finished, nothing remains.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "thoroughly, to completion." Plaster a wall until it is fully coated. Devour a meal until the plate is bare. Both are acts of total coverage.

### WEG- ("away, gone")

**Wegputzen:** to wipe away, to clean off; (colloquially) to polish off, to demolish. The simplest directional prefix — the cleaning gesture is aimed at making something vanish. You wipe a stain *away*. You clean a mark *off*. In colloquial use, *wegputzen* overlaps with *verputzen*: to demolish food, to polish it off, to make it disappear. The difference in emphasis is that *weg-* stresses the disappearance ("it is gone") while *ver-* stresses the thoroughness ("it is completely consumed").

Literal mnemonic: "clean away." The thing that was there has been wiped out of existence.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem's triple meaning enriches every compound.** Because *putzen* means to clean, to decorate, and to plaster, each prefix can activate a different facet. *Aufputzen* bridges cleaning and decorating. *Verputzen* bridges plastering and eating. *Herausputzen* turns cleaning into display. This makes the family richer — and less predictable — than a single-sense stem like *legen*.

**Separable prefixes stay spatial and concrete.** *Ab-, auf-, aus-, heraus-, weg-* all describe physical directions: off, up, out, out-from-within, away. Even when the meaning is figurative (*herausputzen* = to dress up), the spatial image remains recoverable. You can picture the motion.

**The inseparable prefix transforms.** *Ver-* is the only inseparable prefix in this family, and it behaves characteristically: it takes the stem's meaning and pushes it to completion or transforms it into something new. Plastering is cleaning-as-total-coating. Devouring is cleaning-as-total-consumption. In both cases, *ver-* signals that the action has gone all the way through.

**English "polish off" mirrors German logic.** English speakers already use "polish" — a cleaning word — to mean "devour" ("polish off a pizza"). German does the same thing with *verputzen* and *wegputzen*. The metaphor is identical: finishing food is imagined as cleaning a surface until nothing is left. Where the two languages share the same leap, use it as an anchor.

**HERAUSPUTZEN is the signature verb.** If there is one compound to remember from this family, it is *herausputzen*. The image of cleaning something *out into visibility* — polishing a hidden gem until it shines publicly — captures the creative potential of German prefix verbs. The prefix does not merely modify the stem; it tells a small story about transformation and presentation.
