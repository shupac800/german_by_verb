# TRAGEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Tragen** means to carry, to bear, to wear. The core image is weight held up: a hand carrying a bag, a body bearing a load, a person wearing a coat. English "bear" works the same way — it means to carry physically, to wear, to endure, to give birth to. Hold this parallel in mind throughout: where English uses "bear," German often uses *tragen*. Nearly every prefix below reshapes that basic act of carrying — redirecting it, completing it, or abstracting it.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from, down")

**Abtragen:** to carry off, to wear down. The prefix *ab-* pulls the carrying action downward and away. Soil is *abgetragen* — carried off, removed. A debt is *abgetragen* — carried away, paid off piece by piece. Clothing is *abgetragen* — worn down, worn thin through long carrying. In every case, the prefix signals gradual removal or reduction through sustained bearing.

Literal mnemonic: "bear off" or "wear down." A coat borne too long is worn off; a debt borne steadily is paid off.

### AN- ("on, onto, up to")

**Antragen:** to carry to, to offer. The prefix *an-* brings something toward someone. You carry a proposal up to a person — you offer it, put it forward. *Antragen* is formal: one *trägt* a position *an*, presenting it for acceptance. The gesture is of approaching with something held out.

Literal mnemonic: "bear unto." Think of bearing a gift to someone — you carry it to them and offer it.

### AUF- ("up, upon, on")

**Auftragen:** to carry upon, to apply onto. The prefix *auf-* lifts the carrying action onto a surface. Paint is *aufgetragen* — applied, spread on. A task is *aufgetragen* — laid upon someone, charged to them. Food is *aufgetragen* — brought up and served. The common thread: something is carried upward onto something else.

Literal mnemonic: "bear upon." You bear paint upon a wall, a duty upon a person, a dish upon the table.

### AUS- ("out, outward, through to the end")

**Austragen:** to carry out, to deliver, to bear to term. The prefix *aus-* completes the carrying action outward. Mail is *ausgetragen* — carried out and delivered. A pregnancy is *ausgetragen* — carried through to completion, borne to term. A dispute is *ausgetragen* — carried out to resolution, settled. The prefix signals that the bearing reaches its destination or conclusion.

Literal mnemonic: "bear out." A letter is borne out to its recipient. A child is borne out to birth.

### BEI- ("beside, alongside, to")

**Beitragen:** to carry alongside, to contribute. The prefix *bei-* brings your portion alongside others'. You carry something to the group and add it to the common effort. *Beitragen* always implies joining a collective: contributing money, ideas, or effort toward a shared goal.

Literal mnemonic: "bear alongside." You bear your share and lay it beside everyone else's.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Betragen:** to bear oneself, to amount to. The prefix *be-* wraps the carrying action around the subject or object. Reflexively, *sich betragen* means to bear oneself — to behave, to conduct oneself. In its mathematical sense, a sum *beträgt* a certain amount — it bears up to, it comes to that total. Both senses involve the full weight of something being assessed.

Mnemonic: "be-" = "thoroughly, in full." How you *bear yourself* = how you behave. What a total *bears up to* = what it amounts to.

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Eintragen:** to carry into, to enter. The prefix *ein-* directs the carrying action inward — into a book, a list, a register. You carry a name into a ledger: you register, you enroll, you inscribe. The physical image is of bearing something into an enclosure and setting it down inside.

Literal mnemonic: "bear in." You bear your name into the register. English "enter" (from Latin *intrare*) captures the same inward motion.

### ER- (completion, endurance to the end)

**Ertragen:** to bear through, to endure. The prefix *er-* signals that the bearing is sustained to its limit. Where *tragen* simply carries, *ertragen* carries all the way through — bearing pain, hardship, or provocation without giving way. This is the emotional and psychological dimension of bearing: endurance.

Mnemonic: "bear it out." To *ertragen* is to bear something through to the end without breaking. English "endure" and "bear" overlap here — "I can't bear it" = *Ich kann es nicht ertragen*.

### FORT- ("away, onward")

**Forttragen:** to carry away. The prefix *fort-* sends the carrying away from the current location. Straightforward and literal: you pick something up and carry it away.

Literal mnemonic: "bear forth." The object is borne away from here.

### HIN- ("thither, toward a point away from the speaker")

**Hintragen:** to carry there, to bring there. The prefix *hin-* directs the carrying toward a destination away from the speaker. You pick something up and carry it to that place over there. Simple and directional.

Literal mnemonic: "bear thither." You carry it to where it needs to go.

### VER- (transformation, endurance, consumption)

**Vertragen:** to bear with, to tolerate, to get along with. The prefix *ver-* here signals sustained bearing that transforms the relationship between bearer and burden. You *verträgst* spicy food — you can bear it, your body handles it. You *verträgst dich* with a neighbor — you bear with each other, you get along. The emphasis is on compatibility: what you can carry without trouble.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "bear through to a settled state." If you can *vertragen* something, you have absorbed it and remain at peace.

### VOR- ("before, forward, in front of")

**Vortragen:** to carry before, to present. The prefix *vor-* brings the carrying action forward before an audience. You carry your words, your report, your poem before listeners — you present, deliver, recite. A lecture is *vorgetragen*, a speech is *vorgetragen*. The image is of stepping forward and bearing your material out in the open.

Literal mnemonic: "bear before." You bear your presentation before the audience. English "bring forward" is a close parallel.

### WEG- ("away, gone")

**Wegtragen:** to carry away. The prefix *weg-* removes the object from the scene. Like *forttragen*, this is straightforward: pick up and carry off. *Weg-* emphasizes that the thing is gone from where it was.

Literal mnemonic: "bear away." The debris is borne away, cleared from the site.

### ZUSAMMEN- ("together")

**Zusammentragen:** to carry together, to compile. The prefix *zusammen-* gathers what was scattered. You carry pieces from various places and set them together in one spot. Information is *zusammengetragen* — compiled, gathered from many sources. Evidence is *zusammengetragen* — collected and assembled.

Literal mnemonic: "bear together." Think of gathering armfuls and carrying them to a single pile.

### ZU- ("to, toward, additional")

**Zutragen:** to carry to, to report, to occur. The prefix *zu-* directs the carrying toward a recipient or a point. Physically, you carry something to someone. Figuratively, gossip is *zugetragen* — carried to someone's ear, reported. Events *tragen sich zu* — they carry themselves toward reality, they occur. The thread linking these senses is arrival at a destination.

Literal mnemonic: "bear to." News is borne to someone. Events bear themselves into being.

### ÜBER- ("over, across, beyond")

**Übertragen:** to carry over, to transfer, to broadcast. The prefix *über-* lifts the carrying across a gap. Rights are *übertragen* — carried over from one person to another, transferred. A signal is *übertragen* — carried over the airwaves, broadcast. A text is *übertragen* — carried over from one language to another, translated. As an adjective, *übertragen* means figurative — meaning that has been carried over from the literal to the metaphorical. Every sense involves carrying something across a boundary.

Literal mnemonic: "carry over." English "transfer" (Latin *trans* + *ferre* = carry across) is an exact conceptual parallel. "Broadcast" is carrying a signal over distance. "Figurative" is meaning carried over from the literal.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays physical; the prefix goes abstract.** *Tragen* never stops meaning "to carry." But the prefixes push that carrying into metaphor. *Beitragen* (carry alongside) becomes "to contribute." *Vortragen* (carry before) becomes "to present." *Übertragen* (carry over) becomes "to transfer" or "to broadcast." The body's gesture of picking something up and moving it is projected onto intellectual and social actions.

**English "bear" works the same way.** Bear a load, bear a child, bear fruit, bear witness, bear a grudge, bear in mind — English "bear" undergoes the same metaphorical extensions as *tragen*. This parallel is a powerful learning anchor: wherever English says "bear," German likely says *tragen* with a prefix.

**Separable prefixes tend to be spatial and concrete.** *Ab, an, auf, aus, ein, fort, hin, vor, weg, zusammen, zu* — these are directional words that preserve a physical trajectory. You can picture the carrying motion in each case.

**Inseparable prefixes tend to be transformative.** *Be-* (behave, amount to), *er-* (endure), *ver-* (tolerate, get along), *über-* (transfer, broadcast) — these fuse with the verb and shift its meaning into behavior, psychology, or abstraction. *Übertragen* is the most versatile, spanning transfer, translation, broadcasting, and figurative language — all from "carry over."

**GETRAGEN was removed.** The original list included *getragen* as a separate entry, but it is simply the past participle of *tragen*, not a prefixed verb. When you see *getragen* in context, it means "carried" or "worn," not a distinct verb with its own meaning.
