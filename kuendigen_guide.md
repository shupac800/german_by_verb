# KÜNDIGEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Kündigen** means to give notice, to terminate, to cancel. The root is *Kunde* — knowledge, tidings, a making-known. To *kündigen* is to make something known formally: that a contract is ending, that an employee is dismissed, that a lease is up. Hold this image in mind: a declaration that something is over. The word always points forward from the speaker toward a conclusion — you are announcing an end.

---

## The Prefixes

### AN- ("on, onto, toward")

**Ankündigen:** to announce on, to give notice toward. Here the prefix reverses the stem's direction entirely. Where *kündigen* declares an ending, *ankündigen* declares a beginning — what is coming, not what is finished. You *kündigst* a visit *an* — you announce it in advance. A storm *kündigt sich an* — it announces itself, it signals its own arrival. The prefix *an-* pushes the declaration toward something approaching, onto the horizon. The formal ending becomes a formal heralding.

Literal mnemonic: "notice onto" or "notify toward." English "announce" carries the same forward thrust — Latin *annuntiare* means to report toward someone. *Ankündigen* is its Germanic mirror: to give notice of what is on its way.

### AUF- ("up, open, undone")

**Aufkündigen:** to give notice up, to cancel open. The prefix carries a sense of undoing, of breaking open what was sealed. To *aufkündigen* a treaty is to break it open — to declare it dissolved. An alliance is *aufgekündigt* — the bond is split apart by formal notice. Where plain *kündigen* ends a contract through normal procedure, *aufkündigen* has a sharper edge: it revokes, it renounces, it tears up. The *auf-* lifts the lid off a commitment and exposes its termination.

Literal mnemonic: "notice open" or "cancel up." Think of breaking open a sealed agreement — the seal is cracked, the obligation undone.

### VER- (intensification, outward distribution, completion)

**Verkündigen:** to make known forth, to proclaim outward. The inseparable *ver-* here acts as an intensifier and distributor — it takes the act of making known and broadcasts it widely, completely. To *verkündigen* is not merely to mention but to proclaim, to declare with authority, to preach. A verdict is *verkündigt* — announced with finality to all present. The Gospel is *verkündigt* — preached, carried outward to every ear. Where *kündigen* is a private notice and *ankündigen* is a public preview, *verkündigen* is a solemn broadcast. The prefix drives the declaration outward to its fullest extent.

Literal mnemonic: "forth-announce." Think of a herald standing in the square, voice carrying in every direction. The message is not placed quietly on a desk — it is cast outward and completed.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem always means "to make known formally," but the prefixes reshape what is made known and how.** Plain *kündigen* makes an ending known. *Ankündigen* makes a coming event known. *Aufkündigen* makes a revocation known. *Verkündigen* makes a truth or decree known to all. The core gesture — a formal declaration — never changes; only its object and scope do.

**Ankündigen is the surprise of the family.** The stem points toward termination, yet *an-* flips it to herald beginnings. This is a good example of how a prefix can override a stem's typical domain. The logic is spatial: *kündigen* sends notice outward (the end is declared), while *ankündigen* sends notice toward the listener about what is approaching. The direction shifts from conclusion to anticipation.

**Separable vs. inseparable tracks the concrete-abstract divide.** *Ankündigen* (sep.) and *aufkündigen* (sep.) involve practical, procedural acts — announcing an event, canceling a contract. *Verkündigen* (insep.) is elevated and ceremonial — proclaiming a verdict, preaching a gospel. The inseparable prefix fuses with the stem to produce something grander and less reversible than the separable forms.

**English parallels help but diverge.** "Announce" maps onto *ankündigen*. "Denounce" or "renounce" captures some of *aufkündigen*. "Proclaim" fits *verkündigen*. But German keeps all four verbs visibly connected through the shared stem *künd-*, while English scatters them across Latin roots (*nounce*, *claim*, *voke*). Seeing the German family as one system — a single act of formal notice, redirected by prefixes — is more economical than memorizing four unrelated English verbs.
