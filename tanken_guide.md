# TANKEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Tanken** means to fuel, to refuel, to fill up with fuel. The image is simple and modern: pumping fuel into a tank. The word derives from *Tank* (tank, fuel tank), itself borrowed from English. Unlike most stems in this collection, *tanken* is narrowly concrete — it rarely drifts into metaphor. The prefixes here mostly fine-tune the manner or completeness of fueling rather than launching into abstraction.

---

## The Prefixes

### AUF- ("up, upon, replenishing")

**Auftanken:** to tank up. The prefix *auf-* adds the sense of filling upward, topping off, replenishing. Where *tanken* is the basic act of fueling, *auftanken* emphasizes restoring the supply to full. Colloquially, Germans extend this beyond vehicles — you *tankst auf* at a vacation, recharging your energy.

Literal mnemonic: "tank up." English "fuel up" captures the same emphasis on completeness.

### BE- (makes the verb transitive, targets an object)

**Betanken:** to fuel (something). The prefix *be-* redirects the action onto a specific object. Where *tanken* is intransitive ("I fuel up"), *betanken* takes a direct object: the aircraft is *betankt*, the ship is *betankt*. This is technical and formal — you will encounter it in aviation and logistics more than in everyday speech.

Mnemonic: "be-" stamps the fueling action onto the vehicle. You are not just fueling — you are fueling *this plane*.

### NACH- ("after, additionally, supplementary")

**Nachtanken:** to tank again, to refuel mid-journey. The prefix *nach-* means "after" or "more." You have already fueled once, and now you fuel again — a supplementary stop. On a long drive, you *tankst nach* when the gauge drops. The prefix adds the idea of repetition or continuation.

Literal mnemonic: "after-tank." You tank after the first fill has been partly used.

### VOLL- ("full, completely")

**Volltanken:** to tank full. The prefix *voll-* means full, and the compound is transparent: fill the tank completely, to the brim. At a German gas station, *Einmal volltanken, bitte* is the standard phrase — "fill it up, please."

Literal mnemonic: "full-tank." English "fill up" and German *volltanken* are exact parallels.

### ZU- ("to, toward, additional")

**Zutanken:** to tank additionally, to add more fuel. The prefix *zu-* in its additive sense pushes more fuel toward what is already there. Similar to *nachtanken*, but *zutanken* emphasizes the incremental addition rather than the temporal sequence.

Literal mnemonic: "tank to (it)." You add fuel to what is already in the tank.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays concrete throughout.** Unlike most verb stems in this collection, *tanken* resists metaphorical extension. The prefixes adjust the completeness, timing, or grammatical targeting of fueling, but the core image — putting fuel into a tank — never really shifts. This makes *tanken* one of the easiest prefix families to learn.

**Separable prefixes dominate.** All prefixes except *be-* are separable, reflecting their spatial and concrete character. *Be-* is the sole inseparable prefix, and its role is purely grammatical — making the verb transitive so it can take a direct object.

**Overlap is high.** Several of these verbs are near-synonyms: *auftanken*, *nachtanken*, *zutanken*, and *volltanken* all mean some version of "refuel." The differences are in emphasis: *voll-* stresses completeness, *nach-* stresses repetition, *auf-* stresses replenishment, *zu-* stresses addition. In casual speech, Germans often use *tanken* or *volltanken* and ignore the finer distinctions.

**English parallel: "fill up," "top off," "refuel."** English uses separate phrasal verbs where German uses prefixes, but the logic is the same — small spatial words modifying a core action.
