# REIßEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Reißen** means to tear, to rip, to rend, to jerk, to yank. It implies violent, sudden force — the sharp motion that splits material apart or wrenches something from where it was. Hold this image in mind: hands gripping cloth or paper and pulling in opposite directions until it gives. Nearly every prefix below redirects that tearing force — aiming it at a surface, into a crowd, across a boundary, or inward upon the self.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abreißen:** to tear off, to tear away from. The prefix directs the ripping motion away from the whole. A ticket stub is *abgerissen* — torn off at the perforated line. A building is *abgerissen* — torn down, demolished, ripped away from the cityscape. A conversation *reißt ab* — it breaks off, tears away into silence. Whether physical or figurative, something that was attached is severed by force.

Literal mnemonic: "tear off." English uses this identically — "tear off a strip," "tear down a building."

### AN- ("on, onto, beginning")

**Anreißen:** to tear on, to tear into the surface. The prefix brings the tearing action into initial contact. You *reißen* a package *an* — you tear it open just enough to start. A scribe *reißt* a line *an* — scores the surface, marking it by scratching into it. The image is of force that initiates rather than completes: a first rip, a starting scratch, a line scored onto metal.

Literal mnemonic: "tear into" or "scratch on." Think of the initial nick that starts a tear.

### AUF- ("up, open")

**Aufreißen:** to tear open, to tear upward. The prefix lifts and splits. A letter is *aufgerissen* — torn open. A door is *aufgerissen* — flung open with violent force. A wound *reißt auf* — it tears open, splits apart. The direction is always outward and apart: whatever was closed or sealed is ripped open by sudden force.

Literal mnemonic: "tear open." "He tore open the envelope" = *Er hat den Umschlag aufgerissen.*

### AUS- ("out, outward")

**Ausreißen:** to tear out. The prefix pulls the tearing action outward from something. A page is *ausgerissen* — torn out of a book. A weed is *ausgerissen* — pulled out, uprooted. From this physical image comes the figurative: a person *reißt aus* — tears themselves out of a situation, runs away, bolts. The escapee has torn free and pulled out.

Literal mnemonic: "tear out." "She tore out the page" for the physical sense; "he tore out of there" for the flight.

### DURCH- ("through")

**Durchreißen:** to tear through, to tear in two. The prefix sends the tearing force all the way through the material. A rope *reißt durch* — it tears in half. Paper is *durchgerissen* — ripped through from one edge to the other. Clean, complete severance: the tear passes through and nothing holds.

Literal mnemonic: "tear through." The rip goes through and the two halves separate.

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Einreißen:** to tear in, to tear into. Two distinct images share the same inward direction. (1) A wall is *eingerissen* — torn down, pulled inward upon itself, demolished. (2) A bad habit *reißt ein* — it tears into the fabric of daily life, sets in, takes hold. The demolition is physical tearing-in; the habit is a metaphorical tear that widens and becomes established once it starts.

Literal mnemonic: "tear in." A crack that "tears in" to a wall undermines it. A practice that "tears in" to a routine becomes entrenched.

### ENT- ("away from, wresting from")

**Entreißen:** to tear away from, to snatch from. The prefix marks violent removal from someone's possession. You *entreißen* a weapon from an attacker — you wrench it away. Fate *entreißt* a loved one — snatches them from you. The tearing is directed away from a holder: *ent-* provides the "from whom" that makes this verb about wresting rather than mere ripping.

Literal mnemonic: "tear from." "She wrenched the knife from his hand" = *Sie hat ihm das Messer entrissen.*

### HERAUS- ("out from within")

**Herausreißen:** to tear out from inside. Stronger and more specific than *ausreißen* — the *heraus-* emphasizes extraction from an interior. A tooth is *herausgerissen*. Pages are *herausgerissen* from a bound book. Figuratively, a last-minute save *reißt es heraus* — tears the situation out of the jaws of defeat, rescues it from inside the disaster.

Literal mnemonic: "tear out (from in there)." "That goal tore it out for us" = that goal saved us at the last moment.

### HIN- ("thither, toward, away from the speaker")

**Hinreißen:** to tear toward, to carry away. The prefix pulls the listener or viewer toward something irresistibly — the force of *reißen* redirected into emotional transport. A performance *reißt hin* — it tears you toward it, carries you away, enraptures. *Hinreißend* is one of German's strongest compliments: ravishing, thrilling, captivating. The logic is that beauty or power tears you out of yourself and pulls you toward it.

Literal mnemonic: "tear thither." You are torn toward the source of fascination. English "carried away" captures the same idea — moved by force beyond your own control.

### LOS- ("loose, free, released")

**Losreißen:** to tear loose, to tear free. The prefix releases — and when you release a tearing force, things break free. A dog *reißt sich los* — tears itself free from the leash. A prisoner *reißt sich los* — breaks free from a captor's grip. The image is always of something held that rips away from restraint.

Literal mnemonic: "tear loose." "He tore loose from the guard's grip" = *Er hat sich losgerissen.*

### MIT- ("along with, together")

**Mitreißen:** to tear along, to sweep along. The prefix adds company — the tearing force drags others with it. A flood *reißt* everything *mit* — sweeps it along, carries it away. A crowd *reißt* onlookers *mit* — drags them into the excitement. A speaker *reißt* the audience *mit* — carries them along on a wave of enthusiasm. The physical image (a current dragging debris) maps perfectly onto the emotional one (charisma dragging hearts). *Mitreißend* means gripping, riveting — a force you cannot resist joining.

Literal mnemonic: "tear along." "The floodwaters tore everything along with them." "Her speech tore the audience along."

### UM- ("around, over, re-")

**Umreißen** has two lives depending on separability:

**(sep.) Umreißen:** to tear over, to knock over. The prefix tips the tearing force sideways and downward. You *reißen* a pole *um* — you knock it down, topple it. A clumsy gesture *reißt* a glass *um*. The image is of violent contact that sends something from upright to flat.

**(insep.) Umreißen:** to outline, to sketch the contours of. Here *um-* means "around" — you trace a line around a subject, delineating its shape without filling it in. To *umreißen* a plan is to sketch its outlines, to give its broad strokes. The metaphor is of drawing a torn, rapid line around something to mark its boundaries.

Literal mnemonic: (sep.) "tear over" — knocked down; (insep.) "tear around" — traced in outline. The separable form destroys; the inseparable form describes.

### VER- (transformation, ruin, intensification)

**Verreißen:** to tear apart (in judgment), to ruin by tearing. *Ver-* pushes the tearing into destructive transformation. A critic *verreißt* a film — tears it to pieces in a review, savages it, pans it. The work emerges shredded. A craftsman *verreißt* a piece — botches it, ruins it through bad handling. In both cases the *ver-* signals that the tearing has gone wrong or gone too far: what was whole is now ruined beyond repair.

Literal mnemonic: "tear wrong" or "tear to ruin." English "tear apart" in "the critics tore the film apart" is the exact equivalent.

### WEG- ("away, gone")

**Wegreißen:** to tear away, to rip away. The simplest directional prefix — it removes by force. A bandage is *weggerissen*. A child is *weggerissen* from a parent's hand. Wind *reißt* tiles *weg* from a roof. The thing was there; now it has been torn away and is gone.

Literal mnemonic: "tear away." "The storm tore the roof away."

### ZER- ("apart, to pieces, asunder")

**Zerreißen:** to tear to pieces, to tear asunder. The most destructive prefix in German applies its full scattering force to *reißen*. Paper is *zerrissen* — shredded. Clothing is *zerrissen* — ripped to tatters. A heart is *zerrissen* — torn apart by grief. The prefix does not just rip; it multiplies the rip until nothing whole remains. *Zerrissen* as an adjective means torn, conflicted — a person pulled apart by opposing forces.

Literal mnemonic: "tear asunder." "She tore the letter to pieces" = *Sie hat den Brief zerrissen.*

### ZURÜCK- ("back, backward")

**Zurückreißen:** to tear back, to jerk back. The prefix reverses the direction — the violent force pulls something backward. A hand is *zurückgerissen* from a hot surface. A horse is *zurückgerissen* by the reins. The steering wheel is *zurückgerissen* to avoid a crash. Always abrupt, always a sudden yank in the reverse direction.

Literal mnemonic: "tear back." "He jerked the wheel back" = *Er hat das Steuer zurückgerissen.*

### ZUSAMMEN- ("together")

**Zusammenreißen:** to tear together. Paradoxically, this compound means the opposite of tearing apart — it means pulling the scattered pieces back into one. Almost always reflexive: *sich zusammenreißen* means to pull yourself together, to get a grip, to gather your dispersed composure into a single controlled whole. The image is of someone whose nerves or emotions are flying apart who grabs them and tears them back together by force of will.

Literal mnemonic: "tear together." English "pull yourself together" uses the same metaphor — reassembling what was coming apart.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays violent; the prefixes steer the violence.** *Reißen* never loses its sense of sudden, forceful rupture. But the prefixes aim that force in every conceivable direction. *Abreißen* tears off. *Aufreißen* tears open. *Durchreißen* tears through. *Zerreißen* tears apart. The tearing gesture remains constant; only the trajectory changes.

**Physical tearing maps reliably onto emotional force.** German extends the ripping metaphor further than English typically does. *Hinreißen* (tear toward = enrapture), *mitreißen* (tear along = captivate), *zusammenreißen* (tear together = regain composure) — these treat emotions as material that can be ripped, pulled, and reassembled. Once you accept that feelings are fabric in German, these compounds feel natural.

**Separable prefixes preserve the spatial image.** *Ab, an, auf, aus, durch, ein, heraus, hin, los, mit, um (sep.), weg, zer, zurück, zusammen* — all of these can be pictured. You can see the direction of the tear. Even the most figurative uses (*einreißen* = become established; *ausreißen* = flee) still echo the physical motion if you look for it.

**Inseparable prefixes transform the meaning more radically.** *Ent-* turns tearing into snatching. *Ver-* turns tearing into ruining or savaging. Inseparable *um-* turns tearing into outlining — the most surprising leap in the family, where violent rupture becomes careful delineation. These must be learned as units, though the spatial logic is still visible beneath the surface.

**English sometimes shares the same construction.** "Tear off" = *abreißen*. "Tear open" = *aufreißen*. "Tear out" = *ausreißen*. "Tear apart" = *zerreißen*. "Tear away" = *wegreißen*. Where English and German align, use the parallel as an anchor. Where they diverge — *hinreißen* (enrapture), *verreißen* (pan a film), *einreißen* (take hold) — notice that German simply extends the tearing metaphor into territory where English abandons it for a different verb entirely.
