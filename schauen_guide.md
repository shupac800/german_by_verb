# SCHAUEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Schauen** means to look, to gaze, to watch, to see. It implies directed visual attention — turning your eyes toward something and taking it in. Hold this image in mind: eyes open, face turned, attention fixed on a point. Nearly every prefix below redirects that gaze — aiming it upward, outward, through, around, or beneath the surface. Where *sehen* is neutral perception (you see whether you intend to or not), *schauen* carries volition: you choose where to look.

## The Prefixes

### AN- ("on, onto, at")

**Anschauen:** to look at, to look onto. The prefix directs the gaze at a target. You *schaust* something *an* — you fix your eyes on it, you give it your visual attention. A painting is *angeschaut* — looked at, regarded, observed. The prefix pins the gaze to its object: not a glance in passing but a deliberate looking-at.

Literal mnemonic: "look at." English works identically — "look at the painting" and *schau das Bild an* share the same structure.

### AUF- ("up, upward")

**Aufschauen:** to look up, to raise one's eyes. The prefix lifts the gaze. You *schaust auf* — you raise your eyes from the page, from the ground, from whatever held them low. The motion is purely vertical: eyes go up.

Literal mnemonic: "look up." "She looked up from her book" = *Sie schaute auf.*

### AUS- ("out, outward")

**Ausschauen:** to look out. Two senses branch from one gesture: (1) you direct your gaze outward, scanning the horizon — to look out, to watch out, to keep a lookout. (2) What looks outward to others is how something appears — so *ausschauen* also means to look (a certain way), to appear. A house *schaut gut aus* — it looks good, it presents itself well to the outward eye.

Literal mnemonic: "look out." English splits these senses between "look out" (watch out) and "look" (appear), but the German keeps them under one roof: what you project outward is how you look.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Beschauen:** to look at thoroughly, to inspect, to examine. *Be-* stamps the action fully onto the object — it takes the casual gaze and presses it close. You do not merely look; you *beschaust* — you examine, inspect, contemplate. A customs inspector *beschaut* goods. A contemplative mind *beschaut* a problem. The prefix removes the distance between looker and looked-at.

Mnemonic: think of "be-" as "all over." To *beschauen* is to look something all over, to turn your gaze across every surface of it.

### DURCH- ("through")

**Durchschauen** — this is one of those prefix verbs where separability changes meaning entirely.

*Separable (durch|schauen):* to look through. The gaze passes physically through an opening or transparent medium. You *schaust durch* a window, through a telescope, through a gap in the fence. The prefix traces the trajectory of the gaze: it enters on one side and exits the other.

*Inseparable (durchschauen):* to see through, to understand, to figure out. The gaze no longer passes through glass — it penetrates deception. You *durchschaust* a liar, a scheme, a pretense. The prefix has fused with the stem and gone abstract: instead of looking through a physical barrier, you look through an intellectual one. The thing that was opaque becomes transparent to you.

Literal mnemonic: separable = "look through" (a window); inseparable = "see through" (a trick). English preserves the same split — "I looked through the glass" vs. "I saw through his lies" — but German marks the distinction grammatically via separability.

### EMPOR- ("up, upward, aloft")

**Emporschauen:** to look up, to raise one's eyes. Like *aufschauen* but more elevated in register — *empor* carries a literary, almost reverent quality. You *schaust empor* to the stars, to the mountaintop, to something above you in both space and dignity. Where *aufschauen* simply lifts the eyes, *emporschauen* lifts them with awe or solemnity.

Literal mnemonic: "gaze upward." Think of the difference between "looking up" and "lifting one's eyes" — *emporschauen* is the latter.

### ER- (achievement, attainment)

**Erschauen:** to behold, to glimpse, to perceive. *Er-* signals that the looking reaches its goal — the gaze achieves its object. You do not just look; you *erschaust* — you catch sight of something, you attain the vision. The prefix converts the act of looking into the moment of successfully seeing. It is rare and literary, carrying a tone of revelation: the eyes searched, and they found.

Mnemonic: "behold." The *er-* marks the moment the gaze lands on its target and truly takes it in.

### HERAUS- ("out from within")

**Herausschauen:** to look out, to peek out, to stick out. The prefix pulls the gaze (or the gazer) outward from an interior. A child *schaut* from behind a curtain *heraus* — peeks out. A handkerchief *schaut heraus* from a pocket — it sticks out, it shows itself from within. The direction is inside-to-outside, toward the observer.

Literal mnemonic: "look out (from in here)." The motion is always from enclosed to exposed.

### HIN- ("thither, toward a point away from the speaker")

**Hinschauen:** to look (over there), to look at something, to watch. The prefix sends the gaze away from the speaker and toward a distant point. You *schaust hin* — you direct your eyes to where the action is. It often carries an undertone of willingness or reluctance: *Schau nicht hin!* — don't look (over there)! The prefix gives the gaze a destination.

Literal mnemonic: "look over there." The *hin* points the gaze outward to a specific spot.

### NACH- ("after, following, checking")

**Nachschauen:** to look after, to look up, to check. Three threads from one prefix: (1) you look after someone departing — your gaze follows them as they leave. (2) You look something up — you consult a reference, checking after the fact. (3) You check on something — you look again, to verify. In every case, *nach-* adds the sense of following up: the gaze comes after the event, after the person, after the first attempt.

Literal mnemonic: "look after." English uses the same phrase — "she looked after him as he walked away" and "look it up" both involve a gaze directed at something already in motion or already recorded.

### UM- ("around, about")

**Umschauen:** to look around, to look about. Almost always reflexive — *sich umschauen*. You turn your gaze in a circle, scanning your surroundings. The prefix wraps the gaze around the viewer: not fixed on one point but sweeping the full field. You *schaust dich um* in a new city — you look around, you take in what surrounds you.

Literal mnemonic: "look around." The prefix does exactly what it says — it curves the straight line of the gaze into a circle.

### ZU- ("to, toward, at")

**Zuschauen:** to watch, to look on, to observe. The prefix directs the gaze toward an ongoing event. You *schaust zu* — you watch as something unfolds. A spectator *schaut zu*. The prefix holds the gaze steady on the action: not a glance but sustained attention directed at a process. The emphasis is on witnessing rather than participating.

Literal mnemonic: "look on." English "look on" captures it exactly — to stand by and watch, directing your gaze at what others are doing.

### ÜBER- ("over, above, across")

**Überschauen:** to survey, to overlook, to take in at a glance. The prefix lifts the gaze above and across — you look over the whole scene from a vantage point. A general *überschaut* the battlefield. A manager *überschaut* the project. The gaze sweeps over the top, taking in the entirety rather than the detail. This is the bird's-eye look, the panoramic scan that grasps the whole at once.

Literal mnemonic: "overlook" in its positive sense — not to miss something, but to look over the whole of it. "Survey" captures it best: the gaze passes over and across the full extent.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays sensory; the prefixes go abstract.** *Schauen* never stops meaning "to look." But the prefixes push that gaze beyond the physical. *Durchschauen* (insep.) moves from looking through glass to seeing through deception. *Überschauen* moves from looking over a landscape to comprehending a situation. *Beschauen* moves from looking at a thing to inspecting its inner nature. The eyes' basic gesture — directing attention at something — is projected onto intellectual and social acts. Once you see this, the figurative senses become natural extensions rather than arbitrary new meanings.

**Separable prefixes are spatial and concrete.** *An, auf, aus, hin, heraus, nach, um, zu* — these preserve a physical direction for the gaze. You can picture where the eyes are pointed. *Aufschauen*: eyes go up. *Umschauen*: eyes sweep around. *Hinschauen*: eyes aim at a far point. Even in figurative use, the spatial image remains legible.

**Inseparable prefixes are transformative and opaque.** *Be-, er-, über-* — these fuse with the stem and shift its meaning further from the literal. *Beschauen* is not "look at" but "examine." *Erschauen* is not "look" but "behold." *Überschauen* is not "look over" but "survey." The gaze is transformed by the prefix into something more than ordinary looking.

**Durchschauen is the showpiece.** The separable/inseparable split dramatizes how German uses prefix attachment to mark the leap from concrete to abstract. *Durch|schauen* (separable): the gaze goes through a physical medium — a window, a lens. *Durchschauen* (inseparable): the gaze penetrates an illusion — the prefix has bonded to the verb so tightly that the physical "through" becomes metaphorical "through." When you see the same prefix behave differently based on stress and separability, you are watching German build abstraction from space in real time.

**English sometimes preserves the same logic.** "Look at" = *anschauen*. "Look up" = *aufschauen*. "Look around" = *umschauen*. "Look on" = *zuschauen*. "See through" = *durchschauen* (insep.). Where English and German share the metaphor, use it as a bridge. Where they diverge — particularly with the inseparable prefixes — notice that German extends the spatial metaphor further, using "look" where English switches to entirely different verbs like "examine," "survey," or "behold."
