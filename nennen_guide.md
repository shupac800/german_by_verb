# NENNEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Nennen** means to name, to call, to mention, to cite. It is the act of attaching a word to something — singling it out by giving it a designation. Hold this image in mind: pointing at something and declaring what it is called. The two prefixes below each reshape that act of naming — one intensifies it into formal labeling, the other drives it toward completion in the form of official appointment.

---

## The Prefixes

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Benennen** (insep.): to name thoroughly, to name officially. *Be-* takes the basic act of calling something by a name and stamps it fully onto the object. Where *nennen* might be casual — "they call it X" — *benennen* is deliberate and systematic: to designate, to label, to term. A street is *benannt* after a person — formally named, not just casually referred to. A species is *benannt* — given its official designation. A problem is *benannt* — identified and labeled so it can be addressed. In every case, *be-* pushes *nennen* from the informal toward the authoritative.

Mnemonic: think of "be-" as "all over, thoroughly." To *benennen* is to name something so that the name sticks — to stamp a label onto it. English "denominate" captures a similar escalation from casual naming to formal designation.

### ER- (achievement, completion)

**Ernennen** (insep.): to name to completion, to name into a role. *Er-* signals that an action reaches its decisive endpoint. To *ernennen* someone is not merely to call them something but to confer a position upon them — to appoint, to nominate, to designate for office. The naming becomes an act of transformation: before, the person was unnamed in the role; after, they hold it. A minister is *ernannt* — named into the position conclusively. An ambassador is *ernannt* — the act of naming completes the appointment.

Mnemonic: *er-* = "into being." To *ernennen* is "to name into existence" as an officeholder. English "appoint" carries the same finality — you do not tentatively appoint someone; the naming is the deed.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays everyday; the prefixes formalize.** *Nennen* never stops meaning "to call, to name." But both prefixes elevate that act. *Benennen* pushes naming toward labeling and classification; *ernennen* pushes it toward authority and appointment. The casual gesture of saying someone's name is projected onto institutional and official actions.

**Both prefixes are inseparable — and both are transformative.** Neither *be-* nor *er-* can stand alone as a word. They fuse with *nennen* and shift its meaning away from the literal. This is typical: inseparable prefixes tend to abstract and formalize, while separable ones tend to preserve concrete, spatial meaning. Since *nennen* is itself already abstract (naming is a mental act, not a physical one), its prefixed forms move further into institutional territory rather than into physical metaphor.

**English parallels help.** "Name" in English can do much of what these three verbs do, but German distributes the labor. "They named it X" = *nennen*. "They named it officially" = *benennen*. "They named her to the post" = *ernennen*. Notice that English uses the same verb and relies on context, while German encodes the distinction in the prefix. Once you see *be-* as "thoroughly, formally" and *er-* as "into completion, into a role," the three verbs sort themselves naturally.
