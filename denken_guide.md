# DENKEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Denken** means to think — to reason, to consider, to turn something over in the mind. English "think" is its direct cognate, descended from the same Germanic root. Hold this image in mind: a mind reaching toward an idea, grasping it, weighing it. Nearly every prefix below redirects that mental reaching — outward into invention, backward into memory, downward into careful analysis, or across into a wholly changed perspective.

---

## The Prefixes

### AUS- ("out, outward, to completion")

**Ausdenken:** to think out, to think up. The prefix pulls the thinking outward — from the interior of the mind into the open, where it becomes a concrete idea. You *denkst dir etwas aus* — you think something out of nothing, you devise it, invent it, concoct it. A plan is *ausgedacht* — thought out to completion. A story is *ausgedacht* — fabricated, conjured from the imagination. The prefix insists that the thinking produce something: a scheme, a lie, a new machine.

Literal mnemonic: "think out." English uses the same construction — "I thought up a plan" or "think something out" both capture the outward, productive direction.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Bedenken:** to think upon thoroughly. *Be-* is not a spatial word but a grammatical intensifier — it stamps the act of thinking fully onto an object. To *bedenken* is to think something over carefully, to take it into account, to weigh it with due seriousness. The noun *Bedenken* (misgivings, reservations) captures what happens when you think something over thoroughly — you discover concerns. Where *denken* can be idle or casual, *bedenken* is deliberate: you give the matter the full weight of your attention.

Mnemonic: think of "be-" as "all over." To *bedenken* a decision is to lay your thinking all over it, turning it around, examining every angle before committing.

### DURCH- ("through, from end to end")

**Durchdenken:** to think through. The prefix drives the thinking forward from beginning to end, from premise to conclusion, missing nothing along the way. To *durchdenken* a problem is to reason through it completely — to follow every thread, test every assumption, arrive at the far side with full understanding. This is analytical, systematic thinking. A plan that is *durchdacht* has been thought through: it holds up because no step was skipped.

Literal mnemonic: "think through." English preserves the identical metaphor — "I need to think this through" means exactly what *durchdenken* means.

### ER- (achievement, bringing into being)

**Erdenken:** to think into existence. *Er-* often signals that an action reaches its goal, that something is achieved or brought forth. To *erdenken* is to think something up — not casually, but with the emphasis on the result: the idea now exists. A new method is *erdacht* — conceived, devised, brought into being through thought. Where *ausdenken* stresses the outward process of inventing, *erdenken* stresses the moment of arrival: the idea is achieved.

Mnemonic: "think into being." The *er-* prefix marks completion — what was not yet conceived is now conceived. Think of *erfinden* (to invent), *erschaffen* (to create) — *erdenken* belongs to this family of bringing-forth.

### GE- (fossilized prefix — here creating an archaic, elevated verb)

**Gedenken:** to hold in thought, to commemorate; also, to intend. This is one of the most interesting members of the family, because it carries two distinct meanings from different historical layers. In its primary modern use, *gedenken* means to commemorate — to hold the dead or the past in deliberate, solemn thought. *Wir gedenken der Opfer* — we hold the victims in our collective memory. The *ge-* prefix, once a marker of completeness or collectivity, gives the thinking a ceremonial weight: this is not casual remembering but public, purposeful remembrance.

The second meaning — to intend, to plan to — survives in formal and literary registers: *Ich gedenke abzureisen* — I intend to depart. Here the thinking is forward-directed: you hold a future action in mind. The two senses share a core image: the mind deliberately holding something — the past in remembrance, the future in intention.

Mnemonic: "think of" in the solemn sense. English "to think of the fallen" captures the commemorative meaning. The intentional sense echoes archaic English: "I think to leave tomorrow."

### HINZU- ("in addition, added to")

**Hinzudenken:** to think in addition, to mentally supplement. The prefix adds something to what is already present. You look at an incomplete picture and *denkst* the missing piece *hinzu* — you supply it in your imagination. A text leaves something unsaid, and the reader must *hinzudenken* what the author implies. The verb is modest and practical: it names the mental act of filling in what is absent.

Literal mnemonic: "think on top of." You add a thought to what is already there, supplementing reality with imagination.

### NACH- ("after, following, in pursuit of")

**Nachdenken:** to think after, to think upon with care. This is one of the most common and important members of the family. The prefix *nach-* implies following — the mind follows the subject patiently, turning it over, circling back, pursuing understanding. To *nachdenken* is to reflect, to ponder, to deliberate — not quickly but slowly, with sustained attention. *Darüber muss ich nachdenken* — I need to think about that — is the sentence Germans reach for when a question deserves more than an instant answer.

Literal mnemonic: "think after." The mind goes after the problem and stays with it. English "reflect" captures the depth, but *nachdenken* is warmer and more everyday — it is what you do at the kitchen table, not only in the philosopher's study.

### UM- ("around, over, into a new direction")

**Umdenken:** to think around, to rethink, to change one's way of thinking. The prefix implies a turn — the mind pivots, abandons its old orientation, faces a new direction. This is not merely reconsidering a single question but shifting an entire framework. *Umdenken* is culturally prominent in German public discourse: environmental policy demands *Umdenken*, economic crises force *Umdenken*, social change requires *Umdenken*. The word carries moral weight — it suggests that the old thinking was insufficient and a new mindset is necessary.

Literal mnemonic: "think around" — turn your thinking around to face a different direction. English "rethink" is close but lacks the sense of a fundamental pivot. *Umdenken* is less "think again" and more "think differently."

### VER- (displacement, transformation, error)

**Verdenken:** to think against someone, to hold a thought against them. *Ver-* here carries its adversarial, displacing force. To *verdenken* is to blame someone for a thought or action — to hold it against them. The verb almost always appears in the negative: *Ich kann es ihm nicht verdenken* — I can't blame him, I can't hold it against him. The *ver-* twists the neutral act of thinking into judgment: the thought is directed against the person, displacing sympathy.

Mnemonic: "think amiss of someone." The *ver-* prefix pushes the thinking off-course — from neutral consideration into reproach. But German most often uses this verb to say: I *don't* think ill of them for it.

### ZURÜCK- ("back, backward, in return")

**Zurückdenken:** to think back. The prefix turns the mind's direction from the present toward the past. You *denkst zurück* — you cast your thoughts backward in time, recalling what was. It is gentler than formal remembering: less a summoning of facts and more a drifting back, letting memory surface. *Wenn ich an meine Kindheit zurückdenke* — when I think back to my childhood.

Literal mnemonic: "think back." English uses the identical construction — "I often think back to those days."

### ÜBER- ("over, above, across")

**Überdenken:** to think over, to reconsider. The prefix lifts the mind above the problem for a second pass — you have already thought about it once, and now you return to examine it from above, reassessing, reviewing. To *überdenken* a decision is to look at it again with fresh eyes, testing whether it still holds. The word implies that your first thinking may have been hasty or incomplete, and a more elevated, distanced perspective is needed.

Literal mnemonic: "think over." English "think it over" and "reconsider" both map directly onto *überdenken* — the mind passes over the ground again, this time from higher up.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**There is a gradient of depth and care.** The family maps a spectrum of mental effort. *Denken* is ordinary thinking — quick, casual, undirected. *Bedenken* adds weight: you think carefully, you take into account. *Nachdenken* adds patience: you sit with the problem, reflecting. *Durchdenken* adds rigor: you think systematically from start to finish. Learning to feel this gradient — from idle thought to disciplined analysis — is one of the rewards of this verb family.

**Invention has two flavors.** Both *ausdenken* and *erdenken* mean to think something up, but they differ in emphasis. *Ausdenken* stresses the process (thinking outward, devising), while *erdenken* stresses the result (the idea is achieved, brought into existence). A child *denkt sich eine Geschichte aus* (makes up a story, playfully); an engineer *erdenkt* a new system (conceives it, with the weight of creation).

**Gedenken stands apart.** Its dual meaning — commemorate and intend — makes it unusual even for German speakers. The commemorative sense is public and solemn; the intentional sense is literary and slightly archaic. What unites them is the idea of deliberate, sustained holding-in-mind: whether you hold the past or the future, you do so with purpose.

**Umdenken carries cultural weight.** Where English might say "we need to rethink this," German reaches for *umdenken* to signal something larger — not revisiting a single decision but pivoting an entire worldview. It appears in political speeches, environmental campaigns, and social commentary. The word implies that mere reconsideration (*überdenken*) is not enough; a fundamental change of direction is required.

**English cognates help more than you might expect.** "Think out" = *ausdenken*. "Think through" = *durchdenken*. "Think over" = *überdenken*. "Think back" = *zurückdenken*. Where English and German share the metaphor, use it as an anchor. Where they diverge — *verdenken* (to blame), *gedenken* (to commemorate) — the divergence itself teaches you how German extends the act of thinking into territories English covers with entirely different verbs.
