# FEHLEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Fehlen** means to be missing, to be absent, to lack, to be short of. In older and regional usage it also carried the sense of erring, making a mistake — the noun *Fehler* (mistake, error, fault) preserves this meaning directly. The word is related to English "fail," both descending from Latin *fallere* (to deceive, to be wanting). Hold this double image in mind: something that is not where it should be, and the wrongness that results from that absence. A missing piece is a flaw; to be absent is to fall short; to err is to miss the mark.

This family is unusual among German prefix verb families. Where most stems hold steady while prefixes redirect them in transparent ways, fehlen's prefixed forms — befehlen, empfehlen, verfehlen — have drifted so far from the base verb that native speakers no longer feel the connection. The historical links are real but deeply buried. This makes the family more a study in etymology than in productive prefix logic.

## The Prefixes

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Befehlen:** to command, to order, to give orders, to instruct, to charge, to enjoin. This is one of the most opaque prefix formations in German. How does *be-* + *fehlen* (to be missing) produce "to command"? The connection is historical and no longer transparent. In Middle High German, *bevelhen* meant to entrust, to hand over for safekeeping — to commit something into someone's care. The underlying logic may have been: to address what is lacking, to fill the gap by assigning responsibility. Over centuries, "entrusting" hardened into "ordering." The person entrusted with a task became the person commanded to do it. The sense of absence or need that once motivated the entrusting has vanished entirely from the modern word.

Mnemonic: there is no clean mnemonic from the parts. Befehlen must be learned as its own word. But knowing it shares a root with fehlen can help you remember it belongs to this family — and the old sense of "entrusting" lingers in the related noun *Befehl* (command, order) and the military phrase *zu Befehl* (at your command — originally, "at your trust").

### EMP- (= ENT- before f: "away from, out of, forth")

**Empfehlen:** to recommend, to advise, to suggest, to commend, to counsel. The prefix *emp-* is a phonetic variant of *ent-* that appears before *f* — the same shift is visible in *empfinden* (ent- + finden) and *empfangen* (ent- + fangen). Like befehlen, this compound is etymologically real but semantically opaque. The older meaning was to entrust, to commit into someone's hands — to send forth with a commendation. When you *empfehlen* a restaurant, you are, in the buried logic of the word, entrusting someone to it — committing them to its care. The reflexive *sich empfehlen* (to take one's leave) preserves the old meaning most clearly: you commend yourself to someone's keeping as you depart.

Mnemonic: again, no transparent path from the parts. But the old sense of "commending into someone's care" bridges the gap between fehlen's world of absence and empfehlen's world of recommendation. To recommend is to say: "this will not fail you; I entrust you to it."

### VER- (transformation, displacement, error, intensification)

**Verfehlen:** to miss, to fail to hit, to fail to achieve. This is the most transparent compound in the family — the one where the prefix and the stem still visibly cooperate. *Ver-* in its error sense means "wrongly, astray" (as in *verlegen* = to misplace, *verlaufen* = to lose one's way). Combined with fehlen in its old sense of "to miss the mark, to err," the result is emphatic: to miss entirely, to go wrong completely. You *verfehlen* a target — the arrow goes astray. You *verfehlen* the entrance — you walk right past it. You *verfehlen* your calling — you fail to reach what you were meant for. The prefix intensifies the missing: not merely absent, but missed through misdirection.

Literal mnemonic: "miss wrongly" or "fail away." The ver- amplifies fehlen's sense of absence into active failure. Where fehlen says "it's not here," verfehlen says "you missed it."

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**This family is largely opaque — and that is itself the lesson.** Most prefix guides show you how prefixes predictably reshape a stem. Fehlen's family does the opposite: it shows what happens when centuries of semantic drift obscure the connections. Befehlen and empfehlen are historically related to fehlen, but no modern speaker derives their meanings from the stem. These must be learned as independent vocabulary items. Recognizing the family helps with memorization — these words share conjugation patterns and a common ancestor — but it will not help you deduce meanings from parts.

**The old meaning of fehlen was broader than the modern one.** Today fehlen primarily means "to be missing." But the older senses — to err, to fail, to fall short — survive in the derivatives. *Fehler* (mistake) and *verfehlen* (to miss) carry the "error" meaning. *Befehlen* and *empfehlen* carry the old "entrusting" meaning that grew from addressing what was lacking. The modern stem has narrowed; the prefixed forms preserve the older breadth.

**Emp- is ent- in disguise.** Whenever *ent-* meets an *f*, it becomes *emp-*: *empfehlen*, *empfinden*, *empfangen*. This is a pure phonetic rule, not a separate prefix. Once you recognize it, these words become easier to file mentally — they belong with the *ent-* family, not with some mysterious *emp-* category.

**English "fail" is a distant cousin.** Both fehlen and "fail" trace back to Latin *fallere*. This shared ancestry means the conceptual territory overlaps more than you might expect: failing, falling short, being found wanting, missing the mark. When you encounter fehlen and its compounds, the English word "fail" — with all its shadings of absence, error, and shortcoming — is a surprisingly reliable anchor.
