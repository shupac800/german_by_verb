# BEGINNEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Beginnen** means to begin, to start, to commence. It implies the deliberate initiation of an action or process — setting something into motion. Hold this image in mind: the moment a thing that was not yet happening starts to happen. Unlike many German base verbs, *beginnen* already contains the inseparable prefix *be-*, fused to an old Germanic root *-ginnen* (which no longer exists as a standalone verb). This matters, as we will see below.

---

## The Prefixes

### WIEDER- ("again, anew, once more")

**Wiederbeginnen:** to begin again, to restart, to recommence. The prefix adds repetition — something that had begun, then stopped, now begins a second time. Classes *beginnen wieder* after a holiday. Negotiations *beginnen wieder* after a breakdown. The image is of a process returning to its starting point and launching forward once more.

Literal mnemonic: "begin again." English uses the same construction freely — "we begin again on Monday." The German simply fuses "again" and "begin" into a single separable verb: *Wir beginnen am Montag wieder.*

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**BEGINNEN already carries an inseparable prefix.** The *be-* in *beginnen* is the same *be-* that appears in *belegen*, *bekommen*, *bestehen*, and hundreds of other verbs. It was once a productive prefix applied to the root *-ginnen*, but that root died out centuries ago, leaving *beginnen* as an opaque unit — a prefix verb whose stem no longer has independent life. Most German speakers do not feel *be-* as a separable component; they experience *beginnen* as a simple, indivisible word meaning "to start."

**Double-prefixing is rare in German.** German strongly resists stacking one prefix on top of another. Since *beginnen* already has the inseparable prefix *be-*, the language has almost no room to attach further prefixes. You will not find *abbeginnen*, *aufbeginnen*, or *verbeginnen* — such forms would violate the structural principle that a verb takes one prefix, not two. This is the same reason you do not see *verbelegen* or *anbedeulen*: once the inseparable prefix slot is filled, the verb is essentially closed to further prefixation.

**WIEDER- is the exception that proves the rule.** *Wieder-* can attach to almost any verb regardless of its existing structure, because it functions more like an independent adverb ("again") than like a true prefix. It does not compete for the same structural slot that *be-*, *ver-*, or *er-* occupy. This is why *wiederbeginnen* is possible while other prefix combinations are not. Even so, many speakers simply say *wieder beginnen* as two separate words rather than fusing them into one compound verb.

**Where English has a rich family, German has a lone verb.** English speakers are accustomed to a constellation of prefix forms around "begin" — but these come from Latin and French rather than from Germanic word-building: "commence," "recommence," "initiate," "inaugurate." German achieves these nuances through different verb stems entirely (*anfangen*, *einleiten*, *eroffnen*) rather than by prefixing *beginnen*. The lesson: not every German verb is a prefix playground. Some verbs, especially those that already contain an inseparable prefix, remain largely as they are — and the work of variation is done by synonyms rather than by morphology.
