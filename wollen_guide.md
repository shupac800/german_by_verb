# WOLLEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Wollen** means to want, to wish, to desire, to intend, to be willing. It is one of German's six modal verbs — words that modify other verbs to express ability, permission, obligation, or desire. *Wollen* expresses volition: the will to do something. Hold this image: a mind reaching forward toward something it desires. The English cognate is "will" (as in "I will do it," originally meaning "I want to do it"), and the connection is direct — both come from the same Germanic root expressing desire and intention.

---

## The Prefixes

### LOS- ("loose, free, released, off")

**Loswollen:** to want to get loose, to want to leave. The prefix adds the direction of departure to the wanting. You don't just *wollen* — you *wollen los*, you want to be off, to get going, to break free. *Ich will los* = I want to leave, I want to get started, I want to be on my way. The image is of someone straining toward the door, ready to be released into motion.

Literal mnemonic: "want loose" or "want off." English captures the same feeling in "I want out" or "I want to get going" — the desire to be released from the current situation into forward motion.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**Modal verbs resist prefixing.** *Wollen* has almost no prefixed forms because modal verbs describe internal states (wanting, being able, being allowed) rather than external actions. Prefixes that imply physical direction have little to attach to — you cannot "wind up wanting" or "lay down wanting" in any natural way. Only *los-*, which expresses the desire to depart, finds a natural fit, because departure is the one spatial direction that pure wanting can take.

**Loswollen follows a productive pattern.** German freely attaches *los-* to verbs of wanting and readiness: *loswollen* (want to go), *loskönnen* (be able to get away), *losmüssen* (have to get going). The *los-* prefix creates a family of "ready to depart" expressions that English handles with multi-word phrases.

**Wollen vs. English "will."** Modern English "will" has drifted from desire to simple future tense ("I will go tomorrow" no longer implies wanting). German *wollen* retains the original volitional force — it always means wanting, never mere futurity. This is worth remembering: every time you encounter *wollen*, there is active desire behind it, not just a prediction about the future.
