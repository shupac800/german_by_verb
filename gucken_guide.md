# GUCKEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Gucken** means to look, to peek, to watch. It is a colloquial and informal synonym of *schauen* and *sehen*, more common in northern Germany than in the south. Where *sehen* is neutral and *blicken* is literary, *gucken* is everyday and casual — the verb you use at the kitchen table, not in the lecture hall. Hold this image in mind: eyes turning toward something with casual curiosity. The prefixes below redirect that gaze — aiming it at a target, through a barrier, away from something uncomfortable, or onto someone else's work.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abgucken:** to copy, to crib — literally "to look off." The prefix pulls the gaze away from your own work and onto someone else's. A student *guckt ab* — they peek at a neighbor's paper and copy the answer. The spatial image is precise: your eyes leave your own page ("off") and land on theirs. English has no single-word equivalent, but "to crib" or "to copy by looking" captures it. The construction "to look off someone" — looking off their work to take what you see — makes the logic transparent.

Literal mnemonic: "look off." Your eyes come *off* your own paper and land on someone else's.

### AN- ("on, onto, up against")

**Angucken:** to look at, to watch, to eye. The prefix directs the gaze onto a specific target. You *guckst* someone *an* — you look at them, you direct your eyes onto them. A picture is *angeguckt* — examined, looked upon. This is the most common and straightforward compound: the casual everyday verb for "to look at something."

Literal mnemonic: "look on." Your gaze lands on the object and stays there.

### AUS- ("out, outward, spread")

**Ausgucken:** to look out for, to scan for, to seek. The prefix sends the gaze outward, searching. You *guckst* nach etwas *aus* — you look out across a space, scanning for something specific. A sailor *guckt aus* from the crow's nest. A shopper *guckt sich etwas aus* — they look around and pick something out. The gesture is of eyes sweeping outward across a field of possibilities.

Literal mnemonic: "look out." English uses the same construction — "look out for a good restaurant" means to scan outward until you spot one.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Begucken:** to look at, to have a look at, to inspect. The inseparable *be-* takes the casual glance and stamps it fully onto an object, making the looking thorough and direct. You *beguckst* a new apartment — you look it over, you inspect it. The prefix converts the intransitive gaze into a transitive examination: the object receives the full weight of the looking.

Mnemonic: think of *be-* as "all over." To *begucken* something is to look it all over, to give it a proper once-over.

### DURCH- ("through")

**Durchgucken:** to look through, to peep through, to peer through. The prefix sends the gaze through a barrier — a window, a keyhole, a crack. You *guckst durch* the curtains. You *guckst durch* a telescope. The eyes penetrate a surface and reach the other side. The image is always of vision passing through an obstacle to see what lies beyond.

Literal mnemonic: "look through." English works identically — "look through the window," "peer through the fog."

### HERAUS- ("out from within")

**Herausgucken:** to look out, to peek out, to appear, to show. The prefix pulls the gaze — or the subject — outward from an interior. A child *guckt* from behind a curtain *heraus* — they peek out from hiding. A shirt collar *guckt heraus* from under a sweater — it shows, it sticks out, it is visible. The direction is always from inside to outside, toward the speaker.

Literal mnemonic: "look out (from in here)." Something that *herausguckt* is peeking out from where it was hidden.

### HIN- ("thither, toward a point away from the speaker")

**Hingucken:** to look, to glance, to pay attention. The prefix directs the gaze toward a specific point away from the speaker. *Guck mal hin!* — look over there, direct your eyes to that spot. To *nicht hingucken* is to not pay attention, to not bother looking. The prefix adds intentionality: this is not a passive glance but a deliberate directing of the eyes toward something.

Literal mnemonic: "look there." The gaze is sent out to a definite point: look at that, pay attention to it.

### HINWEG- ("away over, past")

**Hinweggucken:** to overlook, to look past, to ignore, to disregard — literally "to look away over." The prefix is a compound direction: *hin* (toward and away) plus *weg* (away, gone). The gaze is directed up and over an obstacle, passing beyond it without engaging. You *guckst* over a flaw *hinweg* — you see it but choose to let your eyes pass beyond it, refusing to fix on it. The spatial image is of eyes deliberately sailing over something as if it were not there.

Literal mnemonic: "look away over." English "to look past" or "to overlook" captures the same motion — the eyes travel over the thing without stopping.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**Gucken mirrors blicken almost exactly.** Both are vision verbs, and the prefixes redirect the gaze the same way in both families. *Angucken* and *anblicken* both mean "to look at." *Hingucken* and *hinblicken* both mean "to look toward." *Hinweggucken* and *hinwegblicken* both mean "to look past." The difference is register: *blicken* is literary and elevated, *gucken* is colloquial and informal. If you know one family, you essentially know the other — the prefixes do the same spatial work regardless of which stem they attach to.

**The prefix family is small but clean.** Compared to a verb like *legen* with its twenty-plus prefix compounds, *gucken* has only eight. This is typical of colloquial verbs: they carry the most common, most physically transparent prefixes and leave the rarer or more abstract ones to their formal counterparts. You will find *versehen* and *übersehen*, but not *vergucken* or *übergucken* in standard use.

**Abgucken stands out.** Most vision-verb prefixes redirect the gaze spatially — toward, through, out, past. But *abgucken* redirects it socially: the eyes come *off* your own work and land on someone else's, with the intent to copy. It is the schoolroom verb, the one teachers scold children for. The "off" is not about removing vision but about diverting it — pulling it away from where it should be and placing it where it should not.

**Hinweggucken is the most layered.** A double prefix (*hin-* + *weg-*) producing a deliberate, intentional act of non-seeing. You do not fail to see — you choose to let your gaze pass over. English "overlook" works the same way: the eyes go *over* and *past*, and the thing beneath is left unexamined. The word implies agency — this is not blindness but chosen disregard.
