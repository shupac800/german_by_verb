# KÖNNEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Können** means to be able to, to can, to know how to. It is a modal verb — it expresses capacity, permission, or skill rather than a concrete physical action. Hold this in mind: *können* is the raw idea of ability, of having the power or the means. The prefixed forms below do not reshape a physical gesture the way spatial stems do. Instead, they attach a direction to the ability itself — specifying *where* or *through what* one is able to go.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from, down")

**Abkönnen:** to be able to take it, to stand, to endure. The prefix *ab-* pulls the action toward tolerating or absorbing something that comes at you. When you say someone *kann etwas ab*, you mean they can handle it — they have the ability to take a blow, a drink, a piece of criticism without going down. The "off" in *ab-* suggests deflection: the capacity to shrug something off.

Literal mnemonic: "can off" — you can brush it off, you can take it. English uses "can take it" or "can handle it" for the same idea; German routes it through the modal verb itself.

Examples: *Er kann nichts ab* — he can't take anything, he can't handle criticism. *Sie kann einiges ab* — she can endure quite a lot.

### DURCH- ("through")

**Durchkönnen:** to be able to get through. The prefix supplies the obstacle and the path: something stands between you and the other side, and *durchkönnen* says you have the ability to pass through it. This is a colloquial, elliptical form — the verb of motion (*gehen*, *kommen*, *fahren*) is dropped, and only the modal with its direction remains: "can through."

Literal mnemonic: "can through." Where English says "can get through," German compresses the whole phrase into prefix plus modal. The gate is open — *wir können durch* — we can get through.

Examples: *Die Tür ist offen, wir können durch* — the door is open, we can get through. *Der Weg war versperrt; niemand konnte durch* — the path was blocked; nobody could get through.

### HERAUS- ("out from within")

**Herauskönnen:** to be able to get out. The prefix points outward from an enclosed space. Like *durchkönnen*, this is an elliptical construction — the verb of motion is implied and the modal carries the directional prefix alone. *Herauskönnen* says you have the ability to exit, to emerge, to get out from inside something.

Literal mnemonic: "can out (from in here)." English would say "can get out" — German folds the direction into the modal. *Ich kann hier nicht heraus* — I can't get out of here.

Examples: *Das Tier konnte nicht mehr aus dem Käfig heraus* — the animal couldn't get out of the cage anymore. *Wir können erst heraus, wenn der Regen aufhört* — we can only get out once the rain stops.

## Patterns Worth Noting

**Modal verbs absorb direction, not action.** Unlike concrete stems like *legen* (to lay), where prefixes redirect a physical gesture, *können* is already abstract. Its prefixed forms do not transform what the ability looks like — they specify where the ability leads. The result is a set of compressed, colloquial expressions where the prefix carries the entire spatial meaning and the modal supplies the capacity.

**The verb of motion is implied.** All three prefixed forms are elliptical. Full forms would be *durchgehen können*, *herauskommen können*, *abkönnen* (which has lexicalized more fully). In casual spoken German, the motion verb drops out and the modal absorbs the prefix. This is a distinctly German pattern with no direct parallel in English — English always needs the motion verb ("can get through," "can get out").

**Separable and colloquial.** All three forms are separable and belong to informal spoken registers. You will hear *Ich kann da nicht durch* far more often than you will read it. This marks them as living, everyday formations — the language compressing what it says most often into the shortest possible shape.

**The family is small because *können* is abstract.** Physical stems like *legen*, *stehen*, or *gehen* generate dozens of prefix compounds because their concrete actions can be redirected in many ways. A modal verb offers less raw material for prefixation. The few forms that do exist follow a clear pattern: spatial prefix plus implied motion, filtered through the idea of ability.
