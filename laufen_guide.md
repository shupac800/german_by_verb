# LAUFEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Laufen** means to run, to walk, to function. It implies continuous forward motion — feet carrying a body, a machine turning, a process ticking along. Hold this image in mind: something in motion that keeps going. Nearly every prefix below redirects, limits, or transforms that basic momentum — giving it a destination, a terminus, a reversal, or a figurative second life.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from, down")

**Ablaufen:** to run off, to run down. Picture a clock whose spring unwinds until it stops — time runs off and is gone. A contract *läuft ab* — its remaining time runs away until none is left, so it expires. Shoes *laufen sich ab* — the soles run down through wear. An event *läuft ab* — it runs its course from start to finish, it proceeds. In every case the prefix drains the motion: something runs until it runs out.

Literal mnemonic: "run off." English "time ran out" captures the same spatial logic — the resource drains away.

### AN- ("on, onto, up to")

**Anlaufen:** to run on, to run up to. The prefix pushes the motion toward a starting point or a surface. A machine *läuft an* — it starts up, begins running. A mirror *läuft an* — moisture runs onto the glass and it fogs up, tarnishes. A face *läuft rot an* — blood runs up to the surface and the person flushes. The common thread is approach and contact: motion arriving at something and making an impression.

Literal mnemonic: "run on" or "run up." An engine "runs on" (starts); a surface "fogs on" (moisture gathers onto it).

### AUF- ("up, upon, onto")

**Auflaufen:** to run up onto. The prefix lifts the motion onto something. A ship *läuft auf* a sandbar — it runs up onto the ground and is stuck. Costs *laufen auf* — they run up, they mount, they accumulate. The image is always of motion that hits an obstacle or a surface and piles up there.

Literal mnemonic: "run up on." English "run aground" is the nautical sense; "costs run up" is the financial one.

### AUS- ("out, outward, to the end")

**Auslaufen:** to run out. Liquid *läuft aus* a container — it leaks, it drains away. A ship *läuft aus* the harbor — it runs out to sea, it sets sail. A product line *läuft aus* — it is phased out, it runs to its end. The prefix sends the motion outward or carries it to completion. The nautical sense is especially vivid: the ship runs out from the safety of port into open water.

Literal mnemonic: "run out." English uses this identically — a lease "runs out," a ship "runs out to sea," a tank "runs out."

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Belaufen:** to run upon thoroughly. *Be-* transforms *laufen* into a transitive verb that stamps the running onto a total. *Sich belaufen auf* means to amount to, to add up to — the running arrives at a sum. The image is of many small movements accumulating until they reach a number: the bill runs up to a total.

Mnemonic: think of "be-" as "all the way up to." The costs have run all the way up to €500.

### DURCH- ("through")

**Durchlaufen** (sep.): to run through. You run through a space from one side to the other. Water *läuft durch* a filter. A person *läuft durch* a park.

**Durchlaufen** (insep.): to traverse, to go through entirely. The inseparable form emphasizes completion — you have gone through the whole thing. A student *durchläuft* a training program — traverses it from beginning to end. The separable form is physical passage; the inseparable form is thorough experience.

Literal mnemonic: "run through." English "run through" works for both — "run through the park" (physical) vs. "run through the program" (complete it).

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Einlaufen:** to run in. The prefix directs the motion inward. A ship *läuft ein* — it runs into the harbor, it arrives. Fabric *läuft ein* — it runs inward on itself, it shrinks. Both images involve contraction toward a center: the ship contracts its distance to port; the cloth contracts its dimensions.

Literal mnemonic: "run in." English "the ship runs in" (arrives at port) and "the shirt ran" (shrank in the wash) echo the same senses.

### ENT- ("away from, escaped from")

**Entlaufen:** to run away from. The prefix separates the runner from the starting point. A dog *entläuft* its owner — it escapes, it flees. The prefix adds the note of separation that plain *laufen* lacks: not just running, but running away from someone's control.

Literal mnemonic: "run from." Think of "escape" (from Latin *ex-* + *cappare*) — the motion is out and away.

### FEST- ("firm, fixed, stuck")

**Festlaufen:** to run firm, to run stuck. The motion meets an immovable resistance and halts. A ship *läuft sich fest* — it runs aground and is fixed in place. Negotiations *laufen sich fest* — they stall, stuck on an obstacle. The prefix arrests the motion: what was running freely is now locked in place.

Literal mnemonic: "run fast" (in the old English sense of "fast" = fixed). The ship is "run fast" against the bottom.

### FORT- ("away, onward, gone")

**Fortlaufen:** to run away, to run forth. The prefix pushes the runner away from the current position with no specific destination. A child *läuft fort* — it runs off, flees. Straightforward: the motion is simply directed away.

Literal mnemonic: "run forth." "The child ran forth from the yard and was gone."

### HERAUS- ("out from within")

**Herauslaufen:** to run out from inside. The motion starts inside an enclosure and emerges outward, toward the speaker. Children *laufen heraus* from a building — they come running out. Transparent and literal.

Literal mnemonic: "run out (from in here)."

### HER- ("hither, toward the speaker")

**Herlaufen:** to run here, to come running. The prefix pulls the motion toward the speaker's position. Someone *läuft her* — they arrive on foot, running toward you. The emphasis is on direction: the motion ends where the speaker stands.

Literal mnemonic: "run here." "Come running" captures it.

### HIN- ("thither, away from the speaker, toward a point")

**Hinlaufen:** to run there, to go there on foot. The prefix pushes the motion away from the speaker toward a destination. You *laufen hin* — you head to a place. The counterpart of *herlaufen*: motion directed outward to a goal.

Literal mnemonic: "run thither." "I ran over there."

### NACH- ("after, following")

**Nachlaufen:** to run after. The prefix puts the runner behind someone else, in pursuit. You *laufen* someone *nach* — you chase after them, you pursue them. The image is always of a follower trailing a leader.

Literal mnemonic: "run after." English works identically — "to run after someone."

### UM- ("around, over, down")

**Umlaufen** (sep.): to run over, to knock down. The separable form means to collide and topple — you run into someone and bowl them over. The prefix implies a change of orientation: what was upright is now down.

**Umlaufen** (insep.): to run around, to circulate. The inseparable form means to orbit, to revolve — the motion goes around and comes back to where it started. Blood *umläuft* the body. A rumor *umläuft* the office. Planets *umlaufen* the sun.

Literal mnemonic: "run around" (insep.) vs. "run over" (sep.). Circulation vs. collision.

### UNTER- ("under, beneath")

**Unterlaufen:** to run under. Errors *unterlaufen* someone — they slip in beneath one's awareness, they occur unnoticed. The image is of something that runs under the barrier of attention: the mistake creeps below your guard and surfaces only after the fact. To undermine or circumvent a rule is the same gesture — running beneath the obstacle rather than confronting it.

Literal mnemonic: "run under." Think of errors that "slip under the radar" — they *unterlaufen* you. English "undermine" uses the same spatial logic: digging under the foundation.

### VER- (transformation, displacement, scattering)

**Verlaufen:** to run astray, to run into transformation. *Ver-* pushes the motion off course or toward dissolution. An event *verläuft* well — it runs its altered, final course, it proceeds, it turns out a certain way. Reflexively, *sich verlaufen* means to lose one's way — to run wrongly, to take the wrong path. Colors *verlaufen* — they run and bleed into each other, dispersing. A crowd *verläuft sich* — it scatters, it dissipates.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "astray" or "into change." You *verlaufen* yourself = run off the right path. A crowd *verläuft* = dissolves away. An event *verläuft* = runs its transformed course.

### VOR- ("before, forward, in front of")

**Vorlaufen:** to run ahead, to run in front. The prefix sends the runner forward, out in advance of others. A child *läuft vor* — it runs ahead of the group.

Literal mnemonic: "run fore." Think of a forerunner — one who runs before.

### WEG- ("away, gone")

**Weglaufen:** to run away. The most direct escape verb in the family. Something *läuft weg* — it runs away and is gone. A dog, a child, a prisoner — whatever was here has departed at speed.

Literal mnemonic: "run away." No metaphor needed — English and German match exactly.

### ZU- ("to, toward, closed")

**Zulaufen:** to run toward. Two images: (1) lines *laufen zu* a point — they converge, they taper. (2) A stray cat *läuft jemandem zu* — it runs toward someone and stays, it wanders in and adopts a new home. In both cases the prefix directs the motion toward a destination and lets it arrive.

Literal mnemonic: "run to." Converging lines "run to a point"; a stray "runs to you."

### ZURÜCK- ("back, backward, in return")

**Zurücklaufen:** to run back. The prefix reverses the motion. You *laufen zurück* — you return on foot to where you started. Literal, transparent, and common.

Literal mnemonic: "run back." English works identically.

### ZUSAMMEN- ("together")

**Zusammenlaufen:** to run together. Separate streams *laufen zusammen* — they converge, they flow into one. People *laufen zusammen* — they gather, they assemble in one place. The prefix bundles multiple motions into a single point of meeting.

Literal mnemonic: "run together." "The rivers run together at the confluence."

### ÜBER- ("over, above, across")

**Überlaufen** (sep.): to run over, to overflow. A pot *läuft über* — the liquid runs over the rim. A bathtub *läuft über* — it overflows. The separable prefix sends the motion up and over a boundary. *Es überläuft mich* — a shiver runs over me.

**Überlaufen** (insep.): to run over to the other side. A soldier *überläuft* to the enemy — he defects, he deserts. The inseparable prefix transforms the running into a crossing of allegiance: the motion goes over a line that was not meant to be crossed.

**Überlaufen** (adj.): overrun, overcrowded. A tourist spot is *überlaufen* — too many people have run over it, flooded into it, and now it is packed beyond comfort.

Literal mnemonic: "run over." A pot "runs over" (overflow). A traitor "runs over" to the other side (defection). A beach is "overrun" (overcrowded). Three meanings, one spatial image: motion that crosses a boundary.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays physical; the prefixes go abstract.** *Laufen* never stops meaning "to move forward on foot" (or "to keep going"). But the prefixes redirect that motion into metaphor. *Ablaufen* (run off) becomes "to expire." *Unterlaufen* (run under) becomes "to slip in unnoticed." *Belaufen* (run upon) becomes "to amount to." The body's act of running is projected onto time, errors, and arithmetic. Once you see the original motion, the abstractions become mnemonic aids rather than arbitrary vocabulary.

**Separable prefixes tend to be spatial and concrete.** *Ab, an, auf, aus, ein, fest, fort, hin, her, nach, weg, vor, zu, zurück, zusammen* — these are words that can stand alone as adverbs or prepositions. They preserve a physical directionality even in figurative use. You can usually picture someone running in that direction.

**Inseparable prefixes tend to be transformative and opaque.** *Be-, ent-, durch-* (insep.), *über-* (insep.), *um-* (insep.), *unter-, ver-* — these fuse with the verb and often shift its meaning away from literal running. *Verlaufen* is not really about running at all — it is about proceeding, losing one's way, or dispersing. *Belaufen* has nothing to do with feet — it is about totals. These must often be learned as units.

**Nautical imagery runs through the family.** Ships *laufen ein* (enter port), *laufen aus* (set sail), *laufen auf* (run aground), *laufen fest* (run aground and stick). The harbor is the anchor metaphor: entering and leaving it, or failing to navigate it, generates four distinct verbs. For English speakers who know "set sail," "run aground," and "make port," the German parallels are immediately graspable.

**ÜBER- is the most layered prefix.** Separable, it means overflow — liquid running over a rim. Inseparable, it means defection — a person running over to the enemy. As an adjective, it means overcrowded — a place overrun by too many. All three meanings are "running over a boundary," but the nature of the boundary (a pot's edge, a political allegiance, a capacity limit) determines the sense. Watch for stress: *ÜBERlaufen* (separable, stress on prefix) vs. *überLAUFEN* (inseparable, stress on stem).

**English sometimes preserves the same logic.** "Run out" = *auslaufen*. "Run after" = *nachlaufen*. "Run away" = *weglaufen*. "Run over" = *überlaufen*. "Run aground" = *auflaufen* / *festlaufen*. Where English and German share the metaphor, use it as a bridge. Where they diverge — especially with *verlaufen* (proceed, get lost, disperse) and *unterlaufen* (slip in, circumvent) — notice that German extends the running image further than English does, using "run" where English switches to entirely different verbs like "expire," "amount to," or "occur."
