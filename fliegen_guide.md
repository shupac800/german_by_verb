# FLIEGEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Fliegen** means to fly — cognate with English "fly" (both descend from the same Germanic root). It covers the full range: birds fly, planes fly, passengers fly, pilots fly. Hold this image in mind: a body moving through the air under momentum. Nearly every prefix below redirects that flight — launching it, ending it, exposing what it reveals, or abstracting it into metaphor.

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abfliegen:** to fly off, to depart by air. The prefix pulls the flight away from its starting point. A plane *fliegt ab* — it lifts off and leaves. Birds *fliegen ab* — they migrate, flying away from where they have been. The gesture is always departure: flight that begins by separating from a place.

Literal mnemonic: "fly off." English uses the same construction — "the bird flew off," "the flight departs."

### AN- ("on, onto, toward")

**Anfliegen:** to fly toward, to approach. The prefix pushes the flight toward contact. A pilot *fliegt einen Flughafen an* — flies toward an airport, approaches it. The flight has a target: it is directed at something, closing distance. The gesture is approach, not arrival — the plane is still in the air, heading in.

Literal mnemonic: "fly at" or "fly toward." Think of a plane on approach, angling in toward the runway.

### AUF- ("up, open")

**Auffliegen:** to fly up. Literally, a bird *fliegt auf* — startled from the ground, it bursts upward. But the figurative sense is the one worth remembering: a conspiracy *fliegt auf* — it is blown open, exposed, uncovered. The logic connects: what was hidden on the ground is suddenly launched into the air where everyone can see it. A scandal that *auffliegt* has been flushed from cover, like a bird startled into visibility. The image is vivid — concealment ends the moment something flies up into plain sight.

Literal mnemonic: "fly up" and therefore "fly open." What flies up can no longer hide.

### AUS- ("out, outward")

**Ausfliegen:** to fly out, to leave the nest. The prefix sends the flight outward from an enclosure. Young birds *fliegen aus* — they leave the nest for the first time. People *fliegen aus* — they go out, take a trip. The image is always emergence: flight directed outward from where one has been contained.

Literal mnemonic: "fly out." English "to fly the nest" captures the same idea of departure into independence.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Befliegen:** to fly over, to serve a route by air. *Be-* stamps the action of flying fully onto an object. An airline *befliegt eine Strecke* — it flies a route, it serves that route with regular flights. The prefix converts intransitive motion into transitive coverage: you are not merely flying, you are flying *something* — covering it, servicing it, making it yours.

Mnemonic: think of "be-" as "all over." To *befliegen* a route is to fly it thoroughly and regularly, to claim it as served airspace.

### DURCH- ("through")

**Durchfliegen:** to fly through. In its literal, separable sense: a bird *fliegt durch* a gap — it passes through without stopping. A plane flies nonstop, flying through without a layover. But as an inseparable verb, *durchfliegen* takes an unexpected turn: to fail, to flunk. The logic is the same motion reinterpreted — you fly through the exam without anything catching, without anything sticking. You pass through and out the other side with nothing to show for it.

Literal mnemonic: "fly through." Nonstop flight is flying through without pause. Failing an exam is flying through without purchase — nothing held.

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Einfliegen:** to fly in, to test-fly. The prefix directs flight inward. Supplies are *eingeflogen* — flown in, delivered by air into a location. A new aircraft is *eingeflogen* — test-flown, broken in, its handling tested and settled. The gesture is always inward: bringing something in by air, or working an aircraft into a reliable state.

Literal mnemonic: "fly in." English uses the same construction — "they flew in supplies," "the pilot flew in the new model."

### ENT- ("away from, escaped from")

**Entfliegen:** to fly away, to escape by flight. *Ent-* signals separation and removal. A bird *entfliegt* its cage — it flies away, free and gone. The prefix carries an air of finality: what has *entflogen* is not merely in the air but departed, escaped, beyond reach.

Literal mnemonic: "fly away from." The bird has fled — flight as escape.

### FORT- ("away, onward, gone")

**Fortfliegen:** to fly away, to fly off. The simplest "away" prefix applied to flight — no metaphor, no abstraction. A bird *fliegt fort* — it flies away and is gone. Where *entfliegen* implies escape from confinement, *fortfliegen* is neutral departure: it was here, it flew, now it is elsewhere.

Literal mnemonic: "fly forth." Gone, simply gone.

### HERAUS- ("out from within")

**Herausfliegen:** to fly out from inside. Literally, something flies out of an enclosure — a ball flies out a window, a bird flies out of a room. But the colloquial sense is vivid and worth learning: to be fired, to be thrown out, to be expelled. You *fliegst heraus* — you fly out of your job, your school, your position. The image is forceful ejection: you do not walk out, you are launched out, expelled with the velocity of something flung from the inside. It carries the energy of being tossed, not the dignity of leaving.

Literal mnemonic: "fly out (from in there)." Getting fired is being sent flying out the door.

### VER- (transformation, displacement, fading)

**Verfliegen:** to vanish, to dissipate, to blow over. This is *ver-* at its most transformative: it takes the act of flying and converts it into the act of disappearing. A scent *verfliegt* — it flies away into nothing, it fades. Anger *verfliegt* — it dissipates, it passes. Time *verfliegt* — it flies and is gone. The prefix does not redirect flight; it makes flight the mechanism of erasure. What was present has flown itself into nonexistence. The flying is not a journey to a destination — it is the process of ceasing to be. This is one of the most elegant *ver-* formations: motion itself becomes the metaphor for dissolution.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "away into transformation." What *verfliegt* has flown itself out of existence — not relocated but dissolved.

### WEG- ("away, gone")

**Wegfliegen:** to fly away, to be blown away. The prefix adds simple departure. A hat *fliegt weg* — it is blown away by the wind. A bird *fliegt weg* — it flies off. Where *verfliegen* implies dissolution, *wegfliegen* implies only relocation: the thing still exists, it is just elsewhere, carried away by air.

Literal mnemonic: "fly away." The paper flew away in the wind.

### ZU- ("to, toward, closed")

**Zufliegen:** to fly toward. Literally, a bird *fliegt jemandem zu* — it flies toward someone. But the figurative sense is the interesting one: things *fliegen jemandem zu* — they come easily, they arrive as if by flight. Talent, success, knowledge — when they *zufliegen*, they land in your lap without effort. The image is of a bird that comes to you unbidden, settling on your hand. What flies toward you requires no chase.

Literal mnemonic: "fly to." Things that "fly to" you arrive effortlessly, like a bird alighting on an outstretched hand.

### ÜBER- ("over, above, beyond")

**Überfliegen:** to fly over, to skim-read. As a flight verb, it is straightforward: a plane *überfliegt* a country — it flies over it, crossing above without landing. But the second meaning is where English and German run in parallel: to *überfliegen* a text is to skim it, to fly your eyes over the page without landing on any word for long. English "fly over" captures the aerial sense; "skim" captures the reading sense but loses the flight metaphor that German preserves. In German, skimming a document is literally flying over it — the same verb, the same gesture, whether the surface below you is terrain or text.

Literal mnemonic: "fly over." You fly over the land without stopping; you fly over the page without stopping. The parallel is exact.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays physical; the prefix goes abstract.** *Fliegen* never stops meaning "to fly." But the prefixes push that flight into metaphor. *Auffliegen* (fly up) becomes "to be exposed." *Verfliegen* (fly away) becomes "to vanish." *Herausfliegen* (fly out) becomes "to be fired." The body's experience of flight is projected onto social and psychological events. Once you see this, the figurative meanings become less arbitrary.

**Separable prefixes tend to preserve the physics of flight.** *Ab, an, auf, aus, ein, fort, weg, zu* — these are spatial words that tell you where the flight goes. Up, down, in, out, toward, away. Even in figurative use, the direction usually maps onto the meaning. Something that *auffliegt* (flies up) becomes visible; something that *wegfliegt* (flies away) is gone from where it was.

**Inseparable prefixes tend to transform the nature of the flight.** *Be-, ent-, ver-, über-* — these fuse with the verb and shift its meaning further from the literal. *Ver-* is the most dramatic: it converts flight from movement into disappearance. *Be-* converts flight from an intransitive act into transitive coverage. *Über-* extends flight into the domain of reading. These forms must often be learned as units.

**English sometimes preserves the same logic.** "Fly off" = *abfliegen*. "Fly over" = *überfliegen*. "Fly out" = *herausfliegen*. Where English and German share the metaphor, use it as an anchor. Where they diverge, notice the pattern: German often extends the flight metaphor further than English does — using "fly" where English switches to an entirely different verb. English says "to be exposed"; German says "to fly up." English says "to dissipate"; German says "to fly away into nothing." The German forms remember that these abstractions began as motion through the air.
