# SEHEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Sehen** means to see, to look, to watch. It is the fundamental verb of visual perception — not the passive reception of light, but the directed act of taking in the world through the eyes. Hold this image in mind: eyes turning toward something, registering it, making sense of it. Nearly every prefix below redirects that gaze — pointing it up, back, through, inward — or abstracts it from literal sight into understanding, judgment, or expectation.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Absehen:** to see off from, to see away from. Two distinct gestures. First, you look ahead and see something coming from a distance — you foresee, you predict. The gaze reaches out and picks up what is still far off. Second, you look away from something deliberately — you refrain, you disregard. The eyes turn aside and let it pass. Both senses share the prefix's core meaning of separation: either separating the future from the present by seeing across the gap, or separating yourself from a concern by choosing not to look at it.

Literal mnemonic: "see off." You "see off" a consequence before it arrives (foresee), or you "see off from" a demand (refrain).

### AN- ("on, onto, at")

**Ansehen:** to see at, to look onto. The prefix directs the gaze at something — you fix your eyes on it. Physically, you look at a painting, watch a film, view a document. Socially, the same directed gaze becomes judgment: you regard someone, consider them, view them as something. *Ansehen* as a noun means reputation — how one is "looked at" by the world.

Literal mnemonic: "look at." English works identically — "I looked at him" is neutral; "the way people look at him" is reputation.

### AUF- ("up, upon")

**Aufsehen:** to see up, to look up. The simplest compound in the family — you raise your eyes. *Aufsehen* as a noun means sensation or stir: something that makes everyone look up from what they were doing. *Aufsehen erregen* — to cause a stir — is literally to provoke an upward glance from the crowd.

Literal mnemonic: "look up." A commotion makes heads come up.

### AUS- ("out, outward")

**Aussehen:** to see out, to look outward. But the meaning has shifted entirely from the act of looking to the result of being looked at. *Aussehen* means to appear, to look, to seem — how something presents itself outwardly. *Das sieht gut aus* — that looks good. The prefix pushes the seeing outward, toward the surface: what is visible on the outside, the external impression a thing gives off.

Literal mnemonic: "look out" in the sense of "how it looks on the outside." The outward face of things.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Besehen:** to look at thoroughly, to inspect, to examine. *Be-* stamps the gaze fully onto the object. Where *ansehen* is simply to look at, *besehen* is to look all over — to turn something around in your hands and inspect it from every angle. The prefix converts casual seeing into deliberate scrutiny.

Mnemonic: "be-" = "all over." To *besehen* is to see something all over, to examine it completely.

### DURCH- ("through")

**Durchsehen:** to see through. The prefix pushes the gaze through something from one side to the other. Literally, you look through a window, a telescope, a stack of papers. Figuratively, you go through a document — checking it, reviewing it, looking at every part as your eyes pass through from beginning to end.

Literal mnemonic: "see through." English "look through the files" captures both senses — the physical motion and the act of reviewing.

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Einsehen:** to see into. The gaze penetrates inward — into a document, into a situation, into the logic of something. Literally, you look at records, inspect files. Figuratively, you see into the truth of a matter: you understand, you realize, you recognize. *Ich sehe ein, dass...* — I see into the fact that, I recognize that. The step from "seeing into" to "understanding" is natural: genuine comprehension feels like seeing past the surface into the interior.

Literal mnemonic: "see in" or "see into." English "insight" carries the same metaphor — seeing into something yields understanding.

### ENTGEGEN- ("toward, counter to, in the face of")

**Entgegensehen:** to see toward, to look toward what is coming. You direct your gaze at what approaches — you look forward to something, you expect it, you await it. The prefix implies that the thing seen is moving toward you: you face it. *Einer Sache entgegensehen* — to face something, whether eagerly or with apprehension.

Literal mnemonic: "look toward." You stand and look toward what is coming your way.

### ER- (achievement, result, extraction)

**Ersehen:** to see out of, to gather from. *Er-* here signals extraction — pulling a result out of the act of seeing. You look at a document and *ersehen* the conclusion from it: you see from the evidence, you gather, it is evident. The prefix converts raw seeing into a cognitive yield.

Mnemonic: "see forth from." You extract insight from what you see — the information is "seen out" of the source.

### GLEICH- ("equal, same, alike")

**Gleichsehen:** to see the same, to look alike. A transparent compound — the prefix declares that two things look equal. To resemble someone is to "same-look" them.

Literal mnemonic: "look the same." *Er sieht seinem Vater gleich* — he looks the same as his father.

### HERAUS- ("out from within")

**Heraussehen:** to see out from inside, to look out, to peek out. You are inside — a window, a hiding place — and your gaze (or your face) emerges outward. Something *sieht heraus* when it peeks out, protrudes visibly from behind something.

Literal mnemonic: "look out (from in here)."

### HER- ("hither, toward the speaker")

**Hersehen:** to see hither, to look here. The prefix pulls the gaze toward the speaker. *Sieh her!* — look here! Look this way! A command that redirects someone's eyes to where you are standing.

Literal mnemonic: "look here." Direct and spatial.

### HIN- ("thither, toward a point away from the speaker")

**Hinsehen:** to see thither, to look there. The counterpart of *hersehen* — the gaze goes outward, away from the speaker, toward a point. *Genau hinsehen* means to look closely, to pay attention — to actually direct your eyes to the thing. The prefix emphasizes deliberate attention: not just seeing passively but looking toward something with purpose.

Literal mnemonic: "look there." "Look at it" — with the emphasis on actually directing your eyes.

### HINWEG- ("away over, past")

**Hinwegsehen:** to see past, to look over and beyond. The compound prefix *hinweg* means "away over" — your gaze passes over something and keeps going, as if it were not there. You overlook, you disregard, you turn a blind eye. The image is deliberate: you could see it, but your eyes travel past it, over it, refusing to register it.

Literal mnemonic: "look away over." English "overlook" captures the same motion — the eyes pass over the thing without stopping.

### NACH- ("after, following, checking")

**Nachsehen:** to see after. Three related gestures from the same prefix. You look after someone departing — your gaze follows them as they go. You look something up — you check after the fact, you verify by looking again. You overlook a fault — you let it pass, you see past it with leniency, as if looking after the person's better qualities instead.

Literal mnemonic: "look after." English uses this in all three senses: "she looked after him as he left," "look it up and check," "I'll overlook it this time."

### UM- ("around, about")

**Umsehen:** to see around, to look about. Almost always reflexive: *sich umsehen* — to look around oneself. You turn your gaze in a circle, surveying your surroundings. The prefix wraps the act of seeing around the viewer.

Literal mnemonic: "look around." The gaze goes around in all directions.

### VER- (transformation, error, intensification)

**Versehen:** one of the most layered *ver-* verbs, because *ver-* pushes the basic act of seeing into three very different directions:

- *Carrying out:* to oversee something in the sense of attending to it — to perform a duty, to carry out a function. You *versehen* an office by keeping your eyes on it, tending it. The prefix transforms seeing into sustained, responsible attention.
- *Providing:* to see to it that something is equipped — to provide, to supply. *Mit etwas versehen* means furnished with, equipped with. You have "seen to" the supplies.
- *Error (reflexive):* *sich versehen* — to see wrongly, to make a mistake. The *ver-* twists the seeing astray, just as *verlegen* twists the laying. You looked, but you looked wrong; your vision slipped.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "mis-" or "through into transformation." *Versehen* a duty = see through it, attend to it. *Versehen* with supplies = see to it. *Sich versehen* = mis-see, err.

### VOR- ("before, forward, in advance")

**Vorsehen:** a prefix that splits its meaning between inseparable and separable use — and the split is instructive.

*Inseparable (vorsehen):* to see before, to see ahead — to plan, to provide for, to intend, to schedule. The prefix fuses with the stem to create foresight turned into action. What is *vorgesehen* has been seen in advance and arranged accordingly. A law *sieht vor* — it provides for, it envisions. The seeing is forward-looking and authoritative.

*Separable (sich vorsehen):* to see before oneself in the sense of watching out — to be careful, to take care. *Sieh dich vor!* — watch out! Look ahead of yourself and see the danger before you walk into it. The seeing is forward-looking and defensive.

Literal mnemonic: inseparable = "foresee and arrange" (planning); separable = "look out in front" (caution). Both involve seeing what lies ahead, but one organizes it and the other dodges it.

### WEG- ("away, gone")

**Wegsehen:** to see away, to look away. The simplest directional compound — you turn your gaze away. You avert your eyes. No metaphor, no abstraction: the thing was there, and you chose not to look at it.

Literal mnemonic: "look away." Eyes that were directed at something are redirected elsewhere.

### WIEDER- ("again, once more")

**Wiedersehen:** to see again. Perhaps the most emotionally charged compound in the family, though its construction is the simplest: the prefix adds "again" to the seeing. *Auf Wiedersehen* — until the seeing-again, until we see each other once more. Reunion distilled into a single word.

Literal mnemonic: "see again." A farewell that is also a promise: we will see each other again.

### ZURÜCK- ("back, backward")

**Zurücksehen:** to see back, to look back. The gaze reverses direction — you look behind you, either literally (turning your head) or figuratively (looking back over your life, your past, your memories). The prefix sends the seeing backward in space or time.

Literal mnemonic: "look back." "Looking back, I can see..." — the same metaphor in both languages.

### ZU- ("to, toward, watching")

**Zusehen:** to see to, to look toward and keep looking — to watch, to look on. The prefix pushes the gaze toward something and holds it there. You *siehst zu* as a spectator: you watch something happen. From sustained watching comes the idea of making sure: *zusehen, dass...* — to see to it that, to make sure that. You watch over the outcome.

Literal mnemonic: "see to." English "see to it" captures the supervisory sense perfectly. "I just stood there and looked on" captures the spectator sense.

### ÜBER- ("over, above, beyond")

**Übersehen:** to see over, to look over. Two meanings from the same spatial image, depending on emphasis:

- *Survey:* you stand above and see over the whole — you survey, you take in the full view. Your eyes pass over everything and register it all. The vantage point is high and commanding.
- *Miss:* your eyes pass over something and fail to catch it — you overlook, you miss, you fail to notice. The gaze sweeps over the surface and the detail is lost.

The irony is elegant: the same motion of seeing over produces both comprehensive vision and blind spots. Whether *übersehen* means "I saw everything" or "I missed it" depends on whether the gaze was thorough or hasty.

Literal mnemonic: "look over." English "overlook" carries the same double meaning — "overlook the valley" (survey) vs. "overlook the error" (miss).

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays perceptual; the prefixes go cognitive.** *Sehen* never stops meaning "to see." But the prefixes push raw perception into understanding (*einsehen*), judgment (*ansehen*), expectation (*entgegensehen*), planning (*vorsehen*), and error (*sich versehen*). Sight becomes a metaphor for all of cognition. This parallels English — we "see" what someone means, we "look into" a problem, we have "insight" and "oversight" — but German builds these metaphors more systematically through prefixation.

**Separable prefixes tend to be spatial and concrete.** *Ab, an, auf, aus, durch, ein, her, hin, nach, um, weg, zu, zurück* — these preserve a physical direction for the gaze. You can picture where the eyes go. Even when the meaning is figurative (*einsehen* = understand), the spatial image (seeing into) remains recoverable.

**Inseparable prefixes tend to be transformative and opaque.** *Be-, er-, ver-, über-* fuse with the verb and shift it furthest from literal seeing. *Versehen* (carry out, provide, err) has almost nothing to do with eyes anymore. *Besehen* (examine) still involves looking but the *be-* turns passive perception into active scrutiny. These must be learned more as units than decoded from parts.

**Übersehen is a case study in prefix ambiguity.** The same word means both "to survey completely" and "to miss entirely" — comprehensive vision and its opposite. This is not a flaw but a feature of the prefix system: *über-* (over) can mean "over the whole surface" or "past without stopping." English "overlook" behaves identically. When you encounter such double-edged prefixes, context is everything.

**Vorsehen shows how sep./insep. splits meaning.** Inseparable *vorsehen* (to plan, to provide for) treats the "seeing ahead" as an institutional, authoritative act — the law foresees, the schedule provides. Separable *sich vorsehen* (to watch out, to be careful) treats the same forward-looking gaze as personal vigilance. The grammatical distinction (fused prefix vs. separating prefix) maps onto a conceptual one: official foresight vs. individual caution.

**English often preserves the same logic.** "Look up" = *aufsehen*. "Look through" = *durchsehen*. "See to it" = *zusehen*. "Overlook" = *übersehen*. "Insight" = *Einsicht* (from *einsehen*). Where English and German share the metaphor, use it as an anchor. Where they diverge — *aussehen* (appear) is far from "look out," *versehen* (carry out/provide/err) is far from "mis-see" — notice that German has pushed the spatial metaphor of seeing further than English typically does, building entire conceptual domains on the foundation of where the eyes are pointed.
