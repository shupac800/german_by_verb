# MÜSSEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Müssen** means to must, to have to, to need to, to be obliged to. It is a modal verb — it expresses necessity, obligation, or compulsion rather than a concrete physical action. Hold this in mind: *müssen* is the raw idea of being compelled, of having no choice but to act. The prefixed forms below do not reshape a physical gesture the way spatial stems do. Instead, they attach a direction to the compulsion itself — specifying *where* one is obliged to go.

## The Prefixes

### DURCH- ("through")

**Durchmüssen:** to have to go through, to have to get through. The prefix supplies the obstacle: something stands between you and the other side, and *durchmüssen* says you are obliged to pass through it. This is an elliptical, colloquial form — the verb of motion (*gehen*, *kommen*) is dropped, and only the modal with its direction remains: "must through." The compulsion may be physical (a narrow passage, a crowd) or figurative (a difficult phase, a bureaucratic process).

Literal mnemonic: "must through." Where English says "have to get through it," German compresses the whole phrase into prefix plus modal. *Da musst du durch* — you have to get through it, you just have to push through.

Examples: *Die Prüfung ist hart, aber du musst da durch* — the exam is tough, but you have to get through it. *Wir müssen hier durch, es gibt keinen anderen Weg* — we have to get through here, there's no other way.

### FORT- ("away, onward, gone")

**Fortmüssen:** to have to leave, to have to go away. The prefix adds departure: *fort-* points away from the current place, and the modal supplies the obligation. You are compelled to leave. The motion verb (*gehen*) is implied and dropped. *Fortmüssen* is slightly more literary than *wegmüssen* — *fort-* carries a touch of formality, suggesting going forth rather than simply going away.

Literal mnemonic: "must forth." English "I must away" (archaic/poetic) captures the same compression — dropping the motion verb and letting the direction carry the meaning.

Examples: *Ich muss fort, es ist schon spät* — I have to leave, it's already late. *Er musste fort, bevor die anderen kamen* — he had to go away before the others arrived.

### HER- ("hither, toward the speaker")

**Hermüssen:** to have to come here. The prefix points toward the speaker: someone or something is obliged to come to where you are. The motion verb (*kommen*) is implied. This is the compulsive counterpart to a summons — not "come here" as an invitation but "must come here" as an inevitability or insistence.

Literal mnemonic: "must hither." The direction is toward the speaker: whatever is needed must come this way. *Das Paket muss her* — the package has to come here, we need it here.

Examples: *Ein neuer Computer muss her* — a new computer is needed (literally: must come here). *Frisches Brot muss her* — fresh bread has to come, we need fresh bread here.

### WEG- ("away, gone")

**Wegmüssen:** to have to go, to have to leave. The prefix is the plainest "away" in German — no formality, no particular direction, just departure from the current spot. You are obliged to be elsewhere. Like *fortmüssen* but more colloquial and everyday. The motion verb (*gehen*) is dropped; only the compulsion and the direction remain.

Literal mnemonic: "must away." *Ich muss weg* is one of the most common exit lines in spoken German — "I have to go," compressed to its bare minimum: obligation plus away.

Examples: *Ich muss weg, mein Zug fährt gleich* — I have to go, my train is leaving soon. *Der Fleck muss weg* — the stain has to go (must be removed).

### ZURÜCK- ("back, backward, in return")

**Zurückmüssen:** to have to go back, to have to return. The prefix reverses the direction: you are compelled to retrace your path, to return to where you came from. The motion verb (*gehen*, *fahren*, *kommen*) is dropped. The obligation may be practical (you forgot something) or structural (a deadline, a duty pulling you back).

Literal mnemonic: "must back." English "I have to get back" works the same way — the direction is backward, the force is obligation.

Examples: *Ich muss zurück ins Büro* — I have to go back to the office. *Wir müssen vor Mitternacht zurück* — we have to be back before midnight.

## Patterns Worth Noting

**Modal verbs absorb direction, not action.** Unlike concrete stems like *legen* (to lay), where prefixes redirect a physical gesture, *müssen* is already abstract. Its prefixed forms do not transform what the obligation looks like — they specify where the obligation leads. The result is a set of compressed, colloquial expressions where the prefix carries the entire spatial meaning and the modal supplies the compulsion.

**The verb of motion is always implied.** Every prefixed form here is elliptical. Full forms would be *durchgehen müssen*, *fortgehen müssen*, *herkommen müssen*, *weggehen müssen*, *zurückgehen müssen*. In casual spoken German, the motion verb drops out and the modal absorbs the prefix. This is a distinctly German pattern with no direct parallel in English — English always needs the motion verb ("have to get through," "have to go back").

**All forms are separable and colloquial.** Every compound in this family is separable and belongs to informal spoken registers. You will hear *Ich muss weg* far more often than you will read it in formal prose. These are living, everyday formations — the language compressing what it says most often into the shortest possible shape.

**The family mirrors *können* but substitutes compulsion for ability.** Where *durchkönnen* means "to be able to get through," *durchmüssen* means "to have to get through." Where *wegkönnen* would mean "to be able to leave," *wegmüssen* means "to have to leave." The two modal families run in parallel: same directional prefixes, same implied motion verbs, same colloquial register. The only difference is the modal force — capacity versus necessity.

**The family is small because *müssen* is abstract.** Physical stems generate dozens of prefix compounds because their concrete actions can be redirected in many ways. A modal verb offers less raw material for prefixation. The forms that exist all follow one clear pattern: spatial prefix plus implied motion, filtered through the idea of obligation.
