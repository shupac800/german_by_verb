# KRIEGEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Kriegen** has two lives. In everyday colloquial German, it means to get, to receive, to catch — the informal twin of *bekommen*. In formal and literary German, it means to wage war. Hold both images: an open hand receiving something, and two armies clashing. The colloquial sense dominates modern usage by far, and nearly every prefix below works with "get." But one prefix — *be-* — reaches back to the older martial meaning and revives it.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abkriegen:** to get (hit) off something, to catch some of it. The prefix pulls part of something away and directs it at you. A bullet fragment is *abgekriegt* — you got hit by a piece flying off. A share of the cake is *abgekriegt* — you got some off the whole. The image is always partial: not the full thing, but a portion that separates and comes your way.

Literal mnemonic: "get off" — as in "she got some of the shrapnel off the explosion" or "he got a piece off the deal."

### AUF- ("up, upon, open")

**Aufkriegen:** to get open. The prefix lifts or pries apart, and you succeed in doing so. A stuck jar lid is *aufgekriegt* — you managed to get it open. A knot is *aufgekriegt* — you worked it loose. The emphasis falls on success against resistance: the thing was shut, sealed, or stuck, and you got it open.

Literal mnemonic: "get open." English uses "get it open" in exactly the same way — the German simply fuses the idea into a single verb.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive — here reviving the martial stem)

**Bekriegen:** to wage war on, to fight, to battle. This is the one prefix that activates the old, formal meaning of *kriegen* — war. *Be-* stamps the action fully onto a direct object: you wage war *on* someone, you fight *them*. Two nations *bekriegen sich* — they war against each other. Reflexively, it captures mutual, sustained conflict. While the rest of the family is colloquial and light, *bekriegen* is heavy, literary, and martial.

Mnemonic: think of *be-* as "upon" with full force. To *bekriegen* is to bring war upon someone — total, directed hostility.

### HERAUS- ("out from within")

**Herauskriegen:** to get out, to extract — specifically information. You reach into a puzzle, a mystery, a tangle and pull the answer out. To *herauskriegen* is to find out, to figure out, to solve. The prefix says "out from inside": the answer was hidden within the problem, and you got it out.

Literal mnemonic: "get out (of it)." English "get it out of him" (extract a confession) and "work it out" carry the same logic. German makes the directionality more vivid: you reach *heraus* — out from in there.

### HIN- ("thither, toward a point, into place")

**Hinkriegen:** to get it there, to get it right. The prefix points toward a goal — a desired result, a correct outcome — and you manage to reach it. To *hinkriegen* is to pull something off, to manage, to fix. A broken radio is *hingekriegt* — you got it working. A tricky recipe is *hingekriegt* — you managed it. The focus is on competent achievement: the thing needed doing, and you got it *hin* — to where it should be.

Literal mnemonic: "get (it) there." "I got it there in the end" = I managed to make it work.

### UNTER- ("under, beneath, down")

**Unterkriegen:** to get under, to get down. The prefix pushes downward, and what goes down is morale, spirit, resistance. To *unterkriegen* someone is to wear them down, to demoralize them. The common reassurance *Lass dich nicht unterkriegen!* — don't let them get you down — makes the image plain: someone is trying to push you under, and you must resist being laid low.

Literal mnemonic: "get (someone) under" or "get down." English "don't let it get you down" is a near-perfect parallel.

### ZURÜCK- ("back, backward, in return")

**Zurückkriegen:** to get back. The prefix reverses the direction. You lent something, and now you get it back. You lost something, and now you recover it. The logic is pure return: what went away comes back to you.

Literal mnemonic: "get back." English works identically — "I want to get my money back." German simply compresses it: *zurückkriegen*.

### ZUSAMMEN- ("together")

**Zusammenkriegen:** to get together, to gather, to scrape together. The prefix consolidates. You *kriegen* scattered things *zusammen* — you round them up, collect them, assemble them into one place. Money is *zusammengekriegt* — scraped together from various sources. People are *zusammengekriegt* — gathered, assembled. The effort implied is often considerable: the pieces are dispersed, and getting them together takes work.

Literal mnemonic: "get together." "We got enough money together for the trip" = *Wir haben genug Geld zusammengekriegt.*

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem splits in two, and the prefixes choose sides.** Seven of eight prefixed forms work with the colloquial "get" meaning. Only *bekriegen* activates the old martial sense. This is not accidental: *be-* is an inseparable, formal prefix that gravitates toward literary register, while the separable prefixes (*ab-, auf-, heraus-, hin-, unter-, zurück-, zusammen-*) stay in the everyday, spoken world where *kriegen* = "get" lives.

**"Kriegen" mirrors English "get" with striking precision.** English "get" is one of the most versatile colloquial verbs: get back, get open, get together, get down, get it right. German *kriegen* parallels this almost one-to-one. *Zurückkriegen* = get back. *Aufkriegen* = get open. *Zusammenkriegen* = get together. *Unterkriegen* = get down. *Hinkriegen* = get it right. Where English uses "get + particle," German uses *kriegen + prefix*. This makes the *kriegen* family one of the most intuitive for English speakers.

**Every prefix here is separable except one.** *Be-* stands alone as the inseparable prefix, and it is also the only one that shifts register and meaning entirely — from casual "getting" to formal "warring." The separable prefixes all preserve the colloquial tone and the basic "get" sense, merely redirecting it spatially: off, open, out, down, back, together.

**The colloquial tone matters.** In formal writing, Germans reach for *bekommen* (to receive), *erhalten* (to obtain), or *schaffen* (to manage). *Kriegen* and its compounds belong to spoken German — the kitchen, the playground, the casual conversation. Learning them signals not just vocabulary but register awareness: you know when to sound like a book and when to sound like a person.
