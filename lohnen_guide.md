# LOHNEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Lohnen** means to be worthwhile, to reward, to pay. At its core sits the noun *Lohn* — wages, reward, recompense. The verb built from it carries two faces: an impersonal one ("it is worthwhile," *es lohnt sich*) and a transitive one ("to reward someone"). Hold this double image in mind: something that pays back, whether as abstract value or as concrete compensation. The three prefixed forms below all pull *lohnen* toward the concrete, transactional side — payment rendered for work or merit — but each frames the transaction differently.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from, completion")

**Ablohnen** (sep.): to pay off, to pay wages to. The prefix *ab-* signals discharge — settling an obligation and clearing it away. You *lohnst* a worker *ab* — you pay them their wages and the debt is done. The image is of a ledger line being crossed off: the work was performed, the wages are laid down, the account is closed. There is no praise here, no recognition of excellence — only the mechanical settling of what is owed.

Literal mnemonic: "reward off" or "pay off." Think of paying someone off — discharging the obligation and being done with it.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Belohnen** (insep.): to reward, to recompense, to remunerate. The prefix *be-* stamps the action fully onto its object — it transforms *lohnen* from a general notion of worth into a directed act: you *belohnen* a specific person for a specific deed. A child is *belohnt* for good behavior. Bravery is *belohnt* with a medal. The emphasis is on merit: the reward answers something praiseworthy. Where *ablohnen* settles a wage, *belohnen* honors an achievement.

Mnemonic: think of *be-* as "bestow upon." To *belohnen* is to bestow a reward upon someone who has earned it. English "reward" itself carries this flavor — it recognizes and reciprocates.

### ENT- ("from, forth, as a result of")

**Entlohnen** (insep.): to pay, to compensate, to remunerate. The prefix *ent-* here functions not as removal but as extraction — drawing forth the appropriate payment in response to labor. You *entlohnen* a worker for their effort: you compensate them, you render payment proportional to what they have done. The word is more formal than *ablohnen* and more systematic than *belohnen*. It belongs to the language of contracts and payroll — structured compensation rather than spontaneous reward or simple wage-settling.

Literal mnemonic: "reward forth" — the payment comes forth as a consequence of the work. Think of *Entlohnung* (compensation) as the formal system by which effort is converted into pay.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**Three words, three framings of the same transaction.** All three prefixed forms describe giving someone money for something done, but the social register shifts each time. *Ablohnen* is the most mechanical — paying wages, clearing the books. *Belohnen* is the most personal — rewarding merit, acknowledging excellence. *Entlohnen* is the most institutional — compensating labor within a formal structure. Choosing among them tells the listener not just *that* someone was paid, but *how* the speaker views the payment.

**The stem holds the family together.** Unlike sprawling families like *legen*, the *lohnen* family is small and tightly clustered around a single concept: returning value for value. There is no spatial metaphor here, no journey from concrete to abstract. The stem is already abstract — worth, reward — and the prefixes simply adjust the social lens through which the transaction is viewed.

**Separable vs. inseparable tracks a concrete/abstract divide.** *Ablohnen* is separable: it describes a visible, completable act — handing over wages. *Belohnen* and *entlohnen* are inseparable: they describe concepts that fuse with the verb — rewarding and compensating are not physical gestures you can pull apart into prefix and stem. The grammatical distinction mirrors the conceptual one.

**English has the same three-way split but uses unrelated words.** German keeps *Lohn* visible in all three forms, making the family transparent. English scatters the same ideas across "pay off" (ablohnen), "reward" (belohnen), and "compensate" (entlohnen) — three words with no shared root. Recognizing that German bundles them under one stem is itself the insight: where English sees three separate acts, German sees one act (*lohnen*) performed in three different social registers.
