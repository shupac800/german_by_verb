# STEHLEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Stehlen** means to steal, to rob, to pilfer. The core image is of taking something that belongs to someone else — furtively, illicitly, without permission. Hold this picture: a hand reaching out and removing what is not yours. The two prefixes below reshape this basic act — one redirects it onto a person, the other converts it into quiet, secretive movement.

---

## The Prefixes

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive, directing the action onto a person)

**Bestehlen** (insep.): to steal from, to rob. Without the prefix, *stehlen* takes the stolen object as its direct object — *ich stehle das Geld* (I steal the money). With *be-*, the grammar pivots: the victim becomes the direct object — *ich bestehle dich* (I steal from you, I rob you). The act of stealing is stamped onto a person rather than aimed at a thing. You are not merely taking something; you are doing the taking *to someone*. The shift is from "steal the wallet" to "rob the man."

This is a classic *be-* pattern. Just as *belegen* redirects *legen* onto an object (to cover it, claim it, lay upon it thoroughly), *bestehlen* redirects *stehlen* onto a person. Other parallels: *antworten* (to answer) becomes *beantworten* (to answer something); *dienen* (to serve) becomes *bedienen* (to serve someone). The prefix consistently converts an intransitive or indirectly transitive verb into one that acts directly upon its target.

Mnemonic: think of *be-* as "upon." To *bestehlen* someone is to commit theft *upon* them — to steal all over them, to make them the object of the crime rather than the loot.

### WEG- ("away, gone")

**Wegstehlen** (sep.): *(sich wegstehlen)* to steal away, to sneak away, to slip off. Here the act of stealing loses its criminal content entirely and keeps only its manner — the furtiveness, the quiet, the hope of not being noticed. You do not take anything; you take *yourself* away. *Sich wegstehlen* means to remove yourself from a place the way a thief would: silently, unobtrusively, without drawing attention.

English uses exactly the same metaphor. "She stole away from the party" means she left quietly. "He stole out of the room" means he slipped through the door without being seen. The physical act of theft is gone; what remains is the stealth. German and English agree perfectly here: in both languages, "stealing" can mean simply "moving like a thief."

Literal mnemonic: "steal away." The English phrase and the German verb are mirror images of each other.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The family is tiny, and that itself is telling.** Most concrete action verbs in German (*legen*, *stehen*, *schlagen*, *tragen*) generate large prefix families because their physical actions lend themselves to many spatial redirections. *Stehlen* resists this. Stealing is not a gesture that can be meaningfully redirected upward, downward, inward, or apart. You cannot "steal something together" or "steal something open." The verb's meaning is too socially specific — too bound to the concept of illicit taking — to be freely combined with spatial prefixes the way a neutral physical action like "lay" or "carry" can.

**The inseparable prefix transforms the grammar; the separable prefix transforms the meaning.** *Bestehlen* keeps the core meaning (theft) but changes who receives the action. *Wegstehlen* keeps the manner (stealth) but discards the core meaning entirely. These are two fundamentally different strategies for building compound verbs, and this small family puts them side by side with unusual clarity.

**English preserves the same metaphorical extension.** "To steal away" is alive in modern English, making *sich wegstehlen* one of the most transparent German compound verbs for an English speaker. Use this as an anchor: if you can say "steal away" in English, you already understand *wegstehlen*. The harder verb is *bestehlen*, because English does not use "steal" with a person as a direct object — we say "rob someone" but "steal something," and German's *be-* prefix is doing the work that English assigns to a completely different verb.
