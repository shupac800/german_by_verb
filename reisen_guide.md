# REISEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Reisen** means to travel, to journey. It implies movement across distance — not a commute or an errand, but a real trip from one place to another. Hold this image in mind: a person with a packed bag setting out on a road. Every prefix below reshapes that basic journey — specifying its direction, its boundary, its purpose, or its social dimension.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abreisen:** to travel off, to depart. The prefix pulls the traveler away from where they are. You *reist ab* from a hotel, from a city, from a conference — you break away from the current location and begin the journey home or onward. The emphasis falls on the act of leaving, not on where you are headed. The suitcase is closed, the bill is paid, and you are gone.

Literal mnemonic: "travel off." Think of "setting off" — the moment of departure, the breaking of contact with the place you have been.

### AN- ("on, onto, toward")

**Anreisen:** to travel to, to arrive, to make one's way. The prefix pushes the journey toward a destination. Where *abreisen* is about leaving, *anreisen* is about arriving — the approach, the final leg. Guests *reisen an* — they travel toward you and show up. The *Anreise* (noun) is the journey to a place, the getting-there part of a trip.

Literal mnemonic: "travel on toward." The traveler comes on, draws near, arrives at the destination.

### AUS- ("out, outward")

**Ausreisen:** to travel out (of the country). The prefix directs the journey outward across a national border. This is an official word — it belongs to the language of passports and customs. You *reist aus* when you leave a country. The *Ausreise* is your departure from a nation's territory, the outbound crossing.

Literal mnemonic: "travel out." You pass out through the border and leave the country behind.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Bereisen:** to travel through thoroughly. *Be-* stamps the action onto an object — instead of simply traveling, you travel *a region*. To *bereisen* a country is to tour it, to cover it, to visit it systematically. A sales representative *bereist* a territory — works it, travels all over it. The prefix converts an intransitive act of movement into a transitive act of coverage.

Mnemonic: think of "be-" as "all over." To *bereisen* a land is to travel all over it, leaving no corner unvisited.

### DURCH- ("through")

**Durchreisen:** to travel through. As a separable verb, you pass through a place on your way somewhere else — the city is a waypoint, not a destination. You *reist durch* Berlin on your way to Warsaw. As an inseparable verb (*durchreisen*), the emphasis shifts to thoroughness — to traverse, to cross the full extent of a region. Either way, the core image is the same: the journey enters one side and exits the other.

Literal mnemonic: "travel through." The place is a passage, not a stop.

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Einreisen:** to travel in (to the country). The mirror of *ausreisen*. The prefix directs the journey inward across a border. You *reist ein* when you arrive in a foreign country. The *Einreise* is your entry into a nation's territory, the inbound crossing. Like *ausreisen*, this word lives in the world of immigration law, visa stamps, and border control.

Literal mnemonic: "travel in." You pass in through the border and enter the country.

### HER- ("hither, toward the speaker")

**Herreisen:** to travel here, toward where the speaker is. The prefix pulls the journey in the speaker's direction. It is simple and uncommon in everyday speech, but transparent: the traveler's destination is *here*, where I am standing.

Literal mnemonic: "travel hither." The direction is toward the speaker.

### HIN- ("thither, away from the speaker")

**Hinreisen:** to travel there, away from the speaker. The counterpart of *herreisen*. The prefix pushes the journey outward toward a point away from the speaker. You *reist hin* to a place that is "over there." The emphasis falls on the outbound direction — going, not coming.

Literal mnemonic: "travel thither." The direction is away from the speaker, toward a distant point.

### MIT- ("with, along")

**Mitreisen:** to travel along, to travel with. The prefix adds company. You join someone else's journey — you travel *with* them, as part of their party. The image is of a companion falling into step beside the main traveler, sharing the road.

Literal mnemonic: "travel with." Think of "come along" — the prefix simply adds togetherness to the journey.

### NACH- ("after, behind, following")

**Nachreisen:** to travel after, to follow. The prefix places the traveler behind someone else in time. They left first; you *reist nach* — you set out later, following the same route, trying to catch up or meet them at the destination. The journey is the same, but the timing is offset.

Literal mnemonic: "travel after." One person goes ahead; you follow after.

### VER- (transformation, displacement)

**Verreisen:** to go away on a trip, to be away traveling. *Ver-* here signals displacement — a change of state from "present" to "gone." To *verreisen* is to leave home and be elsewhere. It is not about the direction or the destination but about the absence: "She is *verreist*" means she is away, she has gone off traveling, she is not here. The word captures the social fact of being gone rather than the physical act of moving.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "away into a changed state." Someone who is *verreist* has passed from here into away. The trip has transformed their status from present to absent.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The border pair: ein- and aus-.** *Einreisen* and *ausreisen* are mirror images — traveling in versus traveling out — and both belong to the formal vocabulary of borders and immigration. German treats national boundaries as containers you enter (*ein-*) or exit (*aus-*). English uses entirely different verbs ("enter the country" / "leave the country"), but German keeps the same stem and simply flips the directional prefix.

**The departure-arrival pair: ab- and an-.** *Abreisen* and *anreisen* mirror each other the same way — one marks the moment of leaving, the other the moment of arriving. The stem does not change; only the prefix rotates the traveler's position relative to the place. *Ab-* breaks contact; *an-* makes contact.

**Separable prefixes preserve direction.** Almost every prefix here is separable — *ab, an, aus, ein, durch, her, hin, mit, nach* — and each one specifies a concrete spatial relationship. You can picture the arrow: off, toward, out, in, through, here, there, with, after. The traveler's body moves; the prefix says which way.

**Ver- changes state, not direction.** Unlike the spatial prefixes, *ver-* does not point anywhere. It transforms the traveler's condition — from home to away, from present to absent. This is typical of *ver-*: it cares about the change itself, not the direction of the change.

**Be- changes grammar, not direction.** *Bereisen* is the only form that takes a direct object — you *bereist* a country the way you *cover* a territory. The prefix converts the intransitive "I travel" into the transitive "I travel a region." This is *be-*'s standard function across German: it aims the verb at an object and stamps the action onto it.

**English splits what German compounds.** English uses "depart," "arrive," "enter," "leave," "follow," "tour" — entirely separate verbs for each nuance. German keeps *reisen* and stacks a prefix on top. Once you learn the prefix inventory, you can decode almost any compound on sight: *nachreisen* = travel + after = follow someone's journey. The system is more regular than English, but you must trust the prefixes to do their work.
