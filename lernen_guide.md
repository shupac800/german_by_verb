# LERNEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Lernen** means to learn, to study, to acquire knowledge. It implies an active process of taking something in — absorbing a skill, a fact, a way of doing things. Hold this image in mind: a person sitting with material before them, drawing it into their understanding. Every prefix below reshapes that basic act — directing it, completing it, reversing it, or restarting it.

---

## The Prefixes

### AN- ("on, onto, toward")

**Anlernen:** to learn on, to train onto. The prefix pushes the action toward contact — someone is brought up against a skill and taught to perform it. *Anlernen* means to train, to teach on the job, to instruct. The learning is practical and applied: not deep study but hands-on initiation. An *angelernter Arbeiter* is a semi-skilled worker — someone who has been trained onto the task rather than having studied it formally.

Literal mnemonic: "learn on." Think of on-the-job training — the knowledge is laid on from the outside, pressed onto the learner through practice rather than theory.

### AUS- ("out, outward, to completion")

**Auslernen:** to learn out, to learn to the end. The prefix carries the action outward to its natural boundary — you learn until there is nothing left to learn. *Auslernen* means to finish one's training, to complete an apprenticeship. The prefix signals exhaustion of the process: the learning has been carried all the way out. The common saying *Man lernt nie aus* ("you never finish learning") uses precisely this logic — the learning is never fully "outed."

Literal mnemonic: "learn out." You have learned everything out of the subject — extracted all the knowledge it contains, or completed the training to its end.

### ER- (achievement, completion, thorough attainment)

**Erlernen:** to learn to completion, to master. *Er-* signals that an action reaches its terminal point, its full result. Where *lernen* is the ongoing process, *erlernen* is the achieved outcome. You do not *erlernen* casually — you *erlernen* a language, a craft, a musical instrument. The prefix converts the open-ended act of learning into a finished accomplishment. The thing has been thoroughly acquired.

Literal mnemonic: "learn through to the end." Compare *erschlagen* (to strike dead), *erringen* (to win through struggle) — the *er-* always marks the point where effort arrives at its goal. *Erlernen* is learning that has arrived.

### UM- ("around, over, anew")

**Umlernen:** to learn around, to learn anew. The prefix implies a turning-over, a change of direction. To *umlernen* is to retrain, to relearn, to rethink — to abandon what you once knew and learn a new way around the same territory. The image is of someone who has walked a familiar path and must now learn a different route. A worker whose industry changes must *umlernen*. A doctor who encounters new research must *umlernen*. The old knowledge is not simply added to — it is turned over and replaced.

Literal mnemonic: "learn around" or "re-learn." Think of making a U-turn in your understanding: what you knew pointed one way, and now you must learn your way around to a new direction.

### VER- (displacement, wrongness, loss)

**Verlernen:** to learn away, to learn wrongly into loss. *Ver-* here carries its force of displacement and error — the learning goes astray, drifts away, is lost. To *verlernen* is to unlearn, to forget how to do something, to lose an ability through disuse. You once knew it, but the knowledge has been *ver-*'d — carried off in the wrong direction, displaced from your grasp. A pianist who stops practicing *verlernt* the difficult passages. A language not spoken for years is slowly *verlernt*.

Literal mnemonic: "un-learn" or "learn away." English "unlearn" captures the idea perfectly — the prefix negates and reverses the original act. What was learned is now mis-placed, gone.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays processual; the prefixes mark endpoints and reversals.** *Lernen* never stops meaning "to learn." But the prefixes define where that learning goes. *Erlernen* drives it to completion. *Verlernen* reverses it into loss. *Umlernen* restarts it on a new heading. *Auslernen* exhausts it. The bare stem is an open road; the prefixes tell you where the road ends.

**Separable prefixes stay concrete and directional.** *An-, aus-, um-* are words that can stand alone. *Anlernen* trains someone onto a skill. *Auslernen* finishes the training out. *Umlernen* turns the learning around. You can picture the spatial gesture in each case, even though the action is mental.

**Inseparable prefixes transform more deeply.** *Er-* and *ver-* fuse with the verb and shift its meaning further from the literal. *Erlernen* does not just mean "learn more" — it means "learn to the point of mastery." *Verlernen* does not mean "learn badly" — it means "lose the ability entirely." These prefixes do not add a direction; they change what the verb fundamentally achieves.

**English sometimes preserves the same logic, sometimes not.** "Unlearn" maps neatly onto *verlernen*. "Retrain" captures *umlernen*. But English has no single word for *erlernen* — we must say "master" or "acquire fully," losing the connection to the root verb "learn." And where German says *auslernen* (learn out, finish training), English switches to "complete an apprenticeship" — an entirely different construction. Notice where the parallels hold and where German extends the prefix logic further than English does.
