# REIHEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Reihen** means to line up, to arrange in a row, to string. The core image is placing things one after another so they form a sequence — beads on a thread, people in a line, events in order. Hold this picture: a hand threading objects onto a cord, each one taking its place beside the last. The three prefixes below adjust where and how that sequencing happens.

---

## The Prefixes

### AN- ("on, onto, up against")

**Anreihen** (sep.): to row onto, to join the row. The prefix directs the action toward contact with an existing sequence. You do not start a new line — you attach yourself to one already formed. *Sich anreihen* means to join the queue, to line up behind the others. The image is of one more bead sliding onto the string, one more person stepping to the back of the line.

Literal mnemonic: "row on." You row yourself on to what is already there, the way English says "tack on to the end."

### AUF- ("up, upon, open")

**Aufreihen** (sep.): to row up, to string up onto. The prefix lifts the action onto a surface or support. Pearls are *aufgereiht* — strung up onto a thread. Soldiers are *aufgereiht* — lined up in formation. Where the bare stem simply means arranging in sequence, *auf-* emphasizes the act of mounting each item into its place along the line. The row is assembled deliberately, piece by piece, up onto whatever holds it together.

Literal mnemonic: "string up" or "line up." Think of stringing beads up onto a necklace or lining items up along a shelf.

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Einreihen** (sep.): to row in, to place into a row. The prefix directs the action inward — into an existing structure. Two senses follow from the same gesture. First, to classify or categorize: placing something into a rank or category is slotting it into its proper row among others. Second, to join or fall in line: *sich einreihen* means to insert yourself into the formation. In both cases you take something loose and file it into an ordered arrangement.

Literal mnemonic: "row in." Filing a document means slotting it in among others; falling in line means stepping in to the formation. English "enroll" (from French *enrôler*, to enter onto a roll) captures a similar idea — placing a name into a list.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays concrete; the prefixes stay close to it.** *Reihen* is a small, tidy family. Unlike stems such as *legen* or *stellen*, it has not been stretched into dozens of abstract senses. All three prefixed forms preserve the physical image of arranging things in sequence. The abstraction is mild: *einreihen* extends "placing into a row" to "classifying," but even that metaphor is visible — a category is a mental row.

**All three prefixes are separable and spatial.** *An-, auf-, ein-* are concrete directional particles. They tell you where the sequencing happens: *onto* the existing line (*an-*), *up onto* a support (*auf-*), or *into* a structure (*ein-*). There are no inseparable, opaque prefixes here — the family is transparent from end to end.

**The prefixes shade a single gesture rather than transforming it.** With a large family like *legen*, prefixes can push the stem into unrecognizable territory (*widerlegen* = to refute). Here, every variant still plainly means "to put things in a row." The differences are subtle and positional: joining the row (*an-*), assembling the row (*auf-*), fitting into the row (*ein-*). Learning the family is less about memorizing new meanings and more about feeling which spatial direction the sequencing takes.

**English parallels help.** "Line up" covers *aufreihen* and part of *anreihen*. "Fall in" or "fall in line" maps neatly onto *sich einreihen*. "String together" matches *aufreihen* when the row is a thread. Where German uses a different prefix for each nuance, English tends to rely on the same two or three phrasal verbs — but the underlying spatial logic is shared.
