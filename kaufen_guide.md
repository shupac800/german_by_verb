# KAUFEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Kaufen** means to buy, to purchase, to acquire. It implies a deliberate exchange — handing over money (or something of value) to take possession of something. Hold this image in mind: a hand reaching out with payment and receiving goods in return. Nearly every prefix below reshapes that basic transaction — redirecting it, intensifying it, reversing it, or abstracting it into metaphor.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abkaufen:** to buy off, to buy from. The prefix pulls the action away from someone — you buy something *off* another person. The focus shifts from the goods to the seller: the transaction removes something from their possession. *Ich habe ihm das Auto abgekauft* — I bought the car off him. Colloquially, *jemandem etwas abkaufen* can also mean to buy someone's story — to accept what they are telling you, as if purchasing their words.

Literal mnemonic: "buy off." English uses this naturally — "I bought it off a friend."

### AN- ("on, onto, up against")

**Ankaufen:** to buy onto, to buy into one's collection. The prefix pushes the purchase toward accumulation — you are adding something to your holdings by buying it. Where plain *kaufen* is a simple transaction, *ankaufen* suggests a deliberate acquisition, often of property, art, or assets. A museum *kauft* a painting *an* — it purchases it as an intentional addition to its collection.

Literal mnemonic: "buy on" in the sense of acquiring and attaching to what you already have. Think of building up a collection one purchase at a time.

### AUF- ("up, upon, complete")

**Aufkaufen:** to buy up. The prefix drives the action toward totality — you buy everything available, sweeping the market clean. A company *kauft* its competitors *auf* — it buys them up, absorbing them. An investor *kauft* all the stock *auf*. The gesture is upward and exhaustive: you buy until there is nothing left to buy.

Literal mnemonic: "buy up." English works identically — "they bought up all the land" means they purchased every available parcel.

### AUS- ("out, outward, empty")

**Auskaufen:** to buy out. The prefix pushes the action outward until the source is emptied. You buy until the store is bought out — its shelves stripped bare. *Ausverkauft* (sold out) is the past participle of *ausverkaufen*, the reflexive cousin. The image is of draining a vessel: the buying continues outward until nothing remains inside.

Literal mnemonic: "buy out." "They bought out the entire stock" — every last item was purchased and removed.

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Einkaufen:** to buy in. The prefix directs the purchase inward — you bring goods into your household, into your pantry, into your life. *Einkaufen* is the everyday German word for shopping: going out and bringing things in. *Ich muss noch einkaufen* — I still need to do the shopping. *Einkaufen gehen* — to go shopping. The prefix domesticates the transaction: the focus is not on the exchange itself but on stocking up, filling the home.

Literal mnemonic: "buy in." English preserves this in "buying in supplies" — bringing provisions inward for storage and use.

### ER- (achievement at a cost, completion through effort)

**Erkaufen:** to buy through sacrifice, to purchase at a price. *Er-* signals that the acquisition was hard-won — something was paid beyond mere money. Freedom is *erkauft* with blood. Victory is *erkauft* at a terrible cost. The prefix converts the neutral act of buying into a transaction that exacts a deeper toll. Where *kaufen* is commercial, *erkaufen* is existential.

Mnemonic: "buy dearly." The thing was obtained, but the price was steep. Think of the English phrase "a hard-bought victory."

### LOS- ("loose, free, released")

**Loskaufen:** to buy loose, to buy free. The prefix means release — and the buying is the mechanism of that release. You pay to set someone free. A prisoner is *losgekauft* — ransomed. A hostage is *losgekauft* — redeemed through payment. The image is of bonds being severed by the act of purchase: money cuts the rope.

Literal mnemonic: "buy loose" or "buy free." English "ransom" captures the concept, but the German is more transparent: you literally buy someone into looseness.

### VER- (transformation, reversal)

**Verkaufen:** to sell. This is the most dramatic prefix transformation in the set. *Ver-* reverses the direction of the transaction entirely: where *kaufen* means to buy, *verkaufen* means to sell. The prefix functions as a transformer — it takes the act of buying and flips it to the other side of the counter. *Ich verkaufe mein Auto* — I am selling my car. The seller was once the buyer's counterpart; *ver-* captures that mirror relationship.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "away into change." When you *verkaufen*, you change the direction of the exchange — goods move away from you, money moves toward you. The fundamental act of *kaufen* is inverted.

### ZU- ("to, toward, additional")

**Zukaufen:** to buy additionally, to buy on top of. The prefix pushes an additional purchase toward what already exists. You already have some; now you *kaufen zu* — you buy more to supplement. A company *kauft* expertise *zu* — it acquires additional capability. A farmer *kauft* land *zu* — adding to existing holdings. The gesture is accumulative: layering new purchases onto prior ones.

Literal mnemonic: "buy to" in the sense of "add to by buying." Think of topping up — each *Zukauf* supplements what is already there.

### ZURÜCK- ("back, backward, in return")

**Zurückkaufen:** to buy back. The prefix reverses the direction of a prior transaction — what was once sold is purchased back. A company *kauft* its own shares *zurück* — it repurchases them. A seller *kauft* an item *zurück* from a dissatisfied customer. The image is circular: the goods travel away through a sale and return through a repurchase. The buying goes backward along the same path.

Literal mnemonic: "buy back." English works identically — "the company bought back its stock" = *Das Unternehmen hat seine Aktien zurückgekauft.*

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays commercial; the prefixes shift the frame.** *Kaufen* never stops meaning "to buy." But the prefixes redirect who benefits, what is at stake, and how the transaction is oriented. *Einkaufen* (buy in) is domestic. *Erkaufen* (buy at a cost) is existential. *Verkaufen* (buy reversed) is the seller's perspective. The same hand reaching for goods is repurposed by each prefix.

**Separable prefixes tend to be spatial and concrete.** *Ab, an, auf, aus, ein, los, zu, zurück* — these preserve a physical directionality. You can picture where the goods are going: inward (*ein*), upward and exhaustively (*auf*), back the way they came (*zurück*). Even when the usage is figurative, the spatial image provides a foothold.

**Inseparable prefixes tend to be transformative and opaque.** *Er-* and *ver-* fuse with the verb and alter its character more radically. *Erkaufen* does not just buy — it buys at a moral or existential price. *Verkaufen* does not modify the buying — it reverses it into selling entirely. These must be learned as units rather than decoded mechanically from parts.

**English often shares the same logic.** "Buy up" = *aufkaufen*. "Buy out" = *auskaufen*. "Buy back" = *zurückkaufen*. "Buy off" = *abkaufen*. Where English and German share the construction, use it as an anchor. The most instructive divergence is *verkaufen*: where German adds a prefix to "buy" to produce "sell," English uses an entirely unrelated word. German sees buying and selling as two faces of the same act; English treats them as separate verbs.
