# TRINKEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Trinken** means to drink. It is one of the most elemental human verbs — taking liquid into the body. Hold this image: a glass raised, liquid flowing inward. The prefixes below are few but sharply distinct: they redirect the drinking toward its beginning, its completion, its excess, or its fatal consequence.

---

## The Prefixes

### AN- ("on, onto, beginning")

**Antrinken:** to drink on, to start drinking. The prefix marks the onset of the action. *Sich einen antrinken* means to drink oneself into a buzz — the drinking has begun and the first effects have latched on. The *an-* here functions like "getting a start on" — you have laid the first layer of intoxication onto yourself.

Literal mnemonic: "drink on." You've drunk enough that the alcohol has taken hold — it's "on" you. Think of "getting one on" in colloquial English.

### AUS- ("out, outward, to completion")

**Austrinken:** to drink out, to drink to the end. The prefix drives the action to completion — every drop is drawn out of the glass. *Trink aus!* means "Finish your drink!" The gesture is exhaustive: the container is emptied, the liquid is drunk out entirely.

Literal mnemonic: "drink out." You drink the glass out — empty it completely. English "drink up" captures the same finality.

### BE- (intensifier / reflexive transformation)

**Betrinken:** to drink oneself into a state. *Be-* stamps the action of drinking fully onto the subject — the drinker becomes the object of the drinking. *Sich betrinken* means to get drunk, to drink until the alcohol has thoroughly overtaken you. The prefix turns drinking from an activity into a self-inflicted transformation.

Mnemonic: "be-drunk" — to make oneself drunk through drinking. The *be-* intensifies and turns the action inward: you have drunk yourself into a new state.

### ER- (completion to a fatal or terminal point)

**Ertrinken:** to drink to death — but not by drinking. *Er-* signals that the action reaches its terminal, irreversible point. Here the "drinking" is done by the lungs: water enters where air should be. To *ertrinken* is to drown — to be consumed by liquid rather than consuming it. The prefix converts *trinken* from a voluntary act of sustenance into a fatal inversion: the water drinks you.

Mnemonic: "drink to the end" — the terrible end. The *er-* marks finality, just as in *erschlagen* (struck dead), *ersticken* (suffocated). The liquid finishes you.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays physical; the prefixes modulate degree and consequence.** *Trinken* never stops meaning "to drink." But the prefixes create a spectrum from beginning (*an-*) through completion (*aus-*) to excess (*be-*) to death (*er-*). This is a tight, almost narrative arc: you start drinking, you finish your glass, you drink too much, you drown.

**Separable prefixes mark stages of the action.** *An-* begins it; *aus-* completes it. These are concrete, directional, and easy to picture.

**Inseparable prefixes mark transformation.** *Be-* transforms the drinker (sober to drunk); *er-* transforms the situation terminally (alive to dead). These are the deep, fused prefixes where the action is no longer about the glass but about what happens to the person.

**English sometimes preserves the same logic.** "Drink up" = *austrinken*. "Get drunk" = *sich betrinken*. "Drown" requires a completely different English word — German keeps *trinken* visible inside *ertrinken*, making the watery death transparent: to be "drunk in" by water. This is one of those places where German's compositional logic is more vivid than English's.
