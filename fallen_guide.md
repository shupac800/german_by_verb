# FALLEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Fallen** means to fall — a direct English cognate from the same Germanic root. Where *legen* implied deliberate placement by a hand, *fallen* implies the opposite: uncontrolled downward motion, the surrender to gravity, the loss of footing or position. Hold this image in mind: something dropping from where it stood. Nearly every prefix below redirects that plunge — giving it a direction, a cause, a figurative target, or an entirely metaphorical dimension.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abfallen:** to fall off, to fall away. The prefix pulls the falling motion outward from an attachment point. A leaf *fällt ab* — it falls off the branch. Terrain *fällt ab* — it falls away, slopes downward from where you stand. From this physical image come the figurative senses: quality *fällt ab* — it drops off, declines. A follower *fällt ab* — they fall away from their leader, defecting, deserting, apostatizing. The common thread is separation through descent: something that was attached or aligned drops away.

Literal mnemonic: "fall off." English uses this identically — "fall off a cliff," "fall off in quality," "fall away from the faith."

### AN- ("on, onto, up against")

**Anfallen:** to fall on, to fall upon. The prefix directs the fall toward a target. An attacker *fällt an* — springs upon, falls against someone. But the second meaning is equally logical: costs *fallen an* — they fall onto you, they accrue, they land on your account. In both cases something arrives by falling — violently or administratively — and lands against you.

Literal mnemonic: "fall on." English preserves both senses: "the dog fell on the intruder" and "the costs fall on the buyer."

### AUF- ("up, upon, open")

**Auffallen:** to fall up into notice, to stand out. This is one of the family's most vivid metaphors. The prefix reverses the expected direction — instead of falling down and vanishing, something falls *up*, rises into the visual field, catches the eye. A bright coat *fällt auf* — it falls upward into attention. A mistake *fällt auf* — it pops up, becomes conspicuous. The logic: what falls up cannot be ignored.

Literal mnemonic: "fall up." Imagine something leaping off the ground into your line of sight — that is *auffallen*. Where most things that fall disappear, what *auffällt* insists on being seen.

### AUS- ("out, outward")

**Ausfallen:** to fall out. The prefix sends the fall outward, out of its expected place. Hair *fällt aus* — it falls out of the scalp. Teeth *fallen aus* — they drop out of the jaw. A class *fällt aus* — it falls out of the schedule, is cancelled. An exam *fällt gut aus* — it falls out well, turns out a certain way. The unifying image is departure from a container or plan: whatever falls out is no longer where it was supposed to be.

Literal mnemonic: "fall out." English matches perfectly — "my hair is falling out," "the event fell out of the calendar," "it fell out well."

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Befallen:** to fall upon thoroughly, to afflict. As with *belegen*, the *be-* prefix stamps the action fully onto an object — it takes the intransitive "fall" and makes it transitive, giving it a victim. Disease *befällt* a population — it falls upon them, overtakes them. Fear *befällt* someone — it descends on them completely. English preserves this verb directly: "to befall" means exactly the same thing.

Mnemonic: "befall." This is the rare case where no translation is needed — the English word *is* the German word, inherited intact.

### DURCH- ("through")

**Durchfallen:** to fall through. The prefix sends the fall downward through a barrier. Literally, something drops through a gap. Figuratively, a student *fällt durch* — they fall through the exam, they fail, they flunk. A proposal *fällt durch* — it falls through the vote, is rejected. The image is of a sieve: what is too small or too weak passes through and is lost.

Literal mnemonic: "fall through." English uses it the same way — "the deal fell through," "she fell through the cracks."

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Einfallen:** to fall in, to fall inward. This prefix produces a fascinating cluster of meanings, all radiating from one physical image — a structure collapsing inward:

First, the literal: a roof *fällt ein* — it caves in, collapses, falls into itself. Cheeks *fallen ein* — they fall inward, become hollow and sunken.

Second, the cognitive: an idea *fällt mir ein* — it falls into my mind, it occurs to me. The image is of a thought dropping through an opening into consciousness, uninvited, sudden. You do not summon the idea; it falls in.

Third, the military: an army *fällt ein* — it falls into foreign territory, it invades, it raids. The border is breached and forces pour inward.

The logic connecting all three: a boundary is crossed from outside to inside. A roof breaks inward. A thought breaks into awareness. An army breaks into a country. The direction is always the same — through a barrier, into an interior.

Literal mnemonic: "fall in." English "cave-in" captures the first sense. "It fell into my head" captures the second. "The invaders fell in upon the city" captures the third.

### ENT- ("away from, removed from")

**Entfallen:** to fall away from, to escape. The prefix pulls the fall away from the subject's grasp. A name *entfällt* mir — it falls away from my memory, it slips my mind. A requirement *entfällt* — it falls away from the list, is dropped, ceases to apply. The gesture is always one of loss through release: something that should have been held falls away and is gone.

Literal mnemonic: "fall from." Think of a coin falling from your hand — you had it, and it escaped.

### FORT- ("away, onward, gone")

**Fortfallen:** to fall away, to be discontinued. Close to *entfallen* but more administrative: a regulation *fällt fort* — it falls away, is omitted, ceases to exist. A benefit *fällt fort* — it is dropped, discontinued. The prefix carries the fall forward and out of existence.

Literal mnemonic: "fall forth" in the archaic sense of departing. What *fortfällt* has fallen away and is gone.

### GE- (past participle prefix — here fossilized into a new verb)

**Gefallen:** to please, to be pleasing. This is the family's most surprising member. How did "fallen" become "pleasing"? The likely path runs through the old sense of *fallen* as "to turn out, to happen" (still visible in *ausfallen*). Something that *gefällt* has "fallen out well" in someone's judgment — it has landed favorably, settled agreeably. The *ge-* prefix, originally a completive marker, sealed this into a fixed meaning: the thing has fallen into a state that suits you. Over centuries the connection to physical falling faded entirely, leaving only the judgment — *es gefällt mir*, it pleases me, it has landed well with me.

Mnemonic: think of something "falling into favor." A painting *gefällt* you — it has fallen into your good graces, landed right in your aesthetic sweet spot. The opposite, *missfallen*, confirms the logic: what falls wrongly displeases.

### HERAUS- ("out from within")

**Herausfallen:** to fall out from inside. A child *fällt aus dem Bett heraus* — tumbles out of bed. A word *fällt heraus* — slips out of conversation. The prefix specifies direction: outward from an enclosed space toward the speaker.

Literal mnemonic: "fall out (from in here)." Straightforward and physical.

### HER- ("hither, toward the speaker")

**Herfallen:** to fall upon, to pounce on. The prefix draws the fall toward the speaker's position — someone or something descends on a target. Wolves *fallen über die Beute her* — they fall upon the prey. Guests *fallen über das Buffet her* — they descend upon the food. The image is of a predatory drop — a hawk falling toward its target.

Literal mnemonic: "fall upon (toward here)." English "fall upon" captures the same aggressive arrival.

### HIN- ("thither, down, toward a point away from the speaker")

**Hinfallen:** to fall down, to tumble to the ground. The prefix gives direction: downward and away. A child *fällt hin* — trips and hits the ground. This is the simplest, most physical "fall" in the family — the basic human experience of losing balance and going down.

Literal mnemonic: "fall down (over there)." "She fell down" = *Sie ist hingefallen.*

### MISS- ("wrongly, badly")

**Missfallen:** to displease, to fall badly. The prefix marks the falling as wrong — what *missfällt* has fallen unfavorably, has landed badly in someone's judgment. This is the exact mirror of *gefallen*: where *gefallen* is falling into favor, *missfallen* is falling into disfavor.

Literal mnemonic: "misfall." If something "falls well" pleases, then something that "misfalls" displeases. The symmetry is clean.

### UM- ("over, around, down")

**Umfallen:** to fall over, to topple. The prefix tips the fall sideways — from upright to flat. A tree *fällt um* — it falls over. A person *fällt um* — they keel over, collapse. Figuratively, someone who *umfällt* in an argument has toppled, caved, reversed their position.

Literal mnemonic: "fall over." English works identically — "the vase fell over," "he fell over from exhaustion."

### VER- (transformation, displacement, intensification)

**Verfallen:** to fall into ruin, to decay, to expire. The *ver-* prefix transforms the fall into a process of deterioration — a slow, irreversible plunge. A building *verfällt* — it falls progressively into disrepair, decays. A coupon *verfällt* — it falls past its validity, expires, lapses. A person *verfällt* einer Sucht — they fall into an addiction, become enslaved to it. As an adjective, *verfallen* means dilapidated, ruined, or hopelessly addicted. In every case, the fall is not a single event but a gradual, terminal descent.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "away into irreversible change." A *verfallenes* house has fallen away from soundness. An expired ticket has fallen past its date. An addict has fallen away into dependency.

### VOR- ("before, forward, in front of")

**Vorfallen:** to fall before, to occur. Something *fällt vor* — it falls forward into view, it happens, it takes place. An incident *ist vorgefallen* — it has occurred, has presented itself. In medicine, an organ *fällt vor* — it prolapses, falls forward out of its cavity. Both senses share the same spatial logic: something emerges from its usual place and falls into the open.

Literal mnemonic: "fall forth." An event that "falls forth" has come forward into reality.

### WIDER- ("against, counter" — here as WIDERFAHREN)

**Widerfahren:** to befall, to happen to. Strictly, the stem here shifts to *fahren* (to travel, to go), but the word belongs to this semantic family. Something *widerfährt* you — it comes against you, encounters you, happens to you. Justice *widerfährt* someone — it meets them. An injustice *widerfährt* someone — it strikes against them. The prefix marks the event as arriving in opposition to the subject's path — something that comes at you rather than something you sought.

Literal mnemonic: "come against." What *widerfährt* you is what fate drives against your course.

### ZER- ("apart, to pieces, asunder")

**Zerfallen:** to fall apart, to disintegrate. The prefix scatters the fall — instead of a single downward drop, the thing breaks into pieces as it goes. A wall *zerfällt* — it crumbles, decomposes. A substance *zerfällt* — it decays into component parts. An empire *zerfällt* — it breaks up, fragments. Where *verfallen* is slow deterioration of a whole, *zerfallen* is the moment the whole ceases to be whole — it fractures into parts.

Literal mnemonic: "fall asunder." English "fall apart" is the exact equivalent — "the old barn is falling apart."

### ZU- ("to, toward, closed")

**Zufallen:** to fall shut, to fall to someone. The prefix has two distinct forces here. First, the physical: a door *fällt zu* — it falls to, swings closed, slams shut by gravity. The fall's direction is toward closure. Second, the distributive: a prize *fällt jemandem zu* — it falls to someone, accrues to them, is awarded. An inheritance *fällt zu* — it falls toward the heir. One image is a closing; the other is an arriving. Both involve falling toward a destination.

Literal mnemonic: "fall to." "The door fell to" (shut). "The honor fell to her" (was awarded). English preserves both.

### ZURÜCK- ("back, backward")

**Zurückfallen:** to fall back, to relapse. The prefix reverses the fall — backward, to a previous position. A runner *fällt zurück* — falls back behind the pack. A patient *fällt zurück* — relapses, falls back into illness. A city *fällt zurück* — reverts to its former owner. The motion is always rearward: ground that was gained is lost, progress is undone.

Literal mnemonic: "fall back." English is identical — "fall back to old habits," "fall behind."

### ZUSAMMEN- ("together")

**Zusammenfallen:** to fall together. Two meanings from one image. First, physical collapse: a tent *fällt zusammen* — it crumples, caves in, falls in on itself. The parts fall together into a heap. Second, temporal coincidence: two events *fallen zusammen* — they fall together in time, they coincide. In both cases, separate things converge into one — either destructively (collapse) or neutrally (coincidence).

Literal mnemonic: "fall together." A collapsing building falls together into rubble. Two birthdays that fall together coincide.

### ÜBER- ("over, upon, beyond")

**Überfallen:** to fall over upon, to ambush. The prefix sends the fall over and down onto a victim — the motion of a predator dropping from above. Robbers *überfallen* a bank — they fall upon it, raid it, hold it up. Sleep *überfällt* you — it falls over you suddenly, catches you off guard. The attack is always sudden, always from a position of advantage, always overwhelming. English preserves the same metaphor perfectly in military language: "to fall upon the enemy."

Literal mnemonic: "fall over/upon." "The bandits fell upon the travelers" — *Die Banditen überfielen die Reisenden.* The parallel is exact.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**English and German share more "fall" metaphors than almost any other verb family.** "Fall off," "fall through," "befall," "fall upon," "fall apart," "fall back," "fall out" — in case after case, the English phrasal verb and the German prefix verb use the same spatial logic. This makes *fallen* one of the most learner-friendly verb families in German. Use the English parallels as anchors; they are reliable here.

**"Falling" metaphors cluster around three domains: destruction, cognition, and judgment.** Destruction is natural — what falls breaks (*zerfallen, zusammenfallen, einfallen, verfallen*). But the cognitive senses are equally rich: *einfallen* (an idea falls into the mind), *auffallen* (something falls up into notice), *entfallen* (a fact falls away from memory). And the judgment pair *gefallen/missfallen* shows falling as a metaphor for how something lands with you — favorably or not. These three domains repeat across the family.

**The *ge-/miss-* pair is a rare symmetry.** *Gefallen* (to please) and *missfallen* (to displease) form a clean positive-negative pair, both built on the idea of something "falling well" or "falling badly" in someone's estimation. This kind of neat opposition is unusual in German prefix verbs and worth memorizing as a unit.

**EINFALLEN is the family's most productive metaphor.** From one physical image — a roof caving inward — German derives collapse, mental occurrence, and military invasion. The connecting logic is always the same: a boundary is breached and something pours through from outside to inside. Recognizing this pattern makes all three meanings feel inevitable rather than arbitrary.

**Separable vs. inseparable follows the usual rule, with consequences.** *Überfallen* (inseparable, to ambush) keeps its prefix fused because the meaning has abstracted away from literal spatial motion. *Umfallen* (separable, to topple over) keeps its prefix loose because you can still picture the physical direction. *Befallen* and *gefallen* are inseparable because *be-* and the fossilized *ge-* always are. As with *legen*, the inseparable prefixes tend to carry meanings further from the physical stem.
