# HELFEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Helfen** means to help, to assist, to aid. It is a direct cognate of English "help" — one of those core Germanic verbs that survived unchanged in both languages. Hold this image in mind: one person extending a hand to another. The prefixes below mostly redirect that gesture — specifying the direction, duration, or goal of the assistance — but they rarely abstract it far from its root meaning. Helping stays helping.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abhelfen:** to remedy, to fix, to put right. The prefix pulls help away from a person and aims it at a problem. You do not help *someone* — you help *off* a deficiency, a grievance, an evil. A *Missstand* (abuse) is *abgeholfen* — the help removes it, lifts it off. The gesture is subtractive: the problem was sitting there, and the help takes it away.

Literal mnemonic: "help off." Think of helping a problem off your back — lifting it away and discarding it.

### AUF- ("up, upward")

**Aufhelfen:** to help up, to give a hand up. The most physical of the prefixed forms. Someone has fallen; you extend your hand and help them *auf* — upward, back to their feet. The prefix simply adds vertical direction to the basic gesture of assistance.

Literal mnemonic: "help up." English uses the identical construction — "help someone up off the ground."

### AUS- ("out, outward")

**Aushelfen:** to help out, to lend a hand, to fill in. The prefix sends help outward from yourself toward someone else's need. You step out of your own routine to cover for a colleague, to fill a gap, to stand in temporarily. The help is directed outward and is usually short-term — you are not taking over, just bridging a shortage.

Literal mnemonic: "help out." English works the same way — "Can you help out at the shop today?" means exactly *aushelfen*.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Behelfen** (reflexive: *sich behelfen*): to make do, to manage, to get by, to improvise. This is the interesting one. *Be-* turns the help inward and thorough — you help yourself all over, but with insufficient means. *Sich behelfen* implies that proper resources are lacking: you patch together what you have, you improvise a solution, you manage with less. The help is complete in effort but inadequate in material. A student *behilft sich* with a borrowed textbook. A traveler *behilft sich* with broken phrasebook German.

Mnemonic: think of "be-" as "thoroughly, all over." You are helping yourself thoroughly — but only because no one else is helping and you do not have what you really need. Self-sufficient resourcefulness born from necessity.

### DURCH- ("through")

**Durchhelfen:** to help through, to see someone through. The prefix adds a passage: help is not a single act but sustained support across a difficult stretch. You help someone *through* an illness, *through* a crisis, *through* an exam. The help accompanies the person from one side of the difficulty to the other.

Literal mnemonic: "help through." English uses the same image — "She helped me through a rough time."

### HERAUS- ("out from within")

**Heraushelfen:** to help out of, to extricate, to get someone out of a difficulty. Stronger than *aushelfen* — the prefix specifies that the person is trapped *inside* something and the help pulls them out. You help someone *heraus* out of a predicament, out of debt, out of a tight spot. The gesture is extractive: reaching into a bad situation and pulling someone free.

Literal mnemonic: "help out (from in there)." Think of reaching into a hole to pull someone out.

### HINWEG- ("away, past, over")

**Hinweghelfen:** to help over, to help someone get past something. The prefix points beyond an obstacle. The difficulty is not removed (that would be *abhelfen*); instead, the person is helped over it, past it, so they can continue. You help someone *hinweg* over a loss, over a disappointment, over a setback. The grief remains, but the person moves past it with your help.

Literal mnemonic: "help over and away." Think of helping someone climb over a wall and continue on their way.

### MIT- ("with, along, together")

**Mithelfen:** to help along, to pitch in, to lend a hand. The prefix means participation — you join in with others who are already working. You do not lead the effort; you add your hands to it. *Mithelfen* is communal: everyone *hilft mit* when the barn needs raising.

Literal mnemonic: "help with." English says "help with the dishes" — *mithelfen* carries the same spirit of joining in.

### NACH- ("after, following, additional")

**Nachhelfen:** to give a nudge, to help along, to assist further. The prefix suggests a secondary push — the process was already underway but stalled or slowed, and you supply additional help to keep it moving. You *helfen nach* when luck needs a nudge, when a stuck drawer needs a tap, when a student needs extra tutoring. The help comes *after* the initial effort and supplements it.

Literal mnemonic: "help after" or "help along." Think of giving a cart a push when it slows on a hill — helping it along after it started.

### VER- (transformation, redirection)

**Verhelfen:** to help someone obtain something, to enable, to assist in getting. Here *ver-* transforms the basic act of helping into targeted enablement. The help is not general kindness — it is directed *toward a specific result*. You *verhelfen* someone *zu* something: to a job, to a victory, to their rights. The prefix converts helping from an open gesture into a transactional one: the help has a deliverable.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "into a changed state." *Verhelfen* means to help someone into possession of something they did not have before. The help transforms their situation.

### WEG- ("away, gone")

**Weghelfen:** to help away, to help someone get away. The simplest directional addition — the help moves someone or something away from where they are. You help someone away from a bad situation, away from danger, away from a place they no longer want to be.

Literal mnemonic: "help away." The direction is departure: the help enables leaving.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem resists abstraction.** Unlike *legen*, which gets stretched into metaphors for refutation (*widerlegen*), publication (*verlegen*), and deliberation (*überlegen*), *helfen* stays remarkably close to its literal meaning across all prefixes. Helping up, helping out, helping through, helping over — the prefixes redirect the direction of assistance, but the core act remains concrete. You can picture a hand reaching in every case.

**English often has the same construction.** "Help up" = *aufhelfen*. "Help out" = *aushelfen*. "Help through" = *durchhelfen*. This is one of the easier prefix families precisely because the spatial metaphors translate almost directly. Where English would say "help someone through a crisis," German says *jemandem durch eine Krise durchhelfen* — the logic is identical.

**The two inseparable prefixes are the most interesting.** *Behelfen* and *verhelfen* are the forms where the prefix does more than point a direction. *Be-* turns helping reflexive and desperate — making do, improvising, helping yourself because you must. *Ver-* turns helping instrumental — enabling a specific outcome, transforming the recipient's situation. These two are worth learning as vocabulary items rather than decoding on the fly.

**Abhelfen shifts the object of help.** Most *helfen* compounds help a person. *Abhelfen* helps a *problem* — it remedies a situation rather than assisting an individual. This makes it structurally unusual in the family and worth noting as an exception to the pattern.
