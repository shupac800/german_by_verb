# WENDEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Wenden** means to turn, to turn over, to reverse. It implies rotating or redirecting something — changing its orientation or direction. Hold this image in mind: a hand turning a page, flipping a pancake, or spinning a steering wheel. Every prefix below redirects that turning motion — away from danger, toward a purpose, into opposition, or out of someone's possession.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abwenden:** to turn away, to turn off. The prefix pulls the turning gesture away. You *wenden* your gaze *ab* — you turn it away, you avert your eyes. You *wenden* danger *ab* — you turn it away, you ward it off. Whether the object is physical (a face turned away) or abstract (a catastrophe deflected), the motion is the same: turning something so it faces elsewhere.

Literal mnemonic: "turn off" or "turn away." English "avert" (from Latin *avertere*, to turn away) is the exact equivalent.

### AN- ("on, onto, toward")

**Anwenden:** to turn onto, to apply. The prefix directs the turning gesture toward a purpose. You take a method or a tool and *wenden* it *an* — you turn it onto the problem, you apply it. Knowledge is *angewandt* — applied, turned onto practical matters. The image is of rotating a tool so its working edge meets the surface.

Literal mnemonic: "turn onto." You turn a technique onto a task — you apply it.

### AUF- ("up, upon, consuming")

**Aufwenden:** to turn up, to use up. The prefix adds a sense of consumption — you turn resources into the task until they are spent. You *wenden* time, money, or effort *auf* — you expend it, you spend it on something. The image is of pouring out, turning everything upward until the vessel is empty.

Literal mnemonic: "turn up" in the sense of "use up." You turn your reserves upward and out until they are gone.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Bewenden:** to let it turn where it will. This is an unusual *be-* verb — it survives almost exclusively in the phrase *es dabei bewenden lassen* (to leave it at that, to let the matter rest). The idea is that the turning has reached a point where you let it be, allow it to settle where it stands. The *be-* stamps completeness onto the turning: it has turned enough.

Mnemonic: "let it be turned." The matter has turned to where it is, and you leave it there.

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Einwenden:** to turn in against, to object. The prefix directs the turning inward, against a position. You *wenden* an argument *ein* — you insert a counter-turn, an objection. The gesture is of someone turning into the flow of a discussion to block it, to raise a protest. An *Einwand* (objection) is literally something turned inward against a claim.

Literal mnemonic: "turn in (against)." You turn inward to push back — you object.

### ENT- ("away from, removal")

**Entwenden:** to turn away from the owner, to steal. The *ent-* prefix removes something by turning it away from its rightful place. To *entwenden* is to spirit something away — to purloin, to pilfer. The word has a formal, almost literary quality. The image is of something being deftly turned out of someone's possession and into your own.

Literal mnemonic: "turn away from." The object is turned away from its owner — stolen, spirited off.

### HIN- ("thither, toward a point")

**Hinwenden:** to turn toward, to direct toward. The prefix points the turning gesture at a specific destination. You *wenden* yourself *hin* — you turn toward something, you face it. A simple, physical directional compound.

Literal mnemonic: "turn to (over there)." You turn your body or attention toward a point in the distance.

### UM- ("around, over, reversal")

**Umwenden:** to turn around, to turn over. The prefix adds full rotation — not just a partial turn but a complete reversal. You *wenden* a steak *um* — you flip it over. You *wenden* sich *um* — you turn yourself around to face the other way. The prefix intensifies the turning into a full revolution or inversion.

Literal mnemonic: "turn around." You turn all the way around — 180 degrees.

### VER- (transformation, consumption, intensification)

**Verwenden:** to turn to use, to employ. The *ver-* prefix transforms the turning into purposeful deployment. You *verwenden* materials — you turn them to use, consume them in service of something. The word is close to *anwenden* but emphasizes the using up or channeling of the thing turned. Where *anwenden* stresses application of a method, *verwenden* stresses utilization of a resource.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "through, into change." You turn something through a process until it has been fully used.

### WEG- ("away, gone")

**Wegwenden:** to turn away. The simplest "away" prefix — you turn your face or body away and leave it turned. No metaphor, no abstraction. You *wenden* yourself *weg* — you turn away, you avert your gaze.

Literal mnemonic: "turn away (and gone)." The face is turned away and stays turned.

### ZU- ("to, toward")

**Zuwenden:** to turn toward. You direct the turning motion at something or someone. You *wenden* your attention *zu* — you turn toward, you devote. You *wenden* resources someone *zu* — you turn them toward that person, you give to them. The prefix creates a directed, purposeful turn: facing something and committing to it.

Literal mnemonic: "turn to." "She turned to face him" = *Sie wandte sich ihm zu.* "To devote resources to a cause" = to turn them toward it.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays rotational; the prefix specifies the direction.** *Wenden* never stops meaning "to turn." But the prefixes aim that turning in different directions. *Abwenden* (turn away) becomes "to avert." *Anwenden* (turn onto) becomes "to apply." *Zuwenden* (turn toward) becomes "to devote." The hand is always turning — only the destination changes.

**Separable prefixes tend to be spatial and concrete.** *Ab, an, auf, ein, hin, um, weg, zu* — these preserve a directional quality you can visualize. *Abwenden* turns away; *umwenden* turns around; *zuwenden* turns toward.

**Inseparable prefixes tend to be transformative.** *Be-, ent-, ver-* fuse with the verb and shift the meaning further from physical turning. *Entwenden* (turn away from) becomes "to steal." *Verwenden* (turn through use) becomes "to utilize." These require learning as units.

**English often shares the logic through Latin.** "Avert" = turn away = *abwenden*. "Convert" = turn with/together. "Divert" = turn aside. "Revert" = turn back. English uses Latin *vertere* (to turn) where German uses *wenden*. The prefix logic is often identical — only the root word differs. Recognizing this parallel makes the German compounds far less arbitrary.
