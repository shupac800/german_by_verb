# ZEICHNEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Zeichnen** means to draw, to sketch, to depict. It implies making marks that represent something — lines on paper that create an image or convey meaning. Hold this image in mind: a hand moving a pen across a surface, leaving a deliberate mark. The word shares its root with *Zeichen* (sign, mark), so every compound below is fundamentally about marking, signing, or signifying.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abzeichnen:** to draw off, to mark off. Two distinct images emerge from the same prefix. First, the copying sense: you draw off from an original — you reproduce it, you initial a document (mark it off as reviewed). Second, the emerging sense: something *zeichnet sich ab* — it draws itself off from the background, it looms, it becomes visible, it takes shape. In both cases, a mark separates from its source and becomes distinct.

Literal mnemonic: "draw off." A copy is "drawn off" from the original. A shape "draws off" from the fog.

### AN- ("on, onto, upon")

**Anzeichnen:** to draw on, to mark on. The prefix pushes the mark onto a surface — you *zeichnen an* a wall, a board, a piece of wood. It means to mark, to indicate, to note by making a visible sign on something. The gesture is simple: pencil goes onto surface.

Literal mnemonic: "mark on." "Mark it on the board" — same logic.

### AUF- ("up, upon, onto a record")

**Aufzeichnen:** to draw upon a record, to record. The prefix lifts the marking action onto a lasting medium — paper, tape, a log. You *zeichnen auf* a conversation (record it), events (write them down), data (plot it). The mark is preserved, stored, made permanent.

Literal mnemonic: "set down on record." Think of "writing up" — committing something to a permanent surface.

### AUS- ("out, outward, distinguishing")

**Auszeichnen:** to mark out, to distinguish. The prefix pulls one item out from the group by marking it. To *auszeichnen* with a medal is to single someone out for honor — to mark them as outstanding. To *auszeichnen* merchandise is to mark it with a price — to distinguish each item with its cost. In both cases, the marking sets something apart from the rest.

Literal mnemonic: "mark out." English "outstanding" carries the same spatial logic — standing out from the crowd.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Bezeichnen:** to mark thoroughly, to designate. *Be-* stamps the marking action fully onto an object — you cover it with a label, a name, a designation. To *bezeichnen* something is to call it by a specific term, to denote it, to signify what it is. The prefix makes the mark definitive: this thing is now named, categorized, labeled.

Mnemonic: "be-" = "all over, definitively." To *bezeichnen* is to place a definitive mark upon — to designate.

### DURCH- ("through")

**Durchzeichnen:** to draw through, to trace. The prefix sends the drawing action through a translucent surface — you lay paper over an image and draw through, reproducing the lines beneath. Simple, physical, transparent.

Literal mnemonic: "draw through." Tracing is drawing through paper laid on top.

### EIN- ("in, into")

**Einzeichnen:** to draw in, to mark in. The prefix directs the mark inward — into a map, into a diagram, into an existing drawing. You add a detail by drawing it into the whole. A route is *eingezeichnet* on a map — drawn into the existing picture.

Literal mnemonic: "draw in." "Draw it in on the map" — the mark goes into an existing framework.

### NACH- ("after, following")

**Nachzeichnen:** to draw after, to copy, to trace. The prefix means following — you draw after an original, reproducing its lines. Similar to *durchzeichnen* but the emphasis is on following the model rather than the physical act of tracing through paper.

Literal mnemonic: "draw after." You draw after the original, following its lead.

### UM- ("around, re-, anew")

**Umzeichnen:** to draw again, to redraw. The prefix implies transformation — you take the original drawing and remake it. The result is a new version, redrawn, reworked.

Literal mnemonic: "redraw." The drawing is done over, turned around into a new form.

### UNTER- ("under, beneath")

**Unterzeichnen:** to draw under, to sign. The prefix places the mark beneath — under the text, at the bottom of the document. To *unterzeichnen* is to sign, to put your name under the terms you are agreeing to. The gesture is formal: your mark goes under the document as an endorsement.

Literal mnemonic: "undersign." English "undersigned" preserves exactly this logic — "the undersigned agrees to the terms."

### VER- (transformation, displacement, distortion)

**Verzeichnen:** to mark with transformation. *Ver-* pulls the marking action in two directions. First, the recording sense: to register, to note, to catalog — the mark is placed into a formal record (*Verzeichnis* = index, directory). Second, the distortion sense: to misrepresent, to depict inaccurately — *ver-* twists the drawing so it no longer matches reality. One preserves faithfully; the other warps.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "into a register" or "into distortion." *Verzeichnen* a success = record it. *Verzeichnen* reality = distort it. Context decides.

### ZURÜCK- ("back")

**Zurückzeichnen:** to draw back, to redraw. An uncommon compound — you draw again, returning to the task.

Literal mnemonic: "draw back" in the sense of redoing.

### ÜBER- ("over, beyond, too much")

**Überzeichnen:** to draw over, to oversubscribe. When a share offering is *überzeichnet*, it has been marked for purchase beyond its available quantity — too many people have signed up, the drawing has gone over the limit.

Literal mnemonic: "over-subscribe." The signing (drawing) has exceeded the available amount.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem means both "draw" and "sign" — and the prefixes exploit both.** Some compounds lean on the drawing sense (*abzeichnen* = copy, *durchzeichnen* = trace, *nachzeichnen* = reproduce). Others lean on the signing/marking sense (*unterzeichnen* = sign, *auszeichnen* = distinguish, *bezeichnen* = designate). Keeping both senses of the stem in mind makes the family easier to navigate.

**Separable prefixes stay close to the physical act.** *Ab, an, auf, ein, nach, um, zurück* — these describe what happens to a line on paper: it is drawn off, drawn on, drawn upon, drawn in, drawn after, drawn again, drawn back. The spatial logic of the prefix maps directly onto the drawing gesture.

**Inseparable prefixes move into abstraction.** *Be-, unter-, ver-, über-* all produce meanings that have left drawing behind. *Bezeichnen* (designate), *unterzeichnen* (sign a contract), *verzeichnen* (register or distort), *überzeichnen* (oversubscribe) — these are institutional, formal, or figurative. The inseparable prefix fuses so tightly with the stem that the original image of drawing disappears.

**English "sign" and "design" share the same root as *zeichnen*.** Latin *signum* and Germanic *zeichen* are cognates. This means "designate" = *bezeichnen*, "undersigned" = *unterzeichnet*, and "sign" = *zeichnen* are not just translations but etymological cousins. Use these connections as memory anchors.
