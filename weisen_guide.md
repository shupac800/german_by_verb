# WEISEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Weisen** means to show, to point, to direct. It implies guiding attention or indicating direction — a finger pointing the way. Hold this image in mind: someone extending a hand toward something and saying "look there" or "go there." Nearly every prefix below redirects that pointing gesture — toward a rejection, a proof, a transfer, or an instruction.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abweisen:** to point away, to show off. The prefix turns the pointing gesture into a refusal — you direct someone away from you. A petitioner is *abgewiesen* — turned away, shown the door. An appeal is *abgewiesen* — dismissed, pointed off. The image is of someone being shown the direction "away."

Literal mnemonic: "show off" in the old sense of "wave off." You point someone away from you.

### AN- ("on, onto, toward")

**Anweisen:** to point toward, to show onto. The prefix directs someone toward a task or a place. You *weisen* someone *an* — you instruct them, point them onto the right path. The word also carries the sense of assigning: directing resources toward a purpose.

Literal mnemonic: "show onto." You point someone onto their task — "here is what you do."

### AUF- ("up, upon, open")

**Aufweisen:** to show up, to display. The prefix lifts the showing gesture upward into visibility. Something *weist etwas auf* — it shows, it displays, it exhibits a quality. A building *weist* cracks *auf* — it shows cracks. The sense is of qualities rising to the surface and becoming visible.

Literal mnemonic: "show up." Features "show up" in something — they become apparent.

### AUS- ("out, outward")

**Ausweisen:** to show out. Two main strands: (1) to show outward — to identify, to prove who you are by showing credentials. (2) to show someone out — to expel, to deport, to banish from a country. In both cases something is directed outward: identity is shown outward for inspection, or a person is shown out through the border.

Literal mnemonic: "show out." You show your papers out for inspection, or you are shown out of the country.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Beweisen:** to show thoroughly, to prove. The *be-* prefix stamps the showing action fully onto its object. You don't just show something — you show it so completely that it becomes proof. To *beweisen* is to demonstrate with such force that doubt is overcome. The prefix transforms casual showing into definitive evidence.

Mnemonic: think of *be-* as "all over." You show something so thoroughly, from every angle, that it is proven beyond question.

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Einweisen:** to show in, to point into. You direct someone into a role, a position, or an institution. A new employee is *eingewiesen* — shown into their duties, briefed. A patient is *eingewiesen* — admitted into a hospital, directed inward. The prefix channels the guidance gesture into an enclosure or a new situation.

Literal mnemonic: "show in." You show someone in — into their new job, into the ward.

### ER- (achievement, completion)

**Erweisen:** to show forth conclusively. The *er-* prefix signals that the showing reaches its full result. To *erweisen* is to prove, to render, to demonstrate definitively. *Sich als nützlich erweisen* — to show oneself to be useful, to prove useful in the end. The prefix takes the pointing gesture to its conclusion: the thing pointed to is confirmed.

Mnemonic: "show to completion." What is *erwiesen* has been shown so fully that it stands as fact.

### HERAUS- ("out from within")

**Herausweisen:** to show out from inside. You direct someone out of a room or building — you show them the door. A straightforward, literal compound: the pointing gesture sends someone from inside to outside.

Literal mnemonic: "show out (from in here)." You point toward the exit.

### HIN- ("thither, toward a point away from the speaker")

**Hinweisen:** to point toward, to point thither. You direct attention to something over there. *Hinweisen auf* — to point out, to refer to, to draw attention to. The prefix gives the showing gesture a specific target in the distance. This is one of the most common *weisen* compounds: to indicate, to flag, to signal.

Literal mnemonic: "point to (over there)." "I'd like to point out that..." = *Ich möchte darauf hinweisen, dass...*

### NACH- ("after, following, verifying")

**Nachweisen:** to show after, to show by following up. The prefix implies pursuing something to its source and producing evidence. To *nachweisen* is to prove by investigation — to detect, to verify, to account for. You follow the trail and then show what you found.

Literal mnemonic: "show after checking." You look into something and then show the proof.

### UNTER- ("under, beneath")

**Unterweisen:** to show from underneath, to teach. The inseparable *unter-* transforms the showing gesture into systematic instruction. To *unterweisen* is to instruct, to train — to provide foundational guidance from below. The image is of a teacher underneath the student, supporting them, building up their knowledge from the ground.

Mnemonic: "underpinning with guidance." The teacher lays a foundation of knowledge beneath the student.

### VER- (transformation, displacement, intensification)

**Verweisen:** to show away, to show elsewhere. The *ver-* prefix pushes the pointing gesture into several directions: (1) to reprimand — to point the finger at someone in blame. (2) to expel — to point someone away permanently, to relegate. (3) to refer — to redirect attention elsewhere, to point to another source. The common thread is redirection: the showing is aimed away from where it was.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "away into change." A student is *verwiesen* from school — shown away. A reader is *verwiesen* to another chapter — shown elsewhere.

### VOR- ("before, forward, in front of")

**Vorweisen:** to show before, to present. You hold something forward for inspection. To *vorweisen* credentials is to present them — to show them before the authority. The prefix is spatial and social: what is *vorgewiesen* is held out in front, displayed for another's examination.

Literal mnemonic: "show forth." You hold your qualifications forward — "here, look at these."

### ZURÜCK- ("back, backward")

**Zurückweisen:** to show back, to point back. You refuse something by directing it back where it came from. An offer is *zurückgewiesen* — pushed back, rejected. An accusation is *zurückgewiesen* — repelled, thrown back at the accuser. The prefix reverses the direction of the gesture: instead of receiving, you point it back.

Literal mnemonic: "show back." You send the thing back where it came from.

### ZU- ("to, toward, additional")

**Zuweisen:** to show to, to point toward someone. You direct a resource or a role to a specific recipient. Budget is *zugewiesen* — allocated, pointed toward its destination. A task is *zugewiesen* — assigned, directed to a person. The prefix channels the showing gesture toward a target with precision.

Literal mnemonic: "show to." You point the resource to its recipient — "this goes to you."

### ÜBER- ("over, across, beyond")

**Überweisen:** to show over, to send across. The inseparable *über-* carries the action across a boundary. Money is *überwiesen* — transferred, sent across from one account to another. A patient is *überwiesen* — referred, directed over to a specialist. The image is of something being pointed across a divide and arriving on the other side.

Literal mnemonic: "show over." You send something over — money over to another account, a patient over to another doctor.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays directional; the prefix specifies the target.** *Weisen* never stops meaning "to point, to show." But the prefixes aim that pointing in different directions. *Hinweisen* (point thither) becomes "to indicate." *Zurückweisen* (point back) becomes "to reject." *Überweisen* (point over) becomes "to transfer." The finger points the same way every time — only the direction changes.

**Separable prefixes tend to be spatial and concrete.** *Ab, an, auf, aus, ein, hin, nach, vor, weg, zurück, zu* — these preserve a physical directionality. You can picture where the pointing gesture goes. *Abweisen* points away; *aufweisen* points up; *hinweisen* points over there.

**Inseparable prefixes tend to be transformative and opaque.** *Be-, er-, ver-, unter-, über-* — these fuse with the verb and shift the meaning further from literal pointing. *Beweisen* (show thoroughly) becomes "to prove." *Verweisen* (show away) becomes "to reprimand" or "to refer." These must often be learned as whole units.

**English sometimes preserves the same logic.** "Point out" = *hinweisen*. "Turn away" = *abweisen*. "Show up" = *aufweisen*. "Refer" (from Latin *referre*, to carry back/across) = *überweisen* or *verweisen*. Where English and German share the metaphor, use it as an anchor. Where they diverge — especially with the inseparable prefixes — notice how German extends the spatial metaphor of "pointing" into domains where English uses entirely unrelated verbs.
