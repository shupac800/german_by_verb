# ZAHLEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Zahlen** means to pay, to settle, to hand over money. Where *zählen* (with Umlaut) counts abstractly, *zahlen* counts out coins — it is the transactional verb, the one that closes a debt. Hold this image in mind: a hand pushing money across a counter. Every prefix below redirects that payment — sending it back, sending it down, splitting it up, or intensifying it.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from, down to zero")

**Abzahlen:** to pay off. The prefix drives the payment downward toward zero — you pay away at a debt until it is gone. A mortgage is *abgezahlt* — paid off in installments, each payment chipping away at the balance. The gesture is gradual and persistent: paying off, paying down, paying away.

Literal mnemonic: "pay off." English works identically — "pay off a loan" means to reduce it to nothing through repeated payments.

### AN- ("on, toward, initial contact")

**Anzahlen:** to pay on, to make an initial payment. The prefix marks the beginning of a transaction — the first touch of money against a debt. *Anzahlen* is to put down a deposit, to pay on account. You have not finished paying; you have only begun, laying the first payment onto the pile.

Literal mnemonic: "pay on account." Think of "putting money on it" — the first installment.

### AUS- ("out, outward, to completion")

**Auszahlen:** to pay out. The prefix drives the money outward — out of the treasury, out of the account, into someone's hands. Wages are *ausgezahlt* — paid out to workers. A business partner is *ausgezahlt* — bought out, paid in full so they relinquish their stake. The emphasis is on complete disbursement: the money goes out and the obligation is finished.

Literal mnemonic: "pay out." English "payout" captures it exactly — money flowing outward.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Bezahlen:** to pay (for something). *Be-* takes the intransitive *zahlen* and stamps it onto a direct object. Where *zahlen* means simply "to pay" (you go to the counter and pay), *bezahlen* means to pay for a specific thing — to cover its cost, to defray its expense. The prefix makes the payment targeted: you are not just paying in general, you are paying *for* this item, *settling* this bill.

Mnemonic: "be-" = "fully, upon the object." *Bezahlen* is paying directed fully at something. "I paid" (*ich habe gezahlt*) vs. "I paid for the meal" (*ich habe das Essen bezahlt*).

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Einzahlen:** to pay in. The prefix directs the money inward — into an account, into a fund, into a collection. You *zahlen ein* at the bank — you deposit money, paying it into your account. The gesture is the opposite of *auszahlen*: money flows inward rather than outward.

Literal mnemonic: "pay in." English "pay in" works the same way — "I paid in five hundred euros."

### NACH- ("after, later, additional")

**Nachzahlen:** to pay after, to pay later. The prefix places the payment after the original due date or after the original amount — you owe more and must pay the remainder afterward. An underpaid tax bill requires *Nachzahlung* — a supplementary payment made after the fact.

Literal mnemonic: "pay after" or "back-pay." The money comes later than it should have.

### ZURÜCK- ("back, backward, in return")

**Zurückzahlen:** to pay back. The prefix reverses the flow — money that went out is now returned. You *zahlen zurück* what you borrowed. The gesture is restitution: the payment goes back along the path it originally came from.

Literal mnemonic: "pay back." English is identical — "pay me back" = *zahl mir zurück*.

### ZUSAMMEN- ("together")

**Zusammenzahlen:** to pay together, to add up amounts. You take scattered sums and bring them together into one total. The prefix gathers: individual prices are *zusammengezahlt* — totaled, summed into a single figure.

Literal mnemonic: "tally together." Think of adding up a restaurant bill — all the items pulled together into one sum.

### ZU- ("to, toward, additional")

**Zuzahlen:** to pay to, to pay in addition. The prefix adds more money on top of what has already been paid — a co-payment, a surcharge, an extra amount. In the German health system, patients *zahlen zu* — they make an additional payment beyond what insurance covers.

Literal mnemonic: "pay toward" or "pay extra." The money is added to the existing amount.

### ÜBER- ("over, beyond, too much")

**Überzahlen:** to pay over, to overpay. The prefix pushes the payment past the correct amount — you have paid more than was owed. Simple and transparent: *über* = too much, *zahlen* = pay.

Literal mnemonic: "overpay." English works the same way.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays transactional; the prefixes redirect the flow of money.** *Zahlen* is always about money changing hands. The prefixes control the direction: *ein-* sends it in, *aus-* sends it out, *zurück-* sends it back, *ab-* grinds it down to zero, *über-* pushes it past the right amount. Once you see *zahlen* as a money-flow verb, the prefixes become directional arrows.

**Separable prefixes are spatial and concrete.** *Ab, an, aus, ein, nach, zurück, zusammen, zu* — all of these describe a physical direction for the movement of money. You can picture the coins going off, on, out, in, after, back, together, or toward.

**The one inseparable prefix transforms the grammar.** *Bezahlen* is the only inseparable verb here, and its role is grammatical rather than spatial — *be-* makes *zahlen* transitive so it can take a direct object. This fits the general pattern: *be-* does not add a spatial direction but changes how the verb relates to its object.

**English parallels are unusually strong.** "Pay off" = *abzahlen*. "Pay out" = *auszahlen*. "Pay in" = *einzahlen*. "Pay back" = *zurückzahlen*. "Overpay" = *überzahlen*. This is one of the verb families where English and German run almost perfectly in parallel — use these correspondences as anchors.
