# KLEBEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Kleben** means to stick, to glue, to adhere. It implies surfaces bonding — something pressed against something else and holding fast. Hold this image in mind: a hand pressing a label onto a surface and feeling it catch. Nearly every prefix below redirects that basic sticking action — aiming it, enclosing it, intensifying it, or completing it.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abkleben:** to stick off, to tape off. The prefix pulls "away" not in the sense of unsticking, but of *exclusion* — you apply tape to mark off an area, to shield it, to mask it from paint or contact. The sticking creates a boundary that separates what should be affected from what should not. A window frame is *abgeklebt* before painting — taped off, sealed away behind adhesive strips.

Literal mnemonic: "stick off." Think of "tape off an area" — the tape sticks, and the area is set off-limits.

### AN- ("on, onto, up against")

**Ankleben:** to stick on, to paste on. The prefix pushes the action toward a surface. A poster is *angeklebt* — stuck onto a wall. A stamp is *angeklebt* — affixed onto an envelope. The gesture is always contact-seeking: you press something against a surface and it holds. This is the most physically direct prefix — pure attachment, surface meeting surface.

Literal mnemonic: "stick on." English uses the same construction — "stick the label on the box."

### AUF- ("up, upon, open")

**Aufkleben:** to stick upon. You *kleben* something *auf* — you place it upon a surface and press it down. A sticker is *aufgeklebt* onto a laptop. A bandage is *aufgeklebt* onto skin. The difference from *ankleben* is subtle: *auf-* emphasizes the surface being covered, the thing resting *upon* it, while *an-* emphasizes the act of making contact.

Literal mnemonic: "stick upon." A decal is stuck upon a window — it sits on top of the surface.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Bekleben:** to stick all over, to cover with. *Be-* transforms the grammar and spreads the action across the entire object. Where *ankleben* sticks one thing onto a surface, *bekleben* treats the surface as the direct object and covers it. A wall is *beklebt* with posters — stuck over with them, plastered. A notebook is *beklebt* with stickers — papered over. The prefix turns the target surface into the thing being acted upon.

Mnemonic: think of "be-" as "all over." To *bekleben* a wall is to stick things all over it until the wall itself is transformed.

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Einkleben:** to stick in, to paste in. The prefix directs the sticking gesture inward, into an enclosure. A photo is *eingeklebt* into an album — glued into its page, set inside. A clipping is *eingeklebt* into a scrapbook. The image is always insertion: something is placed inside a container or frame and fixed there with adhesive.

Literal mnemonic: "stick in." Think of "pasting in a photo" — the picture goes into the album and stays.

### FEST- ("firm, fixed, tight")

**Festkleben:** to stick fast, to adhere firmly. The prefix intensifies the bond. Something *festgeklebt* is not merely touching a surface — it is locked there, stubbornly attached. A stamp is *festgeklebt* and will not peel off. Chewing gum is *festgeklebt* to the underside of a desk. Figuratively, someone who will not leave their seat is *festgeklebt* — stuck fast, immovable.

Literal mnemonic: "stick fast" (in the old English sense of "fast" = firmly fixed). The thing is stuck and will not budge.

### VER- (transformation, completion, sealing)

**Verkleben:** to stick together transformatively, to glue shut, to seal with glue. *Ver-* pushes the sticking action toward completion and transformation — the bond is thorough, permanent, often irreversible. Surfaces are *verklebt* — glued together so they become one. A wound is *verklebt* — sealed shut with adhesive strips. Eyes are *verklebt* with sleep — stuck shut, gummed together. The prefix always implies that the sticking has changed the state of the thing: open becomes closed, separate becomes joined, usable becomes sealed.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "shut" or "into a new state." *Verkleben* a crack = seal it permanently with glue. *Verklebte Augen* = eyes stuck into a shut state.

### ZU- ("to, toward, closed")

**Zukleben:** to stick shut, to seal. The prefix carries the sense of closure. You *kleben* an envelope *zu* — you seal it, stick its flap shut. A box is *zugeklebt* — taped closed. The motion is always toward shutting: the adhesive draws two surfaces together until no gap remains.

Literal mnemonic: "stick shut." Think of "seal it up" — the "zu" closes the opening.

### ZUSAMMEN- ("together")

**Zusammenkleben:** to stick together, to glue together. You take separate pieces and bond them into one. Broken ceramic is *zusammengeklebt* — its shards glued back together. Pages are *zusammengeklebt* — stuck to each other, fused. Model parts are *zusammengeklebt* — assembled with glue. The prefix is always about joining: multiple things becoming a unit through adhesion.

Literal mnemonic: "stick together." "Glue the pieces together" = *die Teile zusammenkleben.*

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays physical; the prefixes stay fairly concrete.** Unlike *legen*, which drifts readily into abstraction, *kleben* keeps most of its prefixed forms close to the literal act of sticking. This is because adhesion is already a specific, tactile process — there is less room for metaphor when the core action is glue meeting surface rather than the general gesture of placing. Where metaphor does appear, it tends to be vivid and bodily: *festgeklebt* on a chair, eyes *verklebt* with sleep.

**Separable prefixes indicate direction and manner.** *An, auf, ein, zu, zusammen, fest, ab* — each tells you where or how the sticking happens. *An* = onto. *Auf* = upon. *Ein* = into. *Zu* = shut. *Zusammen* = together. *Fest* = firmly. *Ab* = off (as exclusion). If you know the prefix, you can predict the meaning with unusual reliability. This is a verb family where the system works transparently.

**Inseparable prefixes shift scope or completeness.** *Be-* widens the sticking from a single attachment to a full covering. *Ver-* deepens it from temporary contact to permanent sealing. Both are inseparable, both transform the action rather than simply redirecting it.

**English parallels are strong.** "Stick on" = *ankleben*. "Stick in" = *einkleben*. "Stick together" = *zusammenkleben*. "Stick shut" = *zukleben*. "Stick fast" = *festkleben*. English "stick" and German "kleben" share much of the same prefix logic, making this one of the easier verb families for English speakers to internalize. Where they diverge, notice that German uses *bekleben* (cover by sticking) and *verkleben* (seal by sticking) where English would abandon the word "stick" entirely and switch to "plaster over" or "seal."
