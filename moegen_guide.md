# MÖGEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Mögen** is a verb with two faces. In one, it expresses personal feeling: to like, to be fond of. *Ich mag Kaffee* — I like coffee. In the other, it is a modal verb expressing possibility or concession: may, might. *Das mag sein* — that may be so. These two senses — preference and possibility — seem unrelated in modern German, but they share an ancient root: both involve an inner inclination or disposition. To like something is to be inclined toward it; to say something "may" happen is to grant that circumstances are inclined to allow it. Hold both senses in mind: liking and allowing. The single prefixed form below transforms both.

## The Prefixes

### VER- (transformation, intensification)

**Vermögen** (insep.): to be able to, to be capable of, to have the power to. Here *ver-* performs one of its most striking transformations. The stem *mögen* — a verb of feeling and inclination — is converted into a verb of capacity and power. The shift is not random: if you *like* doing something, you are drawn to it; if you *ver-mögen* something, you have gathered enough internal force to actually accomplish it. The prefix pushes subjective preference across the threshold into objective ability. You no longer merely fancy doing something — you *can* do it.

The noun **Vermögen** carries the transformation further. It means fortune, assets, wealth — the accumulated power to act in the world. It also means ability or faculty in a general sense (*Denkvermögen* = the power of thought). In every case, the word has traveled the same path: from inner inclination (*mögen*) through *ver-*'s transformative force into realized capacity.

Literal mnemonic: "may through and through." Where *mögen* says "I might," *vermögen* says "I can." Where *mögen* tilts toward something, *vermögen* has the strength to reach it.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**Ver- transforms feeling into power.** This is the central drama of the MÖGEN family. The stem lives in the domain of taste, preference, and tentative possibility. The prefix *ver-* pushes it into the domain of capacity and achievement. English has no single parallel — we move from "like" to "be able to" without any visible morphological bridge. German makes the connection explicit: the road from wanting to doing runs through *ver-*.

**The noun preserves what the verb has become.** *Vermögen* as a noun (fortune, ability) shows the end point of the transformation. Once *mögen* has been intensified by *ver-* into actual capability, that capability can be treated as a possession — something you have, not just something you feel. A person's *Vermögen* is their accumulated power, whether financial or intellectual.

**A tiny family with outsized conceptual range.** Most verb stems in German generate dozens of prefixed forms. MÖGEN produces only one — but that one verb, *vermögen*, moves from emotion to ability to wealth, covering ground that English needs three or four unrelated words to traverse. The smallness of the family makes the transformation sharper: there is only one prefix, and it changes everything.
