# SAGEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Sagen** means to say, to tell. It is one of the most basic acts of speech — uttering words, making something known. Hold this image in mind: a person opening their mouth and delivering a statement. Nearly every prefix below reshapes that utterance — redirecting it, formalizing it, negating it, or abstracting it into metaphor.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Absagen:** to say off. The prefix pulls the speech act away — you withdraw a commitment that was previously given. To *absagen* a meeting is to cancel it, to say it off, to unsay the arrangement. To *absagen* an invitation is to decline it — you say yourself away from the event. The gesture is always one of retraction: words that undo a prior agreement.

Literal mnemonic: "say off." English "call off" captures the same logic — speech that removes rather than establishes.

### AN- ("on, onto, toward")

**Ansagen:** to say on, to say toward. The prefix directs the speech at an audience. To *ansagen* is to announce — to say something onto the record, to declare it publicly. A radio host *sagt an* — they say the next item toward the listeners. The word implies formality and projection: speech aimed outward for everyone to receive.

Literal mnemonic: "say on" or "say forth." Think of an announcer saying something on the air.

### AUF- ("up, upon, open")

**Aufsagen:** to say up. The prefix lifts speech into performance. To *aufsagen* is to recite — to say something up from memory, to deliver it aloud as a prepared piece. A child *sagt ein Gedicht auf* — they say a poem up, producing it from what they have stored inside. The "up" suggests surfacing: words that were held within now come up and out.

Literal mnemonic: "say up." Think of "speaking up" — producing words that were previously held inside.

### AUS- ("out, outward, fully")

**Aussagen:** to say out. You bring words out into the open — fully, with consequence. In legal contexts, *aussagen* means to testify, to give evidence, to make a formal statement. The speech is drawn out completely; nothing is held back. More broadly, a text or artwork *sagt etwas aus* — it says something out, it expresses or conveys meaning. In both cases, the "out" implies completeness and exposure.

Literal mnemonic: "say out." English "speak out" and "state out loud" carry the same force — words that are fully externalized.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Besagen:** to say upon, to say thoroughly about. The *be-* prefix stamps the saying fully onto an object. A law *besagt* something — it says upon the matter, it states, it means, it signifies. The word is impersonal and documentary: it tells you what a text or fact says about a subject. You rarely *besagen* something yourself; rather, documents and evidence *besagen* things.

Mnemonic: think of *be-* as "about" or "concerning." What does the contract *besagen*? What does it say about the matter? It states, it signifies.

### DURCH- ("through")

**Durchsagen:** to say through. The prefix pushes speech through a channel — an intercom, a phone line, a chain of people. To *durchsagen* is to announce or pass on a message, to say something through to those who need to hear it. The image is of words being transmitted through a medium to reach the other end.

Literal mnemonic: "say through." Think of "passing the word through" a network — a message relayed from person to person or broadcast through a loudspeaker.

### ENT- ("away from, removed from")

**Entsagen:** to say away from, to unsay one's claim. *Ent-* signals separation and removal. When you *entsagen* something, you speak yourself away from it — you renounce it, give it up, relinquish your hold. The word carries weight and gravity: *entsagen* is not casual refusal but solemn renunciation. One *entsagt* worldly pleasures, power, a throne. The act of saying becomes an act of letting go — speech as severance.

Literal mnemonic: "say away from." You say yourself away from what you once held — you formally declare that it is no longer yours.

### HER- ("hither, toward the speaker")

**Hersagen:** to say hither, to say forth. The prefix draws the speech toward the listener, producing it in a continuous stream. To *hersagen* is to recite, to reel off, to rattle off — to say something from memory in a steady flow directed outward. Where *aufsagen* emphasizes the act of recalling, *hersagen* emphasizes the delivery: words coming forth one after another, sometimes mechanically.

Literal mnemonic: "say forth." Think of someone rattling off a list — the words come streaming out toward you.

### LOS- ("loose, free, released")

**Lossagen:** to say loose, to say oneself free. The prefix releases. *Sich lossagen* means to renounce, to disavow, to break with — you speak yourself loose from a bond, an allegiance, a relationship. Where *entsagen* is about relinquishing a possession or pleasure, *lossagen* is about severing a tie: you say yourself free from a group, an ideology, a person.

Literal mnemonic: "say loose." You cut the rope with your words — you declare yourself untethered.

### NACH- ("after, behind")

**Nachsagen:** to say after. Two distinct images. (1) You say something after someone else — you repeat their words, you echo them. (2) You say something after someone in their absence — you say things *about* them, you attribute qualities or rumors to them. *Man sagt ihm nach, dass...* — people say of him that... The "after" can be temporal (repeating) or directional (talking behind someone's back, for good or ill).

Literal mnemonic: "say after." "Say after me" = repeat. "What they say after him" = what is attributed to him.

### UNTER- ("under, beneath")

**Untersagen:** to say under, to place a prohibition beneath. *Unter-* here acts as a barrier laid below an action — speech that blocks from underneath. To *untersagen* is to forbid, to prohibit, to ban. The authority *untersagt* an activity by placing their word beneath it like a locked door: you cannot proceed because the saying stands in your way below. This is not casual discouragement but formal, legal prohibition — an official "no" with force behind it.

Literal mnemonic: "undersay." Think of an authority undercutting your plans with a firm prohibition — their word sits beneath your intended action and prevents it from standing.

### VER- (transformation, displacement, negation)

**Versagen:** to say awry, to mis-say, to say into failure. This is the most dramatic transformation in the family. *Ver-* twists the act of saying until it becomes its opposite — not speaking, not delivering, not functioning. To *versagen* is to fail, to break down, to malfunction. A machine *versagt* — it refuses to speak, it gives no response. A person's nerves *versagen* — they fail to perform. In its older, more transparent sense, *versagen* means to deny, to refuse — to say "no," to withhold what was asked for. The path from denial to failure is short: what consistently refuses eventually breaks down. *Ver-* takes the act of saying and corrupts it into silence, refusal, and collapse.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "away into wrongness." *Versagen* your consent = deny, refuse. *Versagen* as a machine = fail to deliver. The saying has been twisted until nothing comes out. The noun *das Versagen* (failure) preserves this: it is the state of having nothing to say, nothing to give.

### VOR- ("before, forward, in front of")

**Vorsagen:** to say before, to say in front of someone. You speak the words first so another person can follow. To *vorsagen* is to prompt — to say the line before the actor who has forgotten it. A parent *sagt vor*, and the child repeats. In school, *vorsagen* is whispering the answer to a classmate — saying it forward, feeding them the words they need.

Literal mnemonic: "say before." Think of a prompter saying the line before the performer. English "prompt" lacks the spatial clarity that *vorsagen* offers.

### ZU- ("to, toward, additional, closed")

**Zusagen:** to say to — a prefix that points speech toward someone, and in doing so creates three related but distinct meanings. (1) *Promise:* you say something to someone as a commitment — you assure, you pledge. Your words go toward them as a guarantee. (2) *Accept:* you say "to" an invitation — you confirm, you accept. Where *absagen* says you off, *zusagen* says you toward. (3) *Appeal:* something *sagt Ihnen zu* — it says itself toward you, it suits you, it appeals. The object speaks to your taste, addresses itself to your preference. All three senses share the same gesture: words or qualities directed toward, arriving at, landing with someone.

Literal mnemonic: "say to." "I'll say yes to that" = accept. "I say to you: it will be done" = promise. "That speaks to me" = it appeals.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays verbal; the prefixes go social.** *Sagen* never stops meaning "to say." But the prefixes push that saying into social and institutional roles. *Ansagen* (say toward) becomes "to announce." *Untersagen* (say under) becomes "to forbid." *Aussagen* (say out) becomes "to testify." The mouth's simple act of speaking is projected onto legal, ceremonial, and interpersonal contexts. Once you see this, the abstractions feel less arbitrary.

**Separable prefixes tend to be spatial and concrete.** *Ab, an, auf, aus, durch, her, los, nach, vor, zu* — these are words that can stand alone as adverbs or prepositions. They preserve a physical directionality even in figurative use. You can usually picture where the speech is going: out, toward, forward, away.

**Inseparable prefixes tend to be transformative and opaque.** *Be-, ent-, unter-, ver-* — these fuse with the verb and shift its meaning further from the literal. *Ver-* is the most extreme: it takes "to say" and turns it into "to fail." *Ent-* takes "to say" and turns it into "to renounce." These must often be learned as units rather than decoded from parts, though the underlying logic rewards study.

**Cancellation and commitment form a natural pair.** *Absagen* (say off = cancel) and *zusagen* (say to = accept) are perfect opposites — one says you away from the event, the other says you toward it. Learning them together anchors both.

**Renunciation comes in two flavors.** *Entsagen* and *sich lossagen* both mean to renounce, but they target different things. *Entsagen* renounces possessions, pleasures, claims — things you hold. *Sich lossagen* renounces ties, allegiances, associations — bonds that hold you. The distinction mirrors the prefixes: *ent-* removes something from you; *los-* frees you from something.

**English sometimes preserves the same logic.** "Say off" is unusual in English, but "call off" works identically to *absagen*. "Testify" (from Latin *testis* + *facere*) parallels *aussagen* — making one's saying external and official. "Gainsay" (to say against, to contradict) echoes the oppositional force of *widersprechen* and hints at the combative potential of speech prefixes. Where English and German share the metaphor, use it as an anchor. Where they diverge — especially with *versagen*, where saying becomes failing — notice the gap: German extends the speech metaphor into territory English handles with entirely different words.
