# WERDEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Werden** means to become, to get, to turn into. It is one of the most fundamental verbs in German, serving both as a main verb of transformation and as an auxiliary for the future tense and the passive voice. As a stem for prefixed compounds, however, it is remarkably limited — only one common compound exists.

---

## The Prefixes

### LOS- ("loose, free, released")

**Loswerden:** to become loose from, to get free of. The prefix means release, separation — and when combined with *werden* (to become), the result is "to become free of something." You *wirst* a cold *los* — you get rid of it, you become released from it. You *wirst* an annoying guest *los* — you shed them, you become free. The image is of something clinging to you that finally lets go.

Literal mnemonic: "become loose (from)." English "get rid of" captures it, but the German is more vivid — you literally *become free* of the thing.

---

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**Werden is too fundamental to compound easily.** Most German stems accumulate many prefixed forms because their core meaning — laying, turning, throwing — can be redirected by prefixes in many directions. *Werden* is different: "to become" is already so abstract and all-encompassing that prefixes have little room to reshape it. You cannot meaningfully say "become away" or "become in" — the verb resists spatial modification.

**Loswerden is the exception because los- adds separation.** The one productive combination works because *los-* provides something *werden* lacks: a clear physical image of detachment. "Become loose from" is vivid and specific in a way that other prefix combinations with *werden* would not be.

**The auxiliary function dominates.** For English speakers, the most important thing about *werden* is not its compounds but its role as a helper verb: *Ich werde gehen* (I will go — future), *Es wird gemacht* (It is being done — passive). This auxiliary function is why the verb appears everywhere in German despite having almost no prefixed family.
