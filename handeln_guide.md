# HANDELN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Handeln** means to act, to trade, to deal. It comes from *Hand* (hand) and is cognate with English "handle." The verb bridges two domains: action (doing something) and commerce (dealing, trading, bargaining). Hold both images in mind: a hand reaching out to do something, and a hand reaching out to strike a deal. This dual nature — acting and trading — runs through the entire prefix family. Nearly every compound below draws on one sense or the other, and some draw on both.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abhandeln:** to deal off, to deal away. Two distinct gestures. First, to deal with a topic — to treat it, to cover it, to work through it methodically until it has been dispatched. A subject is *abgehandelt* when it has been fully dealt with and set aside. Second, to bargain something down — to deal away part of the price. You negotiate off a portion of what was asked.

Literal mnemonic: "deal off." You deal a topic off your list — it is covered, finished, handled. Or you deal off some of the price — you bargain it down.

### AUS- ("out, outward, through to completion")

**Aushandeln:** to deal out, to negotiate through. The prefix pushes the dealing to completion. You don't just negotiate — you negotiate *out* a result. An agreement is *ausgehandelt* when every point has been hammered out, worked through, and settled. The image is of two parties dealing back and forth until a finished product emerges.

Literal mnemonic: "deal out" or "hammer out." English uses the same logic — "we hammered out the details" means we worked through them until they were done.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Behandeln:** to handle, to treat. This is the most common compound in the family. *Be-* takes the broad, undirected act of handling and stamps it onto a specific object. A doctor *behandelt* a patient — treats them, applies care directly to them. A book *behandelt* a subject — treats it, addresses it thoroughly. A machine is *behandelt* with care — handled, managed. Where *handeln* floats freely (to act, to deal in general), *behandeln* always demands an object: you treat *something*, you handle *something*.

Mnemonic: think of *be-* as "all over, directly upon." To *behandeln* is to lay your handling directly onto a thing — a patient, a topic, a surface. English "treat" works the same way: you treat a wound, treat a subject, treat someone well.

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Einhandeln:** to deal in, to trade in. Literally, to bring something inward through dealing. You acquire something by trading for it — you deal it into your possession. Figuratively, and more commonly, *sich etwas einhandeln* means to earn yourself something unwanted — to bring trouble upon yourself. You dealt carelessly and now you have dealt *in* a problem.

Literal mnemonic: "deal in" or "trade in." You trade something in and receive something back — but what you receive may be trouble you didn't expect.

### ENTGEGEN- ("against, counter to")

**Entgegenhandeln:** to act against, to contravene. The prefix sets the action in direct opposition. You do not merely act — you act *counter to* something: a rule, an order, a principle. To *entgegenhandeln* is to violate, to go against. The construction is transparent: *entgegen* (against) + *handeln* (act) = to act against.

Literal mnemonic: "act against." English "contravene" carries the same logic — *contra* (against) + *venire* (come) — but German builds it from native parts.

### ER- (achievement, acquisition through effort)

**Erhandeln:** to obtain by dealing, to acquire through bargaining. *Er-* signals that the action reaches a successful endpoint. You deal and deal until you have *er-*dealt — secured the thing through negotiation. The prefix converts open-ended trading into a completed acquisition. Something *erhandelt* was won at the bargaining table, not given freely.

Mnemonic: "deal to completion." *Er-* marks the finish line — you bargained until you got what you were after.

### MISS- ("wrongly, badly")

**Misshandeln:** to mistreat, to abuse, to maltreat. The prefix is transparent and harsh: *miss-* = English "mis-." To *misshandeln* is to handle wrongly — to treat someone badly, to batter, to ill-treat. Where *behandeln* is care directed at an object, *misshandeln* is cruelty directed at a person. The same transitive focus, but with the sign reversed.

Literal mnemonic: "mishandle." English preserves the exact same construction — to mishandle someone is to treat them roughly. German pushes it further into physical abuse.

### VER- (transformation, extension, intensification)

**Verhandeln:** to negotiate, to discuss terms. *Ver-* extends the basic act of dealing into formal, sustained negotiation. You don't just deal — you deal *through* a process, transforming casual commerce into structured deliberation. In legal contexts, *verhandeln* means to try a case, to hear proceedings — the dealing becomes judicial. A *Verhandlung* is a negotiation or a trial, depending on the setting.

Mnemonic: *ver-* stretches dealing into something larger and more formal. A market stall *handelt*; a courtroom *verhandelt*. The prefix elevates the transaction into procedure.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The dual nature persists.** *Handeln* means both "to act" and "to trade," and this split echoes through the compounds. *Abhandeln* (deal with a topic / bargain down), *einhandeln* (acquire by trading / bring trouble on yourself), *entgegenhandeln* (act against) — each prefix latches onto one sense or both. When you encounter a new *handeln* compound, ask first: is this about doing or about dealing? Often the answer is both.

**Be- and ver- dominate.** *Behandeln* and *verhandeln* are by far the most frequent compounds. They sit at opposite ends of the spectrum: *behandeln* is direct, hands-on treatment of a specific object; *verhandeln* is extended, procedural negotiation between parties. Together they cover the two poles of the stem — handling things and making deals.

**Inseparable prefixes reshape the verb deeply.** *Be-, er-, miss-, ver-* all fuse permanently with the stem, and each pulls *handeln* into a distinct register. *Behandeln* (treat), *erhandeln* (acquire by bargaining), *misshandeln* (abuse), *verhandeln* (negotiate) — these are essentially independent verbs that must be learned as units. The prefix logic is recoverable but the meanings have solidified.

**Separable prefixes stay closer to the literal.** *Ab-, aus-, ein-, entgegen-* preserve a spatial or directional sense that you can picture. Dealing off, dealing out, dealing in, acting against — these compose transparently. Where the inseparable compounds require you to learn a fixed meaning, the separable ones let you reason from parts.

**English cognates help.** "Handle" and "deal" both descend from the same conceptual family as *handeln*. "Mistreat" maps to *misshandeln*. "Negotiate" maps to *verhandeln*. "Treat" maps to *behandeln*. Where English and German share the metaphor, lean on it. Where they diverge — particularly with *ver-* extending into legal proceedings — notice that German keeps the commercial root visible where English imports Latin (*negotiate* from *negotium*).
