# MACHEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Machen** means to make, to do, to cause, to produce. It is one of the most general verbs in German — a workhorse with almost no sharp edges. Where *legen* implies a specific physical gesture (placing something down), *machen* is open-ended: it points at bringing something about, getting something done, causing a state to exist. Hold this image in mind: a pair of hands shaping raw material into a result. Nearly every prefix below channels that formless "doing" into a specific direction, giving it a target, a boundary, or a twist.

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abmachen:** to make off, to make away. Two distinct domains share the same logic of separation and settlement. *(Agree)* To settle something is to make it done, to finalize it and set it aside as resolved — *eine Abmachung* is an agreement, something that has been "made off" the table of open questions. *(Remove)* To take something off — to detach a label, to remove a piece — is to make it away from the surface it was on.

Literal mnemonic: "make off." English "make off with something" captures the removal sense. For the agreement sense, think of "making something done and dusted — off the agenda."

### AN- ("on, onto, up against")

**Anmachen:** to make on. The prefix pushes the action toward contact, activation, ignition. *(Turn on)* You *machst* the light *an* — you make it on, you bring it into the state of being active. *(Light)* You *machst* a fire *an* — you make it catch, bring flame into contact with fuel. *(Food)* You *machst* a salad *an* — you dress it, applying seasoning onto it, making it come alive with flavor. *(Flirt)* Colloquially, you *machst* someone *an* — you make a move on them, directing your energy toward contact.

Literal mnemonic: "make on." The light is made on. The salad is made on with dressing. The person is made on — approached. Each sense is an act of turning something on, activating it, bringing it into a live state.

### AUF- ("up, upon, open")

**Aufmachen:** to make open. The prefix lifts, unseals, unfastens. You *machst* a door *auf* — you make it open. A bottle is *aufgemacht* — its cap removed, its seal broken. A button is *aufgemacht* — unfastened, undone. The gesture is always one of releasing what was closed: making something open, making the sealed accessible.

Literal mnemonic: "make open." English "open up" captures the same doubling of direction — *aufmachen* is its German equivalent.

### AUS- ("out, outward, off, completed")

**Ausmachen:** to make out. This is one of the most versatile prefixed forms in the family, with four clearly separate senses all radiating from "making something out" in different directions. *(Turn off)* You *machst* the light *aus* — you make it out, extinguish it, end its activity. A candle is *ausgemacht* — put out. *(Agree)* You *machst* a time *aus* — you make out an arrangement, settle the terms. This overlaps with *abmachen* but emphasizes finding the result through discussion. *(Constitute)* Something *macht* a large part *aus* — it makes up, it amounts to, it constitutes. The parts "make out" the whole. *(Matter)* *Das macht nichts aus* — that doesn't make anything out, it doesn't matter, it doesn't bother anyone. If something fails to "make out" any effect, it is inconsequential.

Literal mnemonic: "make out." English uses this same construction in strikingly parallel ways — "make out a figure in the dark" (discern/constitute), "make out all right" (amount to something). The "turn off" sense is uniquely German: to make a flame *out* is to extinguish it.

### DARAN- ("at it, to it, toward it")

**Daranmachen:** *(sich daranmachen)* to make oneself at it. The reflexive form is essential — you direct yourself toward a task and begin. *Sich daranmachen* is to set about something, to get down to work. The prefix *daran* points at the thing: "at that" — and you make yourself engage with it.

Literal mnemonic: "make at it." English "get at it" or "set about it" carries the same directed energy. You point yourself at the task and begin making.

### DURCH- ("through")

**Durchmachen:** to make through. You push through something from beginning to end, enduring whatever lies between. A hard night is *durchgemacht* — lived through, suffered through, not abandoned halfway. An illness is *durchgemacht* — experienced in full, from onset to recovery. The prefix demands completion: you do not stop in the middle; you make it all the way through.

Literal mnemonic: "make through." English "go through" is the closest parallel — "she went through a lot" maps directly onto *sie hat viel durchgemacht*.

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Einmachen:** to make in, to put into. The prefix directs the making inward — into a jar, a can, a sealed container. *Einmachen* means to preserve, to can, to bottle. Fruit is *eingemacht* — made into a preserved state by sealing it inside. The image is domestic and literal: making food go *in* so it keeps.

Literal mnemonic: "make in" or "put in." English "put up" (as in "put up preserves") uses a different spatial metaphor, but the German is more transparent — you make it *into* the jar.

### FEST- ("firm, fixed, tight")

**Festmachen:** to make firm. You take something loose and make it fixed, secure, immovable. *(Secure)* A rope is *festgemacht* — fastened, tied tight. A shelf is *festgemacht* — attached, secured to the wall. *(Nautical)* A boat is *festgemacht* — moored, made fast to the dock. *(Determine)* A detail is *festgemacht* — pinned down, nailed in place. In every case, something that was floating, uncertain, or unattached is made firm.

Literal mnemonic: "make fast." English nautical language preserves this exactly — "make fast" means to secure a line. *Festmachen* is the direct equivalent.

### FORT- ("away, onward, gone")

**Fortmachen:** to make away, to make off. The simplest escape verb — you make yourself away from where you are. *Mach fort!* — get going, hurry off. The prefix adds only direction: away from here.

Literal mnemonic: "make off." English "make off" (to leave quickly) is a near-perfect match.

### GLEICH- ("equal, same, level")

**Gleichmachen:** to make equal. You take what is uneven and make it level, uniform, the same. Surfaces are *gleichgemacht* — leveled. Differences are *gleichgemacht* — erased, equalized. The prefix is the adjective *gleich* (equal) pressed into service as a direction: make it toward sameness.

Literal mnemonic: "make equal." English "equalize" is Latin where German is Germanic, but the logic is identical — forcing things into the same state.

### HERAUS- ("out from within")

**Herausmachen:** to make out from inside. Literally, to remove something from within — to take it out. A stain is *herausgemacht* — worked out of the fabric. Reflexively, *sich herausmachen* means to come along well, to develop nicely — as if drawing the best version of yourself out from within.

Literal mnemonic: "make out (from in here)." The reflexive sense is charming: someone who *sich herausmacht* is making something good come out of themselves.

### HER- ("hither, toward the speaker, from a source")

**Hermachen:** *(sich hermachen über)* to make oneself over something, to fall upon it. The reflexive is essential: you throw yourself at something with appetite or eagerness. *Sich über das Essen hermachen* — to fall upon the food, to dig in. The prefix *her* pulls the action toward the speaker and implies vigorous arrival: you come at the thing and make yourself busy with it.

Literal mnemonic: "make over" in the aggressive sense. Think of "falling upon" or "setting upon" — you arrive and consume.

### LOS- ("loose, free, released")

**Losmachen:** to make loose. You release what is bound, unfasten what is tied, free what is held. A knot is *losgemacht* — untied. A dog is *losgemacht* — unleashed. The prefix means release, and the action is liberation: making something free from its constraint.

Literal mnemonic: "make loose." English "loosen" is the same idea without the making — German builds it from "make" + "loose" with satisfying transparency.

### MIT- ("with, along, together")

**Mitmachen:** to make with, to do along. You join an activity already in progress — you make alongside others, you participate. A game is *mitgemacht* — joined in. A trend is *mitgemacht* — followed, gone along with. The prefix is social: it puts you beside the others, doing what they do.

Literal mnemonic: "make with." English "go along with" is the closest equivalent. *Mitmachen* says: I will make what you are making, together.

### NACH- ("after, following, imitating")

**Nachmachen:** to make after. You see what someone has done and you make it again, following their model. To *nachmachen* is to imitate, to copy, to reproduce. A child *macht* a parent's gestures *nach*. A forger *macht* a signature *nach*. The prefix means "after" in the sense of "in pursuit of, following the same path."

Literal mnemonic: "make after." English "copy after" or "model after" uses the same trailing logic — you come after the original and repeat it.

### VER- (transformation, displacement, away into change)

**Vermachen:** to make away, to make over to someone. This is the sole inseparable prefix in the family, and *ver-* here carries its sense of transfer and finality. To *vermachen* is to bequeath — to make something over to someone through a will, to give it away permanently upon death. The action is irrevocable: what is *vermacht* passes out of your possession for good. Unlike every other prefix in this list, *ver-* fuses inseparably with *machen*, signaling that the transformation is deep and complete — not a casual "making toward" but a permanent displacement.

Literal mnemonic: "make over" in the legal sense. "She made over her estate to her children" — that is *vermachen*. The *ver-* seals it shut: no taking it back.

### VOR- ("before, forward, in front of")

**Vormachen:** to make before someone, to perform in front of them. Two senses grow from the same root image. *(Demonstrate)* You *machst* something *vor* — you perform the action in front of a learner so they can see how it is done. A teacher *macht* the exercise *vor*. *(Deceive)* You *machst* someone something *vor* — you put on a performance, a show, a false front. What you display "before" them is not real; you are fooling them, deluding them with a manufactured appearance.

Literal mnemonic: "make before." To demonstrate is to make before someone's eyes so they learn. To deceive is to make before someone's eyes so they are fooled. The difference is whether the performance is honest.

### WEG- ("away, gone")

**Wegmachen:** to make away. You cause something to disappear — you remove it, get rid of it, clean it off. A stain is *weggemacht* — made away, erased. Clutter is *weggemacht* — cleared out. The prefix is blunt and final: the thing is made gone.

Literal mnemonic: "make away with." English "do away with" is a close match — eliminate, remove, make something no longer present.

### ZU- ("to, toward, closed, shut")

**Zumachen:** to make closed. The counterpart to *aufmachen*. You *machst* a door *zu* — you make it shut. A bottle is *zugemacht* — sealed. A shop is *zugemacht* — closed, shut down for the day or for good. The prefix pushes surfaces together, sealing the gap.

Literal mnemonic: "make to" in the sense of "make shut." Where *aufmachen* opens, *zumachen* closes. The pair is perfectly symmetrical.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem is maximally flexible; the prefix supplies the specificity.** *Machen* alone is almost too vague to be interesting — it just means "do" or "make." But this very vagueness is its strength as a stem. Because *machen* imposes so little of its own meaning, the prefix dominates. *Aufmachen* is purely about the "open" direction. *Zumachen* is purely about the "closed" direction. The stem is a blank surface on which the prefix paints.

**Opposites pair neatly.** *Aufmachen* (make open) and *zumachen* (make shut). *Anmachen* (turn on) and *ausmachen* (turn off). *Festmachen* (make fast) and *losmachen* (make loose). These pairs make the prefix system feel less like arbitrary vocabulary and more like a spatial grammar — learn one, and its opposite becomes predictable.

**AUSMACHEN is the most overloaded form.** Four distinct senses — turn off, agree on, constitute, matter — seem unrelated until you see that *aus* can mean "out" (extinguish), "out" (work out an agreement), "out" (make up a total), and "out" (make out any difference). The single prefix *aus-* fans into multiple metaphors, each taking "make out" in a different figurative direction.

**Separable prefixes dominate.** Of twenty prefixed forms, nineteen are separable — spatial, concrete, and largely predictable from their parts. Only *vermachen* (bequeath) is inseparable, and it carries the characteristic *ver-* finality: the transfer is complete, irrevocable, sealed. This ratio makes *machen* one of the most transparent verb families in German. If you know the prefix's spatial meaning and the stem's "make/do" meaning, you can usually guess the compound.

**English parallels are strong but not exact.** "Make off" = *fortmachen*. "Make out" = *ausmachen* (partially). "Make fast" = *festmachen*. "Make up" = *ausmachen* (constitute). But German pushes "make" further than English does: "make open" (*aufmachen*), "make shut" (*zumachen*), "make loose" (*losmachen*) are constructions English avoids, preferring dedicated verbs (open, shut, loosen). Noticing where German builds from "make" and English switches to a different verb reveals the deeper Germanic logic — *machen* is a universal tool, and the prefix is the blade you attach to it.
