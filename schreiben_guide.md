# SCHREIBEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Schreiben** means to write, to compose, to type, to spell. The core image is the act of setting words down — inscribing marks that carry meaning. Unlike *legen* (placing objects), *schreiben* places language. Nearly every prefix below redirects, intensifies, or abstracts that fundamental act of inscription.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abschreiben:** to write off, to copy. Two distinct gestures share the same prefix. In one, you write something *off* — you depreciate an asset, strike a debt from the books, declare a loss. The writing removes value. In the other, you write something *from* a source — you copy, you transcribe, reproducing text by writing it away from the original onto a new page. Both senses involve the prefix's basic direction: away from the source.

Literal mnemonic: "write off." English uses this exact phrase for financial depreciation and for giving up on something. The copying sense is "write from" — you write it off the original.

### AN- ("on, onto, toward")

**Anschreiben:** to write to, to write onto. The prefix pushes the writing toward a recipient. You *schreiben* someone *an* — you direct a letter at them, you contact them in writing. The gesture is aimed: writing that goes toward a person or institution, landing on their desk.

Literal mnemonic: "write to." English "write to someone" captures the same directional logic.

### AUF- ("up, upon, down onto")

**Aufschreiben:** to write down, to jot down. The prefix seems paradoxical — *auf* means "up," yet the meaning is "write down." The resolution: you write *upon* a surface. The paper is before you, and you place words upon it. The emphasis is on capturing something transient — a thought, a name, a number — by pinning it onto paper before it escapes.

Literal mnemonic: "write upon." You write a note upon a slip of paper. English prefers "write down," but German sees the surface, not the direction.

### AUS- ("out, outward, fully")

**Ausschreiben:** to write out. Two related senses: (1) to write out in full — no abbreviations, the complete uncompressed text laid out; (2) to write out a position or contract for public view — to advertise, to announce, to put out to tender. Both involve the same outward gesture: what was compressed or private is written out into the open.

Literal mnemonic: "write out." English preserves both senses — "write out the number in full" and "write out a job posting."

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Beschreiben:** to describe. The prefix *be-* takes the act of writing and stamps it fully onto an object. You do not simply write — you write *about* something, covering it with words. To describe is to write all over a subject, surrounding it with language until its qualities are visible. The connection to physical writing is old: *beschreiben* once meant literally to write upon a surface (covering it with script), and the figurative sense — covering a topic with words — grew from there.

Mnemonic: think of *be-* as "all over." To *beschreiben* something is to write all over it with words until the reader can see it. English "describe" comes from Latin *describere* — "write down" — but the German prefix makes the metaphor more spatial: you are writing *upon* the subject.

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Einschreiben:** to write in, to register. You write a name *into* a list — you enroll, you sign up, you register. The prefix directs the writing inward, into an official record. The postal sense follows the same logic: a registered letter is one that has been *written into* the postal system's tracking records, given an entry in the log.

Literal mnemonic: "write in." English "enroll" comes from French "en rolle" — to write into the roll. *Einschreiben* is the Germanic equivalent.

### HERAUS- ("out from within")

**Herausschreiben:** to write out from inside. You reach into a text and extract something by writing it out — copying a passage from a longer work, pulling a quotation from its source. The prefix emphasizes that the writing moves outward from an enclosed body of text.

Literal mnemonic: "write out (from within)." You write out the key passage from the book.

### HER- ("hither, toward the speaker")

**Herschreiben:** to write here, to note down. A simple directional compound — writing directed toward the speaker's location. Uncommon in practice, but transparent in meaning.

Literal mnemonic: "write here."

### MIT- ("with, along, together")

**Mitschreiben:** to write along, to take notes. The prefix means "along with" — you write simultaneously with what is happening. A student *schreibt mit* during a lecture: the words go down on paper alongside the spoken words, keeping pace. The image is of parallel motion: speech flows and writing flows with it.

Literal mnemonic: "write along." "I was writing along as the professor talked" captures the sense perfectly.

### NACH- ("after, following, imitating")

**Nachschreiben:** to write after. You write following a model or source — from dictation, someone speaks and you write after them. The prefix carries the sense of reproduction: you follow someone else's words and set them down. The writing comes *after* the original utterance or text.

Literal mnemonic: "write after." Taking dictation is writing after the speaker's voice.

### UM- ("around; over, anew")

**Umschreiben** exists in two forms, and the difference is critical:

*Separable (um-):* to rewrite, to revise, to transfer. The prefix carries its "over again" sense — you write something *over*, producing a new version. A manuscript is *umgeschrieben* — rewritten, reworked from the ground up. A property title is *umgeschrieben* — transferred, written over into a new name.

*Inseparable (um-):* to paraphrase, to circumscribe. Now *um-* means "around." You write *around* something — you circle the meaning without touching it directly. To paraphrase is to restate by writing around the original phrasing. To circumscribe is to draw a boundary around, to define limits. The writing encircles rather than replaces.

Literal mnemonic: separable = "write over" (do it again); inseparable = "write around" (circle the meaning). The stress tells you which: *UMschreiben* rewrites; *umSCHREIBen* paraphrases.

### UNTER- ("under, beneath")

**Unterschreiben:** to write under. You place your name *under* a document — you sign it. The signature goes beneath the text, beneath the terms, beneath the agreement. By writing under, you accept what is written above. This is one of the most transparent prefix verbs in German: to sign is literally to write underneath.

Literal mnemonic: "underwrite." English "underwrite" originally meant the same thing — to write one's name under a document, accepting the risk. *Unterschreiben* preserved the literal sense while English "underwrite" drifted into insurance and finance.

### VER- (transformation, displacement, error)

**Verschreiben:** three distinct forces of *ver-* converge in one verb:

- *Authority/prescription:* to prescribe — a doctor writes out a medication order, issuing an authoritative document. The *ver-* prefix carries the sense of official dispensation, writing that transforms a request into a binding instruction.
- *Dedication:* *sich verschreiben* — to devote oneself to, to dedicate oneself to. You write yourself over to a cause, transferring your commitment entirely. The reflexive form implies that you have signed yourself away.
- *Error:* *sich verschreiben* — to make a slip of the pen, to misspell. Here *ver-* carries its "wrongly" sense: you write astray, your hand deviates from what you intended.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "away into change." A prescription writes medication *into being*. Devotion writes the self *away* to a cause. A slip of the pen writes *wrongly*. Context tells you which *ver-* is at work.

### VOR- ("before, forward, in front of")

**Vorschreiben:** to write before, to prescribe. You write something *before* another person — laying down the rules in advance, placing instructions before them that they must follow. To prescribe, to stipulate, to dictate: all are acts of writing that comes *before* the action, constraining it. The tone is authoritative — what is *vorgeschrieben* is mandatory.

Literal mnemonic: "write before." The regulations are "written before" you, already in place when you arrive. English "prescribe" uses the same logic — Latin *prae* + *scribere* = "write before."

### ZURÜCK- ("back, in return")

**Zurückschreiben:** to write back. The simplest return gesture — someone wrote to you and you write back. The prefix reverses the direction of the correspondence.

Literal mnemonic: "write back." Identical in English and German.

### ZUSAMMEN- ("together")

**Zusammenschreiben:** to write together. In German, this has a specific grammatical meaning: to write two words as one, to join them into a compound. German's famous capacity for compound words makes this verb a practical necessity — you need a word for the act of writing words together.

Literal mnemonic: "write together." Two words become one when they are written together.

### ZU- ("to, toward")

**Zuschreiben:** to write to, to ascribe, to attribute. You direct the writing *toward* someone or something, assigning credit or blame. To *zuschreiben* success to hard work is to write the success *to* the cause — you point the pen at the source and declare the connection. The prefix carries the directional force of attribution: meaning flows *toward* the designated origin.

Literal mnemonic: "write to." English "ascribe" comes from Latin *ad* + *scribere* — "write to." The German and English metaphors are identical: you write a quality *to* its source.

### ÜBER- ("over, above, across")

**Überschreiben:** to write over. Two related senses: (1) to transfer, to sign over, to make over — you write ownership *over* to another person, passing the title across; (2) to overwrite — you write new data *over* old, replacing what was there. Both involve the same gesture: one layer of writing supersedes another. The property is written over into a new name; the file is written over with new content.

Literal mnemonic: "overwrite." English uses "overwrite" for the data sense and "write over" or "sign over" for the transfer sense. German uses one verb for both.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays concrete; the prefixes abstract it.** *Schreiben* never stops meaning "to write." But the prefixes push writing into signing (*unterschreiben*), prescribing (*vorschreiben*), describing (*beschreiben*), and attributing (*zuschreiben*). The hand holding the pen is always there underneath the abstraction. Once you see the writing gesture in "describe" or "prescribe," the verbs become less arbitrary.

**Separable prefixes tend to be spatial and literal.** *Ab, an, auf, aus, ein, mit, nach, zurück, zusammen* — these preserve a physical directionality. You can picture where the writing goes: down onto paper (*auf*), along with speech (*mit*), back to the sender (*zurück*). The meanings stay close to the spatial image.

**Inseparable prefixes tend to be transformative.** *Be-, ver-, unter-, über-* — these fuse with the verb and shift it further from literal writing. *Beschreiben* transforms writing into depicting. *Verschreiben* splits into prescribing, devoting, and erring. These must often be learned as units, though the prefix logic remains visible once you know where to look.

**UMSCHREIBEN is the showcase for separable vs. inseparable.** The same letters produce two different verbs depending on stress and separability. Separable *umschreiben* (rewrite) treats *um-* as "over again" — a do-over. Inseparable *umschreiben* (paraphrase, circumscribe) treats *um-* as "around" — circling the meaning. This pair is worth memorizing as a prototype: when *um-* separates, it means repetition or reversal; when it fuses, it means encirclement.

**Latin and German often mirror each other.** *Unterschreiben* = subscribe (write under). *Vorschreiben* = prescribe (write before). *Beschreiben* = describe (write upon/about). *Zuschreiben* = ascribe (write to). *Überschreiben* = superscribe/overwrite (write over). This is not coincidence — both language families mapped spatial prefixes onto *scribere/schreiben* in parallel. Where the Latin-English and Germanic metaphors align, use the cognate as an anchor. Where they diverge — *verschreiben* has no single English equivalent — notice what German sees that English does not.
