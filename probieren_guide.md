# PROBIEREN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Probieren** means to try, to taste, to test. It comes from Latin *probāre* (to test, to prove) and carries the idea of putting something to the trial — tasting a soup, testing a method, attempting something to see if it works. Hold this image in mind: someone reaching out to sample, to check, to see what happens. The prefixes below reshape that basic act — redirecting it onto the body, intensifying it to completion, or extending it across a whole series.

---

## The Prefixes

### AN- ("on, onto, up against")

**Anprobieren:** to try on. The prefix pushes the trying toward contact with the body. You *probieren* clothing *an* — you put it on yourself to see how it fits, how it looks, whether it works. The act of testing is directed onto the wearer: the garment is held against or placed upon you as a trial. This is the standard everyday word for trying on clothes in a shop fitting room.

Literal mnemonic: "try on." English uses the identical construction — "try on a jacket" is exactly *eine Jacke anprobieren*.

### AUF- ("up, upon, open")

**Aufprobieren:** to try on. Like *anprobieren*, this means to try on clothing or accessories, but it is less common and carries a slightly more casual or regional flavour. Where *an-* emphasizes contact (placing the garment onto the body), *auf-* suggests placing something up onto yourself — a hat *aufprobiert* is set up on the head, a pair of glasses placed up on the nose. In practice, most speakers reach for *anprobieren* first; *aufprobieren* you will encounter but need not prefer.

Literal mnemonic: "try up (on)." Think of setting a hat up onto your head to see how it looks.

### AUS- ("out, outward, thoroughly")

**Ausprobieren:** to try out, to test, to experiment with. The prefix pushes the trying outward to its full extent. Where *probieren* alone is a quick taste or tentative attempt, *ausprobieren* is thorough — you follow the trial all the way out to a conclusion. A new recipe is *ausprobiert* — cooked and eaten, not just glanced at. A piece of software is *ausprobiert* — run through its paces, not just installed. The prefix insists on completeness: you do not stop at the first impression but try the thing out fully.

Literal mnemonic: "try out." English matches exactly — "try out a new restaurant" means to give it a genuine, complete test, not just peek through the window.

### DURCH- ("through, from one end to the other")

**Durchprobieren:** to try through one after another, to work through by trying. The prefix draws a line through a series. You do not try one thing — you try them all, moving through the set from beginning to end. A wine tasting where every bottle is sampled is a *Durchprobieren*. Trying every flavour at the ice cream shop, every dress on the rack, every setting on the dial — in each case you are trying your way *through* the entire range.

Literal mnemonic: "try through." Think of "working through" a whole list — tasting through every option until none remain untested.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The family is small and transparent.** Unlike stems such as *legen* or *stehen* that generate dozens of prefixed forms with wildly divergent meanings, *probieren* has only four common compounds, and all of them stay close to the literal meaning of "try." No prefix pushes *probieren* into deep metaphor or idiomatic opacity. This makes the family easy to learn as a set.

**Two prefixes share the same territory.** Both *anprobieren* and *aufprobieren* mean "to try on," which is unusual — German prefix verbs rarely overlap so closely. The distinction is subtle: *an-* (onto the body) is the standard, neutral choice; *auf-* (up onto) is rarer and tends to suit items placed on top of you (hats, glasses) rather than wrapped around you. When in doubt, use *anprobieren*.

**Separable prefixes, spatial logic.** All four prefixes are separable, and all four preserve a clear physical direction: *an* = onto, *auf* = up onto, *aus* = outward to completion, *durch* = through from end to end. You can picture the motion in every case. There are no inseparable, opaque compounds in this family — no *ver-*, no *be-*, no *er-*. The Latinate origin of *probieren* may explain this: German speakers tend to build fewer abstract prefix layers on borrowed stems than on native Germanic ones.

**English parallels are exact.** "Try on" = *anprobieren*. "Try out" = *ausprobieren*. "Try through" = *durchprobieren* (though English speakers would more naturally say "work through" or "go through"). Where the German prefix system and English phrasal verbs align this neatly, use the English as your anchor — the spatial logic is the same in both languages.
