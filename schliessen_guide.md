# SCHLIEßEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Schließen** means to close, to shut, to lock — but it also means to conclude and to infer. Hold all three senses together, because they share a single spatial logic: bringing two sides together until they meet. A door closes when its edges meet the frame. An argument closes when its parts come together into a conclusion. An inference closes when evidence and judgment meet in a deduction — you "close the gap" between what you know and what you reason. Nearly every prefix below reshapes one or more of these three gestures: physical closing, intellectual concluding, or logical inferring.

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from, completed")

**Abschließen** (sep.): to close off. The prefix signals completion and separation. Physically, you close something off by locking it — a door is *abgeschlossen*, locked, sealed away from access. Abstractly, you close off a process: a project is *abgeschlossen* — concluded, finished, brought to its endpoint. A contract is *abgeschlossen* — signed, entered into, closed between the parties. In every case the image is the same: the closing gesture reaches its terminal point, and what was open is now shut off, wrapped up, done.

Literal mnemonic: "close off." English uses "close off" for physical barriers and "close out" for finishing — *abschließen* covers both.

### AN- ("on, onto, up against")

**Anschließen** (sep.): to close onto, to connect. The prefix pushes the closing gesture toward contact. You bring something up against something else until the two join. A cable is *angeschlossen* — connected, hooked up, closed onto the device. A person *schließt sich an* — joins a group, closes the gap between themselves and the others. The image is always attachment: two things are brought together until they link.

Literal mnemonic: "close onto." Think of plugging in a cable — you push the connector *onto* the port until it clicks closed.

### AUF- ("up, open")

**Aufschließen** (sep.): to close open — which is to say, to unlock. This looks contradictory until you remember that a lock is a closing mechanism, and *auf-* reverses the closure. You turn the key and the lock opens *up*. The fortress that was *geschlossen* is now *aufgeschlossen* — its barrier has been undone. The prefix negates the stem's default direction.

Literal mnemonic: "close up" in the sense of "undo the closure." English "open up" works the same way — "up" signals release.

### AUS- ("out, outward, away")

**Ausschließen** (sep.): to close out. You shut the door with someone on the outside. Physically, to lock out. Figuratively, to exclude, to shut out, to rule out. A possibility is *ausgeschlossen* — closed out, eliminated, no longer inside the set of options. A person is *ausgeschlossen* — expelled, shut out of the group. The prefix pushes the closing gesture outward: whatever is *aus*-closed stays on the other side.

Literal mnemonic: "close out" or "shut out." English preserves this exactly — "that possibility is shut out" = *ausgeschlossen*.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Beschließen** (insep.): to close upon thoroughly, to decide. The *be-* prefix stamps the action fully onto an object. When a committee *beschließt* something, it closes deliberation upon the matter — sealing the question, resolving it, rendering a decision. The closing is intellectual: the debate is shut, the verdict is laid down. *Beschließen* also means to conclude in the sense of ending — to close out a meeting, a chapter, a ceremony.

Mnemonic: "be-close" = close thoroughly, close the matter. A decision is a door that has been fully shut on further discussion.

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Einschließen** (sep.): to close in. The prefix directs the closing gesture inward, creating containment. Literally, to lock someone in — the door closes with the person inside. By extension, to enclose, to surround — walls *schließen* a courtyard *ein*, closing it in on all sides. Most abstractly, to include: when something is *eingeschlossen*, it is enclosed within the set, contained inside the scope. The same spatial image — closing inward — produces locking in, encircling, and including.

Literal mnemonic: "close in." English "enclose" (from Latin) mirrors the Germanic logic exactly. "The price includes tax" = the tax is closed inside the price.

### ENT- ("away from, out of, un-")

**Entschließen** (insep.): to close away from — to decide, to resolve, to make up one's mind. Always reflexive: *sich entschließen*. The image is striking: you close yourself away from doubt, away from indecision. Where *beschließen* closes deliberation *upon* a matter (the committee decides), *entschließen* closes the self *away from* uncertainty (the individual resolves). You extract yourself from the open state of not-knowing by shutting a door behind you.

Literal mnemonic: "close away from." You close yourself away from hesitation. Once *entschlossen*, the door to doubt is shut behind you.

### ER- (achievement, bringing forth, opening up)

**Erschließen** (insep.): to close into being — which yields the surprising meaning of opening up, developing, making accessible. *Er-* often signals that an action achieves a result, brings something into existence. To *erschließen* land is to develop it, to open it up for use — the resources that were locked away are now brought forth. To *erschließen* meaning from a text is to infer it, to deduce it — you close the logical gap and arrive at understanding. The paradox dissolves when you see that *er-* transforms closing from shutting-down into unlocking-by-completion: the inference closes the circle of reasoning and thereby opens up the conclusion.

Literal mnemonic: "close forth" or "close into existence." Think of "unlock" — you close the key's turn and thereby open the door to new territory.

### UM- ("around, about")

**Umschließen** (insep.): to close around. The prefix wraps the closing gesture into a circle. To enclose, to surround, to encircle, to embrace. Walls *umschließen* a city — they close around it. Arms *umschließen* a person — they close around them in an embrace. The image is always encirclement: the closure forms a ring.

Literal mnemonic: "close around." English "encompass" carries the same spatial logic — "compass" means circle.

### VER- (intensification, sealing, making permanent)

**Verschließen** (insep.): to close intensely, to close for good. The *ver-* prefix drives the closing to its extreme: not just closed but sealed, locked tight, shut beyond casual opening. A jar is *verschlossen* — sealed. A safe is *verschlossen* — locked up. Figuratively, a person is *verschlossen* — closed off, reserved, sealed against emotional access. Where *schließen* simply closes, *verschließen* makes the closure thorough and lasting.

Literal mnemonic: "close away into permanence." Think of the difference between shutting a door and bolting it — *verschließen* is the bolt.

### WEG- ("away, gone")

**Wegschließen** (sep.): to close away. You put something behind a lock and remove it from access. Valuables are *weggeschlossen* — locked away, placed behind closure and removed from the scene. A prisoner is *weggeschlossen* — locked up, closed away from society. The prefix adds simple distance: the thing is not just locked but gone from sight.

Literal mnemonic: "close away." English "lock away" captures it perfectly — shut it and put it out of reach.

### ZUSAMMEN- ("together")

**Zusammenschließen** (sep.): to close together. Separate entities come together and close into a unit. Always reflexive: *sich zusammenschließen*. Companies merge — they close together into one. People unite, form an alliance — they close ranks, close themselves into a collective. The image is of multiple closings converging until the separate parts are sealed into a whole.

Literal mnemonic: "close together." English "close ranks" uses the same metaphor — the spaces between individuals close and they become a unified front.

### ZU- ("to, toward, shut")

**Zuschließen** (sep.): to close to, to lock. The prefix reinforces the basic closing gesture — you close the door *to*, pushing it toward its frame until it shuts. *Zuschließen* is the everyday word for locking a door. Where *verschließen* emphasizes the thoroughness of the seal, *zuschließen* is the plain physical act: turn the key, close the lock *to*.

Literal mnemonic: "close to." English "shut to" preserves this: "pull the door to" means close it. *Zuschließen* adds the turn of the key.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem carries three senses, and the prefixes select among them.** *Schließen* means to close, to conclude, and to infer — but most prefixed forms draw on only one or two of these. The physical-closing sense dominates (*auf-, zu-, ver-, weg-*). The concluding sense surfaces in *ab-* (finish, finalize) and *be-* (decide, resolve). The inferring sense appears in *er-* (deduce, develop by reasoning). Watching which sense each prefix activates is the key to this verb family.

**Separable prefixes stay spatial and concrete.** *Ab, an, auf, aus, ein, weg, zu, zusammen* — these preserve a physical directionality. You can picture the motion: closing off, closing onto, closing open, closing out, closing in, closing away, closing to, closing together. Even when figurative, the spatial image holds.

**Inseparable prefixes transform and abstract.** *Be-, ent-, er-, um-, ver-* — these fuse with the stem and push its meaning further from the literal. *Beschließen* turns closing into deciding. *Entschließen* turns closing into resolving. *Erschließen* turns closing into opening up. These require learning as units, though the spatial logic still rewards examination.

**Closing and opening are two sides of one gesture.** *Aufschließen* (unlock) and *zuschließen* (lock) are mirror images. *Einschließen* (include) and *ausschließen* (exclude) are complementary. *Erschließen* (open up) reverses the stem's default direction. German treats the lock as a single mechanism that both closes and opens — the prefix determines which direction the key turns.

**English sometimes preserves the same logic.** "Shut out" = *ausschließen*. "Lock in" = *einschließen*. "Close off" = *abschließen*. "Lock away" = *wegschließen*. "Close ranks" = *zusammenschließen*. Where English and German share the metaphor, use it as a bridge. Where they diverge — especially in the abstract senses of deciding (*beschließen*, *entschließen*) and inferring (*erschließen*) — notice that German extends the closing metaphor into territory where English switches to entirely different verbs.
