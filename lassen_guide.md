# LASSEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Lassen** means to let, to allow, to leave. It implies permission, release, or withdrawal — the deliberate act of not holding, not intervening, not preventing. Hold this image in mind: an open hand that releases its grip. Nearly every prefix below reshapes that basic gesture — redirecting it, restricting it, completing it, or abstracting it into metaphor. Where *legen* is the hand that places, *lassen* is the hand that lets go.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Ablassen** (sep.): to let off, to let away. The prefix pulls the releasing gesture downward and away. Steam is *abgelassen* — let off, vented. Water is *abgelassen* — drained, let out of a tank. A person who *von etwas ablässt* desists — lets off from a pursuit, releases their grip on it. The image is always relief of pressure: something held in is let away.

Literal mnemonic: "let off." English uses the same construction — "let off steam," "let off the pressure."

### AN- ("on, onto, up against")

**Anlassen** (sep.): to let on. The prefix pushes the releasing gesture toward activation. An engine is *angelassen* — let on, set into motion by releasing the starter. A coat is *angelassen* — left on, allowed to remain on the body. In metalwork, steel is *angelassen* — tempered, heated and allowed to cool slowly. The common thread: something is released into a state of running, staying, or being.

Literal mnemonic: "let on" or "leave on." You leave the motor on; you leave your coat on.

### AUF- ("up, upon, open")

**Auflassen** (sep.): to leave open, to keep open. The prefix lifts and opens. A window is *aufgelassen* — left in its open position. A door is *aufgelassen* — not pulled shut. The gesture is one of non-closure: you let the open state persist, you do not intervene to close.

Literal mnemonic: "leave up / leave open." "Leave the window up" captures the sense exactly.

### AUS- ("out, outward, spread")

**Auslassen** (sep.): to leave out, to let out. The prefix sends the gesture outward. A word is *ausgelassen* — left out, omitted, skipped over. Frustration is *ausgelassen* — let out, vented upon someone. Fat is *ausgelassen* — rendered, melted out of its solid form. In every case something moves from inside to outside, or from inclusion to exclusion.

Literal mnemonic: "let out" and "leave out." English preserves both — "leave out a detail," "let out your anger."

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Belassen** (insep.): to leave something in its current state, thoroughly and deliberately. *Be-* stamps the action fully onto its object. To *belassen* is to let stand, to leave as is — to make a conscious decision not to alter. Where plain *lassen* is casual permission, *belassen* is a firm, transitive resolution: I leave *this thing* alone.

Mnemonic: think of "be-" as "all over." To *belassen* is to pass your hands over something and declare it unchanged — "let it be."

### DURCH- ("through")

**Durchlassen** (sep.): to let through. The prefix opens a passage. A guard *lässt* travelers *durch* — lets them through the checkpoint. A filter *lässt* light *durch* — allows it to pass. The gesture is one of clearing a path: whatever was blocked is now permitted to move from one side to the other.

Literal mnemonic: "let through." "Let me through" = *Lass mich durch.*

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Einlassen** (sep.): to let in. The prefix directs the releasing gesture inward. A guest is *eingelassen* — admitted, let inside. Reflexively, *sich auf etwas einlassen* means to let yourself into something — to get involved, to engage, to enter into a risky affair or negotiation. A floor is *eingelassen* — treated, coated, as though the varnish is let into the wood. The common thread: passage across a threshold, from outside to inside.

Literal mnemonic: "let in." "Don't let him in" and "don't get into that" use the same spatial logic.

### ENT- ("away from, removed from, un-")

**Entlassen** (insep.): to let away from, to release from. The prefix signals separation and removal. An employee is *entlassen* — dismissed, released from service. A patient is *entlassen* — discharged, released from the hospital. A prisoner is *entlassen* — freed, released from custody. In every case the prefix severs a bond: the person is let away from the institution that held them.

Literal mnemonic: "let away from." Think of the door opening and the person walking away — the tie is cut. English "dismiss" (send away) and "discharge" (unload) are less transparent than the German: *ent-* (away from) + *lassen* (let) = let go.

### ER- (achievement, completion)

**Erlassen** (insep.): to let into completion. *Er-* signals that the letting reaches a decisive, final outcome. A law is *erlassen* — issued, enacted, let forth with authority and finality. A debt is *erlassen* — remitted, waived, let go completely. The two senses share a common gesture of authoritative release: the ruler lets the decree go forth into the world; the creditor lets the obligation go entirely.

Mnemonic: "let through to the end." An edict is released into force. A debt is released into nothingness. Both are final and complete.

### FORT- ("away, onward, gone")

**Fortlassen** (sep.): to let go, to leave out. The prefix simply adds the direction "away." You *lässt* someone *fort* — you let them go, allow them to leave. You *lässt* a passage *fort* — you leave it out, omit it from the text. Straightforward and transparent.

Literal mnemonic: "let forth" or "leave off." The thing or person departs and is gone.

### GE- (past participle prefix — here fossilized)

**Gelassen** (adj.): calm, composed, serene. Originally the past participle of *lassen* — one who has let, who has released. Over time it fossilized into a personality trait: a person who is *gelassen* has let go of agitation, released their grip on anxiety. They are unruffled because they have allowed disturbance to pass through without holding on. The word is a quiet philosophy compressed into a participle: composure is the state of having let go.

Mnemonic: "one who has let go." A *gelassener Mensch* is someone who has released the need to control, to react, to grip. English "letting go" as spiritual advice points at the same idea, but German made it into an adjective.

### HERAUS- ("out from within")

**Herauslassen** (sep.): to let out from inside. A cat is *herausgelassen* — let out of the house. A secret is *herausgelassen* — let out from concealment. The prefix is specific about direction: outward from an interior, toward the speaker's side.

Literal mnemonic: "let out (from in there)." More specific than *auslassen* — the source is an enclosed space.

### HIN- ("thither, toward a point away from the speaker")

**Hinlassen** (sep.): to let go there, to allow to go. The prefix gives direction: toward a distant point. You *lässt* someone *hin* — you permit them to go to that place. Uncommon and transparent.

Literal mnemonic: "let go (over there)."

### HINTER- ("behind")

**Hinterlassen** (insep.): to leave behind. The prefix is spatial — *behind* — but the meaning is temporal and existential. A dead person *hinterlässt* an inheritance — leaves it behind for those who follow. A fire *hinterlässt* damage — leaves destruction behind it. A person *hinterlässt* a good impression — leaves it lodged in the minds behind them. The word carries weight: what is *hinterlassen* persists after the one who left has gone.

Literal mnemonic: "leave behind." English uses the same construction — "she left behind a fortune," "the storm left devastation behind it." The German is inseparable because *hinter-* has fused with the verb; this is not casual leaving but legacy.

### LOS- ("loose, free, released")

**Loslassen** (sep.): to let loose, to let go. The prefix means release — and when applied to *lassen*, which already means to let, the result is intensified permission: to set free, to unleash, to release one's hold entirely. You *lässt* the rope *los* — you open your hand. You *lässt* the dog *los* — you unleash it. The image is a doubled releasing: letting the letting happen.

Literal mnemonic: "let loose." "Let go of the rope" and "let loose the hounds" are exact English parallels.

### NACH- ("after, behind, following")

**Nachlassen** (sep.): to let after, to let down. The prefix points backward or downward in intensity. Pain *lässt nach* — it lets up, eases, wanes. Quality *lässt nach* — it slackens, diminishes. A seller *lässt* something *nach* — gives a discount, lets the price come down. The underlying image is of tension releasing gradually: whatever was taut goes slack, whatever was intense softens. As a legal term, *nachlassen* also means to bequeath — to leave behind for those who come after.

Literal mnemonic: "let up" and "let off." English "the rain let up" uses the same metaphor of diminishing intensity.

### UNTER- ("under, beneath")

**Unterlassen** (insep.): to leave under, to let remain undone. The prefix pushes the action beneath the surface — into non-occurrence. To *unterlassen* is to refrain from, to omit, to fail to do. The thing is not done; it is left below the threshold of action. This is *lassen* at its most philosophical: the deliberate decision to let something remain in the unrealized.

Literal mnemonic: "leave undone." Something *unterlassen* never rises to the surface. English "omit" (send away) is less spatial than the German: the action is suppressed, held under.

### VER- (transformation, displacement, intensification)

**Verlassen** (insep.): to leave utterly, to let go completely. *Ver-* intensifies and transforms. As a verb: to *verlassen* a place is to leave it — to let go of it entirely, to walk away. You *verlässt* a person — you abandon them, forsake them. Reflexively, *sich auf jemanden verlassen* means to rely on someone — to let yourself fall completely onto them, to entrust yourself. As an adjective: *verlassen* means abandoned, deserted, desolate — the state of having been completely let go of.

The two senses — abandon and rely — are mirrors. To abandon is to let go of someone utterly. To rely is to let go of yourself utterly, onto someone else. Both require total release.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "completely away." *Verlassen* a city = let go of it completely. *Sich verlassen* on a friend = let yourself go completely into their hands. The adjective captures what remains when someone has been *ver-lassen*: emptiness, desolation.

### VOR- ("before, forward, in front of")

**Vorlassen** (sep.): to let before, to let go ahead. You *lässt* someone *vor* — you allow them to go in front of you, to pass ahead, to be admitted before you. At a doctor's office, *jemanden vorlassen* means to let someone go ahead of you in the queue.

Literal mnemonic: "let before." "Let her go ahead" = *Lass sie vor.*

### WEG- ("away, gone")

**Weglassen** (sep.): to leave away, to leave out. You *lässt* something *weg* — you omit it, drop it, skip it. An unnecessary word is *weggelassen* — left away, excluded. The simplest "away" prefix applied to *lassen*: the thing is allowed to be absent.

Literal mnemonic: "leave away." "Leave that part out" = *Lass das weg.*

### ZER- ("apart, to pieces, asunder")

**Zerlassen** (sep.): to let apart, to melt. The prefix is destructive — it scatters the structure. Butter is *zerlassen* — melted, its solid form let apart into liquid. The image is beautifully precise: you do not force the butter to melt; you simply let it come apart under heat. *Zer-* provides the disintegration; *lassen* provides the permission. The butter is allowed to fall to pieces.

Literal mnemonic: "let asunder." Melting is what happens when you let a solid's structure come apart. Compare *zerlegen* (take apart by laying) with *zerlassen* (let apart by allowing) — one is active, the other passive.

### ZU- ("to, toward, closed")

**Zulassen** (insep.): to let to, to allow toward. The prefix pushes the permission toward an official threshold. To *zulassen* is to permit formally — to license, to authorize, to register. A vehicle is *zugelassen* — registered, officially allowed onto the road. A doctor is *zugelassen* — licensed, allowed to practice. More loosely, *etwas zulassen* means to tolerate it, to allow it to happen. The gesture is of opening the gate toward entry: what is *zugelassen* has been granted passage to legitimacy.

Literal mnemonic: "let to" in the sense of "admit to." "Admitted to practice" and "licensed for road use" both pass through the same gate.

### ZURÜCK- ("back, backward, in return")

**Zurücklassen** (sep.): to leave back, to leave behind. You *lässt* something *zurück* — you go forward and it remains behind. Possessions are *zurückgelassen* — left behind in the old house. A person is *zurückgelassen* — abandoned, left behind while others move on. Where *hinterlassen* implies legacy and inheritance, *zurücklassen* is rawer: the thing simply stays where it was while the subject departs.

Literal mnemonic: "leave back." "She left everything back at the house" = she went forward and her things remained behind.

### ÜBER- ("over, above, beyond")

**Überlassen** (insep.): to leave over to, to hand over. The prefix sends the gesture across a boundary toward someone else. You *überlässt* a decision to a colleague — you leave it over to them, cede it, relinquish your claim. You *überlässt* someone their fate — you hand them over to it, you withdraw and let the other force take charge. The image is of transferring permission itself: you pass your authority over the thing to someone else.

Literal mnemonic: "leave over to." "I'll leave that to you" = *Ich überlasse das dir.* The "over" marks the crossing from your domain into theirs.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays passive; the prefix provides direction.** *Lassen* never stops meaning "to let, to allow, to leave." But the prefixes channel that permission into specific trajectories. *Einlassen* (let in) directs it inward. *Entlassen* (let away from) directs it out of an institution. *Nachlassen* (let after) lets intensity trail off. The core gesture is always the open hand — the prefixes determine which way the released thing travels.

**Separable prefixes tend to be spatial and concrete.** *Ab, an, auf, aus, durch, ein, fort, heraus, hin, los, nach, vor, weg, zurück* — these are words that can stand alone as adverbs or prepositions. They preserve a physical directionality even in figurative use. *Durchlassen* is letting through a gate; *loslassen* is letting go of a rope. You can usually picture the motion.

**Inseparable prefixes tend to be institutional and weighty.** *Be-, ent-, er-, hinter-, über-, unter-, ver-, zu-* — these fuse with the verb and often shift its register toward the formal, legal, or existential. *Entlassen* is not casually "letting go" but officially dismissing. *Erlassen* is not merely releasing but issuing a decree or remitting a debt. *Zulassen* is not just allowing but licensing. The inseparable prefix locks the meaning into something deliberate and consequential.

**GELASSEN and VERLASSEN preserve emotional fossils.** *Gelassen* (calm) is the state of having let go — composure as past participle. *Verlassen* (abandoned) is the state of having been completely left — desolation as past participle. Both reveal how German turns completed actions into permanent emotional conditions. The language asks: *What has been done to this person?* They have been let go of (*verlassen*) or they have let go of everything (*gelassen*). The answer becomes their character or their condition.

**LASSEN and LEGEN as mirrors.** Many prefixes appear with both stems, but the meanings diverge instructively. *Zerlegen* (lay apart) is active — you disassemble with your hands. *Zerlassen* (let apart) is passive — you allow heat to do the work. *Hinterlegen* (lay behind) means to deposit for safekeeping. *Hinterlassen* (leave behind) means to bequeath after death. *Legen* is the hand that places; *lassen* is the hand that releases. The same prefix, applied to placement and to permission, yields sibling words with fascinatingly different temperaments.

**English sometimes preserves the same logic.** "Let off" = *ablassen*. "Let through" = *durchlassen*. "Let loose" = *loslassen*. "Leave out" = *weglassen* or *auslassen*. Where English and German share the construction, use it as an anchor. Where they diverge, notice that German's *lassen* compounds often carry more institutional weight than English's casual "let" — *entlassen* is a dismissal, not just a release; *zulassen* is a licensing, not just a permission.
