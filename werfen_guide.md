# WERFEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Werfen** means to throw, to cast, to hurl. It implies a forceful propulsion — launching something from the hand outward through space. Hold this image in mind: an arm cocked back and then releasing, sending an object flying. Nearly every prefix below redirects that throw — downward, backward, apart, or into abstraction where "throwing" becomes designing, rejecting, or subjugating.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abwerfen:** to throw off. The prefix pulls the throwing action away from the subject. A horse *wirft* its rider *ab* — throws them off, unseats them. A snake *wirft* its skin *ab* — sheds it, throws it off. An investment *wirft* profit *ab* — throws off returns, yields income. In every case, something is being cast away from the source.

Literal mnemonic: "throw off." English uses this exact construction — "the horse threw off the rider," "the investment throws off dividends."

### AN- ("on, onto, toward")

**Anwerfen:** to throw on, to set going. The prefix directs the throw toward engagement. You *wirfst* an engine *an* — you throw it into motion, you crank it, you start it up. The image is of throwing a switch or turning a crank to set machinery in motion. A kick-start is a literal *Anwerfen* — a throw that initiates movement.

Literal mnemonic: "throw on." You throw the engine on — you start it.

### AUF- ("up, upon, open")

**Aufwerfen:** to throw up. The prefix lifts the throw upward. Earth is *aufgeworfen* — piled up, thrown into a mound. A question is *aufgeworfen* — raised, thrown up for discussion. The gesture is always upward: material heaped up, or an issue tossed into the air for everyone to see.

Literal mnemonic: "throw up." Not in the nauseous sense, but "to throw up a question" or "to throw up a barricade."

### AUS- ("out, outward")

**Auswerfen:** to throw out. The prefix directs the throw outward, expelling what was inside. A volcano *wirft* lava *aus* — ejects it, casts it out. A person *wirft* phlegm *aus* — expectorates, spits out. The image is always ejection: something propelled from inside to outside.

Literal mnemonic: "throw out" or "cast out." The body throws out what doesn't belong; the machine ejects what's spent.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Bewerfen:** to throw at. The *be-* prefix takes the throwing action and stamps it onto a target. You don't just throw — you throw *at* something. Children *bewerfen* each other with snowballs — they pelt each other. A wall is *beworfen* with plaster — it is rendered, covered by throwing material at it. The prefix makes the target the grammatical object.

Mnemonic: think of *be-* as "all over." To *bewerfen* is to throw at something until it's covered.

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Einwerfen:** to throw in. The prefix directs the throw inward, into a container or space. A coin is *eingeworfen* — dropped in, inserted into a slot. A letter is *eingeworfen* — posted, thrown into a mailbox. A window is *eingeworfen* — smashed, broken inward by throwing something through it. A remark is *eingeworfen* — interjected, thrown into a conversation.

Literal mnemonic: "throw in." English "interject" (from Latin *intericere*, to throw between) matches the conversational sense exactly.

### ENTGEGEN- ("against, toward, in the face of")

**Entgegenwerfen:** to throw against, to hurl at. The prefix directs the throw toward an oncoming force — you throw something at someone approaching you, or you fling a retort in someone's face. The gesture is confrontational: meeting force with force.

Literal mnemonic: "throw against." You throw something into the teeth of the opposition.

### ENT- ("away from, out of" — here conceptual emergence)

**Entwerfen:** to throw out from the mind, to design. This is one of the most remarkable *ent-* compounds. The prefix signals emergence — something thrown out of the imagination into the world. To *entwerfen* is to draft, to sketch, to design — to cast a concept outward from thought into form. An *Entwurf* (draft, design) is literally "a throw-out," an idea cast onto paper.

Mnemonic: "throw forth from nothing." A designer *entwirft* — throws a vision out of the void and onto the page. Think of how English says "to cast" a vision or "to project" an idea.

### FORT- ("away, onward")

**Fortwerfen:** to throw forth, to throw away. You throw something away from yourself and it is gone. Simple and direct — the prefix adds "away" to the throwing gesture.

Literal mnemonic: "throw forth" in the sense of discarding. The object is thrown away and forgotten.

### HERAUS- ("out from within")

**Herauswerfen:** to throw out from inside. You eject someone or something from an enclosed space. A bouncer *wirft* a patron *heraus* — throws them out. An employer *wirft* an employee *heraus* — fires them, expels them. The prefix emphasizes the transition from inside to outside.

Literal mnemonic: "throw out (from in here)." The troublemaker is thrown out of the building.

### HIN- ("thither, down, toward a point")

**Hinwerfen:** to throw down. The prefix directs the throw downward and away from the speaker. You *wirfst* something *hin* — you toss it down carelessly. Reflexively, *sich hinwerfen* means to throw yourself down — to prostrate yourself. A remark is *hingeworfen* — tossed off casually, thrown out without much thought.

Literal mnemonic: "throw down (over there)." "He threw down his pen in frustration."

### UM- ("around, over, down")

**Umwerfen:** to throw over, to knock over. The prefix implies a change of orientation — from upright to fallen. You *wirfst* a glass *um* — you knock it over. Figuratively, something *wirft* you *um* — it bowls you over, it stuns you, it overwhelms you. The image is of something standing that gets knocked flat.

Literal mnemonic: "throw over." A wind that "throws the fence over" = *wirft den Zaun um.*

### UNTER- ("under, beneath")

**Unterwerfen:** to throw under. The inseparable *unter-* transforms the throw into domination. To *unterwerfen* a people is to subjugate them — to throw them under your authority. Reflexively, *sich unterwerfen* is to submit — to throw yourself under someone else's power. The image is visceral: the conquered are thrown beneath the conqueror.

Literal mnemonic: "throw under." English "subjugate" (from Latin *sub* + *jugum*, under the yoke) uses the same spatial logic. To *unterwerfen* is to throw under the yoke.

### VER- (transformation, rejection, negation)

**Verwerfen:** to throw away, to reject. The *ver-* prefix transforms the throw into a judgment of worthlessness. To *verwerfen* is to reject, to dismiss, to discard — to throw something so far away that it is gone from consideration. A court *verwirft* an appeal — quashes it, throws it out. The prefix carries the sense of definitive disposal.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "away into negation." What is *verworfen* has been thrown away and will not return.

### VOR- ("before, forward, in front of")

**Vorwerfen:** to throw before, to cast in someone's face. You *wirfst* someone something *vor* — you throw an accusation before them, you reproach them with it. The gesture is confrontational: the blame is cast forward, laid at the person's feet. A *Vorwurf* (reproach) is literally "a throw-before."

Literal mnemonic: "throw before (someone's face)." "She threw his failures in his face" = *Sie warf ihm seine Fehler vor.*

### WEG- ("away, gone")

**Wegwerfen:** to throw away. The most literal disposal compound — you throw something away and it is gone. Into the trash, into the void. A *Wegwerfgesellschaft* (throwaway society) is built on this word. The gesture is final: what is *weggeworfen* is no longer wanted.

Literal mnemonic: "throw away." No metaphor needed — it means exactly what it says.

### ZURÜCK- ("back, backward")

**Zurückwerfen:** to throw back. You return the throw to its origin. A ball is *zurückgeworfen* — thrown back. An army is *zurückgeworfen* — repelled, driven back. Light is *zurückgeworfen* — reflected, bounced back. A setback *wirft* someone *zurück* — throws them back in progress. The prefix reverses the direction of the throw.

Literal mnemonic: "throw back." English "repel" (from Latin *repellere*, to drive back) and "reflect" (from *reflectere*, to bend back) capture the same motion.

### ZUSAMMEN- ("together")

**Zusammenwerfen:** to throw together. You take separate things and cast them into one heap. To *zusammenwerfen* is to jumble, to lump together — to throw disparate items into a single pile without distinction.

Literal mnemonic: "throw together." "Don't just throw everything together in one pile."

### ÜBER- ("over, across" — sep. and insep.)

**Überwerfen:** This verb has two lives depending on stress and separability. As a separable verb (*überwirft*): to throw on, to throw over oneself — you throw a coat or shawl over your shoulders. As an inseparable verb (*sich überwerfen*): to fall out with, to quarrel — metaphorically, to throw yourself crosswise with someone, to become entangled in conflict. The same prefix, but separable keeps the physical image while inseparable goes abstract.

Literal mnemonic: Separable — "throw over (your shoulders)." Inseparable — "throw (yourself) over (into conflict with someone)."

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays kinetic; the prefix specifies the trajectory.** *Werfen* never stops meaning "to throw." But the prefixes redirect that throw into remarkably diverse territory. *Einwerfen* (throw in) can mean posting a letter or smashing a window. *Entwerfen* (throw out from the mind) means to design. The arm is always throwing — only the target and purpose change.

**Separable prefixes tend to be spatial and concrete.** *Ab, an, auf, aus, ein, hin, um, vor, weg, zurück, zusammen* — these preserve a physical directionality. You can see the throw's arc. *Abwerfen* throws off; *aufwerfen* throws up; *hinwerfen* throws down.

**Inseparable prefixes tend to be transformative and opaque.** *Be-, ent-, unter-, ver-* fuse with the verb and push the meaning far from literal throwing. *Entwerfen* (to design) and *unterwerfen* (to subjugate) require learning as whole units — the connection to "throw" is there but buried under centuries of metaphor.

**Überwerfen illustrates the sep./insep. split perfectly.** When separable, it is physical and concrete — throwing a garment over your shoulders. When inseparable, it is abstract and relational — falling out with someone. This pattern repeats across many German verbs: the separable form keeps the literal image; the inseparable form has drifted into metaphor.

**English often preserves the same logic through Latin.** "Eject" = throw out = *auswerfen*. "Project" = throw forward = *entwerfen*. "Subject" = throw under = *unterwerfen*. "Reject" = throw back = *verwerfen*. "Interject" = throw between = *einwerfen*. The Latin root *jacere/jectare* (to throw) maps almost perfectly onto *werfen* and its prefixes.
