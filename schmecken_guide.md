# SCHMECKEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Schmecken** means to taste, to savor, to like the taste of. It covers both directions of tasting: perceiving a flavor (*das schmeckt gut* — that tastes good) and actively sampling something (*schmeck mal!* — have a taste!). Hold this image: a tongue meeting food and registering its quality. The two prefixed forms below adjust that basic act — one narrows it to a final check, the other highlights which flavor dominates.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from, to completion")

**Abschmecken:** to taste off, to taste to completion. The prefix *ab-* often signals finishing or finalizing. Here it turns the open act of tasting into a purposeful, concluding step: you taste the dish one last time and adjust the seasoning until it is right. A soup is *abgeschmeckt* with salt, pepper, or a squeeze of lemon — the cook samples, corrects, and signs off. The gesture is quality control at the end of a process: tasting something *off* the checklist.

Literal mnemonic: "taste off" in the sense of "finish off the tasting." The cook tastes the dish, tweaks it, and checks it off as done.

### VOR- ("before, forward, in front of, dominant")

**Vorschmecken:** to taste forth, to taste before everything else. The prefix *vor-* pushes something to the foreground. When a flavor *vorschmeckt*, it steps forward ahead of the others — it is the prevailing taste, the one that announces itself first. Garlic *schmeckt vor* in an over-seasoned sauce; smokiness *schmeckt vor* in a peated whisky. The prefix does not describe the taster's action but the flavor's behavior: it presents itself in front of all the rest.

Literal mnemonic: "fore-taste." English "foretaste" means a preview, an advance sample — German *vorschmecken* captures the same "out in front" logic, applied to whichever flavor leads the ensemble.

---

## Patterns Worth Noting

**The family is small but tidy.** With only two prefixed forms, *schmecken* illustrates how even a compact verb family follows the same spatial logic found in larger ones. *Ab-* finalizes; *vor-* foregrounds. Both prefixes behave here exactly as they do across hundreds of other German verbs.

**Both prefixes stay concrete and separable.** There are no inseparable, abstract transformations in this family — no *ver-*, *er-*, or *be-* compounds pulling the stem into distant metaphor. Tasting remains tasting; the prefixes only redirect when or how the tasting happens.

**English parallels help.** "Taste off" is not standard English, but "finish off" and "check off" use *off* in the same completive way as *ab-*. "Foretaste" maps directly onto *vorschmecken*. Lean on these echoes.
