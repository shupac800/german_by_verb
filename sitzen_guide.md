# SITZEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Sitzen** means to sit, to be seated, to be located. Unlike *legen* (to lay) or *stellen* (to place), *sitzen* is stative — it describes not an action upon an object but a state the subject is in. Hold this image: a person seated in a chair, occupying a fixed position. The body is still, the position is established. This stative quality — being rather than doing — runs through every prefixed form below. German has a family of such posture verbs (*sitzen, liegen, stehen*) that double as metaphors for how things exist in the world: fitting, belonging, being stuck, being in charge. The prefixes do not redirect a gesture the way they redirect *legen*; instead they reshape the conditions of sitting — where, how long, why, and to what consequence.

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from, completion")

**Absitzen:** to sit off, to sit through to the end. Two images share the prefix. First, *serving time*: you sit through a sentence until the time peels away — *absitzen* a prison term is to sit it off, to endure it to completion. Second, *dismounting*: you move your seated body off a horse or bicycle. In both cases the prefix signals departure from the seated position or from the duration being sat through.

Literal mnemonic: "sit off." English "sit out a sentence" captures the temporal meaning; "get off" captures the physical one.

### AUF- ("up, upon, open")

**Aufsitzen:** to sit up onto. The prefix lifts the body upward onto something — you mount a horse, you climb on. But German extends the image with dark humor: to *aufsitzen* is also to be duped, to fall for something. The logic: you were placed up on something that turned out not to support you, like a rider set on a horse that bolts. You sat up on a lie.

Literal mnemonic: "sit up on." Mounting is sitting up onto the saddle. Being deceived is being "set up."

### AUS- ("out, outward, through to the end")

**Aussitzen:** to sit out. You remain seated while the problem, the storm, the scandal passes around you. *Aussitzen* is a strategy of inaction — you do not flee, you do not fight, you simply sit and wait until the difficulty exhausts itself. The word became famous in German political commentary: to *aussitzen* a crisis is to outlast it by refusing to move.

Literal mnemonic: "sit out." English uses the identical construction — "she sat out the controversy" — with the identical meaning.

### BEI- ("beside, alongside, with")

**Beisitzen:** to sit beside. You take a seat alongside the main participants — not leading, but present, contributing. A *Beisitzer* is an assessor, a lay judge, a committee member who sits in on proceedings. The prefix positions you at the table without placing you at its head.

Literal mnemonic: "sit by." English "sit in on a meeting" captures the same ancillary presence.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Besitzen:** to sit upon thoroughly — to own, to possess, to have. This is the most striking transformation in the family. The inseparable *be-* takes the stative act of sitting and stamps it onto an object: you sit upon something so completely that it becomes yours. Property is what you sit on. Land, a house, wealth, a quality — to *besitzen* any of them is to occupy them entirely, to sit over them as a permanent claim. English "possess" comes from Latin *possidēre* — literally "to sit upon" (*potis* + *sedēre*). The metaphor is universal: ownership is occupation.

Mnemonic: "sit upon." The landowner sits upon his land. The person who *besitzt* courage has planted themselves upon it — it belongs to them. Latin and German arrived at the same image independently.

### DA- ("there")

**Dasitzen:** to sit there, to be sitting there. The prefix pins the sitting to a visible location — it draws attention to the fact that someone is right there, seated, present to be observed. *Da sitzen sie nun* — there they sit. The word often carries a note of helplessness or conspicuousness: just sitting there, doing nothing, unable to act.

Literal mnemonic: "sit there." English "sitting right there" conveys the same emphasis on visible, static presence.

### FEST- ("firm, fixed, tight")

**Festsitzen:** to sit firm, to sit stuck. The prefix locks the sitting into place — you cannot get up, cannot move on. A ship is *festgesessen* on a sandbar — stuck fast. A traveler is *festgesessen* at an airport — stranded. A screw is *festgesessen* — jammed, immovable. The metaphor of sitting becomes a metaphor of immobility: to sit so firmly that departure is impossible.

Literal mnemonic: "sit fast" (in the old sense of "fast" = immovably fixed). English "stuck fast" preserves the same construction.

### HIN- ("thither, down, toward a point")

**Hinsitzen:** to sit down (over there). The simplest directional form — the prefix gives the sitting a destination. You move toward a point and place yourself in a seated position. *Sich hinsetzen* is more common in everyday speech, but *hinsitzen* carries the same transparent meaning.

Literal mnemonic: "sit down (over there)." A straightforward spatial instruction.

### NACH- ("after, behind, following")

**Nachsitzen:** to sit after. The prefix extends the sitting past its normal endpoint. School is over, but you must keep sitting — you stay after class, you serve detention. The punishment is temporal: everyone else has left, and you sit on. German schoolchildren know this word intimately. The image is vivid: the classroom empties, and one student remains, sitting after.

Literal mnemonic: "sit after." English "stay after school" describes the same situation, but German makes it a single, precise verb.

### VOR- ("before, forward, in front of")

**Vorsitzen:** to sit before, to sit at the front. The prefix places you in the forward seat — the chair at the head of the table. To *vorsitzen* is to preside, to chair a meeting, to head an organization. A *Vorsitzender* is a chairperson — literally "one who sits before." The spatial metaphor is clear: authority sits in front, facing the assembly, visible to all.

Literal mnemonic: "sit before." English "chairman" encodes the same image — the person in the chair at the front — but as a noun. German keeps the verb alive: you actively *sit before* the group.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**Sitzen is stative, and so are its compounds.** Where *legen* verbs describe the act of placing, *sitzen* verbs describe the condition of being in place. *Festsitzen* is not "to stick something" but "to be stuck." *Dasitzen* is not "to seat someone there" but "to be sitting there." This stative quality links *sitzen* to *liegen* (to be lying) and *stehen* (to be standing) — German's three great posture verbs, all of which describe states rather than actions, and all of which generate compound verbs by the same prefix logic.

**The prefix reshapes duration and authority, not direction.** With *legen*, prefixes redirect the gesture of placing — inward, outward, together, apart. With *sitzen*, prefixes more often modify how long you sit (*aussitzen, nachsitzen, absitzen*) or what your seated position signifies socially (*vorsitzen, beisitzen, besitzen*). Sitting is already still; the prefixes give that stillness meaning.

**Besitzen reveals a deep metaphor shared with Latin.** German *besitzen* ("sit upon" = own) and Latin *possidēre* ("sit upon" = possess) arrived at the same equation independently: ownership is permanent occupation of a position. This is not borrowing — it is parallel invention. When you learn *besitzen*, you are learning not just a German word but a human metaphor.

**Separable prefixes keep the image concrete.** *Ab, auf, aus, bei, da, fest, hin, nach, vor* — these are all separable, and their meanings stay close to the physical picture of sitting. You can visualize mounting (*auf*), being stuck (*fest*), staying after (*nach*), presiding (*vor*). The one inseparable prefix, *be-*, is the one that abstracts most dramatically, turning sitting into owning.

**English sometimes uses "sit" the same way, sometimes doesn't.** "Sit out" = *aussitzen*. "Sit in" = *beisitzen*. But "own" has no connection to sitting in English (you must go to Latin *possidēre* to find the parallel). And "preside" — from Latin *praesidēre*, "to sit before" — maps exactly onto *vorsitzen*, though English speakers rarely notice the "sit" buried inside "preside." These hidden parallels, once seen, make the German forms feel less foreign and more like a language that simply kept its spatial metaphors visible.
