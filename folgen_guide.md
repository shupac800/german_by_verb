# FOLGEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Folgen** means to follow — cognate with English "follow" (both descend from the same Germanic root). It carries several related senses: to go after someone physically, to obey or comply with instructions, to ensue as a consequence, to succeed in a sequence. Hold this core image in mind: one thing coming after another, whether in space, time, logic, or authority. The prefixes below each reshape that basic gesture of following — narrowing it, intensifying it, or redirecting it toward a specific domain.

## The Prefixes

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Befolgen:** to follow with full commitment — to obey, to comply with, to observe. *Be-* takes the general act of following and stamps it firmly onto a specific object. You do not simply *folgen* in a vague sense — you *befolgen* a rule, an instruction, a piece of advice. The prefix narrows and formalizes the action: it demands a direct object and implies deliberate, thorough adherence. A doctor's orders are *befolgt* — followed to the letter. Laws are *befolgt* — observed and obeyed. Where *folgen* can mean many things, *befolgen* means exactly one: to follow something specific, consciously and completely.

Mnemonic: think of "be-" as "all over, thoroughly." To *befolgen* advice is to follow it through and through — not casually, but as a matter of discipline. English "abide by" captures the same purposeful tone.

### ER- (result, emergence, coming-forth)

**Erfolgen:** to follow forth — to ensue, to occur, to take place. *Er-* signals that something has emerged as a result. When a payment *erfolgt*, it comes forth — it happens, it is carried out. When a response *erfolgt*, it follows as a consequence of the question. The verb is impersonal and formal: things *erfolgen*, people do not. It describes events that issue from prior causes, following forth into reality. The noun **Erfolg** (success) is the thing that "follows forth" from effort — the result that emerges when work bears fruit.

Mnemonic: "follow forth into being." Something *erfolgt* the way a conclusion follows from premises — it comes into existence as the natural next step. *Erfolg* is what follows from doing things right.

### VER- (intensification, relentless pursuit)

**Verfolgen:** to follow relentlessly — to pursue, to chase, to persecute. *Ver-* intensifies the act of following and pushes it toward an extreme. To *verfolgen* someone is not merely to walk behind them but to hunt them, to track them down, to press after them without letting up. Politically, it means to persecute — to follow someone with the intent to harm. More neutrally, you can *verfolgen* a goal (pursue it with determination), a debate (follow it closely), or a strategy (carry it through). In every case, the prefix adds force and persistence: the following becomes purposeful, sustained, and difficult to escape.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "through and through, to the end." To *verfolgen* is to follow someone or something all the way — whether that means chasing a fugitive or pursuing an ambition. English "persecute" (from Latin *persequi*, to follow through) mirrors the same logic.

### NACH- ("after, behind, following")

**Nachfolgen:** to follow after — to succeed, to come after, to take over from. *Nach-* reinforces the temporal and sequential dimension of following. A king's heir *folgt ihm nach* — follows after him, takes his place. A successor is a **Nachfolger** — literally, an "after-follower." The prefix makes explicit what *folgen* sometimes only implies: that the following happens in sequence, one person or thing coming after another in a chain of succession. It can also mean to emulate — to follow after someone's example, to walk the path they walked.

Literal mnemonic: "follow after." English "successor" comes from Latin *succedere* (to go after) — *Nachfolger* is the Germanic equivalent, built from the same transparent logic: the one who follows after.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem is already versatile; the prefixes specialize it.** *Folgen* on its own can mean to follow physically, to obey, to ensue, or to succeed. Each prefix selects one of these latent senses and locks it in. *Befolgen* isolates obedience. *Erfolgen* isolates consequence. *Verfolgen* isolates pursuit. *Nachfolgen* isolates succession. The prefix does not add a foreign meaning — it picks one thread from the stem's bundle and pulls it taut.

**The inseparable prefixes push toward abstraction and formality.** *Befolgen*, *erfolgen*, and *verfolgen* all fuse permanently with the stem. They tend toward official, written, or elevated registers. Laws are *befolgt*, payments *erfolgen*, criminals are *verfolgt*. These are words of institutions — courts, contracts, regulations. The separable *nachfolgen*, by contrast, retains a more concrete, human-scale quality: one person following after another.

**English cognates help more than you might expect.** "Follow" and *folgen* share not just a common ancestor but overlapping metaphors. English "follow orders" parallels *befolgen*. "Follow from" (as in "it follows from this that...") parallels *erfolgen*. "Follow someone relentlessly" parallels *verfolgen*. Where the metaphors align, lean on them. Where German extends further — *Erfolg* as "success," *Nachfolger* as "successor" — notice how the spatial image of one thing coming after another gets projected onto achievement and authority.

**The nouns reveal the logic.** *Erfolg* (success) = the thing that follows forth from effort. *Verfolger* (pursuer) = the one who follows relentlessly. *Nachfolger* (successor) = the one who follows after. *Verfolgung* (persecution, pursuit) = the act of following to the extreme. German builds its abstractions from physical verbs, and the compound nouns preserve the original gesture even when the meaning has traveled far from it.
