# GLEICHEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Gleichen** means to equal, to resemble, to be like. It comes from *gleich* — same, equal, alike — which is cognate with the English suffix "-like" (Old English *gelīc*). Hold this core image: two things placed side by side that match. They are the same in some essential way. Nearly every prefix below takes that basic notion of sameness and reshapes it — directing it toward a goal, extending it into action, or pressing it into the transactional and the abstract.

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abgleichen:** to balance off, to reconcile, to calibrate. The prefix pulls the matching gesture into a process of checking: you hold two things apart and compare them point by point, adjusting until they agree. Data is *abgeglichen* — synchronized, brought into alignment by working through the differences. Instruments are *abgeglichen* — calibrated, their readings matched against a standard and corrected. The "off" in the prefix suggests the systematic work of eliminating discrepancies — you match *off* each item until nothing remains unreconciled.

Literal mnemonic: "match off." Think of checking items off a list — each one verified against the original, ticked off when it agrees.

### AN- ("on, onto, toward")

**Angleichen:** to move toward equality, to assimilate, to adapt. The prefix pushes the matching gesture in a direction — something is being brought *toward* sameness with something else. Prices are *angeglichen* — adjusted to match a standard. A newcomer *gleicht sich an* — adapts, conforms, assimilates into the surrounding culture. The action is never instantaneous; it implies a gradual approach, a closing of the gap between what is different and what is already established.

Literal mnemonic: "match onto" or "liken toward." Something that was different is being drawn toward resemblance — not yet identical, but moving that way.

### AUS- ("out, outward, fully through")

**Ausgleichen:** to balance out, to compensate, to equalize. This is the most common compound of *gleichen*, and the prefix gives it thoroughness — the matching is carried all the way *out*, pushed to completion. A deficit is *ausgeglichen* — balanced out, filled until it equals zero. A disadvantage is *ausgeglichen* — compensated, offset by something equal on the other side. In sports, the *Ausgleich* is the equalizer — the goal that makes the score level again. The noun *Ausgleich* (compensation, equalization) is everywhere in legal, financial, and everyday German: wherever an imbalance must be corrected, *ausgleichen* is the verb that does the work.

Literal mnemonic: "equal out." English uses the same construction — "things will even out," "it all balances out." The German simply builds it from "equal" rather than "even."

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Begleichen:** to settle, to pay, to discharge a debt. As with other *be-* verbs, the prefix takes the action and stamps it fully onto an object — here, a bill or a debt. The underlying logic is vivid: to settle an account is to *make it equal* again, to restore the balance between what was owed and what has been paid. A *Rechnung begleichen* is to pay a bill — to bring the ledger back to zero, back to equality. The metaphor of debt as imbalance and payment as restoration runs deep in German, and *begleichen* makes it explicit.

Mnemonic: think of "be-" as "thoroughly." To *begleichen* is to equalize thoroughly — the debt is zeroed out, the account made whole, the balance restored.

### VER- (transformation, intensification, completion)

**Vergleichen:** to compare, to contrast, to collate. This is one of the essential German verbs, and its logic is transparent: to compare things is to *make them equal* in order to see how they match up. You place two things into a state of provisional sameness — holding them side by side, treating them as if they were alike — so that the similarities and differences become visible. The noun *Vergleich* (comparison) is ubiquitous: *im Vergleich zu* (in comparison to) is one of the most common phrases in analytical German. Note that *ver-* here carries its transformative force — you are not discovering that things are equal, you are *making* them equal as a method, a deliberate act of intellectual alignment that reveals what is truly alike and what is not.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "into a changed state." To *vergleichen* is to put things into a state of equality — to hold them level so you can inspect the match. English "compare" comes from Latin *comparare* (to pair together), but the German word is more revealing: comparison is equalization as a tool of thought.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem carries a state; the prefixes add an action.** *Gleichen* on its own describes a condition — two things are alike. But with prefixes, that static resemblance becomes something you *do*. You equalize (*ausgleichen*), you adapt (*angleichen*), you compare (*vergleichen*), you settle (*begleichen*). The prefixes convert "being equal" into "making equal," each in a different direction and for a different purpose.

**Equality as a metaphor runs wide.** German uses *gleich* where English often reaches for entirely different words. Compensating is "equaling out." Paying a debt is "equaling it thoroughly." Comparing is "making equal." Calibrating is "matching off." Once you internalize *gleich* as the root concept, these verbs stop seeming like unrelated vocabulary items and start behaving like a system.

**The separable/inseparable split maps onto concreteness.** *Abgleichen*, *angleichen*, and *ausgleichen* are separable — their prefixes are spatial adverbs that preserve a sense of direction (off, toward, out). *Begleichen* and *vergleichen* are inseparable — their prefixes are grammatical transformers that shift the verb's meaning further from the literal. As with other German verb families, the separable compounds tend to be more transparent, while the inseparable ones require you to learn the whole unit.

**English cognates help at the root, not the branches.** The connection between *gleich* and "-like" is real and useful — it anchors the core meaning. But English does not build its compounds from "like" the way German builds from *gleich*. English says "compare," "compensate," "settle," "calibrate" — all from Latin or French roots. German says "make equal to see," "equal out," "equal thoroughly," "match off." Where English imports, German constructs. Noticing this difference is itself a kind of *Vergleich*.
