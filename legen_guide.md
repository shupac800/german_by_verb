# LEGEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Legen** means to lay, to put, to place. It implies deliberate placement — setting something down with intention. Hold this image in mind: a hand placing an object on a surface. Nearly every prefix below reshapes that basic gesture — redirecting it, intensifying it, reversing it, or abstracting it into metaphor.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Ablegen:** to lay off, to lay away. Picture removing something from yourself and placing it aside. A coat is *abgelegt* — taken off and set down. Documents are *abgelegt* — filed away, put in their place and dismissed from active concern. The prefix pulls the action away from the subject: you are shedding, discarding, depositing something so it is no longer on or with you.

Literal mnemonic: "lay off." English uses this exact construction — "lay off your coat," "lay aside your doubts."

### AN- ("on, onto, up against")

**Anlegen:** to lay on, to lay against. The prefix pushes the action toward contact. A ship *legt an* — it lays itself against the dock. You *legen Kleidung an* — you lay clothing onto your body. Money is *angelegt* — laid onto something productive, invested. A garden is *angelegt* — laid out, arranged onto the ground. In every case the gesture is the same: placing something so that it makes contact, takes hold, begins.

Literal mnemonic: "lay on." Think of "laying on a coat of paint" or "laying plans on the table."

### AUF- ("up, upon, open")

**Auflegen:** to lay upon. You *legen* the phone receiver *auf* — you place it back up onto the cradle, ending the call. You *legen* a record *auf* — you place it upon the turntable. The prefix lifts the placement onto a surface. The telephone meaning is the most common: "to hang up" is literally "to lay up."

Literal mnemonic: "lay up" or "lay upon." The receiver goes up; a compress is laid upon a wound.

### AUS- ("out, outward, spread")

**Auslegen:** to lay out. Physically, it means to spread things out — laying goods out for display, laying carpet out across a floor. Figuratively, it means to lay out the meaning of something: to interpret, to construe, to explain. You take what is compressed or hidden and spread it open so it can be examined.

Literal mnemonic: "lay out." English works the same way — "let me lay out my interpretation" means to spread your reasoning open for inspection.

### BEI- ("beside, alongside, with")

**Beilegen:** to lay beside. You *legen* a letter *bei* — you enclose something alongside the main item, lay it in beside. From this comes the idea of settling a dispute: you lay something beside the conflict — a concession, a compromise — and it resolves. To attribute a quality to someone is also to lay something alongside them.

Literal mnemonic: "lay beside / lay by." Enclosing a note is laying it by the main document. Settling a dispute is laying peace alongside the conflict.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Belegen:** to lay upon thoroughly. *Be-* is not a spatial word but a grammatical transformer — it takes the action and stamps it fully onto an object. *Belegen* means to cover something with your claim: to occupy a seat, to register for a course (claim your place), to document a fact (lay proof upon it), to impose a tax (lay a burden upon). Every sense involves asserting placement over something.

Mnemonic: think of "be-" as "all over." To *belegen* a sandwich is to lay toppings all over it (a *belegtes Brötchen*). To *belegen* a claim is to lay evidence all over it.

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Einlegen:** to lay in, to lay into. You place something inside a container or medium. A coin is *eingelegt* into a slot. Vegetables are *eingelegt* — laid into brine, pickled. An objection is *eingelegt* — lodged, placed into the record. The prefix directs the laying gesture inward, into an enclosure.

Literal mnemonic: "lay in." English preserves this in "inlay" (wood or metal set into a surface) and "laying in supplies."

### ENT- ("away from, removed from" — here fossilized)

**Entlegen** (adj.): laid away from. *Ent-* suggests separation and removal. Something *entlegen* has been laid far from the center — it is remote, outlying, out of the way. The verb is no longer active; only the adjective remains, but the spatial logic is transparent: placed away from everything else.

Literal mnemonic: "laid away from." A village that is *entlegen* has been "laid off to the side" by geography.

### ER- (achievement, completion, sometimes destruction)

**Erlegen:** to lay down conclusively. *Er-* often signals that an action reaches its terminal point. To *erlegen* an animal is to bring it down — to lay it down permanently. The prefix converts the neutral act of placing into a fatal completion. This is one of a family of *er-* verbs where the prefix means "to do something to the point of finality" (*erschlagen*, *ersticken*, *ertrinken*).

Mnemonic: "lay down for good." The animal is laid down and does not get up.

### FEST- ("firm, fixed, tight")

**Festlegen:** to lay firm. You place something and fix it so it cannot move. To *festlegen* a date is to set it firmly — to nail it down. To *festlegen* rules is to establish them as fixed. Reflexively, *sich festlegen* means to commit yourself — to lay yourself into a fixed position from which you cannot easily retreat.

Literal mnemonic: "lay fast" (in the old English sense of "fast" = firmly fixed). A date is "laid fast."

### FORT- ("away, onward, gone")

**Fortlegen:** to lay forth, to lay away. You place something away from yourself, setting it aside. Straightforward and uncommon — the prefix simply adds the direction "away" to the placing gesture.

Literal mnemonic: "lay forth" in the archaic sense of putting something aside and moving on.

### GE- (past participle prefix — here fossilized)

**Gelegen** (adj.): well-laid, well-placed. Originally the past participle of *legen* — something that has been laid. Over time, it drifted into meaning "conveniently placed, suitable, opportune." A *gelegener Zeitpunkt* is a well-placed moment. The logic: if something has been deliberately laid in a good spot, it is convenient.

Mnemonic: "well-laid." Think of "well-placed" — a well-laid opportunity is one that sits right where you need it.

### HERAUS- ("out from within")

**Herauslegen:** to lay out from inside. A simple, literal compound — you reach into something and place the contents outward. Uncommon in practice, but transparent in meaning.

Literal mnemonic: "lay out (from in here)."

### HIN- ("thither, down, toward a point away from the speaker")

**Hinlegen:** to lay down (away from yourself, onto a surface). The prefix gives direction: downward and away. You *legen* something *hin* — you set it down. Reflexively, *sich hinlegen* means to lay yourself down, to lie down to rest. The most basic, physical sense of placing something flat.

Literal mnemonic: "lay down (over there)." "I laid the book down" = *Ich habe das Buch hingelegt.*

### HINTER- ("behind")

**Hinterlegen:** to lay behind, to leave behind for safekeeping. You place something behind a counter, behind a barrier — you deposit it with someone for holding. A key is *hinterlegt* at the front desk. A security deposit is *hinterlegt* with the landlord. The "behind" is the protective barrier between the depositor and the thing deposited.

Literal mnemonic: "lay behind." You leave something behind the desk, in safekeeping.

### LOS- ("loose, free, released")

**Loslegen:** to lay loose, to let loose. The prefix means release — and when you release the act of *legen*, you burst into action. *Loslegen* means to get started, to launch into something with energy. The image is of someone held back who is suddenly set free: they lay into the task.

Literal mnemonic: "let lay" or "cut loose." English has "let loose" and "lay into" — *loslegen* captures both.

### UM- ("around, over, down")

**Umlegen:** to lay over, to lay around, to lay down. Literally, to tip something over — to lay it from upright to flat. In colloquial speech, this became a euphemism for killing: to lay someone down, to knock them over permanently. A scarf is *umgelegt* — laid around the neck. A lever is *umgelegt* — flipped over. The prefix always implies a change of orientation.

Literal mnemonic: "lay over." A tree blown over has been "laid over." The slang meaning follows naturally.

### UNTER- ("under, beneath")

**Unterlegen:** to lay under. Literally, to place something beneath — to underlay. Carpet is *unterlegt* with padding. Music is *unterlegt* with a bassline. The adjective *unterlegen* (different stress) means inferior: placed beneath, underlaid, in the lower position.

Literal mnemonic: "underlay." English uses the same word — carpet underlay is material laid under the surface.

### VER- (transformation, displacement, error, intensification)

**Verlegen:** one of the most complex *ver-* verbs, because *ver-* itself carries several distinct forces:
- *Displacement:* to lay somewhere else — to relocate, to transfer, to shift a meeting to a new date.
- *Error:* to lay wrongly — to mislay, to misplace. You meant to put it somewhere sensible but *ver-* twisted the action astray.
- *Publication:* to lay forth for distribution — to publish. A publisher (*Verlag*) lays books out into the world.
- *Adjective:* embarrassed — the emotional state of someone who has been "displaced," caught off-balance, laid in the wrong position socially.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "mis-" or "away into change." *Verlegen* your keys = mislay. *Verlegen* a pipeline = relay it elsewhere. *Verlegen* a book = lay it forth. *Verlegen* as a feeling = displaced, caught out of place.

### VOR- ("before, forward, in front of")

**Vorlegen:** to lay before. You place something in front of someone for their inspection. To *vorlegen* a document is to present it, to submit it, to lay it before the authority. To *vorlegen* a meal is to serve it — to lay it before the diner. The prefix is spatial and social: what is *vorgelegt* is placed forward, offered up.

Literal mnemonic: "lay before." "I laid the evidence before the court" = *Ich habe dem Gericht die Beweise vorgelegt.*

### WEG- ("away, gone")

**Weglegen:** to lay away. You place something away from where it was. Put the phone away. Set the book aside. The simplest "away" prefix — no metaphor, no abstraction. The thing is laid elsewhere and left.

Literal mnemonic: "lay away." English "layaway" (reserving an item at a store) captures a related sense.

### WIDER- ("against, counter")

**Widerlegen:** to lay against, to lay counter to. You place something in opposition to a claim — evidence laid against an argument. To refute is to counter-lay, to set down proof that opposes. The image is adversarial: two things are laid side by side, and one negates the other.

Literal mnemonic: "lay against." Think of "laying a counterargument against a claim."

### ZER- ("apart, to pieces, asunder")

**Zerlegen:** to lay apart, to lay asunder. The prefix is destructive — it scatters. You take something whole and lay its pieces out separately. A machine is *zerlegt* — disassembled, its parts laid apart. A chicken is *zerlegt* — carved, its joints laid open. A number is *zerlegt* — factored, broken into its components. The action is always analytical: reducing a whole to its constituent parts by laying them out.

Literal mnemonic: "lay asunder." English "analyze" comes from Greek *analusis* (loosening apart) — *zerlegen* is the Germanic equivalent.

### ZU- ("to, toward, additional, closed")

**Zulegen:** to lay to, to add to. The prefix pushes something toward an existing quantity. You *legen* speed *zu* — you add velocity, you speed up. You *legen* weight *zu* — you add mass, you gain weight. You *legen sich etwas zu* — you acquire something, you add it to your possessions. The gesture is accumulative: laying more onto what is already there.

Literal mnemonic: "lay to" in the sense of "add to." "She laid on another five pounds" captures the same logic.

### ZURÜCK- ("back, backward, in return")

**Zurücklegen:** to lay back. Two distinct images: (1) you lay something back where it came from — you return it, or you lay it back for later, setting it aside in reserve. (2) you lay distance behind you — you cover ground, you travel. The road is *zurückgelegt* — it has been laid behind you as you walked.

Literal mnemonic: "lay back." "We laid back fifty miles today" = we put fifty miles behind us. "Lay back some money" = set it aside.

### ZUSAMMEN- ("together")

**Zusammenlegen:** to lay together. You take separate things and lay them into one. Sheets are *zusammengelegt* — folded, their edges laid together. Departments are *zusammengelegt* — merged, consolidated. Money is *zusammengelegt* — pooled, everyone's contribution laid into a single pile.

Literal mnemonic: "lay together." "Let's lay our money together" = let's pool it.

### ÜBER- ("over, above, beyond")

**Überlegen:** to lay over in the mind. As a verb, *über-* directs the action upward and across — you lay your thoughts over a problem, turning it over mentally, examining it from above. To *überlegen* is to deliberate, to reflect, to think something over. As an adjective, *überlegen* means superior — laid over, on top, above the rest.

Literal mnemonic: "lay over" or "overlay." English "think it over" uses the same spatial metaphor — the mind passes over the problem, examining it from above. "Overlaid upon the competition" captures the adjective.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays physical; the prefix goes abstract.** *Legen* never stops meaning "to place." But the prefixes push that placement into metaphor. *Festlegen* (lay firm) becomes "to define." *Widerlegen* (lay against) becomes "to refute." The body's gesture of placing an object is projected onto intellectual and social actions. Once you see this, the abstractions become less arbitrary.

**Separable prefixes tend to be spatial and concrete.** *Ab, an, auf, aus, ein, hin, weg, zusammen* — these are words that can stand alone as adverbs or prepositions. They preserve a physical directionality even in figurative use. You can usually picture the motion.

**Inseparable prefixes tend to be transformative and opaque.** *Be-, er-, ver-, wider-, über-, hinter-* — these fuse with the verb and often shift its meaning further from the literal. *Ver-* is especially slippery: it can mean displacement, error, completion, or transformation, and only context tells you which. These must often be learned as units rather than decoded from parts.

**English sometimes preserves the same logic.** "Lay out" = *auslegen*. "Underlay" = *unterlegen*. "Overlay" = *überlegen*. "Lay aside" = *weglegen* or *beiseitelegen*. Where English and German share the metaphor, use it as an anchor. Where they diverge, notice *how* they diverge — German often extends the spatial metaphor further than English does, using "lay" where English switches to an entirely different verb.
