# REICHEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Reichen** carries three meanings that share a single physical gesture: the arm extending outward. To *hand* something is to extend your arm toward another person and deliver an object. To *reach* is to extend your arm toward a target. To *suffice* is for something to extend far enough — to reach the threshold of "enough." Hold this triple image in mind: an outstretched arm handing, stretching, and just barely meeting the mark. Every prefix below reshapes one or more of these three gestures.

---

## The Prefixes

### AUS- ("out, outward, fully")

**Ausreichen:** to reach out fully, to extend far enough. The prefix pushes the "suffice" sense outward to completion — something *reicht aus* when it stretches out far enough to cover the need. Your salary *reicht aus* — it extends far enough to meet your expenses. Your vocabulary *reicht aus* — it spreads out enough to handle the conversation. The "handing" sense disappears here; only the sufficiency gesture survives, intensified by *aus-* into a sense of full, adequate coverage.

Literal mnemonic: "reach out (far enough)." Think of a rope that needs to reach across a gap — when it *reicht aus*, it stretches out and makes it.

### DURCH- ("through")

**Durchreichen:** to hand through. The prefix tunnels the handing gesture through a barrier. You *reichen* food *durch* a serving hatch — you pass it through the opening. The image is always a wall, a window, a gap with someone on the other side. Your arm goes through; the object follows.

Literal mnemonic: "reach through." A pharmacist hands your prescription through the window.

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Einreichen:** to hand in. The prefix directs the handing gesture inward — into an institution, into the official record. You *reichen* an application *ein* — you hand it in to the office. You *reichen* a complaint *ein* — you file it, push it into the system. You *reichen* a resignation *ein* — you turn it in. The physical image is of sliding a document through a slot or across a counter into the hands of authority.

Literal mnemonic: "hand in." English uses the exact construction — "hand in your homework," "turn in your report."

### ER- (achievement, attainment, completion)

**Erreichen:** to reach to the point of arrival. *Er-* signals that the reaching gesture achieves its goal. Where *reichen* merely extends, *erreichen* arrives. You *erreichen* the summit — your reaching succeeds. You *erreichen* someone by phone — your attempt to make contact lands. You *erreichen* a compromise — you stretch toward a goal and grasp it. This is one of the most important *er-* verbs: the prefix converts open-ended reaching into conclusive attainment.

Mnemonic: "reach and get there." Every use of *erreichen* implies that the arm extended — and the hand closed around the target. English "achieve" and "attain" both lack the spatial transparency of *er- + reichen*.

### GE- (resultative / fossilized)

**Gereichen:** to reach toward, to result in, to serve as. This is a formal, literary verb. *Ge-* here signals a completed resulting state — something *gereicht* to your honor or your disadvantage. The reaching gesture has become entirely abstract: an action or quality extends toward a consequence. *Es gereicht ihm zur Ehre* — it reaches toward his honor, it redounds to his credit.

Mnemonic: "reach unto." The archaic English "redound to" captures the same stiff, formal quality — something extends forth and arrives at a judgment.

### HER- ("hither, toward the speaker")

**Herreichen:** to hand over (toward here). The prefix pulls the handing gesture toward the speaker. You ask someone to *herreichen* something — to pass it over, to hand it this way. Simple, spatial, and direct. The emphasis is on the direction of delivery: toward you, the one asking.

Literal mnemonic: "hand here." "Hand me that book" = *Reich mir das Buch her.*

### HIN- ("thither, toward a point away from the speaker")

**Hinreichen:** to hand over (away from yourself); to suffice. Two senses survive here. Physically, you *reichen* something *hin* — you extend it outward toward someone else, passing it along. In the sufficiency sense, something *reicht hin* when it stretches far enough to be adequate — a near-synonym of *ausreichen* but slightly less emphatic. The prefix always points away: the arm extends outward and either delivers an object or barely meets a threshold.

Literal mnemonic: "reach forth" or "hand forth." The handing goes away from you; the sufficing stretches out just far enough.

### ZU- ("to, toward")

**Zureichen:** to hand to, to pass to. The prefix directs the handing gesture toward a specific recipient. You *reichen* the salt *zu* — you pass it to someone at the table. Like *herreichen*, this is a simple spatial compound, but *zu-* emphasizes the destination rather than the direction of travel. Something is handed to where it needs to go.

Literal mnemonic: "hand to." "Pass the bread to me" = the most literal English parallel.

### ÜBER- ("over, beyond, across")

**Überreichen:** to hand over with ceremony. The prefix elevates the handing gesture — it lifts it above the everyday and into the formal, the official, the ceremonial. You *überreichen* a prize, a certificate, a bouquet. The act is public, deliberate, and dignified. Where *reichen* is passing the salt and *herreichen* is tossing a tool, *überreichen* is a diplomat presenting credentials or a mayor bestowing a key to the city. The inseparable *über-* fuses with the verb and transforms simple handing into presentation.

Literal mnemonic: "hand over" in the ceremonial sense. English distinguishes "hand someone a letter" from "present someone with an award" — German marks the same distinction by adding *über-*.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The triple stem distributes unevenly across prefixes.** *Reichen* means to hand, to suffice, and to reach — but most prefixes seize on only one or two of these. *Ausreichen* and *hinreichen* take the sufficiency sense. *Durchreichen*, *herreichen*, *zureichen*, and *überreichen* take the handing sense. *Erreichen* takes the reaching sense. *Einreichen* transforms handing into submitting. Knowing which sense a prefix activates is the key to each compound.

**Separable prefixes stay physical and directional.** *Durch-* (through an opening), *ein-* (into an office), *her-* (toward me), *hin-* (away from me), *zu-* (to someone) — each preserves a clear spatial path for the hand and the object it carries. You can picture the arm's trajectory.

**Inseparable prefixes transform the gesture.** *Er-* turns reaching into achieving. *Über-* turns handing into presenting. *Ge-* turns reaching into abstract resulting. These prefixes fuse with the stem and shift it away from the physical — you must learn them as complete units.

**ERREICHEN deserves special attention.** It is among the most common German verbs and covers territory that English splits across "reach," "achieve," "attain," "arrive at," "get to," and "accomplish." The single image — reaching that arrives — unifies all of these. When you *erreichen* something, the outstretched arm finally closes its grip.

**ÜBERREICHEN marks social register.** German often uses prefix choice to signal formality. Where *reichen* and *herreichen* are neutral and everyday, *überreichen* belongs to ceremonies, official acts, and public occasions. The prefix *über-* does not just mean "over" — it elevates the entire transaction. English makes the same move with different words entirely ("present" vs. "hand"), but German does it within the same verb family.

**English parallels are partial but useful.** "Hand in" = *einreichen*. "Hand through" = *durchreichen*. "Hand over" (formally) = *überreichen*. "Reach" (arrive) = *erreichen*. But English "suffice" has no spatial connection to "hand" or "reach" — this is where the German stem's triple meaning diverges most sharply from English. To an English speaker, handing and sufficing feel unrelated; to a German speaker, they are both extensions of the same outstretched arm.
