# STREICHEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Streichen** carries five distinct but related core meanings: to paint, to spread, to stroke, to delete, and to bow (a stringed instrument). The common thread is a hand — or an object held in the hand — moving across a surface in a continuous motion. A brush drawn across a wall paints it. A knife drawn across bread spreads butter. A palm drawn across a surface strokes it. A pen drawn through a word deletes it. A bow drawn across strings produces sound. Hold this image in mind: a long, smooth, horizontal motion across a surface. Every prefix below redirects, intensifies, or abstracts that fundamental gesture.

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from")

**Abstreichen:** to stroke off, to scrape off. The prefix pulls the motion away from the surface. You draw a blade across a container's rim to remove the excess — you *streichen* it *ab*. A plasterer scrapes off surplus plaster. The gesture is always subtractive: the smooth horizontal motion is now in the service of removal.

Literal mnemonic: "strike off." English "strike off" and "scrape off" use the same directional logic — the hand moves across and away.

### AN- ("on, onto, up against")

**Anstreichen:** to stroke onto. The prefix pushes the motion toward the surface, bringing paint or coating into contact with it. To *anstreichen* a wall is to paint it — to draw a brush onto the surface so that color adheres. To *anstreichen* a passage in a book is to mark it — to make a stroke against it. Both senses involve applying something onto a surface through that characteristic horizontal motion.

Literal mnemonic: "paint on" or "mark on." Think of "putting a coat on the wall."

### AUF- ("up, upon, open")

**Aufstreichen:** to stroke upon. You draw a knife across bread and lay butter upon it. You apply ointment upon skin. The prefix lifts the substance up onto the receiving surface. Where *anstreichen* emphasizes contact with the wall (paint), *aufstreichen* emphasizes the material being placed upon a base (spread). The distinction is subtle but real: *auf-* foregrounds the upward layering.

Literal mnemonic: "spread upon." "Spread it on" is the natural English equivalent.

### AUS- ("out, outward, through")

**Ausstreichen:** to stroke out. The prefix extends the motion outward and through. To cross out a word is to draw a line through it, extending the stroke until the word is negated. To smooth out a crease is to draw the hand across the surface until the wrinkle is gone. In both cases the motion pushes outward to completion — the stroke finishes its work.

Literal mnemonic: "strike out." English uses exactly this — "strike out a name" means to draw a line through it and eliminate it.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Bestreichen:** to stroke all over. *Be-* stamps the action thoroughly onto an object, making the surface itself the direct object. You *bestreichen* bread with butter — you spread the entire surface, coating it comprehensively. You *bestreichen* a roast with a glaze. The motion is the same as plain *streichen*, but *be-* ensures total coverage: the whole surface is worked over.

Mnemonic: think of *be-* as "all over." To *bestreichen* is to coat something completely — every part of the surface receives the stroke.

### DURCH- ("through")

**Durchstreichen:** to stroke through. The most literal of the deletion senses. You draw a line directly through a word, a number, a passage. Where *ausstreichen* can mean "smooth out," *durchstreichen* is unambiguous: the stroke passes through the text, negating it. This is what you do with a pen when you want something visibly cancelled.

Literal mnemonic: "strike through." English "strikethrough" (the typographic term) is a direct calque of this verb.

### EIN- ("in, into, inward")

**Einstreichen:** to stroke in, to take in. This prefix produces two vivid images. First, the physical: rubbing a cream or ointment *into* the skin, working it inward with a stroking motion. Second, the figurative — and more common — sense: pocketing money, raking in profits. The image is of sweeping something inward toward yourself with a broad stroke, the way a card player sweeps winnings off the table and into their pile. The prefix directs the stroke inward, toward the collector.

Literal mnemonic: "rake in." English "rake in the profits" captures the same sweeping-inward gesture.

### HERAUS- ("out from within")

**Herausstreichen:** to stroke out from within — to bring something out into the light by emphasizing it. You reach into a text, a presentation, a situation and draw out the important point, stroking it into prominence. To *herausstreichen* a quality is to highlight it, to hold it up for notice. The prefix provides the direction: out from among other things and forward, into focus.

Literal mnemonic: "bring out." English "bring out the key point" or "highlight" follows the same spatial logic — pulling something from the background into view.

### UNTER- ("under, beneath")

**Unterstreichen:** to stroke under. The most physically transparent of the group. You draw a line beneath a word — you underline it. From this literal act of marking the underside comes the figurative sense: to emphasize, to underscore. When you underline a point in an argument, you are drawing attention beneath it, giving it a visible foundation of importance. English and German share the metaphor perfectly: "underscore" is *unter* + *streichen* in translation.

Literal mnemonic: "underscore." This is one of those rare cases where the English word is a near-exact compound translation of the German.

### VER- (transformation, completion, passage)

**Verstreichen:** to stroke away into transformation — and here *ver-* does double duty, producing two distinct senses that only connect at an abstract level.

First, the physical: to spread filler, plaster, or putty into a crack — to work material in until the gap disappears. The *ver-* signals completion and transformation: the crack is *verstrichen*, filled in, smoothed over, gone.

Second, the temporal: time *verstreicht* — it passes, elapses, goes by. The image is of time being stroked away, smoothed out behind you like plaster over a surface, until the gap between then and now has been filled and nothing remains. The *ver-* signals irreversible passage: once time has *verstrichen*, it is transformed into the past and cannot be recovered.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "away into change." Putty is stroked away into the crack until the crack is changed into a smooth surface. Time is stroked away into the past until it has changed into history.

### VOR- ("before, in front of, preliminary")

**Vorstreichen:** to stroke before — to apply a preliminary coat. The prefix situates the action before the main event. You *streichen vor* before you paint properly: an undercoat, a primer, a base layer. The gesture is the same as painting, but *vor-* marks it as preparatory, as the stroke that comes first.

Literal mnemonic: "pre-coat." English "prime" serves the same function — you lay down color before the real color.

### WEG- ("away, gone")

**Wegstreichen:** to stroke away. You draw a line through something and it is gone — deleted, crossed out, removed. The prefix is the simplest directional marker: the stroke carries the thing away. Where *durchstreichen* emphasizes the line passing *through* the text, and *ausstreichen* emphasizes the completed erasure, *wegstreichen* emphasizes disappearance: the word was there, and now it has been stroked away.

Literal mnemonic: "strike away." The stroke carries the word off into nonexistence.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The five-way stem is less chaotic than it looks.** Paint, spread, stroke, delete, bow — these all resolve into a single physical gesture: drawing something smoothly across a surface. Once you hold that image, each meaning is just a different material being drawn across a different surface. A brush across a wall = paint. A knife across bread = spread. A hand across fur = stroke. A pen across a word = delete. A bow across strings = music. The motion never changes; only the context does.

**Deletion is the most productive figurative branch.** Four prefixes — *aus-, durch-, weg-,* and the bare stem — can mean "to cross out" or "to delete." They differ in emphasis: *durchstreichen* (the line goes through), *ausstreichen* (the word is struck out completely), *wegstreichen* (the word disappears), and plain *streichen* (the neutral, general term). This cluster reflects how central the act of crossing-out is to the metaphorical life of the verb.

**Separable prefixes stay spatial and concrete.** *Ab, an, auf, aus, durch, ein, heraus, vor, weg* — these preserve a physical directionality. You can picture the brush or hand moving off, on, upon, out, through, in, up-out, before, or away. Even *einstreichen* in its "pocket the money" sense retains the inward sweep.

**Inseparable prefixes transform and abstract.** *Be-, unter-, ver-* fuse with the stem and shift meaning further from the literal stroke. *Bestreichen* turns the surface into the grammatical object. *Unterstreichen* turns a physical line into intellectual emphasis. *Verstreichen* turns a physical filling-in into the passage of time itself — one of the most striking metaphorical leaps in the family.

**English mirrors German more closely here than with most verb stems.** "Strike through" = *durchstreichen*. "Strike out" = *ausstreichen*. "Underscore" = *unterstreichen*. "Spread on" = *aufstreichen*. "Coat" = *bestreichen*. Where the metaphors align, lean on them. Where they diverge — particularly with *einstreichen* (rake in) and *verstreichen* (elapse) — notice that German extends the stroking image into domains where English abandons it entirely.
