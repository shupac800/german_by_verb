# SUCHEN — A Prefix Guide for English Speakers

## The Stem

**Suchen** means to search, to seek, to look for. It implies an active scanning — eyes moving, attention probing, effort directed toward finding something not yet in hand. Hold this image in mind: someone sweeping a space with their gaze, reaching toward what is missing. Every prefix below reshapes that search — focusing it, completing it, redirecting it, or abstracting it into social and intellectual domains.

---

## The Prefixes

### AB- ("off, away from, systematically")

**Absuchen:** to search off, to search across methodically. The prefix imposes thoroughness and coverage — you do not merely glance, you sweep the entire area, section by section. A field is *abgesucht* for mines — scoured, combed, every patch searched and cleared. A dog *sucht* the ground *ab* for a scent. The prefix converts a loose search into a systematic sweep, leaving nothing unchecked.

Literal mnemonic: "search off" in the sense of checking things off a list. You search each zone and move on to the next until the whole surface has been covered.

### AUF- ("up, upon, out toward")

**Aufsuchen:** to seek out, to seek up. The prefix lifts the search from passive looking to purposeful pursuit — you go to someone or something deliberately. You *suchen* a doctor *auf* — you seek them out, you go to them. You *suchen* a place *auf* — you visit it with intention. The prefix suggests rising from your chair and going toward what you seek, rather than waiting for it to appear.

Literal mnemonic: "seek out" or "look up." English "look someone up" captures the same directed motion — you actively go to find them.

### AUS- ("out, outward, through to selection")

**Aussuchen:** to search out, to pick out. The prefix drives the search to its conclusion: you look through the options and extract one. A child *sucht sich* a toy *aus* — searches through the pile and selects. The emphasis has shifted from the searching process to its result. To *aussuchen* is not just to look but to finish looking, to emerge with a choice in hand.

Literal mnemonic: "seek out" in the sense of singling out. You search out the best one from the rest.

### BE- (intensifier / makes the verb transitive)

**Besuchen:** to seek upon, to visit. *Be-* stamps the action fully onto its object — you do not merely search toward someone, you arrive at them. The original seeking has been completed so thoroughly that it has become a social act: a visit, an attendance. You *besuchen* a friend — you sought them and now you are with them. You *besuchen* a lecture — you attend it, you go to it, you have carried through on the seeking. The search is over; the finding has become the meaning.

Mnemonic: think of "be-" as "all the way to." To *besuchen* someone is to seek them out completely — to seek *at* them, to arrive and stay. English "visit" hides the journey; German preserves it. Every visit began as a search.

### DURCH- ("through, all the way through")

**Durchsuchen:** to search through. The prefix drives the action through an enclosed space — you do not search the surface but penetrate inward, opening drawers, turning over contents, rifling through everything. Police *durchsuchen* an apartment — they search it through, leaving nothing unexamined. You *durchsuchen* your pockets — you rummage through them. The prefix adds depth and invasion to what might otherwise be a surface scan.

Literal mnemonic: "search through." English uses the identical construction — "search through the files," "search through the wreckage."

### ER- (achievement, attainment, reaching a result)

**Ersuchen:** to seek toward a result — to request, to petition. *Er-* signals that the seeking has been formalized into an appeal: you do not simply look for something, you direct your seeking at a person with authority, asking them to provide it. An *Ersuchen* is a formal request — a letter, a petition, a legal motion. The casualness of *suchen* has been elevated into bureaucratic or diplomatic language. You are seeking, but through official channels.

Mnemonic: *er-* = "seek to completion through proper form." Where *suchen* is informal and physical, *ersuchen* is deliberate and institutional. Think of it as seeking that has put on a suit.

### HERAUS- ("out from within")

**Heraussuchen:** to search out from inside. You reach into a collection — a drawer, a pile, a list — and pull your selection outward. Functionally close to *aussuchen*, but the prefix *heraus-* emphasizes the direction more vividly: the chosen thing is drawn out toward the speaker from within a larger mass.

Literal mnemonic: "seek out (from in here)." You dig into the box and pull out the one you want.

### NACH- ("after, behind, following up")

**Nachsuchen:** to search after. Two related senses flow from the prefix. (1) You search for something after the fact, looking it up, checking references — the search follows behind a question. (2) You apply for something — you seek after an official decision, submitting a request and waiting for the answer to follow. In both cases, *nach-* places the search in a sequence: something happened first, and now you are seeking in its wake.

Literal mnemonic: "seek after." "Look it up afterward" captures the first sense. "Apply after" — that is, present your case and wait for the result to follow — captures the second.

### UNTER- ("under, beneath")

**Untersuchen:** to seek under, to search beneath the surface. The prefix drives the search downward and inward — past the visible, into what is hidden. A doctor *untersucht* a patient — examines the body beneath the clothes and skin. A detective *untersucht* a case — probes beneath the surface facts. A scientist *untersucht* a sample — inspects its internal composition. The word covers examination, investigation, and medical checkups alike, because all three involve the same gesture: looking under.

Literal mnemonic: "seek under." English "look into" uses a different spatial metaphor for the same idea. German goes under; English goes in. Both move past the surface.

### VER- (transformation, displacement, intensification)

**Versuchen:** to seek through transformation — to try, to attempt, to tempt. *Ver-* reshapes the seeking into something more charged. (1) As "try" or "attempt," *versuchen* means to pursue something whose outcome is uncertain. The search has become a test — you seek a result and may or may not find it. You *versuchen* to open the door, to solve the problem, to make it work. (2) As "tempt," *versuchen* flips the direction: now something is seeking *you*, pulling you toward it. A *Versuchung* is a temptation — a lure that seeks you out and transforms your will. The Devil *versucht* — he seeks to lead astray.

Mnemonic: *ver-* = "seek into the uncertain." An attempt is a search that might fail. A temptation is a force seeking to change you. Both meanings share the core idea of seeking toward something not yet settled — effort without guaranteed arrival.

## Patterns Worth Noting

A few observations for the English-speaking learner:

**The stem stays concrete; the prefix goes abstract.** *Suchen* never stops meaning "to search." But the prefixes push that search into social, intellectual, and institutional domains. *Besuchen* (seek upon) becomes "to visit." *Ersuchen* (seek to conclusion) becomes "to petition." *Versuchen* (seek through change) becomes "to try." The physical act of scanning for a lost object is projected onto diplomacy, science, and willpower. The body's gesture of looking is preserved beneath every abstraction.

**Separable prefixes tend to stay physical and directional.** *Ab-, auf-, aus-, heraus-, nach-* — these preserve the bodily sense of searching. You can picture the motion: sweeping across (*ab-*), going toward (*auf-*), pulling from a pile (*aus-*, *heraus-*), following up afterward (*nach-*). These verbs feel like searches with a compass heading.

**Inseparable prefixes fuse with the stem and shift the meaning further.** *Be-, durch-, er-, unter-, ver-* — these transform *suchen* into something that no longer feels like searching at all. You would not guess that *besuchen* (visit), *ersuchen* (petition), or *versuchen* (try) contain the idea of seeking unless someone pointed it out. But once you see it, the logic holds: every visit is a completed search, every petition is a formalized search, every attempt is a search with uncertain outcome, every investigation is a search beneath the surface.

**English sometimes preserves the same logic, sometimes diverges.** "Search through" = *durchsuchen*. "Seek out" = *aufsuchen*. But "visit" hides the seeking that German *besuchen* preserves. "Investigate" comes from Latin *vestigare* (to track), while *untersuchen* stays with Germanic "seek under." Where the metaphors align, use them as anchors. Where they diverge, notice what each language chose to emphasize: German keeps the seeker visible in the word; English often replaces the search with its result.
